## Standard Reference Textbooks by Subject

### Anatomy

| Book | Author | Use |
|---|---|---|
| BD Chaurasia's Human Anatomy | BD Chaurasia | Indian standard; most widely used for NEET PG; 4 volumes |
| Gray's Anatomy | Standring | Reference for clinical anatomy details; not read cover-to-cover |
| Clinical Anatomy | Snell | Good for clinical correlations; smaller and focused |
| Last's Anatomy | Last | Regional approach; good for surgical anatomy |

**Volume-wise guide for BD Chaurasia:**
- Vol. 1: Upper limb + Thorax
- Vol. 2: Lower limb + Abdomen
- Vol. 3: Head, Neck, Brain
- Vol. 4: Histology + Embryology (CNS neuroanatomy)

**NEET PG Strategy:** BD Chaurasia (all volumes) + Vishram Singh (for embryology) is sufficient. Do not try to read Gray's unless you have a specific query.

| Book | Author | Use |

### SPM / Community Medicine

| Book | Author | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Park's Textbook of Preventive and Social Medicine | K Park | The definitive Indian standard; updated yearly with national program data |
| Mahajan & Gupta — Textbook of Preventive and Social Medicine | Mahajan & Gupta | Alternative; slightly more concise; good for statistical methods |
| Community Medicine with Recent Advances | Ilyas Bhat | Comprehensive with MCQ integration |

**NEET PG Strategy:** Park's is mandatory — it is the source from which most SPM MCQs are directly derived. Read Park's thoroughly. Pay special attention to biostatistics chapter, nutrition chapter, and national health programs chapter (update every year).

## Study Plan and Strategy

### Phase 1: Reading Phase (Months 1–8)

**Goal:** Cover all subjects once with understanding.

**Daily schedule:**
- 10–12 hours/day
- Morning: 3–4 hours for one subject (theory + reading)
- Afternoon: 2–3 hours continue or switch to a lighter subject
- Evening: 2–3 hours for MCQ practice from PYQs of the subject studied
- Night: 1 hour revision/one-liners

**Notes-making strategy:**
- Do not make elaborate notes — annotate the textbook directly
- Use Post-it notes for key facts within chapters
- Maintain one dedicated notebook per subject for:
  - One-liners that come from MCQ explanations
  - Comparison tables (make your own — they stick better)
  - Mnemonics

**Subject order recommendation:**
1. Anatomy (large, needs time; get it out of the way)
2. Physiology (provides foundation for all clinical subjects)
3. Biochemistry (links with Physiology)
4. Pathology (most important para-clinical; highest weightage)
5. Pharmacology (high yield; return to this often)
6. Microbiology (systematic; do after Pathology)
7. Forensic Medicine (small subject; finish in 2 weeks)
8. SPM/Community Medicine (large but predictable; do in weeks 6–7 approx.)
9. General Medicine (largest clinical subject; allow 3–4 weeks)
10. Surgery (allow 2–3 weeks)
11. OBG (allow 2–3 weeks)
12. Pediatrics (allow 1–2 weeks)
13. ENT (1 week)
14. Ophthalmology (1 week)
15. Psychiatry (1 week)
16. Dermatology (1 week)
17. Orthopedics (1 week)
18. Anesthesia (4–5 days)
19. Radiology (4–5 days)

---

### Phase 2: MCQ Practice Phase (Months 4–10, Overlapping with Phase 1)

**Goal:** Solve subject-wise PYQs and MCQ banks alongside reading.

**Start MCQs:** After completing each subject's first read (not before).

**Daily MCQ target:**
- During reading phase: 50–100 MCQs/day (subject-specific)
- During dedicated MCQ phase: 150–200 MCQs/day

**Analysis method for wrong answers:**
1. Identify category: Concept gap → re-read. Recall gap → add to one-liner list. Trick → note the question pattern.
2. Do NOT simply move on after checking the answer.
3. Update your error notebook.

**How many per day:**
- Pre-clinical subjects: 50–75 MCQs/day per subject
- Clinical subjects: 75–100 MCQs/day per subject
- Grand Test: 200 questions in 3.5 hours (simulate exam conditions)

---

### Phase 3: Revision Phase (Months 9–11)

**Goal:** Rapid high-yield revision; consolidate weak areas.

**Methods:**
- Read only annotations + starred content in textbooks (not full chapters)
- Daily: 50–75 one-liners from master one-liner list
- Solve year-wise NEET PG papers (2024 → 2023 → 2022 → 2021 → 2020)
- Review comparison tables daily (rotate through all 20 tables in 1 week)
- Review mnemonics every 2–3 days

**Revision order (priority-based):**
1. Pathology, Pharmacology, Medicine (highest yield)
2. SPM, Microbiology, Surgery (moderate yield, important)
3. OBG, Pediatrics, Anatomy (solid but predictable)
4. ENT, Ophthalmology, Psychiatry, Dermatology, Orthopedics (smaller, finish fast)
5. Biochemistry, Physiology (concepts clear; just review key values)
6. Forensic, Anesthesia, Radiology (last — small and predictable)

---

### Phase 4: Grand Tests (Months 10–12)

**Goal:** Simulate exam, identify gaps, track performance trends.

**Frequency:**
- Month 10: 1 grand test per week
- Month 11: 2 grand tests per week
- Month 12 (last 4 weeks): 3–4 grand tests per week + full error analysis

**Analysis method after each grand test:**
1. Calculate score and percentile
2. Subject-wise breakdown: identify lowest-scoring subjects
3. Category breakdown: concept gap vs recall vs trick
4. Error notebook update: add new wrong answers
5. Schedule targeted subject re-revision for weak areas

**Recommended grand test sources:**
- PrepLadder Grand Tests (most similar to NEET PG difficulty)
- Marrow Mock Tests
- DAMS Test Series
- Actual NEET PG PYQ papers (2022, 2023, 2024 — use these as anchor tests)

---

### Daily Timetable Example (12-Month Plan)

**Morning Block (6:00 AM – 10:00 AM): 4 hours**
- Theory reading (current subject being covered in phase)
- Annotate directly in textbook

**Mid-Morning Block (10:30 AM – 1:00 PM): 2.5 hours**
- Continuation of theory or move to second subject (lighter one)
- Make comparison tables if relevant topic

**Afternoon Block (2:00 PM – 5:00 PM): 3 hours**
- MCQ practice (subject just covered)
- Analyze wrong answers (30 min mandatory)

**Evening Block (5:30 PM – 8:30 PM): 3 hours**
- Revision of previous subject (one-liners + annotations)
- OR Grand test (on test days — full 3.5 hours, uninterrupted)

**Night Block (9:00 PM – 10:30 PM): 1.5 hours**
- Mnemonics review
- One-liners from master list
- Error notebook review

**Total: 14 hours/day (with breaks)**

Note: Adjust as needed based on personal pace. Consistency matters more than total hours.

### How to Handle Weak Subjects

**Identify weak subjects early:**
- Track performance in each subject from the first grand test
- Any subject where accuracy falls below 50% is a red flag

**Strategy for weak subjects:**
1. Do NOT skip — a weak subject at 60% still yields more marks than abandoning it
2. Simplify: Use MCQ-based review books for that subject instead of textbooks
3. Watch video lectures (PrepLadder/Marrow) — visual + audio helps weak subjects
4. Spend 30 extra minutes daily on the weak subject during revision phase
5. Do subject-specific PYQs — identify if it is specific topics within the subject or the whole subject
6. Use mnemonics aggressively for memory-heavy subjects (Forensic, Microbiology, Pharmacology)

**Subject-specific tips for common weak areas:**

- **Biochemistry** (commonly weak): Focus on metabolic pathways through diagrams. Master 5 core pathways (glycolysis, TCA, fatty acid oxidation, amino acid metabolism, urea cycle). Rest follows from understanding.
- **Anatomy** (memorization-heavy): Use 3D visualization (YouTube/atlas). Understand structural relationships, do not memorize in isolation.
- **SPM** (formula-heavy): Make a one-page formula sheet. Practice numerical MCQs separately from conceptual ones.
- **Radiology** (image-based): Do image-based question practice specifically. PrepLadder/Marrow has dedicated image QBanks.

## Key Things to Avoid

1. **Starting MCQs too early** — Without understanding, MCQs become rote memorization. Start only after completing a topic/chapter.
2. **Reading too many books** — One good book per subject is enough. Two books create confusion and wasted time.
3. **Neglecting PYQ analysis** — Solving PYQs without analyzing wrong answers is the most common mistake.
4. **Ignoring small subjects** — Anesthesia and Radiology are easy marks with focused effort. Do not skip them.
5. **Not updating national program data** — SPM questions are directly from Park's latest edition + MoHFW circulars. Check each year.
6. **Leaving grand test analysis for later** — Analyze within 24 hours of writing the test, while mistakes are fresh.
7. **Changing books mid-preparation** — Stick to chosen books. Changing creates FOMO and gaps.
8. **Social media during study hours** — Standard advice but worth reiterating. Phone in another room during study blocks.
