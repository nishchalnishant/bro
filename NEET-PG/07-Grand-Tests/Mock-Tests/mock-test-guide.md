# Grand Test (Mock Test) Guide for NEET PG Preparation

---

## 1. When to Start Grand Tests

**Rule**: Do not attempt a full-length Grand Test before completing at least 60% of the syllabus.

### Phase-wise GT Readiness

| Phase | Syllabus Completion | GT Type to Start |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Phase 1 | 0–40% | No GTs. Subject-wise tests only after completing each subject |
| Phase 2 | 40–60% | Sectional tests (Pre-clinical + Para-clinical groups) |
| Phase 3 | 60–80% | First full-length GTs. 1 per week maximum |
| Phase 4 | 80–95% | 2 full-length GTs per week |
| Final Month | 95–100% | 3–4 GTs per week (every 2 days) |

**Why this matters**: Taking GTs too early reinforces gaps as correct patterns. Your brain needs a knowledge base to retrieve from — testing an empty database trains panic, not performance.

---

## 2. Frequency of Grand Tests by Phase

| Time to Exam | Recommended Frequency | Focus |
|--------------|----------------------|-------|
| 6+ months out | 1 subject test per subject completed | Subject mastery |
| 3–6 months out | 1 full GT per week + 2 subject tests | Building stamina and identifying gaps |
| 1–3 months out | 2 full GTs per week | Speed and accuracy refinement |
| Final month | 1 GT every 2 days | Exam simulation, last-minute pattern recognition |
| Final 2 weeks | 1 GT every 2–3 days + review days | No new GTs within 3 days of exam |

**Do not take a GT the day before or day of exam.** Use that time for light revision of high-yield facts.

## 3. Types of Tests — When to Use Each

### Subject Tests
- **What**: 50–100 questions from a single subject
- **When to use**: Immediately after completing revision of that subject
- **Purpose**: Confirms retention before moving on; identifies weak subtopics within the subject
- **Platforms**: PrepLadder subject-wise tests, Marrow subject tests

### Sectional Tests
- **What**: 100–150 questions from a group of related subjects (e.g., Pre-clinical: Anatomy + Physiology + Biochemistry)
- **When to use**: After completing a subject group, before starting full-length GTs
- **Purpose**: Tests integration across related subjects; simulates partial exam pressure
- **Groups**:
  - Pre-clinical: Anatomy, Physiology, Biochemistry
  - Para-clinical: Pathology, Pharmacology, Microbiology, FMT, SPM
  - Clinical: Medicine, Surgery, OBG, Pediatrics, Psychiatry, Dermatology, Ortho, Anesthesia, Radiology, ENT, Ophthalmology

### Full-Length Grand Tests
- **What**: 200 questions, all subjects, 3.5 hours
- **When to use**: Phase 3 onwards (60%+ syllabus complete)
- **Purpose**: Real exam simulation, stamina building, time management, percentile tracking
- **Non-negotiable**: Must be taken under full exam conditions (see Section 4)

## 4. Simulation Protocol — How to Take a Test Like the Real Exam

The value of a GT is destroyed if it is not taken under real conditions. Follow this protocol every single time.

### Before the Test
- [ ] Schedule it for **9:00 AM – 12:30 PM** (exact NEET PG time slot)
- [ ] Sleep by 10:30 PM the night before
- [ ] Wake up at 7:00 AM, eat a light breakfast (same as exam day routine)
- [ ] Sit at a desk, not on a bed or couch
- [ ] Phone on silent and face-down in another room, or in a drawer
- [ ] No notes, no books, no tabs open other than the test
- [ ] Use scratch paper for rough work only — no writing formulas before starting

### During the Test
- [ ] No breaks — not even for water or bathroom (train bladder control)
- [ ] No looking up anything mid-test
- [ ] Mark difficult questions and move on — do not spend more than 90 seconds on any question
- [ ] Track time: aim to complete 200 questions in 3 hours, leaving 30 minutes for review
- [ ] Do not pause the test

### After the Test
- [ ] Submit the test when time is up — do not continue after 3.5 hours
- [ ] Take a 15-minute break before starting analysis
- [ ] Begin post-test analysis protocol (see Section 5) within the same day

## 5. Post-Test Analysis Protocol

**This is the most important part.** A GT taken without analysis is preparation time wasted. Analysis time should equal or exceed test time.

### Step 1: Score Calculation and Percentile Interpretation

- Record: Raw score, percentile, rank, and number of correct/incorrect/unattempted
- Calculate accuracy: (Correct / Attempted) × 100
- Calculate attempt rate: (Attempted / 200) × 100
- Benchmark against percentile (see Section 6 for interpretation)

**Target metrics per GT**:
| Metric | Minimum | Target | Excellent |
|--------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Attempt rate | 85% | 92% | 97%+ |
| Accuracy | 60% | 70% | 78%+ |
| Score (/800) | 400 | 520 | 600+ |

### Step 2: Question-by-Question Review

- Review **every wrong question** — not just the ones you flagged
- Review **every correct question you were uncertain about** — lucky correct answers are future wrong answers
- For each wrong question:
  - Read the full question stem again
  - Read all four options with their explanations
  - Identify exactly where your reasoning broke down
- Do not rush this step. 200 questions will take 2–4 hours to review properly

### Step 3: Categorizing Wrong Answers

Assign each wrong answer one of five categories:

| Category | Definition | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Concept Gap** | You did not know the underlying concept | Didn't know mechanism of drug action |
| **Recall Gap** | You knew the concept but could not retrieve the specific fact | Forgot the exact dose/value/classification |
| **Silly Mistake** | You knew it, misread the question, or marked wrong option | Read "EXCEPT" as a positive, clicked wrong option |
| **Trap Question** | A well-constructed distractor that most students get wrong | Classic image-based or clinical scenario twist |
| **Never Studied** | Topic was entirely outside your preparation | Rare disease, obscure fact |

### Step 4: Converting Errors to Action Items

After categorizing:

| Error Type | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Concept Gap | Return to that chapter in textbook/video. Re-read and make a short note |
| Recall Gap | Add to flashcard/revision sheet. Revise within 48 hours, then at 1 week |
| Silly Mistake | Note the pattern (e.g., always misread "EXCEPT"). Create a pre-test checklist item |
| Trap Question | Add to error notebook with the reasoning. Do not panic — these are expected |
| Never Studied | Evaluate if topic is high-yield. If yes, add to revision plan. If no, mark and skip |

### Step 5: Revision Plan Based on Test Results

- List the top 3 subjects by wrong answer count
- For each: identify the top 2–3 subtopics with most errors
- Schedule focused revision of those subtopics within the next 3 days
- Do not attempt another GT until those subtopics have been revised
- Add all wrong answers to the Error Notebook (see Error Notebook Template)

## 7. Red Flags in GT Performance

### Red Flag Patterns and Fixes

| Red Flag | What It Means | Fix |
|----------|--------------|-----|
| Score not improving after 5+ GTs | Revision loop is broken — reviewing but not retaining | Switch revision method (active recall instead of passive reading) |
| Accuracy dropping as attempt rate increases | Guessing without knowledge base | Reduce attempts — only attempt questions with >50% confidence |
| Same subjects always at bottom | Skipping revision of weak subjects | Dedicate 1 week exclusively to the bottom subject |
| Score varies wildly (±100 points between GTs) | Inconsistency from syllabus gaps | More GTs needed, not less — identify the variable |
| High accuracy but low score (low attempts) | Over-cautiousness | Practice timed subject tests to build confidence |
| Excellent score on platform but poor on others | Platform familiarity bias | Rotate platforms — do not repeat the same platform consecutively |
| Clinical subjects consistently outperforming pre-clinical | Pre-clinical revision neglect | Schedule 2 pre-clinical revision days per week |
| Percentile not matching expected score | Platform percentile inflation | Cross-check on 2 platforms before concluding |

## 8. Recommended GT Platforms

| Platform | GT Features | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|----------|------------|-----------|-----------|
| **PrepLadder** | 200Q GTs, subject tests, detailed analytics | Best question explanations, video solutions | Subscription cost |
| **Marrow** | GTs with difficulty tagging, topic-wise breakdown | Largest question bank, PYQ integration | Interface can be slow |
| **DAMS** | Structured GTs with rank projections | Established rank predictor, clinical focus | Fewer analytics features |
| **MagicGuru** | AI-adaptive tests, performance heatmaps | Strong analytics dashboard | Smaller question bank |
| **Prepladder Test Series** | Dedicated test series separate from question bank | Mirrors NEET PG format closely | Access requires separate subscription |

**Recommendation**: Use 2 platforms, not one. Primary platform for daily QBank, secondary platform for GTs to avoid familiarity bias.

## 9. Subject-wise Test Strategy

### Order of Subject Tests (Recommended)

Start subject tests in this order, aligned with typical study sequence:

**Pre-clinical Group** (test after completing each):
1. Anatomy
2. Physiology
3. Biochemistry

**Para-clinical Group**:
4. Pathology
5. Pharmacology
6. Microbiology
7. Forensic Medicine & Toxicology
8. Social & Preventive Medicine (SPM/PSM)

**Clinical Group**:
9. Medicine (largest — split into 2 tests if needed)
10. Surgery
11. OBG
12. Pediatrics
13. Psychiatry
14. Dermatology
15. Orthopedics
16. Anesthesia
17. Radiology
18. ENT
19. Ophthalmology

### Subject Test Rules
- Take the subject test within 3 days of completing subject revision
- Score below 60%: Revise the subject again before moving on
- Score 60–70%: Note weak subtopics, move on, revisit during Phase 3
- Score above 70%: Move on; revisit only high-error subtopics

### Time Management Strategy Within the Test

| Time Block | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| 0:00–0:05 | Read instructions, set mental clock |
| 0:05–2:45 | Attempt all 200 questions (avg 80 sec/question) |
| 2:45–3:15 | Review flagged questions |
| 3:15–3:30 | Final review — check for unattempted questions |

### Marking Strategy
- **Attempt** if confidence > 50%
- **Skip** if complete guess (random 25% is worse than negative marking math)
- **Elimination**: If you can eliminate 2 options, attempt even with uncertainty
- **Never change** an answer unless you have a specific reason — first instinct is usually correct
