## CHOLINERGIC SYSTEM & MUSCARINIC AGENTS (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A 65-year-old man with benign prostatic hyperplasia and narrow-angle glaucoma is prescribed an antispasmodic for irritable bowel syndrome. The most important contraindication to be aware of is administration of which drug class?**
- A) Beta-blockers — can worsen urinary retention by systemic beta-2 blockade
- B) Muscarinic antagonists (anticholinergics) — worsen urinary retention in BPH and precipitate acute angle-closure glaucoma
- C) Alpha-2 agonists — reduce bladder sphincter tone excessively
- D) Cholinesterase inhibitors — worsen BPH by increasing detrusor contraction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Muscarinic (M3) receptors mediate detrusor contraction and facilitate micturition; M3 blockade causes urinary retention. In BPH, where outflow is already impaired, anticholinergics can precipitate acute urinary retention. In narrow-angle glaucoma, M3 blockade causes mydriasis (iris dilator muscle contracts, iris dilator thickness increases), mechanically blocking the drainage angle and precipitating acute angle-closure crisis. Key anticholinergic contraindications: glaucoma (angle-closure), BPH, pyloric stenosis, tachyarrhythmias.

</details>

**Q2. A 4-year-old child accidentally ingests garden insecticide (organophosphate compound). He presents with excessive salivation, lacrimation, miosis, bronchospasm, bradycardia, and muscle fasciculations. What is the correct treatment sequence?**
- A) Neostigmine + diazepam
- B) Atropine (high doses to dry secretions) + pralidoxime (2-PAM) early before aging
- C) Physostigmine + atropine for CNS effects
- D) Pralidoxime alone — sufficient to regenerate AChE

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Organophosphate (OP) poisoning causes irreversible AChE inhibition → ACh excess → muscarinic (SLUDGE/DUMBELS) + nicotinic (fasciculations, weakness) + CNS effects. Treatment: (1) Atropine — high doses (2–4 mg IV repeated) to block muscarinic effects, especially bronchospasm and secretions (endpoint: drying of secretions); (2) Pralidoxime (2-PAM) — reactivates AChE by cleaving the OP-AChE bond, but ONLY before "aging" (phosphonylation becomes permanent, typically 24–48 hours). After aging, pralidoxime is ineffective.

</details>

**Q3. A 70-year-old woman is brought to the ER with delirium, dry flushed skin, mydriasis, urinary retention, tachycardia (HR 130/min), and temperature of 38.5°C after accidental ingestion of multiple tablets. The toxidrome is recognized. Which drug reverses the CNS effects specifically, and why can neostigmine not be used for CNS reversal?**
- A) Naloxone — reverses CNS opioid effects; neostigmine has no CNS activity
- B) Physostigmine — tertiary amine crosses BBB to reverse CNS anticholinergic effects; neostigmine is quaternary, cannot cross BBB
- C) Flumazenil — reverses benzodiazepine CNS effects; neostigmine is not indicated in this toxidrome
- D) Atropine — muscarinic agonist reversal; neostigmine acts peripherally on nicotinic receptors

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — This is anticholinergic toxidrome ("Dry as a bone, blind as a bat, red as a beet, hot as a hare, mad as a hatter"). Physostigmine is a tertiary amine cholinesterase inhibitor that crosses the blood-brain barrier — it reverses CNS anticholinergic effects (delirium, hallucinations). Neostigmine is a quaternary ammonium compound — it does NOT cross the BBB and reverses only peripheral muscarinic/nicotinic effects (e.g., reversal of neuromuscular blockade). Note: physostigmine is contraindicated in TCA overdose (can precipitate seizures/asystole).

</details>

**Q4. A patient is given atropine before an ophthalmic examination for fundoscopy. He experiences blurred vision for several hours and difficulty reading. What muscarinic receptor subtypes and ocular structures are involved?**
- A) M1 blockade — reduces IOP; M2 blockade — causes mydriasis
- B) M3 blockade — ciliary muscle paralysis (cycloplegia → blurred near vision) and iris sphincter paralysis (mydriasis); both mediated by M3 receptors
- C) M2 blockade in the iris sphincter causes mydriasis; M1 blockade in ciliary body causes cycloplegia
- D) Alpha-1 stimulation by atropine (paradoxical) causes both mydriasis and cycloplegia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — The iris sphincter (pupillary constrictor) and ciliary muscle are both innervated by parasympathetic M3 receptors. Atropine (M3 blockade) causes mydriasis (iris sphincter paralyzed → pupil dilates) and cycloplegia (ciliary muscle paralyzed → lens fixed in flat position → loss of accommodation → blurred near vision). These effects are used therapeutically for fundoscopy, uveitis, and in children with amblyopia. Contraindicated in narrow-angle glaucoma (mydriasis worsens angle obstruction).

</details>

## ADRENERGIC SYSTEM — RECEPTORS & SYMPATHOMIMETICS (Q5–Q8)

**Q5. A 25-year-old woman develops anaphylactic shock with urticaria, angioedema, severe bronchospasm, and BP of 70/50 mmHg after a bee sting. Which drug is given immediately and through which receptor mechanisms does it act?**
- A) Noradrenaline — α1 + β1 agonism → vasoconstriction + increased CO; no bronchodilation
- B) Adrenaline (epinephrine) — α1: vasoconstriction ↑BP; β1: ↑HR and contractility; β2: bronchodilation and suppresses mast cell mediator release
- C) Dopamine — low-dose renal vasodilation; not appropriate for anaphylaxis
- D) Phenylephrine — pure α1 agonist → adequate for anaphylaxis as BP is restored

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Adrenaline (epinephrine) is the drug of choice for anaphylaxis. It acts on multiple receptors simultaneously: α1 (vasoconstriction → ↑BP, ↓angioedema), β1 (↑HR and cardiac output), β2 (bronchodilation → ↑airflow; also inhibits mast cell degranulation → ↓histamine release). Route: IM into the anterolateral thigh (faster absorption than SC in shock). Dose: 0.3–0.5 mg (0.3–0.5 mL of 1:1000). Phenylephrine alone cannot address bronchospasm.

</details>

**Q6. A patient in the ICU with septic shock is given dopamine at 2 μg/kg/min. The urine output improves. The dose is then increased to 8 μg/kg/min for refractory hypotension. Which receptors are primarily activated at each dose and what are the expected hemodynamic effects?**
- A) Low dose: β1 (↑HR, ↑CO); High dose: D1/D2 (renal vasodilation)
- B) Low dose (1–5 μg/kg/min): D1 receptors → renal and splanchnic vasodilation → ↑ urine output; Medium dose (5–10 μg/kg/min): β1 → ↑ cardiac output; High dose (>10 μg/kg/min): α1 → vasoconstriction
- C) All doses: dopamine acts only on D1 receptors — dose titration does not change receptor profile
- D) Low dose: α1 → peripheral vasoconstriction; High dose: β2 → peripheral vasodilation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Dopamine has classic dose-dependent receptor activation: Low dose (1–5 μg/kg/min) — D1 > D2 — renal/mesenteric vasodilation, ↑ GFR, ↑ urine output; Medium dose (5–10 μg/kg/min) — β1 predominates — ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, ↑ cardiac output; High dose (>10–20 μg/kg/min) — α1 predominates — vasoconstriction ↑ SVR. This stepwise dose-response relationship is a PYQ HOTSPOT. Note: "low-dose dopamine for renal protection" is now controversial in evidence-based medicine.

</details>

**Q7. An alpha-1 adrenergic blocker (phenoxybenzamine) is administered to a patient prior to a pheochromocytoma surgery. Subsequently, when adrenaline is infused intravenously, the blood pressure falls instead of rising. What pharmacological phenomenon explains this?**
- A) Phenoxybenzamine induces tachyphylaxis to all adrenergic drugs
- B) Epinephrine reversal — with α1 blocked, adrenaline's β2 vasodilatory effects (↓ peripheral resistance) predominate over the blunted α1 vasoconstrictive effect → net hypotension
- C) Phenoxybenzamine directly blocks β receptors at high doses
- D) Compensatory baroreceptor reflex overwhelms the pressor response

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — "Epinephrine reversal" (Dale's vasomotor reversal) is a classic pharmacological concept. Normally, adrenaline raises BP via α1 (vasoconstriction) > β2 (vasodilation). When α1 receptors are blocked, the vasodilatory β2 component is unmasked → peripheral vasodilation → BP falls. This demonstrates why non-selective alpha-blockers are given before beta-blockers in pheochromocytoma management (beta-blockade alone with unopposed alpha → hypertensive crisis). This is a frequently tested PYQ concept.

</details>

**Q8. A 28-year-old woman at 32 weeks gestation develops preterm uterine contractions. She is given a drug that successfully stops contractions. Which drug was most likely used and through what mechanism?**
- A) Atropine — blocks M2 receptors in the uterus, stopping contractions
- B) Salbutamol (albuterol) — β2 agonist → uterine smooth muscle relaxation (tocolysis) via ↑ cAMP
- C) Noradrenaline — α1 agonist → uterine smooth muscle contraction, reducing cervical dilation
- D) Ritodrine — beta-1 selective agonist; cardiac effects are negligible

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Beta-2 adrenergic receptors in uterine smooth muscle mediate relaxation (tocolysis). Salbutamol (albuterol) and ritodrine are β2 agonists used for tocolysis (inhibiting preterm labor). Mechanism: β2 activation → ↑ cAMP → PKA activation → phosphorylation of myosin light chain kinase → ↓ contractility. Side effects of β2 agonist tocolytics: maternal tachycardia, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia, pulmonary edema (with prolonged use). Ritodrine (note: β2 selective, not β1) was specifically developed for tocolysis.

</details>

## CHOLINESTERASE INHIBITORS & REVERSAL AGENTS (Q9–Q11)

**Q9. Following general anesthesia with vecuronium (non-depolarizing neuromuscular blocker), the anesthesiologist administers neostigmine to reverse the blockade. The patient's HR drops to 42/min with associated nausea. Which drug should be co-administered with neostigmine to prevent this?**
- A) Propranolol — reduces reflex tachycardia
- B) Atropine — blocks muscarinic effects of acetylcholine excess from AChE inhibition
- C) Physostigmine — crosses BBB to prevent central bradycardia
- D) Edrophonium — short-acting, reduces bradycardia duration

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Neostigmine inhibits acetylcholinesterase, increasing ACh at all cholinergic synapses (nicotinic NMJ → reverses paralysis; but also muscarinic → bradycardia, salivation, bronchospasm, GI cramps). Atropine is routinely co-administered with neostigmine (or glycopyrrolate — another antimuscarinic) to prevent these unwanted muscarinic side effects while allowing the nicotinic (NMJ reversal) effect to proceed. The dose ratio is typically 0.6–1.2 mg atropine per 2.5 mg neostigmine.

</details>

**Q10. A 60-year-old man with myasthenia gravis is diagnosed using the Tensilon test. Which drug is used in this test and what is the pharmacological basis?**
- A) Neostigmine — given IM, observes sustained improvement in weakness over 30 minutes
- B) Edrophonium (Tensilon) — very short-acting, reversible AChE inhibitor; brief improvement in ptosis/diplopia over 2–5 minutes confirms diagnosis
- C) Pyridostigmine — orally administered; improvement over 24 hours confirms MG
- D) Physostigmine — crosses BBB, transiently increases ACh at NMJ confirming MG

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Edrophonium (Tensilon) is a very short-acting, rapidly reversible cholinesterase inhibitor (t1/2 ~ 5–10 minutes). In myasthenia gravis (autoimmune destruction of nicotinic NMJ receptors), ACh rapidly detaches from the reduced receptor pool. By inhibiting AChE, edrophonium briefly increases available ACh → transient improvement in ptosis, diplopia, or limb weakness within seconds to minutes. The brief duration minimizes risk. Positive response confirms MG; negative response argues against it (though false negatives occur). Long-term therapy uses pyridostigmine (oral, longer-acting).

</details>

**Q11. A patient with organophosphate poisoning is treated with atropine and pralidoxime. Which of the following symptoms is pralidoxime specifically targeting that atropine cannot address?**
- A) Bronchospasm and secretions — muscarinic effects best treated with pralidoxime
- B) Muscle fasciculations, weakness, and paralysis — nicotinic (NMJ) effects; atropine has no nicotinic activity
- C) Miosis — pralidoxime specifically reverses pupillary constriction
- D) Bradycardia — pralidoxime directly accelerates SA node conduction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Organophosphate excess ACh affects both muscarinic and nicotinic receptors. Atropine blocks muscarinic effects (SLUDGE + bronchospasm + bradycardia) but has NO activity at nicotinic receptors. Pralidoxime (2-PAM) regenerates AChE by cleaving the phosphonate-AChE bond, restoring ACh breakdown at both muscarinic and nicotinic synapses. At the NMJ (nicotinic), restored AChE function relieves muscle fasciculations, weakness, and the life-threatening respiratory muscle paralysis that atropine cannot reverse. The complementary roles of atropine (muscarinic block) and pralidoxime (AChE regeneration) are the cornerstone of OP poisoning management.

</details>

## ALPHA BLOCKERS & MIXED ADRENERGIC AGENTS (Q12–Q15)

**Q12. A 58-year-old hypertensive man is prescribed prazosin. Upon taking the first dose at bedtime, he stands up to go to the bathroom and falls — his BP on standing is 85/50 mmHg. What is this phenomenon and how should future doses be managed?**
- A) Prazosin-induced reflex bradycardia — use with digoxin to control heart rate
- B) First-dose hypotension — a pronounced orthostatic fall due to α1 blockade and loss of sympathetic vascular tone; mitigate by starting with low dose at bedtime, rising slowly
- C) Class effect of all antihypertensives — unavoidable with dose titration
- D) Prazosin-induced anaphylaxis — skin test before starting alpha-blockers

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — First-dose hypotension is a well-characterized class effect of alpha-1 blockers (prazosin, terazosin, doxazosin). Blocking α1 receptors on vascular smooth muscle removes sympathetic vasoconstrictor tone. When standing (orthostasis), the normal sympathetic reflex vasoconstriction is blunted → precipitous BP fall → syncope. Management: start with very low dose at bedtime, advise slow position changes. Alpha-1 blockers also relax prostatic smooth muscle (used in BPH). Phentolamine (non-selective α-blocker) is used for pheochromocytoma crisis.

</details>

**Q13. A 72-year-old man with hypertension and angina is on a beta-blocker. He is prescribed a drug for hypertension that works centrally via alpha-2 receptors. This drug is later abruptly stopped, leading to a hypertensive crisis. What is the drug and the mechanism of rebound hypertension?**
- A) Prazosin — abrupt cessation of peripheral α1 blockade → rebound vasoconstriction
- B) Clonidine — α2 agonist reduces central sympathetic outflow; abrupt withdrawal → massive sympathetic rebound → rebound hypertension (especially dangerous with concurrent beta-blocker)
- C) Methyldopa — abrupt cessation → acute catecholamine excess via dopamine accumulation
- D) Hydralazine — abrupt cessation → reflex tachycardia and hypertension via baroreceptors

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Clonidine is a centrally-acting α2 agonist. Presynaptic α2 activation reduces NE release from central sympathetic neurons → ↓ BP, ↓ HR. Abrupt discontinuation causes sudden rebound sympathetic activity → hypertensive crisis. This is particularly dangerous in patients also on beta-blockers (β-blockade prevents compensatory ↑HR, so the full pressor effect is seen unopposed). Clonidine must be tapered slowly. Other uses: ADHD (children), opioid withdrawal, epidural analgesia.

</details>

**Q14. A 30-year-old woman with hypertension in pregnancy is treated with labetalol intravenously in the emergency department. What is the pharmacological basis for choosing labetalol over a pure beta-blocker in this situation?**
- A) Labetalol crosses the placenta less than pure beta-blockers, making it safer in pregnancy
- B) Labetalol blocks both α1 and β1+β2 receptors — α1 blockade reduces vasoconstriction (↓BP) while β1 block reduces HR; combined lower BP without reflex tachycardia; first-line for hypertensive urgency in pregnancy
- C) Labetalol has uterine β2 agonism that reduces fetal distress during maternal hypertension
- D) Labetalol is preferred because it avoids thiazide diuretics which are teratogenic

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Labetalol is a non-selective α1 + β1 + β2 blocker with α:β ratio of 1:7 (IV) to 1:3 (oral). Alpha-1 blockade reduces peripheral vascular resistance (↓BP); beta-blockade prevents reflex tachycardia that would otherwise occur with vasodilation. It is the preferred IV agent for hypertensive emergencies in pregnancy (along with hydralazine). The combined mechanism provides smooth, predictable BP lowering without compromising uteroplacental blood flow. Atenolol is avoided in pregnancy due to IUGR risk.

</details>

**Q15. A patient in the ICU with cardiogenic shock has low cardiac output despite adequate filling pressures. Dobutamine is chosen over dopamine. What is the rationale for this choice in terms of receptor specificity?**
- A) Dopamine at high doses stimulates α1 → ↑ afterload which worsens LV dysfunction; dobutamine (β1 > β2) ↑ contractility without the vasoconstrictive α1 effect, improving CO
- B) Dobutamine activates both D1 and β1 receptors — renal protection is an added benefit
- C) Dopamine cannot be used with beta-blockers; dobutamine can safely be combined
- D) Dobutamine reduces preload by β2-mediated venodilation, which is specifically needed in cardiogenic shock

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A** — In cardiogenic shock, the primary goal is to improve cardiac output (CO) without increasing afterload (which makes the failing LV work harder). Dobutamine (β1 > β2): ↑ contractility (β1) → ↑ CO; mild peripheral vasodilation (β2) → ↓ afterload. Dopamine at doses needed for hemodynamic support (>5–10 μg/kg/min) activates α1 → vasoconstriction → ↑ afterload → ↑ LV wall stress → potentially worsens LV failure. Dobutamine is preferred for cardiogenic shock with adequate BP; dopamine (vasopressor doses) if hypotension is severe and refractory.

</details>
