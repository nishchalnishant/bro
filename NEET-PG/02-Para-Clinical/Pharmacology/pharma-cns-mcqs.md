## BENZODIAZEPINES & BARBITURATES (Q1–Q2)

**Q1. A 30-year-old woman is brought to the emergency department after ingesting an unknown number of diazepam tablets. She is drowsy but arousable, with normal BP and HR. Respiratory rate is 14/min. Which statement about her management is most accurate?**
- A) Benzodiazepine overdose alone is rarely fatal; administer IV flumazenil if needed, but warn of short duration of action
- B) Administer activated charcoal immediately regardless of time since ingestion
- C) IV phenobarbital to prevent seizures from diazepam withdrawal
- D) Hemodialysis is the treatment of choice due to high renal clearance of diazepam

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A** — Benzodiazepine overdose alone (without co-ingestion of alcohol or opioids) is rarely fatal because the ceiling on respiratory depression is high. Flumazenil (competitive BZD antagonist at GABA-A) can reverse sedation but has a very short t1/2 (~1 hour), so resedation can occur and repeated doses may be needed. Flumazenil can precipitate seizures in BZD-dependent patients. Benzodiazepines have large Vd, making dialysis ineffective.

</details>

**Q2. In terms of GABA-A receptor pharmacology, which of the following correctly distinguishes benzodiazepines from barbiturates?**
- A) Benzodiazepines increase duration of Cl⁻ channel opening; barbiturates increase frequency
- B) Benzodiazepines increase frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening; barbiturates increase duration
- C) Both benzodiazepines and barbiturates increase duration of Cl⁻ channel opening
- D) Barbiturates are positive allosteric modulators of NMDA receptors

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — This is a high-yield distinction. Benzodiazepines are positive allosteric modulators of GABA-A that increase the FREQUENCY of chloride channel opening. Barbiturates increase the DURATION of chloride channel opening and at high doses can directly open the channel (hence more dangerous in overdose). Mnemonic: Benzodiazepines = Frequency (BF), Barbiturates = Duration (BD).

</details>

## ANTIEPILEPTICS (Q3–Q6)

**Q3. A 25-year-old woman with epilepsy controlled on valproate plans a pregnancy. Her neurologist continues valproate as it controls her seizures best. She becomes pregnant. At 18 weeks ultrasound, a lumbar meningomyelocele is found. Which mechanism explains this teratogenicity?**
- A) Valproate inhibits folic acid absorption from the gut
- B) Valproate inhibits dihydrofolate reductase → folate deficiency → neural tube defect
- C) Valproate interferes with folate-dependent methylation reactions critical for neural tube closure during weeks 3–4 of embryogenesis
- D) Valproate causes valproate embryopathy through sodium channel disruption in neural tissue

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Valproate is the most teratogenic antiepileptic drug (AED). It disrupts folate metabolism and folate-dependent methylation essential for neural tube closure (which occurs at weeks 3–4, often before pregnancy is recognized). Neural tube defects (primarily spina bifida — myelomeningocele) occur in 1–2% of exposed pregnancies. High-dose folic acid (5 mg/day) should be given preconceptually if valproate cannot be substituted.

</details>

**Q4. A 55-year-old man with epilepsy on phenytoin for 10 years presents for dental work. The dentist notices marked gingival overgrowth. The patient also has coarsened facial features and excess facial hair. Which kinetic property of phenytoin makes dosing challenging?**
- A) First-order kinetics throughout — linear relationship between dose and plasma levels
- B) Zero-order (saturation) kinetics at therapeutic doses — small dose increases cause disproportionately large plasma level rises
- C) Phenytoin undergoes extensive enterohepatic recycling, making levels unpredictable
- D) Phenytoin has a very narrow therapeutic window but follows first-order kinetics like most drugs

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Phenytoin saturates its hepatic hydroxylation (CYP2C9) at therapeutic concentrations, transitioning to zero-order (Michaelis-Menten) kinetics. At this point, a small dose increase produces a large and unpredictable rise in plasma levels, risking toxicity (nystagmus → diplopia → ataxia → encephalopathy). The described features — gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, coarsened facies — are classic chronic phenytoin side effects.

</details>

**Q5. A 45-year-old woman with epilepsy develops low serum sodium (Na 128 mEq/L) while on an antiepileptic drug. She has no other medical conditions. She also takes oral contraceptives. Which AED is most likely responsible for both the hyponatremia and potentially reducing OCP effectiveness?**
- A) Valproate — causes SIADH and inhibits CYP450
- B) Carbamazepine — causes SIADH and induces CYP450 enzymes
- C) Levetiracetam — associated with hyponatremia and enzyme induction
- D) Ethosuximide — SIADH and enzyme induction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Carbamazepine is uniquely associated with SIADH (hyponatremia) among AEDs through its antidiuretic hormone-like effect on collecting ducts. It is also a potent enzyme inducer (CYP3A4), which increases metabolism of oral contraceptives, reducing their efficacy. This double clinical significance — SIADH causing hyponatremia + OCP failure — makes carbamazepine a frequent NEET PG MCQ target.

</details>

**Q6. A 35-year-old patient with severe trigeminal neuralgia presents with episodes of lancinating facial pain triggered by eating and speaking. Which is the drug of choice for this condition?**
- A) Phenytoin — first used historically for trigeminal neuralgia
- B) Valproate — broad-spectrum AED for neuropathic pain
- C) Carbamazepine — drug of choice for trigeminal neuralgia via sodium channel blockade
- D) Gabapentin — GABA analog, preferred due to fewer side effects

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Carbamazepine is the drug of choice (DOC) for trigeminal neuralgia. It blocks use-dependent sodium channels in rapidly firing neurons, reducing the paroxysmal discharges in the trigeminal nucleus. Carbamazepine also used in bipolar disorder and focal seizures. Although phenytoin has historical use and gabapentin is an alternative, carbamazepine remains first-line in guidelines.

</details>

## ANTIPSYCHOTICS (Q7–Q9)

**Q7. A 22-year-old man with schizophrenia on haloperidol for 3 months develops involuntary rhythmic protrusion of the tongue and chewing movements that persist even when he tries to stop. His psychiatrist identifies this as a side effect of the antipsychotic. What is this movement disorder and what is the most appropriate management?**
- A) Akathisia — treat with propranolol
- B) Acute dystonia — treat with benztropine IM
- C) Tardive dyskinesia — reduce/discontinue haloperidol; consider switching to clozapine
- D) Parkinsonism — reduce dose and add benztropine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Tardive dyskinesia (TD) presents as repetitive, involuntary orofacial movements (lip smacking, tongue protrusion, chewing) after months-to-years of dopamine antagonist use. It occurs due to dopamine receptor upregulation (supersensitivity) in the nigrostriatal pathway. It may be irreversible. Management includes reducing or stopping the offending antipsychotic, switching to clozapine (which has low D2 blockade and lowest TD risk), or using VMAT2 inhibitors (tetrabenazine, valbenazine).

</details>

**Q8. A patient with schizophrenia on clozapine for 4 months presents with fever, sore throat, and malaise. CBC shows absolute neutrophil count of 800/mm³. What is the most likely adverse effect and what should be done immediately?**
- A) Clozapine-induced eosinophilia — antihistamines and continue therapy
- B) Agranulocytosis from clozapine — stop clozapine immediately, supportive care, G-CSF if needed
- C) Viral pharyngitis coincidental to clozapine therapy — antibiotics and continue clozapine
- D) Clozapine-induced thrombocytopenia — platelet transfusion; clozapine may be continued

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Clozapine causes agranulocytosis in 1–2% of patients, which can be life-threatening. This is the reason mandatory WBC monitoring is required: weekly for 6 months, then biweekly. An ANC <1500/mm³ requires drug cessation. Despite this risk, clozapine is uniquely effective in treatment-resistant schizophrenia and also reduces suicidality. G-CSF (filgrastim) can be used to accelerate neutrophil recovery.

</details>

**Q9. A 40-year-old man on haloperidol develops sudden-onset high fever (40°C), generalized muscle rigidity (lead-pipe), altered consciousness, diaphoresis, and tachycardia. Serum CPK is markedly elevated. What is the diagnosis and treatment?**
- A) Serotonin syndrome — cyproheptadine and benzodiazepines
- B) Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome — stop antipsychotic, IV dantrolene, bromocriptine, supportive care
- C) Malignant hyperthermia — stop haloperidol, IV dantrolene, ice packs
- D) Anticholinergic toxidrome — physostigmine IV

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) is caused by sudden D2 blockade in the hypothalamus (thermoregulation) and striatum. Classic triad: Hyperthermia + Lead-pipe rigidity + Altered consciousness + Autonomic instability. Markedly elevated CPK (from rhabdomyolysis) is characteristic. Treatment: stop the antipsychotic immediately; IV dantrolene (muscle relaxant); dopamine agonists (bromocriptine, amantadine); ICU supportive care. Differentiated from serotonin syndrome by rigidity (not clonus/hyperreflexia) and absence of serotonergic drug.

</details>

## ANTIDEPRESSANTS & OPIOIDS (Q10–Q15)

**Q10. A 45-year-old depressed patient on phenelzine (MAOI) eats a meal of aged cheddar cheese and red wine at a dinner. Two hours later he develops a severe throbbing headache, flushing, sweating, and BP of 200/120 mmHg. What is the mechanism of this crisis?**
- A) Phenelzine inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase — alcohol accumulates
- B) Tyramine in cheese/wine normally metabolized by gut MAO-A; MAOI blockade allows tyramine absorption → sympathomimetic surge → hypertensive crisis
- C) Direct interaction of tannins in wine with phenelzine → vasoconstriction
- D) Phenelzine inhibits hepatic tyrosine hydroxylase → catecholamine surge

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — This is the "cheese reaction" (tyramine-induced hypertensive crisis). Tyramine in fermented foods (aged cheese, cured meats, red wine) is normally degraded by MAO-A in the gut wall and liver before reaching the systemic circulation. MAOIs block this first-pass elimination, allowing tyramine to flood the circulation, displace NE from sympathetic nerve terminals, and cause a hypertensive crisis. Treatment is phentolamine (alpha-blocker). Patients on MAOIs must follow strict tyramine-restricted diets.

</details>

**Q11. A 35-year-old on sertraline for depression is started on tramadol for pain. Hours later he develops tremor, hyperreflexia, myoclonus, diaphoresis, and fever. Which complication has occurred and what is the treatment?**
- A) SSRI discontinuation syndrome — restart sertraline
- B) Serotonin syndrome — stop both drugs, cyproheptadine, benzodiazepines, supportive care
- C) Neuroleptic malignant syndrome — dantrolene IV
- D) Tramadol overdose — naloxone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Serotonin syndrome results from excess serotonergic activity. Tramadol inhibits serotonin reuptake (in addition to weak opioid activity); combined with an SSRI, serotonin levels surge excessively. Classic triad: hyperthermia + neuromuscular abnormalities (clonus, hyperreflexia, myoclonus, tremor) + altered mental status. Treatment: discontinue serotonergic agents, cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist), benzodiazepines for agitation, cooling measures. Other triggers: SSRI + MAOI, SSRI + linezolid, SSRI + pethidine.

</details>

**Q12. A 28-year-old woman is treated with amitriptyline for neuropathic pain. She accidentally takes 20 tablets. In the emergency room, her ECG shows prolonged QRS (160 ms) and she is lethargic. Which treatment is most critical for the cardiac toxicity?**
- A) IV activated charcoal to bind amitriptyline
- B) IV sodium bicarbonate — alkalinization reduces Na channel blockade
- C) IV lidocaine — class IB to counter the arrhythmia
- D) IV physostigmine — reverses anticholinergic effect responsible for arrhythmia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — TCA overdose causes life-threatening ventricular arrhythmias via sodium channel blockade (wide QRS complex). IV sodium bicarbonate is the cornerstone treatment: it alkalinizes the plasma, which reduces TCA binding to sodium channels, and provides excess sodium to overcome the channel blockade. Target serum pH 7.45–7.55. Physostigmine is NOT recommended (can precipitate seizures in TCA overdose). Lidocaine can worsen Na channel block. TCA OD mnemonic: "3 Cs" — Convulsions, Coma, Cardiac arrhythmia.

</details>

**Q13. A 50-year-old post-surgical patient on morphine for pain control is noted to have pinpoint pupils, respiratory rate of 8/min, and is unresponsive. An opioid antagonist is administered IV. Within minutes he wakes up but 90 minutes later becomes sedated again. Which drug was given and what is the explanation for resedation?**
- A) Naltrexone — long-acting antagonist, resedation should not occur
- B) Naloxone — short t1/2 (~1–2 hours), may wear off before morphine; repeat dosing or infusion needed
- C) Flumazenil — short-acting BZD antagonist, confirming concurrent benzodiazepine use
- D) Physostigmine — opioid reversal via cholinesterase inhibition

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Naloxone is a competitive opioid antagonist with a short half-life of 1–2 hours. Morphine (and long-acting opioids) outlast the naloxone effect, causing resedation. Repeated IV boluses or a continuous infusion of naloxone is required in serious opioid toxicity. Naltrexone is the long-acting oral formulation used in addiction treatment, not acute overdose reversal.

</details>

**Q14. A patient on chronic opioids for cancer pain develops constipation that has persisted throughout 6 months of therapy, despite tolerance developing to the analgesic and euphoric effects. Which statement best explains why constipation persists?**
- A) Constipation is due to mu-opioid receptor desensitization in the CNS — a central effect that tolerates slowly
- B) Tolerance does NOT develop to constipation or miosis — these are persistent opioid effects via peripheral and CNS mechanisms
- C) Constipation is due to histamine release from opioids, a non-receptor mediated effect
- D) Tolerance to constipation takes 12–18 months to develop, unlike analgesia which tolerates in weeks

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — While tolerance rapidly develops to analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, nausea, and sedation; tolerance does NOT develop to constipation or miosis. Opioid-induced constipation is mediated by mu-receptors in the myenteric plexus of the gut (peripheral mechanism), which are not subject to the same desensitization seen centrally. This is clinically important — laxatives should be routinely co-prescribed with chronic opioids. Peripherally-acting mu-opioid receptor antagonists (methylnaltrexone, naloxegol) treat opioid-induced constipation without reversing central analgesia.

</details>

**Q15. A 60-year-old patient with renal failure is given meperidine (pethidine) for post-operative pain. On day 3, he develops agitation, tremors, and myoclonic jerks, then a generalized tonic-clonic seizure. What is the mechanism of this toxicity?**
- A) Meperidine itself accumulates in renal failure and directly causes seizures via kappa receptor overstimulation
- B) Normeperidine (active metabolite) accumulates in renal failure — causes CNS excitatory effects and seizures
- C) Renal failure causes decreased protein binding, raising free meperidine to toxic levels
- D) Meperidine crosses the BBB less in renal failure, paradoxically reducing efficacy and causing withdrawal seizures

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Meperidine is metabolized to normeperidine, an active metabolite with CNS excitatory properties (proconvulsant). Normeperidine is renally cleared; in renal failure it accumulates to toxic levels, causing agitation, tremors, myoclonus, and grand mal seizures. Unlike morphine-induced miosis, meperidine causes mydriasis due to its anticholinergic effect. Meperidine should be avoided in renal failure and in patients receiving MAOIs (risk of serotonin syndrome/excitatory reaction).

</details>
