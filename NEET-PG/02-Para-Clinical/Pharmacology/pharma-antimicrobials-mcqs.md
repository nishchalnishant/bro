## BETA-LACTAMS & CELL WALL INHIBITORS (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A 35-year-old woman develops flushing, erythema over the face and neck, and pruritus during intravenous vancomycin infusion started 10 minutes ago. Her vitals show BP 100/70 mmHg, HR 110/min. Which is the most appropriate next step?**
- A) Stop vancomycin and switch to linezolid permanently
- B) Slow the infusion rate and administer diphenhydramine
- C) Administer IM epinephrine for anaphylaxis
- D) Order serum IgE levels before resuming

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — This is Red Man Syndrome, caused by non-immune histamine release due to rapid vancomycin infusion — it is NOT a true allergic reaction (no IgE involvement). Treatment is to slow or stop the infusion and pretreat with antihistamines. Epinephrine is reserved for true anaphylaxis.

</details>

**Q2. A 28-year-old man with known penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis) develops nosocomial pneumonia with Gram-positive cocci in clusters on sputum Gram stain. MRSA is confirmed on culture. Which antibiotic is most appropriate?**
- A) Ceftriaxone
- B) Meropenem
- C) Vancomycin
- D) Ampicillin-sulbactam

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Vancomycin is the drug of choice for MRSA and is safe in true penicillin allergy (it is a glycopeptide, not a beta-lactam — no cross-reactivity). Ceftriaxone and meropenem are beta-lactams with possible cross-reactivity. MRSA is intrinsically resistant to all standard beta-lactams.

</details>

**Q3. A 60-year-old with hospital-acquired pneumonia is started on imipenem-cilastatin. The patient develops a seizure on day 2 of treatment. Which alternative carbapenem should be substituted?**
- A) Ertapenem
- B) Meropenem
- C) Doripenem is equally epileptogenic — no switch needed
- D) Switch to aztreonam

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Imipenem lowers the seizure threshold and should be avoided in CNS infections or patients with seizure risk. Meropenem has the same spectrum but is significantly less epileptogenic and is preferred for meningitis/CNS infections. Ertapenem lacks Pseudomonas coverage and is not a direct substitute in hospital-acquired infections.

</details>

---

**Q4. Carbapenem resistance in a Klebsiella pneumoniae isolate is reported due to NDM-1. What does NDM-1 stand for and what is its mechanism of resistance?**
- A) New Delhi Metalloprotease-1 — modifies the beta-lactam ring target PBP
- B) New Delhi Metallo-beta-lactamase-1 — hydrolyzes the beta-lactam ring
- C) Non-Durable Membrane — prevents drug entry via porin loss
- D) NDM-1 is an efflux pump that exports carbapenems

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — NDM-1 (New Delhi Metallo-beta-lactamase-1) is a carbapenemase enzyme that hydrolyzes the beta-lactam ring of carbapenems, rendering them inactive. It was first described in India/Pakistan. This is distinct from KPC (Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase). Porin loss is a separate (often co-existing) mechanism.

</details>

## AMINOGLYCOSIDES, MACROLIDES & PROTEIN SYNTHESIS INHIBITORS (Q5–Q8)

**Q5. A 70-year-old diabetic on gentamicin for 10 days develops worsening hearing loss and balance problems. Audiometry shows high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss. The most likely explanation is:**
- A) Cochlear toxicity from amikacin accumulated in perilymph
- B) Gentamicin-induced vestibular and cochlear toxicity via free radical damage to hair cells
- C) Reversible ototoxicity — will resolve after stopping the drug
- D) This is a known side effect of tobramycin only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin) accumulate in the inner ear endolymph and cause hair cell damage via free radicals. Gentamicin preferentially causes vestibular toxicity, while amikacin and neomycin preferentially cause cochlear (auditory) toxicity. The ototoxicity may be irreversible, especially with prolonged use. Risk increases with concurrent loop diuretics.

</details>

**Q6. A 45-year-old man with gastroparesis and delayed gastric emptying is started on a drug for a respiratory tract infection caused by Mycoplasma pneumoniae. The same drug also improves his gastric symptoms. Which drug was prescribed?**
- A) Azithromycin
- B) Clarithromycin
- C) Erythromycin
- D) Roxithromycin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Erythromycin is a motilin receptor agonist, which stimulates gastric motility — this prokinetic effect is used therapeutically in gastroparesis. It is also effective against Mycoplasma (atypical organism). Azithromycin is preferred for Mycoplasma due to fewer GI side effects, but lacks the prokinetic benefit.

</details>

**Q7. A neonate born at 36 weeks develops abdominal distension, ashen-gray skin color, vomiting, and cardiovascular collapse on day 3 of life. The mother received an antibiotic during labor. Which drug is responsible?**
- A) Gentamicin — nephrotoxicity in neonate
- B) Chloramphenicol — Gray Baby Syndrome
- C) Tetracycline — hepatotoxicity
- D) Metronidazole — CNS toxicity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Gray Baby Syndrome results from chloramphenicol toxicity in neonates, who lack sufficient glucuronosyltransferase (UGT) for drug conjugation. Chloramphenicol accumulates to toxic levels, causing cardiovascular collapse, abdominal distension, ashen-gray cyanosis, and vomiting. Chloramphenicol is contraindicated in neonates and should be avoided near term.

</details>

**Q8. A patient on clindamycin for a dental abscess develops profuse watery diarrhea with mucus and cramping 2 weeks after completing therapy. Stool assay is positive for toxin. What is the diagnosis and treatment of choice?**
- A) Clindamycin-induced osmotic diarrhea — loperamide
- B) Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea — oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin
- C) Viral gastroenteritis — supportive care only
- D) Ischemic colitis — surgical referral

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Clindamycin is most classically associated with Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) colitis, caused by disruption of gut flora allowing C. diff overgrowth with toxin production. Treatment is oral vancomycin (first-line for non-severe disease) or fidaxomicin. Metronidazole is an alternative for mild cases. Loperamide is contraindicated as it can precipitate toxic megacolon.

</details>

## FLUOROQUINOLONES & DNA SYNTHESIS INHIBITORS (Q9–Q11)

**Q9. A 65-year-old man on long-term corticosteroids for rheumatoid arthritis is prescribed a fluoroquinolone for a urinary tract infection. Three days later he presents with acute pain and swelling at his right Achilles tendon. Which adverse effect is this?**
- A) Drug-induced lupus from fluoroquinolone
- B) Fluoroquinolone-associated tendinitis/tendon rupture — FDA Black Box Warning
- C) Gout flare triggered by fluoroquinolone
- D) Deep vein thrombosis from immobility

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Fluoroquinolones carry an FDA Black Box Warning for tendinitis and tendon rupture, particularly of the Achilles tendon. Risk factors include age >60, concomitant corticosteroid use, and renal impairment. The mechanism involves disruption of collagen synthesis. This effect is a class effect of all fluoroquinolones and requires prompt discontinuation.

</details>

---

**Q10. A patient taking ciprofloxacin for a urinary infection also takes antacids containing magnesium and aluminum hydroxide. Despite 5 days of therapy, cultures remain positive. The most likely reason is:**
- A) Ciprofloxacin does not cover Gram-negative UTI pathogens
- B) Ciprofloxacin chelation with divalent/trivalent cations reduces oral absorption
- C) Magnesium enhances bacterial resistance to quinolones
- D) Antacids induce CYP enzymes that metabolize ciprofloxacin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Fluoroquinolones (and tetracyclines) form insoluble chelation complexes with divalent/trivalent cations (Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Ca²⁺, Fe²⁺, Zn²⁺) in the GI tract, markedly reducing oral absorption. Antacids, dairy products, and iron supplements must be taken at least 2 hours apart from fluoroquinolones to avoid this interaction.

</details>

**Q11. A patient prescribed metronidazole for bacterial vaginosis attends a party and drinks alcohol. Within 30 minutes she develops flushing, severe nausea, vomiting, and headache. The mechanism of this reaction is:**
- A) Metronidazole directly inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase → acetaldehyde accumulation
- B) Alcohol induces CYP450, increasing metronidazole to toxic levels
- C) Metronidazole blocks dopamine receptors activated by alcohol
- D) IgE-mediated hypersensitivity to alcohol in metronidazole-treated patients

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A** — Metronidazole inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH2), the enzyme that converts acetaldehyde (produced from alcohol) to acetate. Acetaldehyde accumulates causing the disulfiram-like reaction: flushing, nausea, vomiting, tachycardia, and headache. Alcohol must be avoided during therapy and for 48 hours after the last dose. This is the same mechanism as disulfiram (Antabuse) used in alcohol deaddiction.

</details>

## ANTITUBERCULAR & ANTIPROTOZOAL DRUGS (Q12–Q15)

**Q12. A 32-year-old woman with pulmonary TB is started on first-line HRZE therapy. After 8 weeks she complains of difficulty distinguishing red from green traffic lights and decreased visual acuity. Which drug is responsible and what is the monitoring recommendation?**
- A) Isoniazid — peripheral neuropathy, supplement pyridoxine
- B) Rifampicin — optic neuritis, perform monthly LFTs
- C) Ethambutol — retrobulbar optic neuritis, monthly visual acuity and color vision testing
- D) Pyrazinamide — uveitis, baseline ophthalmology review

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Ethambutol causes retrobulbar optic neuritis presenting as red-green color blindness and reduced visual acuity. It inhibits arabinosyl transferase, disrupting arabinogalactan synthesis in mycobacteria. Vision changes are dose-related and potentially reversible if caught early. Monthly visual acuity and color vision testing is mandatory; ethambutol should be avoided in children <5 years who cannot reliably report visual changes.

</details>

**Q13. A newly diagnosed TB patient is started on isoniazid-based therapy. Which preventive supplement should be co-administered and why?**
- A) Vitamin C — INH increases oxidative stress
- B) Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) — INH competitively inhibits pyridoxal phosphate-dependent enzymes → peripheral neuropathy
- C) Folic acid — INH is a folate antagonist
- D) Vitamin B12 — INH blocks intrinsic factor production

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Isoniazid (INH) inhibits pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) synthesis and competes with B6-dependent enzymes, causing peripheral neuropathy (most common serious side effect). Routine supplementation with pyridoxine 25–50 mg/day is recommended, especially in malnourished patients, diabetics, alcoholics, pregnant women, and slow acetylators. INH-induced hepatotoxicity is a separate concern monitored via LFTs.

</details>

**Q14. A 40-year-old man with P. vivax malaria is given chloroquine followed by primaquine for radical cure. Before starting primaquine, which test is mandatory and why?**
- A) Renal function test — primaquine is nephrotoxic
- B) G6PD assay — primaquine can precipitate acute hemolytic anemia in G6PD deficiency
- C) Liver function test — primaquine causes severe hepatotoxicity
- D) Ophthalmology review — primaquine causes irreversible retinopathy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Primaquine generates reactive oxygen species that overwhelm the glutathione-dependent antioxidant system. In G6PD-deficient patients, this causes acute intravascular hemolysis (hemolytic anemia). G6PD testing is mandatory before prescribing primaquine. Primaquine is the only drug that eliminates hepatic hypnozoites of P. vivax and P. ovale, preventing relapse. Chloroquine retinopathy is the concern with long-term chloroquine use, not primaquine.

</details>

**Q15. A 55-year-old HIV-positive patient with cryptococcal meningitis is treated with amphotericin B. On day 5, serum creatinine rises to 2.8 mg/dL and potassium drops to 2.9 mEq/L. What is the mechanism of renal toxicity and how can it be mitigated?**
- A) Amphotericin B binds ergosterol in renal tubule cells — switch to fluconazole; give KCl supplements
- B) Amphotericin B binds cholesterol in renal tubule membranes → tubular damage → distal RTA, hypokalemia; liposomal formulation (AmBisome) reduces nephrotoxicity
- C) Nephrotoxicity is due to cytokine release from antifungal activity — premedicate with corticosteroids
- D) Infusion-related nephrotoxicity — slow infusion rate over 4 hours is sufficient

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Amphotericin B binds ergosterol selectively in fungal membranes but also has affinity for cholesterol in human renal tubular cells, causing direct tubular toxicity manifesting as distal renal tubular acidosis, hypokalemia, and hypomagnesemia. Nephrotoxicity is the dose-limiting side effect. Lipid formulations (liposomal amphotericin B — AmBisome, ABLC) encapsulate the drug and reduce direct renal tubular exposure, significantly decreasing nephrotoxicity while maintaining antifungal efficacy.

</details>
