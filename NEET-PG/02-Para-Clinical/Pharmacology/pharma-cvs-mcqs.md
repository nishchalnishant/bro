## BETA-BLOCKERS & ACE INHIBITORS (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A 55-year-old asthmatic with hypertension and heart failure is prescribed a beta-blocker. The cardiologist specifically chooses a cardioselective agent. Which drug was most likely chosen, and why is selectivity important in this patient?**
- A) Propranolol — non-selective, but preferred in heart failure because it blocks both β1 and β2
- B) Metoprolol — cardioselective (β1 > β2), reducing the risk of beta-2-mediated bronchoconstriction
- C) Carvedilol — alpha + beta blocker, so bronchospasm risk is eliminated by alpha blockade
- D) Sotalol — cardioselective beta-blocker with added antiarrhythmic benefit

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Cardioselective beta-blockers (A-BEAM mnemonic: Atenolol, Bisoprolol, Esmolol, Acebutolol, Metoprolol) preferentially block β1 receptors in the heart with relatively less β2 blockade in the bronchi. This reduces (but does NOT eliminate) bronchoconstriction risk in asthma/COPD. Propranolol is non-selective; sotalol is non-selective with class III properties. Carvedilol blocks α1 + β1 + β2 — used in heart failure but not cardioselective.

</details>

**Q2. A 62-year-old man with hypertension is started on enalapril. After 2 weeks he develops a persistent dry cough. His physician switches to a different antihypertensive in the same class of mechanism but without this side effect. What was the likely switch and why does the replacement drug not cause cough?**
- A) Switched to amlodipine — CCBs do not affect bradykinin
- B) Switched to losartan (ARB) — blocks AT1 receptor without increasing bradykinin; no cough
- C) Switched to captopril — short-acting ACEi has less cough than enalapril
- D) Switched to spironolactone — blocks aldosterone receptor, avoiding the renin-angiotensin axis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — ACE inhibitor-induced cough is caused by accumulation of bradykinin (normally degraded by ACE). ARBs (e.g., losartan, valsartan) block the AT1 receptor downstream, producing similar blood pressure reduction and cardiorenal protection without affecting ACE — so bradykinin levels are not raised and cough does not occur. This is the most common reason to switch from ACEi to ARB in clinical practice.

</details>

**Q3. A 38-year-old woman with recurrent episodes of urticaria and facial swelling is found to have hereditary angioedema (C1-esterase inhibitor deficiency). She is hypertensive. Which antihypertensive class is absolutely contraindicated in her case?**
- A) ARBs — cause bradykinin accumulation like ACE inhibitors
- B) ACE inhibitors — accumulation of bradykinin worsens hereditary angioedema
- C) Calcium channel blockers — potentiate bradykinin-mediated swelling
- D) Thiazide diuretics — deplete C1-esterase inhibitor further

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Hereditary angioedema (HAE) is caused by deficient or dysfunctional C1-esterase inhibitor, leading to uncontrolled bradykinin production. ACE inhibitors prevent bradykinin degradation, dramatically worsening HAE episodes, which can be life-threatening (laryngeal angioedema). ACE inhibitors are absolutely contraindicated in HAE. ARBs do not inhibit ACE and do not increase bradykinin — they are a safer alternative for hypertension in these patients.

</details>

**Q4. A 70-year-old with heart failure and atrial fibrillation is on multiple medications. An ECG shows ST-segment changes with a "reversed tick" (scooped ST segment) pattern. He complains of yellow-green colored vision and nausea. Serum potassium is 2.8 mEq/L. What drug toxicity is this, and what is the definitive treatment?**
- A) Amiodarone toxicity — thyroid function tests and drug cessation
- B) Digoxin toxicity — digoxin-specific Fab antibodies (Digibind/DigiFab)
- C) Loop diuretic toxicity — potassium replacement is curative
- D) Quinidine toxicity — magnesium sulfate for torsades

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Digoxin toxicity presents with the classic triad of GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, anorexia), visual disturbances (yellow-green halos — xanthopsia, blurred vision), and cardiac arrhythmias (almost any rhythm disturbance). The "scooped" or reversed-tick ST depression on ECG is the digoxin effect (not toxicity per se). Hypokalemia (from diuretics) potentiates digoxin toxicity by competing for binding to Na/K-ATPase. Definitive treatment is digoxin-specific Fab antibody fragments (Digibind).

</details>

## ANTIARRHYTHMICS (Q5–Q7)

**Q5. A 50-year-old man has recurrent paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT). He is given an IV drug that causes transient complete heart block for 10 seconds, terminating the tachycardia. He reports transient chest tightness and flushing immediately after injection. Which drug was administered?**
- A) Verapamil — calcium channel blocker terminates PSVT
- B) Adenosine — very short t1/2 (~10 seconds), causes transient AV block, first-line for acute PSVT
- C) Lidocaine — class IB, used for ventricular arrhythmias
- D) Digoxin — increases vagal tone, rate control in AF

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Adenosine is the drug of choice for acute PSVT. It activates adenosine A1 receptors → Gi-coupled → hyperpolarizes AV node → transient AV block that terminates the re-entrant circuit. Its half-life of ~10 seconds means effects are brief. Side effects (flushing, chest tightness, dyspnea) are transient. Adenosine is antagonized by theophylline/caffeine (competitive adenosine receptor blockers) and potentiated by dipyridamole. Avoid in asthma (can cause bronchospasm).

</details>

**Q6. A patient on amiodarone for 2 years presents with progressive dyspnea on exertion. CXR shows bilateral interstitial infiltrates. PFTs reveal a restrictive pattern with reduced DLCO. TFTs show elevated TSH (6.8 mU/L). Which combination of amiodarone adverse effects is present?**
- A) Pulmonary fibrosis (most serious chronic SE) + hypothyroidism — both from amiodarone's iodine content and direct cellular toxicity
- B) Pulmonary edema + hyperthyroidism — from iodine-induced thyrotoxicosis
- C) Pneumonia + hypothyroidism — amiodarone suppresses thyroid iodine uptake
- D) Pleural effusion + euthyroid sick syndrome — not a true adverse effect

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A** — Amiodarone has the longest t1/2 of any antiarrhythmic (40–55 days) and accumulates extensively. Its major adverse effects include: pulmonary fibrosis/pneumonitis (most serious, dose-dependent), both hypo- and hyperthyroidism (37% iodine by weight — can cause either Wolff-Chaikoff hypothyroidism or Jod-Basedow hyperthyroidism), hepatotoxicity, corneal microdeposits, and photosensitivity. Regular monitoring requires TFTs, LFTs, CXR, and PFTs.

</details>

**Q7. A patient with Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome develops AF with a ventricular rate of 200/min with wide bizarre QRS complexes. The team considers IV verapamil for rate control. Why is this contraindicated?**
- A) Verapamil causes reflex tachycardia in WPW via baroreceptor activation
- B) Verapamil blocks AV node but increases conduction through accessory pathway → risk of VF
- C) Verapamil's negative inotropy worsens pre-excitation syndrome hemodynamically
- D) Verapamil prolongs the QT interval, increasing VF risk in WPW

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — In WPW with AF, blocking the AV node with verapamil (or digoxin, adenosine) preferentially forces conduction through the accessory pathway (bundle of Kent), which conducts rapidly without decremental properties. This can accelerate ventricular response to 300+ bpm → degeneration into ventricular fibrillation. Treatment for WPW-AF is IV procainamide (Class IA) or electrical cardioversion. Radiofrequency ablation is curative.

</details>

## STATINS, ANTICOAGULANTS & ANTIPLATELETS (Q8–Q12)

**Q8. A 55-year-old man on atorvastatin for hypercholesterolemia starts antifungal therapy with itraconazole for a nail infection. Two weeks later he develops severe muscle pain and dark-colored urine. CK is 50,000 U/L. What is the mechanism of this interaction?**
- A) Itraconazole induces CYP3A4, increasing atorvastatin metabolism and causing toxic metabolite accumulation
- B) Itraconazole inhibits CYP3A4, reducing atorvastatin metabolism → elevated statin plasma levels → myopathy/rhabdomyolysis
- C) Both drugs independently cause myopathy; their combination simply doubles the risk
- D) Itraconazole inhibits P-glycoprotein, increasing atorvastatin renal clearance paradoxically

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Azole antifungals (itraconazole, ketoconazole, voriconazole) are potent CYP3A4 inhibitors. Atorvastatin, lovastatin, and simvastatin are CYP3A4 substrates. Inhibiting CYP3A4 markedly increases statin plasma levels → mitochondrial toxicity in skeletal muscle → myopathy → rhabdomyolysis (CK >10× ULN with myoglobinuria/dark urine). Pravastatin and rosuvastatin are less affected as they are not primarily CYP3A4 substrates.

</details>

**Q9. A 65-year-old woman is started on warfarin for atrial fibrillation. She is also started on rifampicin for latent TB prophylaxis. Two weeks later her INR is 1.2 (target 2–3) despite dose increases. What is the mechanism of subtherapeutic anticoagulation?**
- A) Rifampicin chelates warfarin in the gut, reducing absorption
- B) Rifampicin induces CYP2C9 (and other CYPs), markedly increasing warfarin metabolism → subtherapeutic levels
- C) Rifampicin displaces warfarin from albumin, increasing free drug but also increasing clearance
- D) Rifampicin inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase — the same target as warfarin — competing for the same site

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Rifampicin is the most potent inducer of CYP450 enzymes (CYP3A4, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP1A2, P-gp). Warfarin (especially S-warfarin) is metabolized by CYP2C9. Enzyme induction accelerates warfarin metabolism, dramatically lowering plasma levels and reducing anticoagulant effect. Patients on both drugs require substantially higher warfarin doses and close INR monitoring. The interaction reverses over weeks after stopping rifampicin (risk of over-anticoagulation).

</details>

**Q10. A 72-year-old man on heparin for 6 days for DVT treatment develops worsening thrombosis. His platelet count has dropped from 220,000 to 60,000/mm³. An ELISA for heparin-PF4 antibodies is positive. What is the diagnosis and what anticoagulant should be used instead?**
- A) Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura — plasma exchange with fresh frozen plasma
- B) Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT) — stop heparin, start argatroban or bivalirudin (direct thrombin inhibitors)
- C) Immune thrombocytopenic purpura — IV immunoglobulin and corticosteroids
- D) HIT — stop heparin, start warfarin immediately to prevent further clotting

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT) is an immune-mediated reaction where IgG antibodies against heparin-PF4 complex activate platelets, causing paradoxical thrombocytopenia WITH thrombosis (the "4 T's": Thrombocytopenia, Timing [5–10 days], Thrombosis, no oTher cause). Heparin must be stopped immediately and replaced with a non-heparin anticoagulant: argatroban or bivalirudin (direct thrombin inhibitors). Warfarin should NOT be started immediately (depletes Protein C first → paradoxical thrombosis); overlap with a direct thrombin inhibitor is required.

</details>

**Q11. A 40-year-old woman takes aspirin 100 mg/day after a TIA. Her platelet count is normal. She undergoes a surgical procedure requiring normal platelet function. How long must aspirin be stopped before surgery, and what is the pharmacological basis for this duration?**
- A) 24 hours — aspirin is rapidly metabolized
- B) 3 days — platelet function returns within 72 hours of stopping aspirin
- C) 7–10 days — aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX-1; recovery requires new platelet generation (platelet lifespan 7–10 days)
- D) 1 month — aspirin has a very long terminal half-life in platelets

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Aspirin irreversibly acetylates cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) in platelets, permanently blocking thromboxane A2 synthesis for the platelet's entire lifespan (~7–10 days). Since platelets are anucleate and cannot synthesize new COX, platelet function is impaired for 7–10 days. Surgery should be deferred 7–10 days after stopping aspirin, or platelet transfusion given for emergencies. This is in contrast to reversible COX inhibitors (e.g., ibuprofen), where platelet function recovers within hours of drug clearance.

</details>

**Q12. A patient taking clopidogrel post-coronary stenting is found to be a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer. Despite therapy, he has another stent thrombosis. Why is clopidogrel less effective in this patient?**
- A) Clopidogrel is not metabolized and acts directly on platelets — CYP status is irrelevant
- B) Clopidogrel is a prodrug requiring CYP2C19 activation to its active thiol metabolite; poor metabolizers have inadequate platelet inhibition
- C) CYP2C19 poor metabolizers overproduce clopidogrel metabolites, causing drug toxicity
- D) Clopidogrel requires CYP3A4, not CYP2C19, for activation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Clopidogrel is an inactive prodrug that requires hepatic CYP2C19 (and CYP3A4) to generate its active thiol metabolite, which irreversibly blocks the platelet ADP receptor (P2Y12). CYP2C19 poor metabolizers (common in East Asians — 14–21% prevalence) produce less active metabolite, resulting in inadequate platelet inhibition and increased risk of stent thrombosis. Prasugrel (requires less CYP2C19) or ticagrelor (direct-acting, no prodrug conversion needed) are preferred in poor metabolizers.

</details>

## DIURETICS & ADDITIONAL CVS (Q13–Q15)

**Q13. A 35-year-old woman with primary hyperaldosteronism is started on spironolactone. After 4 weeks she complains of breast tenderness and menstrual irregularities. These side effects are explained by which property of spironolactone?**
- A) Spironolactone inhibits renal 11β-hydroxylase, causing cortisol-like side effects
- B) Spironolactone has anti-androgenic and progestogenic activity (binds androgen and progesterone receptors) causing gynecomastia/galactorrhea, menstrual irregularities
- C) Spironolactone induces CYP19 (aromatase), increasing estrogen production
- D) Spironolactone-related hyperkalemia directly stimulates breast tissue

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Spironolactone is a non-selective mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist with additional anti-androgenic and progestogenic effects (binds progesterone and androgen receptors). These "off-target" effects cause gynecomastia, breast pain, and galactorrhea in men, and menstrual irregularities in women. Eplerenone is a selective mineralocorticoid antagonist without these hormonal side effects. Both reduce mortality in heart failure (RALES trial for spironolactone; EPHESUS for eplerenone).

</details>

**Q14. A 65-year-old patient on furosemide for heart failure is found to have serum calcium of 8.0 mg/dL (slightly low) and serum potassium of 2.9 mEq/L. His neighbor with nephrolithiasis is told to avoid thiazide diuretics. What explains the opposite effect on calcium between these two diuretic classes?**
- A) Furosemide inhibits PTH secretion; thiazides stimulate it — explaining opposite calcium effects
- B) Loop diuretics block NKCC2 in TAL → impair calcium reabsorption → hypocalcemia; thiazides block NCC in DCT → enhance passive calcium reabsorption → hypercalcemia
- C) Furosemide chelates calcium in tubular fluid; thiazides chelate phosphate and spare calcium
- D) Both cause hypocalcemia — the neighbor was given incorrect advice about thiazides

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Loop diuretics block the Na-K-2Cl co-transporter (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb of Henle. This disrupts the lumen-positive potential that drives calcium reabsorption, causing calciuresis and hypocalcemia. Thiazides block the Na-Cl co-transporter (NCC) in the DCT, causing volume contraction that enhances proximal calcium reabsorption; DCT calcium reabsorption via TRPV5 is also upregulated. Net result: thiazides cause hypercalcemia and are used therapeutically in hypercalciuric nephrolithiasis and nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.

</details>

**Q15. Nitrates are prescribed for stable angina. The patient is also taking sildenafil for erectile dysfunction. Why is this combination absolutely contraindicated?**
- A) Both drugs inhibit PDE5, causing excess cGMP accumulation and severe rebound hypertension
- B) Nitrates release NO → ↑ cGMP → vasodilation; sildenafil inhibits PDE5 (which degrades cGMP) → both pathways converge on massive cGMP accumulation → severe, potentially fatal hypotension
- C) Sildenafil inhibits nitrate metabolism (CYP3A4), raising nitrate levels to toxic doses
- D) The combination causes coronary artery steal phenomenon — blood diverts to systemic circulation worsening angina

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Nitrates release nitric oxide (NO) → activates guanylyl cyclase → ↑ cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation → vasodilation (venous > arterial). Sildenafil (and tadalafil, vardenafil) are PDE5 inhibitors that prevent cGMP degradation. Combining them leads to synergistic cGMP accumulation and profound vasodilation → severe hypotension, potentially fatal. This is an absolute contraindication — nitrates in any form must not be given within 24 hours of sildenafil/vardenafil (or 48 hours of tadalafil).

</details>
