## PHARMACOKINETICS (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A drug has a volume of distribution of 500 L in a 70 kg person. Which of the following best explains this?**
- A) The drug is largely confined to plasma
- B) The drug is extensively bound to plasma proteins
- C) The drug is extensively distributed into tissues
- D) The drug has poor lipid solubility

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

A very large Vd (well above plasma volume ~3 L or total body water ~42 L) indicates the drug is sequestered in peripheral tissues, leaving little in plasma. Drugs with high lipid solubility or tissue binding (e.g., chloroquine, amiodarone) have very large Vd. High plasma protein binding actually keeps drug in plasma, producing a small Vd.

</details>

---

**Q2. Which of the following drugs undergoes zero-order kinetics at therapeutic doses?**
- A) Penicillin
- B) Phenytoin
- C) Digoxin
- D) Warfarin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Phenytoin saturates its hepatic hydroxylation enzymes at therapeutic concentrations, switching from first-order to zero-order (saturation) kinetics — a small dose increase causes a disproportionately large rise in plasma levels. Ethanol is the classic example, but among the options only phenytoin shows this at therapeutic doses.

</details>

**Q3. A patient on rifampicin is started on oral contraceptives. She becomes pregnant. The most likely mechanism is:**
- A) Inhibition of CYP3A4 by rifampicin
- B) Induction of CYP3A4 by rifampicin increasing OCP metabolism
- C) Rifampicin displaces OCP from plasma proteins
- D) Rifampicin inhibits enterohepatic circulation of estrogen

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Rifampicin is the most potent inducer of CYP3A4 (and PgP). It accelerates hepatic metabolism of estrogen and progestins in OCPs, reducing their plasma levels below therapeutic concentrations and causing contraceptive failure. This is a high-yield drug interaction tested repeatedly in NEET PG.

</details>

---

**Q4. The loading dose of a drug is primarily determined by:**
- A) Half-life and clearance
- B) Volume of distribution and desired plasma concentration
- C) Bioavailability and first-pass metabolism
- D) Renal clearance and protein binding

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Loading dose = (Vd × Target plasma concentration) / Bioavailability. It is independent of half-life; its purpose is to rapidly achieve the target concentration. Maintenance dose (not loading dose) depends on clearance and half-life.

</details>

## AUTONOMIC PHARMACOLOGY (Q5–Q8)

**Q5. Which of the following is the drug of choice for anaphylactic shock?**
- A) Dopamine
- B) Norepinephrine
- C) Epinephrine (adrenaline)
- D) Hydrocortisone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Epinephrine is the drug of choice for anaphylaxis. It acts on α1 receptors (vasoconstriction, reverses hypotension), β1 receptors (increases cardiac output), and β2 receptors (bronchodilation, inhibits mast cell degranulation). No other single drug addresses all three life-threatening components simultaneously.

</details>

**Q6. A patient presents with bradycardia, miosis, excessive salivation, and muscle fasciculations after exposure to an organophosphate. Which drug reverses the nicotinic effects?**
- A) Atropine
- B) Pralidoxime (2-PAM)
- C) Diazepam
- D) Physostigmine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Atropine blocks muscarinic effects (DUMBELS: diarrhea, urination, miosis, bradycardia, emesis, lacrimation, salivation) but has NO effect on nicotinic receptors. Pralidoxime reactivates acetylcholinesterase before aging occurs, reversing nicotinic effects (muscle fasciculations, weakness, paralysis). It must be given within 24–48 hours before "aging" of the organophosphate-enzyme bond.

</details>

**Q7. Which adrenergic receptor subtype mediates pre-junctional inhibition of norepinephrine release?**
- A) α1
- B) α2
- C) β1
- D) β2

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Presynaptic α2 autoreceptors provide negative feedback — when activated by released NE, they inhibit further NE release. This is the mechanism of action of clonidine (central α2 agonist causing sympatholysis) and explains why non-selective α-blockers can cause reflex tachycardia by also blocking α2 feedback.

</details>

**Q8. Neostigmine differs from physostigmine in that neostigmine:**
- A) Crosses the blood–brain barrier
- B) Is a reversible cholinesterase inhibitor
- C) Has direct nicotinic agonist activity in addition to AChE inhibition
- D) Is used in glaucoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Neostigmine has both anticholinesterase activity AND direct nicotinic receptor agonist action, making it particularly effective at the neuromuscular junction (used to reverse non-depolarizing NMBs). Physostigmine is lipid-soluble and crosses the BBB; neostigmine is quaternary and does not. Both are reversible AChE inhibitors.

</details>

## CVS PHARMACOLOGY (Q9–Q12)

**Q9. Which of the following antihypertensives is the drug of choice in hypertension with bilateral renal artery stenosis?**
- A) Enalapril
- B) Losartan
- C) Amlodipine
- D) Ramipril

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

ACE inhibitors (enalapril, ramipril) and ARBs (losartan) are contraindicated in bilateral renal artery stenosis because efferent arteriolar dilation leads to loss of filtration pressure and acute renal failure. Calcium channel blockers like amlodipine are safe and preferred in this scenario.

</details>

**Q10. A 60-year-old man on digoxin develops hypokalemia due to diuretic use. He develops new ventricular arrhythmias. The most likely mechanism is:**
- A) Digoxin displaces K+ from Na+/K+ ATPase binding site
- B) Hypokalemia increases digoxin binding to Na+/K+ ATPase
- C) Digoxin directly activates K+ channels
- D) Hypokalemia reduces digoxin renal clearance

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Digoxin and K+ compete for the same binding site on Na+/K+ ATPase. Hypokalemia reduces competition, increasing digoxin binding and toxicity even at therapeutic digoxin levels. This is why hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypercalcemia all potentiate digoxin toxicity.

</details>

---

**Q11. Which class of antiarrhythmic drugs prolongs the action potential duration by blocking K+ channels (phase 3 repolarization)?**
- A) Class I
- B) Class II
- C) Class III
- D) Class IV

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Class III antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, sotalol, ibutilide, dofetilide) block IKr (rapid delayed rectifier K+ channels), prolonging repolarization and increasing the effective refractory period. This prolongs the QT interval — and is also responsible for their pro-arrhythmic risk (torsades de pointes).

</details>

**Q12. The drug of choice for hypertensive emergency in a pregnant woman with eclampsia is:**
- A) Sodium nitroprusside
- B) Hydralazine (IV) or Labetalol
- C) Enalaprilat
- D) Diazoxide

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

IV hydralazine and IV labetalol are first-line for acute hypertensive crises in pregnancy (eclampsia). ACE inhibitors/ARBs are absolutely contraindicated (teratogenic — fetal renal agenesis). Sodium nitroprusside can cause fetal cyanide toxicity. Magnesium sulfate prevents seizures but is not an antihypertensive.

</details>

## CNS PHARMACOLOGY (Q13–Q17)

**Q13. Malignant hyperthermia triggered by succinylcholine or halothane is treated with:**
- A) Bromocriptine
- B) Dantrolene
- C) Diazepam
- D) Cyproheptadine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Malignant hyperthermia is caused by uncontrolled Ca2+ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum via mutant RYR1 channels. Dantrolene directly blocks ryanodine receptors, preventing SR Ca2+ release and terminating the hypermetabolic crisis. Bromocriptine is used for neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS), which is a different entity.

</details>

---

**Q14. Which benzodiazepine is preferred in a patient with hepatic failure?**
- A) Diazepam
- B) Clonazepam
- C) Lorazepam
- D) Alprazolam

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

LOT — Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam — undergo direct glucuronide conjugation without hepatic phase-I (CYP) metabolism, making them safe in liver failure. Diazepam, alprazolam, and clonazepam require CYP3A4/2C19 metabolism and accumulate in hepatic impairment.

</details>

**Q15. A patient on levodopa develops "on-off" fluctuations. The addition of which drug would most reduce these fluctuations by preventing peripheral metabolism of levodopa?**
- A) Selegiline
- B) Carbidopa
- C) Entacapone
- D) Bromocriptine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Entacapone (a peripheral COMT inhibitor) prevents the conversion of levodopa to 3-O-methyldopa in the periphery, prolonging levodopa's plasma half-life and smoothing plasma levels, thereby reducing on-off fluctuations. Carbidopa inhibits peripheral dopa decarboxylase but is already included in standard levodopa preparations; it doesn't address fluctuations as effectively as adding a COMT inhibitor.

</details>

**Q16. Lithium toxicity is potentiated by:**
- A) Furosemide
- B) Thiazide diuretics
- C) Spironolactone
- D) Acetazolamide

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Thiazides cause Na+ depletion, and because Li+ is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule alongside Na+, Na+ depletion leads to compensatory proximal Li+ reabsorption and toxicity. NSAIDs also increase Li+ toxicity. Furosemide is safer (though still requires monitoring). This is a classic NEET PG drug interaction.

</details>

**Q17. The drug of choice for absence (petit mal) seizures is:**
- A) Phenytoin
- B) Carbamazepine
- C) Ethosuximide
- D) Valproate

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Ethosuximide is the drug of choice for pure absence seizures. It blocks T-type (low threshold) Ca2+ channels in thalamic neurons, interrupting the 3 Hz spike-and-wave oscillation characteristic of absence epilepsy. Valproate is used when absence co-exists with generalized tonic-clonic seizures. Phenytoin and carbamazepine are ineffective and can worsen absence seizures.

</details>

## ANTIMICROBIAL PHARMACOLOGY (Q18–Q21)

**Q18. Which antibiotic acts by inhibiting the 30S ribosomal subunit by mimicking tRNA and causing misreading of the mRNA codon?**
- A) Chloramphenicol
- B) Clindamycin
- C) Aminoglycosides
- D) Macrolides

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Aminoglycosides bind irreversibly to the 16S rRNA of the 30S subunit, causing misreading of the mRNA and incorporation of wrong amino acids (nonsense proteins). They are bactericidal (concentration-dependent killing). Chloramphenicol and clindamycin inhibit the 50S subunit; macrolides also act on 50S (block translocation).

</details>

**Q19. A patient with a UTI caused by MRSA is best treated with:**
- A) Vancomycin
- B) Linezolid
- C) Cefazolin
- D) Nitrofurantoin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Vancomycin is the drug of choice for systemic MRSA infections. For uncomplicated MRSA UTI, vancomycin (renally excreted, reaches high urine concentrations) is preferred. Linezolid achieves poor urinary concentrations. Nitrofurantoin covers some MSSA but not reliably MRSA. Cephalosporins like cefazolin are ineffective against MRSA.

</details>

**Q20. Which of the following antitubercular drugs causes optic neuritis as a dose-dependent side effect?**
- A) Isoniazid
- B) Rifampicin
- C) Ethambutol
- D) Pyrazinamide

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Ethambutol causes retrobulbar (optic) neuritis manifesting as reduced visual acuity, loss of red-green color discrimination, and central scotoma — dose- and duration-dependent. Baseline visual acuity testing is mandatory before starting ethambutol. INH causes peripheral neuropathy (prevented by pyridoxine); rifampicin causes hepatotoxicity and orange body fluids; pyrazinamide causes hyperuricemia and hepatotoxicity.

</details>

**Q21. Metronidazole is the drug of choice for all of the following EXCEPT:**
- A) Anaerobic infections (Bacteroides fragilis)
- B) Giardiasis
- C) Cryptosporidiosis

<details>

## ENDOCRINE PHARMACOLOGY (Q22–Q24)

**Q22. A type 2 diabetic on metformin develops severe lactic acidosis. Which condition most commonly precipitates this?**
- A) Hyperkalemia
- B) Renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min)
- C) Hepatic steatosis
- D) Obesity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Metformin is renally cleared; in renal impairment (eGFR <30), it accumulates and inhibits hepatic mitochondrial complex I, impairing gluconeogenesis and causing lactic acidosis. Metformin is contraindicated when eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m². It should also be held before IV contrast administration due to risk of contrast-induced AKI.

</details>

**Q23. Which sulfonylurea is preferred in elderly diabetics because it does NOT produce active metabolites and has the shortest duration of action?**
- A) Glibenclamide (glyburide)
- B) Glimepiride
- C) Glipizide
- D) Chlorpropamide

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Glipizide is the safest sulfonylurea in the elderly because it is metabolized to inactive metabolites and has a short duration of action (~12–16 hours), minimizing the risk of prolonged hypoglycemia. Glibenclamide and chlorpropamide have active metabolites and long durations — dangerous in the elderly with declining renal function.

</details>

**Q24. The drug of choice for thyroid storm (thyrotoxic crisis) is:**
- A) Radioactive iodine (I-131)
- B) Carbimazole alone
- C) Propylthiouracil (PTU) + propranolol + glucocorticoids + Lugol's iodine
- D) Methimazole + atenolol

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Thyroid storm requires a multi-pronged approach: PTU (preferred over methimazole because it also blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion), propranolol (controls adrenergic symptoms and also blocks T4→T3 conversion at high doses), glucocorticoids (reduce hormone release and conversion), and Lugol's iodine (given 1 hour AFTER PTU/MMI to prevent iodine being used for new hormone synthesis — Wolff-Chaikoff effect).

</details>

## ANTICANCER DRUGS (Q25–Q27)

**Q25. Which anticancer drug causes hemorrhagic cystitis as its most feared complication, prevented by mesna and hyperhydration?**
- A) Methotrexate
- B) Cyclophosphamide
- C) Cisplatin
- D) Bleomycin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cyclophosphamide (and ifosfamide) are metabolized to acrolein, which accumulates in the bladder and causes chemical cystitis → hemorrhagic cystitis. Mesna (2-mercaptoethane sulfonate Na+) binds acrolein in the urine, detoxifying it. Cisplatin causes nephrotoxicity and peripheral neuropathy; bleomycin causes pulmonary fibrosis; methotrexate causes mucositis and myelosuppression.

</details>

**Q26. A patient receiving bleomycin develops progressive dyspnea and bilateral pulmonary infiltrates. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Bleomycin-induced cardiomyopathy
- B) Bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis
- C) Opportunistic pneumocystis pneumonia
- D) Bleomycin-induced nephrotoxicity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bleomycin's signature toxicity is pulmonary fibrosis (due to free radical-mediated DNA strand breaks in lung tissue, which has low bleomycin hydrolase). It is dose-dependent and potentiated by high FiO2 (avoid high-flow O2 in patients exposed to bleomycin). Regular pulmonary function tests (DLCO) are mandatory during treatment.

</details>

**Q27. Which targeted therapy is used in CML and works by inhibiting the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase?**
- A) Rituximab
- B) Trastuzumab
- C) Imatinib (Gleevec)
- D) Bevacizumab

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Imatinib (Gleevec) was the first targeted tyrosine kinase inhibitor, designed to block BCR-ABL (the constitutively active kinase produced by the Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) translocation in CML). It revolutionized CML treatment. Rituximab targets CD20 (B-cell lymphomas); trastuzumab targets HER2 (breast cancer); bevacizumab targets VEGF (anti-angiogenic).

</details>

- A) Metronidazole

- C) Amphotericin B
- D) Teicoplanin

**Q29. A patient on isoniazid (INH) for tuberculosis develops peripheral neuropathy. The most likely mechanism is:**
- A) Direct neurotoxicity of INH metabolites
- B) INH-induced pyridoxine (B6) deficiency
- C) INH-induced folate deficiency
- D) INH-induced pellagra

<details>

INH competes with pyridoxal phosphate (active B6) for the enzyme pyridoxine kinase and also increases urinary excretion of pyridoxine, causing functional B6 deficiency → peripheral neuropathy (sensory > motor). ## CYP450 DRUG INTERACTIONS (Q31–Q35)

**Q31. A 55-year-old man on warfarin for atrial fibrillation is started on fluconazole for oral candidiasis. Three days later he presents with gum bleeding and INR is 8.5. The most likely mechanism is:**
- A) Fluconazole displaces warfarin from plasma protein binding sites
- B) Fluconazole inhibits CYP2C9, reducing warfarin metabolism and increasing its plasma levels
- C) Fluconazole induces CYP3A4, increasing warfarin clearance
- D) Fluconazole increases vitamin K absorption

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Warfarin (S-warfarin, the more active enantiomer) is primarily metabolized by CYP2C9. Fluconazole is a potent inhibitor of CYP2C9 (and CYP3A4), thus blocking warfarin metabolism, raising plasma warfarin levels and increasing anticoagulant effect — causing dangerous bleeding. This is a classic NEET PG drug interaction. Other CYP2C9 inhibitors: amiodarone, metronidazole. The one-liner: warfarin + fluconazole = CYP2C9 inhibition → elevated INR + bleeding risk.

</details>

**Q32. A 40-year-old epileptic woman on carbamazepine starts taking the antifungal voriconazole. After 2 weeks, she develops a fungal infection despite treatment. The mechanism of treatment failure is:**
- A) Voriconazole inhibits carbamazepine metabolism, increasing toxicity
- B) Carbamazepine induces CYP3A4 and CYP2C19, accelerating voriconazole metabolism
- C) Voriconazole activates P-glycoprotein, excreting carbamazepine
- D) Drug interaction occurs at the renal tubular level

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Carbamazepine is a potent inducer of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP2C19. Voriconazole is extensively metabolized by CYP2C19 and CYP3A4. Carbamazepine induction dramatically increases voriconazole clearance, reducing its plasma levels below therapeutic concentrations → treatment failure. This combination is actually CONTRAINDICATED by voriconazole prescribing information. The one-liner: carbamazepine = strong CYP inducer → reduces levels of CYP-metabolized drugs (voriconazole, OCPs, cyclosporine).

</details>

**Q33. A patient on simvastatin for hyperlipidemia is prescribed clarithromycin for a respiratory infection. Two weeks later, he presents with proximal muscle weakness and elevated CK (8000 U/L). The complication and mechanism is:**
- A) Clarithromycin direct myotoxicity
- B) Rhabdomyolysis due to clarithromycin inhibiting CYP3A4 and increasing simvastatin plasma levels
- C) Autoimmune myopathy triggered by antibiotic
- D) Hypokalemia from clarithromycin-induced GI losses

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Simvastatin (and lovastatin, atorvastatin) is metabolized by CYP3A4. **Q34. A 60-year-old man on warfarin is also taking omeprazole, aspirin, and phenytoin. Which of these drugs causes the GREATEST increase in warfarin effect?**
- A) Omeprazole (CYP2C19 inhibitor with minor CYP2C9 effect)
- B) Aspirin (displaces warfarin from protein binding + additive GI bleeding)
- C) Phenytoin (initial CYP2C9 inhibition → later induction)
- D) All have equal effect

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Phenytoin has a complex biphasic interaction with warfarin: initial co-administration can transiently INHIBIT warfarin metabolism (raising INR), followed by CYP induction reducing warfarin levels over time. The net effect is unpredictable and dangerous — frequent INR monitoring is essential. Aspirin increases bleeding risk additively (platelet inhibition + GI mucosal damage) even without changing INR. The one-liner: phenytoin + warfarin = unpredictable biphasic interaction (initial inhibition then induction) → monitor INR closely.

</details>

**Q35. A 35-year-old HIV-positive man on antiretroviral therapy (lopinavir/ritonavir) is prescribed rifampicin for TB. The physician must change the regimen. Ritonavir's primary pharmacological role in combination ART is:**
- A) Antiviral activity against HIV reverse transcriptase
- B) CYP3A4 inhibitor — pharmacokinetic booster increasing lopinavir levels
- C) P-glycoprotein inducer enhancing CNS penetration
- D) Direct protease inhibitor used at full therapeutic dose

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ritonavir at low "boosting" doses (100 mg) is used as a pharmacokinetic booster — it potently inhibits CYP3A4 (and CYP2D6), increasing the plasma AUC of co-administered protease inhibitors (lopinavir, atazanavir, darunavir) by up to 10-fold. This allows lower doses and improved pharmacokinetics. Rifampicin, a potent CYP3A4 inducer, counteracts ritonavir boosting and causes PI treatment failure — the combination is avoided. The one-liner: ritonavir = CYP3A4 inhibitor used as pharmacokinetic booster; rifampicin = CYP3A4 inducer = CONTRAINDICATED with PI-based ART.

</details>

## ADVERSE DRUG EFFECTS (Q36–Q40)

**Q36. A 30-year-old woman with seizures is started on carbamazepine. After 2 weeks, she develops fever, a diffuse erythematous maculopapular rash that progresses to blistering and skin peeling affecting >30% body surface area, and mucosal involvement (oral and conjunctival erosions). The diagnosis and causative drug class is:**
- A) Drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS); allopurinol
- B) Toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN); anticonvulsants (especially aromatic — carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine)
- C) Fixed drug eruption; NSAIDs
- D) Stevens-Johnson syndrome; sulfonamides only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) = >30% BSA skin detachment + mucosal involvement. Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) = <10%; SJS-TEN overlap = 10–30%. The most common culprit drugs: aromatic anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine), sulfonamides (co-trimoxazole), allopurinol, NSAIDs. Both SJS and TEN are T-cell mediated immune reactions with keratinocyte apoptosis. Mortality in TEN is ~30%. The one-liner: carbamazepine/lamotrigine/sulfonamides → SJS/TEN risk = skin emergency, stop drug immediately, treat in burns unit.

</details>

**Q38. A 25-year-old man with Crohn's disease on azathioprine presents with pancytopenia. TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase) enzyme activity is tested and found to be absent. The mechanism of azathioprine toxicity in this patient is:**
- A) Azathioprine is converted to active 6-MP which is NOT detoxified, accumulating toxic thioguanine nucleotides
- B) Azathioprine directly suppresses bone marrow by chelating iron
- C) Azathioprine oxidation by xanthine oxidase produces reactive oxygen species
- D) Azathioprine is renally cleared and accumulates due to renal impairment

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Azathioprine is converted to 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP) → then either: (a) detoxified by TPMT to methylmercaptopurine (inactive), or (b) anabolized to 6-thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN, active but toxic at high levels). Low/absent TPMT → all 6-MP goes to toxic 6-TGN → myelosuppression. TPMT genotyping before azathioprine is strongly recommended. Allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase (which metabolizes 6-MP) — dangerous combination with azathioprine. The one-liner: absent TPMT = azathioprine toxicity (myelosuppression); always check TPMT before starting azathioprine/6-MP.

</details>

**Q39. A 50-year-old man on amiodarone for atrial fibrillation develops progressive dyspnea, dry cough, and bilateral ground-glass opacities on CT chest. PFTs show a restrictive pattern with reduced DLCO. The diagnosis and next step is:**
- A) Amiodarone-induced pulmonary fibrosis; stop amiodarone, start corticosteroids
- B) Amiodarone-induced hepatotoxicity; LFTs and liver biopsy
- C) Heart failure exacerbation; increase diuretics

**Q46. A 70-year-old man with heart failure (EF 30%) and atrial fibrillation is on digoxin 0.25 mg/day. Which electrolyte abnormality most potentiates digoxin toxicity?**
- A) Hyperkalemia
- B) Hypokalemia
- C) Hypernatremia
- D) Hypermagnesemia

<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Digoxin inhibits Na+/K+-ATPase. Hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity because K+ and digoxin compete for the same binding site on Na+/K+-ATPase — low extracellular K+ means less competition, so digoxin binds more effectively, enhancing toxicity. The one-liner: hypokalemia + hypomagnesemia = increase digoxin toxicity; antidote = digibind (Fab fragments) for life-threatening toxicity.
