## 1. PHARMACOKINETICS

### Bioavailability
- **Definition**: Fraction of administered drug that reaches systemic circulation unchanged
- **Oral bioavailability affected by**: first-pass metabolism, gut wall metabolism, absorption
- **IV route**: bioavailability = 100% (reference standard)
- **F = (AUC oral / AUC IV) × 100**

### First-Pass Effect (Pre-systemic Metabolism)
- Occurs in gut wall and liver before drug reaches systemic circulation
- **High first-pass drugs**: morphine, nitroglycerin, propranolol, lidocaine, aspirin, testosterone, levodopa
- **Consequence**: oral dose >> IV dose needed for same effect
- **Nitroglycerin**: given sublingually/transdermally to bypass first-pass

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Nitroglycerin is given sublingually to avoid first-pass effect

### Volume of Distribution (Vd)
- **Formula**: Vd = Dose / Plasma concentration
- **Vd = 3-5 L**: stays in plasma (large MW, protein bound) — heparin, warfarin
- **Vd = 10-20 L**: distribution into interstitial fluid
- **Vd > 20 L (very high)**: extensive tissue binding — chloroquine, digoxin, amiodarone

| Drug | Vd | Implication |
|------|-----|-------------|
| Heparin | ~0.06 L/kg | Plasma only |
| Warfarin | ~0.14 L/kg | Highly protein bound |
| Chloroquine | ~200-800 L/kg | Extensive tissue binding |
| Digoxin | ~7 L/kg | Stored in muscle/tissue |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: High Vd = NOT dialyzable (drug in tissues, not plasma)

### Protein Binding
- **Acidic drugs bind albumin**: warfarin, phenytoin, NSAIDs, furosemide
- **Basic drugs bind alpha-1 acid glycoprotein**: propranolol, lidocaine, quinidine
- **Only free (unbound) drug** is pharmacologically active
- **Displacement interactions**: one drug displaces another → increased free drug → toxicity
- **Warfarin + aspirin**: aspirin displaces warfarin → bleeding risk

### Half-Life (t1/2)
- **t1/2 = 0.693 × Vd / CL**
- Time to reach 50% of initial concentration
- **Steady state reached in 4-5 half-lives**
- **Drug eliminated in ~4-5 half-lives** (>94% eliminated)
- **Short t1/2**: phenytoin, warfarin (NOT true — warfarin t1/2 = 36 hrs)
- **Long t1/2**: amiodarone (40-55 days), digoxin (36-48 hrs)

### Clearance
- **CL = Vd × Ke (elimination rate constant)**
- **CL = Dose / AUC**
- **Hepatic CL**: depends on hepatic blood flow and extraction ratio
- **High extraction ratio drugs**: first-pass effect significant (morphine, propranolol)
- **Low extraction ratio drugs**: changes in protein binding affect clearance

### Zero-Order vs First-Order Kinetics

| Parameter | Zero-Order | First-Order |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Rate | Constant amount/time | Constant fraction/time |
| Enzyme | Saturated | Not saturated |
| t1/2 | Not constant | Constant |
| Examples | **Alcohol, phenytoin (at high doses), aspirin (high doses), heparin** | Most drugs |
| Graph (log conc vs time) | Curved | Straight line |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Phenytoin - zero order kinetics (capacity-limited/saturation kinetics) — small dose increase → disproportionate rise in plasma levels → toxicity

### Loading Dose / Maintenance Dose

- **Loading Dose = (Vd × Target Css) / F**
- **Maintenance Dose = CL × Target Css / F**
- **Loading dose**: used when rapid onset needed (digoxin, amiodarone, phenytoin)
- **Digoxin loading dose**: 0.5–1 mg given in divided doses (digitalizing dose)

### Enzyme Induction

**Mnemonic: "CRAP GPS"** — Carbamazepine, Rifampicin, Alcohol (chronic), Phenytoin, Griseofulvin, Phenobarbitone, Sulphonylureas

| Inducer | Drugs whose levels DECREASE |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **Rifampicin** (most potent inducer) | Warfarin, OCPs, cyclosporine, azoles, protease inhibitors |
| **Phenytoin** | Warfarin, OCPs, doxycycline |
| **Carbamazepine** | Warfarin, OCPs, valproate, thyroid hormones |
| **Phenobarbitone** | Vitamin D metabolism (→ rickets), bilirubin, steroids |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Rifampicin + OCP → contraceptive failure

### Enzyme Inhibition

| Inhibitor | Mechanism | Consequence |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Ketoconazole** | CYP3A4 inhibition | ↑ warfarin, cyclosporine, statins |
| **Cimetidine** | Multiple CYP inhibition | ↑ warfarin, theophylline, phenytoin |
| **Erythromycin/Clarithromycin** | CYP3A4 | ↑ statins (rhabdomyolysis) |
| **Fluoxetine/Paroxetine** | CYP2D6 | ↑ TCAs, codeine effects |
| **Metronidazole** | CYP2C9 | ↑ warfarin → bleeding |
| **Grapefruit juice** | CYP3A4 in gut | ↑ felodipine, statins |

## 2. AUTONOMIC PHARMACOLOGY

### Cholinergic System

#### Muscarinic Receptors (M1-M5)

| Receptor | Location | Effect | Selective Agonist/Antagonist |
|----------|---------|--------|------------------------------|
| **M1** | Ganglia, CNS, gastric parietal cells | ↑ gastric acid, cognitive | Pirenzepine (blocks) |
| **M2** | Heart (SA/AV node) | ↓ HR, ↓ conduction | — |
| **M3** | Smooth muscle, glands, eye | Contraction, secretion, miosis | Darifenacin (bladder specific) |
| **M4** | CNS | — | — |
| **M5** | CNS | — | — |

**Muscarinic effects (SLUDGE/DUMBELS):**
- **SLUDGE**: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis
- **DUMBELS**: Defecation/Diarrhea, Urination, Miosis/Muscle weakness, Bradycardia/Bronchospasm/secretions, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation/Sweating

#### Nicotinic Receptors
- **Nm (muscle type)**: Neuromuscular junction — depolarizing (suxamethonium) and non-depolarizing (tubocurarine) blockers act here
- **Nn (ganglionic type)**: Autonomic ganglia — blocked by hexamethonium, trimethaphan

### Anticholinesterases

| Type | Examples | Reversal |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Reversible - carbamate** | Neostigmine, physostigmine, pyridostigmine, edrophonium | Atropine |
| **Reversible - short acting** | Edrophonium (Tensilon test - myasthenia gravis diagnosis) | Atropine |
| **Irreversible - organophosphates** | Malathion, parathion, TEPP, tabun, sarin (nerve gases) | **Atropine + Pralidoxime (2-PAM)** |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Pralidoxime works by regenerating AChE before "aging" occurs (within 24-48 hrs for most OPs). After aging, pralidoxime is INEFFECTIVE.

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Physostigmine (tertiary amine) crosses BBB — used for atropine/TCA poisoning. Neostigmine (quaternary) does NOT cross BBB.

### Muscarinic Blockers — Atropine

**Mechanism**: Competitive antagonist at muscarinic receptors

**Effects**:
- **Heart**: ↑ HR (M2 block) — used in sinus bradycardia
- **Eye**: Mydriasis, cycloplegia (M3 block)
- **Glands**: ↓ secretions (dry mouth, dry skin, anhidrosis)
- **Smooth muscle**: ↓ GI motility, bronchodilation, urinary retention
- **CNS**: Delirium, hallucinations (high doses)

**Atropine Toxicity Mnemonic**: "**Dry as a bone, blind as a bat, red as a beet, hot as a hare, mad as a hatter**"
- Dry mouth/skin (dry as bone)
- Mydriasis (blind as bat)
- Flushing (red as beet)
- Hyperthermia (hot as hare)
- Delirium (mad as hatter)
- **Tachycardia** (distinctive)

**Atropine Uses**:
1. Organophosphate poisoning (antidote)
2. Preoperative medication (↓ secretions)
3. Sinus bradycardia
4. AV block (temporary)
5. Peptic ulcer (pirenzepine preferred)
6. Mydriasis for fundoscopy

**Contraindications**: Glaucoma (angle-closure), BPH, pyloric stenosis

### Adrenergic System

#### Alpha Receptors

| Receptor | Location | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| **α1** | Vascular smooth muscle, iris (dilator), bladder sphincter, prostate | Vasoconstriction, mydriasis, urinary retention |
| **α2** | Presynaptic (autoreceptor), pancreatic β-cells, platelets | ↓ NE release, ↓ insulin, platelet aggregation |

#### Beta Receptors

| Receptor | Location | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| **β1** | Heart, kidney (JGA) | ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, ↑ renin release |
| **β2** | Bronchi, vascular SM (skeletal muscle), uterus, liver | Bronchodilation, vasodilation, uterine relaxation, glycogenolysis |
| **β3** | Adipose tissue, bladder detrusor | Lipolysis, bladder relaxation |

### Sympathomimetics — Key Drugs

| Drug | Receptors | Dose-dependent | Clinical Use |
|------|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Adrenaline (Epinephrine)** | α1, α2, β1, β2 | Low dose: β predominates (↓BP possible); High dose: α predominates (↑BP) | Anaphylaxis (drug of choice), cardiac arrest, additive to LA |
| **Noradrenaline** | α1, α2, β1 (weak β2) | ↑ BP → reflex bradycardia | Septic shock (vasopressor) |
| **Dopamine** | D1, D2, β1, α1 | **Low dose (1-5 μg/kg/min): D1 → renal/splanchnic vasodilation; Medium (5-10): β1 → ↑CO; High (>10): α1 → vasoconstriction** | Cardiogenic shock, acute heart failure |
| **Dobutamine** | β1 > β2 | ↑ contractility > ↑ HR | Acute heart failure, stress echo |
| **Phenylephrine** | α1 selective | Vasoconstriction, reflex bradycardia | Nasal decongestant, hypotension |
| **Clonidine** | α2 (central) | ↓ sympathetic outflow → ↓ BP, ↓ HR | Hypertension, ADHD, opioid withdrawal |
| **Salbutamol (Albuterol)** | β2 selective | Bronchodilation, uterine relaxation | Asthma, preterm labor tocolysis |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Adrenaline reversal (epinephrine reversal) — if α blocked first, then adrenaline given → only β effects → hypotension instead of hypertension

### Beta-Blockers

**Mechanism**: Competitive antagonism at β-adrenoceptors

#### Cardioselective (β1 selective) — "A BEAM" Mnemonic
**A**tenolol, **B**isoprolol, **E**smolol, **A**cebutolol, **M**etoprolol

| Property | Non-selective | Cardioselective |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Receptors blocked | β1 + β2 | β1 > β2 |
| Bronchospasm risk | Higher | Lower (safer in asthma — NOT safe, just safer) |
| Diabetes masking | Yes | Less |
| Examples | Propranolol, timolol, carvedilol, labetalol, sotalol, nadolol | Atenolol, metoprolol, bisoprolol, esmolol |

**Additional properties**:
- **Labetalol/Carvedilol**: α + β blockade (non-selective) — used in hypertensive crisis in pregnancy (labetalol), heart failure (carvedilol)
- **Sotalol**: β-blocker + class III antiarrhythmic (K channel block)
- **Pindolol/Acebutolol**: Intrinsic sympathomimetic activity (ISA) — less bradycardia/less cold extremities
- **Timolol**: Ophthalmic — glaucoma (↓ aqueous production)
- **Esmolol**: Ultra-short acting (IV) — t1/2 = 9 min — perioperative tachycardia/hypertension

**Contraindications**: Asthma, COPD, 2nd/3rd degree AV block, cardiogenic shock, sick sinus syndrome
**Beta-blocker withdrawal**: Rebound hypertension, tachycardia, angina — taper slowly

## 3. CVS PHARMACOLOGY

### Antihypertensives

#### ACE Inhibitors
**Drugs**: Captopril (short-acting, SH group), Enalapril (prodrug), Lisinopril, Ramipril, Perindopril

**Mechanism**: Block ACE → ↓ angiotensin II → vasodilation; ↑ bradykinin → **cough** (SE)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | ACE inhibition → ↓ Ang II → ↓ aldosterone → ↓ Na retention |
| **Key SE** | **Dry cough** (bradykinin), hyperkalemia, **angioedema** (bradykinin - contraindicate in hereditary angioedema), first-dose hypotension, teratogenic (Class D) |
| **Contraindications** | Pregnancy (bilateral renal artery stenosis, hyperkalemia, history of angioedema) |
| **Indications** | HTN, heart failure (↓ mortality), post-MI, diabetic nephropathy, proteinuria |
| **Renal protection** | ↓ efferent arteriolar tone → ↓ intraglomerular pressure → ↓ proteinuria |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: ACE inhibitor cough → switch to ARB (no bradykinin accumulation)

#### ARBs (Angiotensin Receptor Blockers)
**Drugs**: Losartan, Valsartan, Candesartan, Irbesartan, Telmisartan, Olmesartan

- **Mechanism**: AT1 receptor blockade
- **NO cough** (no bradykinin accumulation)
- **Same contraindications as ACEi** (pregnancy, bilateral RAS, hyperkalemia)
- Losartan: uricosuric — used in hypertension + gout

#### Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

| Type | Drugs | Main Effect | Uses |
|------|-------|------------|------|
| **DHP (Dihydropyridines)** | Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Felodipine, Nicardipine | Vascular selective → vasodilation → reflex tachycardia | HTN, angina, Raynaud's |
| **Non-DHP** | Verapamil, Diltiazem | Heart selective → ↓ HR, ↓ AV conduction | Arrhythmias, angina, HTN |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Verapamil — most cardiodepressant CCB (avoid with beta-blockers → complete heart block)

**Side effects of DHPs**: Peripheral edema, flushing, headache, reflex tachycardia (especially nifedipine)
**Non-DHP SE**: Constipation (verapamil most), bradycardia, AV block, negative inotropy

#### Diuretics

| Class | Drug | Mechanism | Site | Key SE |
|-------|------|-----------|------|--------|
| **Thiazides** | HCTZ, Chlorthalidone, Indapamide | Na-Cl co-transporter block | DCT | Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, **hypercalcemia**, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia |
| **Loop** | Furosemide, Bumetanide, Ethacrynic acid | Na-K-2Cl co-transporter block | TAL of Loop of Henle | **Hypokalemia**, hypocalcemia, ototoxicity (ethacrynic acid > furosemide), metabolic alkalosis |
| **K-sparing** | Spironolactone, Eplerenone (aldosterone antagonist); Triamterene, Amiloride (ENaC blockers) | Aldosterone blockade / ENaC block | Collecting duct | **Hyperkalemia**, gynecomastia (spironolactone), metabolic acidosis |
| **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors** | Acetazolamide | CA inhibition | PCT | Metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia, renal stones |
| **Osmotic** | Mannitol | Osmotic effect | PCT, descending limb | Pulmonary edema (if given in heart failure), contraindicated in anuria |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Thiazides → hypercalcemia (used in nephrogenic DI, hypercalciuria); Loop diuretics → hypocalcemia

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Spironolactone → reduces mortality in heart failure (RALES trial); also used in primary hyperaldosteronism, PCOS

### Antiarrhythmics — Vaughan-Williams Classification

#### Class I — Sodium Channel Blockers

| Subclass | Drugs | Properties | Uses |
|----------|-------|-----------|------|
| **IA** | Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide | ↑ AP duration, ↑ ERP, moderate Na block, K block too | AF, VT, WPW |
| **IB** | Lidocaine, Mexiletine, Phenytoin | ↓ AP duration, preferentially binds inactivated Na channels (ischemic/fast heart) | Ventricular arrhythmias (especially post-MI), digoxin-induced arrhythmias |
| **IC** | Flecainide, Propafenone | ↑↑ PR/QRS/QT, markedly slows conduction, no change in AP duration | AF/AFL, SVT (avoid post-MI — proarrhythmic) |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Quinidine — cinchonism (tinnitus, headache, visual disturbances), torsades de pointes (↑ QT), thrombocytopenia

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Lidocaine — Class IB, first-line for VT in post-MI, given IV only (high first-pass orally)

#### Class II — Beta-Blockers
- Propranolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol
- **↓ SA node automaticity, ↓ AV conduction**
- Used in: SVT, AF rate control, post-MI arrhythmias

#### Class III — Potassium Channel Blockers

| Drug | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| **Amiodarone** | Longest t1/2 (40-55 days), also has class I/II/IV properties, **multiple side effects** (thyroid/pulmonary/hepatic/corneal/skin) |
| **Sotalol** | Also beta-blocker, torsades risk |
| **Ibutilide** | AF/AFL conversion |
| **Dofetilide** | AF maintenance |

**Amiodarone Side Effects** (must memorize):
- **Thyroid**: Both hypo AND hyperthyroidism (contains iodine — 37% iodine by weight)
- **Pulmonary**: Pulmonary fibrosis/pneumonitis (most serious chronic SE)
- **Hepatic**: Hepatotoxicity (transaminase elevation)
- **Corneal**: Microdeposits (almost universal, mostly asymptomatic)
- **Skin**: Photosensitivity, blue-grey discoloration
- **Neurological**: Peripheral neuropathy, tremor, ataxia
- **Monitoring**: TFT, LFT, CXR, pulmonary function, eye exam

#### Class IV — Calcium Channel Blockers (Non-DHP)
- **Verapamil, Diltiazem**
- ↓ SA node automaticity, ↓ AV conduction
- Used in: SVT (PSVT termination/prevention), AF rate control
- **Contraindicated in WPW** (↑ accessory pathway conduction → VF)

#### Other Antiarrhythmics
- **Adenosine**: Drug of choice for acute PSVT — very short t1/2 (10 sec), causes transient AV block
  - SE: flushing, chest tightness, bronchospasm (avoid in asthma)
  - Antagonized by: theophylline/caffeine (adenosine receptor blockers)
  - Potentiated by: dipyridamole
- **Digoxin**: ↑ vagal tone → ↓ AV conduction; rate control in AF with heart failure
  - **Toxicity**: GI (nausea, vomiting), visual (yellow-green halos), arrhythmias (virtually any), hyperkalemia worsens toxicity
  - **Treatment of digoxin toxicity**: Digoxin-specific Fab antibodies (Digibind)

### Antianginals

#### Nitrates
**Mechanism**: Release NO → cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation → venodilation (preload ↓) > arteriolar dilation (afterload ↓)

| Drug | Route | Duration | Use |
|------|-------|---------|-----|
| GTN (Nitroglycerin) | SL, transdermal, IV | SL: 30 min; patch: 24 hrs | Acute angina attack, chronic stable angina |
| Isosorbide dinitrate (ISDN) | Oral, SL | 4-6 hrs | Prophylaxis |
| Isosorbide mononitrate (ISMN) | Oral | 8-12 hrs | Prophylaxis |

**Nitrate Tolerance**: Develop with continuous use
- Mechanism: Depletion of sulfhydryl groups, neurohormonal activation
- Prevention: **Nitrate-free interval of 8-12 hours** (eccentric dosing)
- **Tolerance does NOT develop with sublingual GTN** (used acutely)

**Nitrate SE**: Headache (most common — vasodilation), postural hypotension, reflex tachycardia, methemoglobinemia (high doses)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Nitrates + Sildenafil (PDE5 inhibitor) → **severe hypotension** — CONTRAINDICATED

**Variant (Prinzmetal) Angina**: Nitrates + CCBs are drugs of choice; beta-blockers CONTRAINDICATED (can worsen coronary spasm)

### Antiplatelets

#### Aspirin
- **Mechanism**: Irreversible acetylation of COX-1 (and COX-2)
- **Effect**: ↓ TXA2 production (platelets) → ↓ platelet aggregation
- **Platelet effect lasts 7-10 days** (platelet lifespan — cannot make new COX)
- **Low dose (75-150 mg)**: antiplatelet — COX-1 in platelets (thromboxane↓)
- **Intermediate dose (650-4000 mg)**: analgesic, antipyretic
- **High dose (>4 g/day)**: anti-inflammatory (COX-2 effect on endothelial prostacyclin also decreased at high doses)
- **Reye's syndrome**: aspirin in children with viral illness → hepatic encephalopathy (avoid <12 years)
- **Aspirin toxicity (Salicylism)**: Tinnitus, dizziness, hyperventilation
- **Overdose**: Initially respiratory alkalosis → then metabolic acidosis (mixed)

#### Other Antiplatelets

| Drug | Mechanism | Uses |
|------|-----------|------|
| **Clopidogrel** | ADP receptor (P2Y12) irreversible block | ACS, PCI, stroke prevention |
| **Ticagrelor** | P2Y12 reversible block | ACS (preferred over clopidogrel post-ACS) |
| **Prasugrel** | P2Y12 irreversible (prodrug, more potent) | PCI |
| **Tirofiban, Eptifibatide** | GP IIb/IIIa inhibitor | Acute ACS/PCI |
| **Abciximab** | GP IIb/IIIa inhibitor (monoclonal Ab) | PCI |
| **Dipyridamole** | ↑ cAMP (PDE inhibition), ↑ adenosine | Combined with aspirin for stroke |
| **Cilostazol** | PDE3 inhibitor → ↑ cAMP | Peripheral arterial disease |

### Anticoagulants

#### Heparin vs Warfarin

| Feature | Heparin (UFH) | LMWH (Enoxaparin) | Warfarin |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Mechanism** | Activates antithrombin III → inhibits IIa, Xa, IXa | Activates AT-III → mainly inhibits Xa | Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase → ↓ factors II, VII, IX, X, Protein C & S |
| **Route** | IV/SC | SC | Oral |
| **Onset** | Immediate | 1-2 hrs | 3-5 days (warfarin lag — why?) |
| **Monitoring** | **aPTT** (1.5-2.5× control) | **Anti-Xa levels** (or none routinely) | **PT/INR** (target 2-3) |
| **Reversal** | **Protamine sulfate** (1 mg/100 U heparin) | Partial with protamine | **Vitamin K** (slow, 12-24 hrs) or **Fresh Frozen Plasma** (immediate) |
| **Crosses placenta** | No (safe in pregnancy) | No | **Yes — teratogenic (avoid 1st trimester, near term)** |
| **HIT** | Yes (more common) | Less common | No |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Warfarin initially PROCOAGULANT — Protein C (t1/2 = 8 hrs) depleted before clotting factors → paradoxical thrombosis → overlap with heparin for 4-5 days

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT) — IgG antibody against heparin-PF4 complex → thrombosis + thrombocytopenia → treat with **argatroban** or **bivalirudin** (NOT warfarin initially)

#### Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)

| Drug | Target | Reversal Agent |
|------|--------|---------------|
| **Dabigatran** | Direct thrombin (IIa) inhibitor | **Idarucizumab** (Praxbind) |
| **Rivaroxaban** | Factor Xa inhibitor | **Andexanet alfa** |
| **Apixaban** | Factor Xa inhibitor | **Andexanet alfa** |
| **Edoxaban** | Factor Xa inhibitor | Andexanet alfa |

**DOACs advantages**: Fixed dosing, no routine monitoring, fewer drug interactions, fewer food interactions

## 4. CNS PHARMACOLOGY

### Anxiolytics/Hypnotics

#### Benzodiazepines (BZDs)
**Mechanism**: Positive allosteric modulators of **GABA-A receptor** → ↑ frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening (barbiturates ↑ duration)

**Key BZDs by use**:
- **Anxiolytic**: Diazepam, Lorazepam, Clonazepam
- **Hypnotic**: Triazolam (shortest), Temazepam, Flurazepam (longest → hangover)
- **Anticonvulsant**: Clonazepam, Diazepam (status epilepticus), Clobazam
- **Muscle relaxant**: Diazepam
- **Pre-anesthetic**: Midazolam (amnesic, short-acting)
- **Alcohol withdrawal**: Chlordiazepoxide, Diazepam

**Flumazenil**: Competitive BZD antagonist at GABA-A receptor; short t1/2 (1 hr) → resedation possible; may precipitate seizures in BZD-dependent patients

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: BZDs — ↑ frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening; Barbiturates — ↑ duration; Alcohol — both + glycine/NMDA effects

**BZD SE**: Sedation, amnesia (anterograde), respiratory depression (especially with opioids/alcohol), dependence
**BZD OD**: Rarely fatal alone; treat with flumazenil
**Paradoxical excitement**: Especially in elderly

### Antidepressants

#### SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)
**Drugs**: Fluoxetine, Paroxetine, Sertraline, Citalopram, Escitalopram, Fluvoxamine

**Mechanism**: Block 5-HT (serotonin) reuptake transporter → ↑ synaptic 5-HT

**Key features**:
- **First-line** for depression, anxiety disorders, OCD (fluvoxamine/fluoxetine), PTSD, bulimia (fluoxetine)
- **Fluoxetine**: Longest t1/2 (days-weeks due to active metabolite norfluoxetine) — good if compliance issue, less withdrawal
- **Paroxetine**: Most anticholinergic SSRI; shortest t1/2 → worst discontinuation syndrome; inhibits CYP2D6
- **Sertraline**: Safest in pregnancy and in elderly

**SE**: GI (nausea, diarrhea), sexual dysfunction (most common reason for discontinuation), insomnia, SIADH (hyponatremia), weight gain (except fluoxetine — weight neutral)

**Serotonin Syndrome**: Serotonin excess — **TRIAD: Hyperthermia + Neuromuscular abnormalities (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor) + Mental status changes**
- Triggers: SSRI + MAOI, SSRI + tramadol/linezolid/triptan
- Treatment: Cyproheptadine (5-HT antagonist), supportive care

#### SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors)
**Drugs**: Venlafaxine, Duloxetine, Desvenlafaxine, Milnacipran

- **Duloxetine**: Diabetic neuropathy pain, stress urinary incontinence, fibromyalgia, depression
- **Venlafaxine**: Anxiety disorders, depression; increases BP at high doses

#### TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)
**Drugs**: Amitriptyline, Imipramine, Clomipramine, Nortriptyline, Desipramine

**Mechanism**: Block 5-HT and NE reuptake + histamine/muscarinic/alpha-1 blockade

**Uses**:
- Depression (second-line)
- **Imipramine**: Childhood enuresis (nocturnal bedwetting)
- **Clomipramine**: OCD (most serotonergic TCA)
- **Amitriptyline**: Neuropathic pain, migraine prophylaxis, fibromyalgia
- **Nortriptyline**: Least sedating, best tolerated

**TCA Toxicity (3 Cs — Convulsions, Coma, Cardiac arrhythmias)**:
- **Anticholinergic**: Dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, blurred vision, tachycardia
- **Antihistamine**: Sedation, weight gain
- **Alpha-1 blockade**: Orthostatic hypotension
- **Cardiac**: QT prolongation, wide QRS (Na channel block), ventricular arrhythmias
- **TCA OD**: Most common cause of death = ventricular arrhythmia; Treat with **sodium bicarbonate** (↑ pH → reduces Na channel block)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: TCA overdose → ECG shows wide QRS → treat with **IV sodium bicarbonate**

#### MAOIs (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors)
**Drugs**: Phenelzine, Tranylcypromine, Isocarboxazid (irreversible); Moclobemide (reversible — RIMA)

**Mechanism**: Inhibit MAO-A (5-HT, NE) and MAO-B (dopamine, tyramine)

**Hypertensive Crisis (Cheese Reaction)**:
- Tyramine in food (aged cheese, red wine, cured meats) normally metabolized by MAO-A in gut
- MAOI blocks this → tyramine absorbed → massive sympathomimetic release → **hypertensive crisis**
- Treatment: **Phentolamine** (α-blocker)

**Serotonin Syndrome**: MAOI + SSRI/TCA/meperidine (pethidine) → AVOID

**Moclobemide**: Reversible, selective MAO-A inhibitor — less cheese reaction risk; used in depression and social anxiety

**Selegiline**: Selective MAO-B inhibitor — used in Parkinson's disease; at low doses no cheese reaction

### Antipsychotics

#### Typical (First Generation) Antipsychotics
**Mechanism**: D2 receptor blockade (mainly)

| Potency | Drugs | SE Profile |
|---------|-------|-----------|
| **High potency** | Haloperidol, Fluphenazine, Trifluoperazine, Thiothixene | More EPS, less sedation/anticholinergic |
| **Medium potency** | Perphenazine | Intermediate |
| **Low potency** | Chlorpromazine, Thioridazine, Mesoridazine | Less EPS, more sedation/anticholinergic/hypotension |

**Extra-Pyramidal Side Effects (EPS)**:

| EPS | Timing | Treatment |
|-----|--------|----------|
| **Akathisia** | Weeks | Beta-blockers (propranolol), BZDs |
| **Acute dystonia** | Hours-days | Benztropine, diphenhydramine (IM) |
| **Parkinsonism (drug-induced)** | Weeks-months | Reduce dose, benztropine |
| **Tardive dyskinesia** | Months-years | Irreversible; reduce/stop drug; clonazepam, tetrabenazine; switch to clozapine |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Tardive dyskinesia — most common with high-potency typical antipsychotics; involuntary orofacial movements; potentially irreversible

**Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)**:
- **TRIAD**: Hyperthermia + Rigidity (lead-pipe) + Altered consciousness + Autonomic instability (tachycardia, sweating, BP lability)
- **Lab**: ↑↑ CPK (creatine kinase), leukocytosis, myoglobinuria
- **Cause**: D2 blockade in hypothalamus (thermoregulation) and striatum
- **Treatment**: Stop drug, supportive; **Dantrolene** (muscle relaxant), **Bromocriptine/Amantadine** (dopamine agonist)

**Chlorpromazine specific SE**: Photosensitivity, cholestatic jaundice, corneal/lens deposits
**Thioridazine**: Retinal pigmentation (retinitis pigmentosa-like), **QT prolongation** — most cardiotoxic typical

#### Atypical (Second Generation) Antipsychotics
**Mechanism**: D2 block + 5-HT2A block (and others)

| Drug | Key Features/SE |
|------|----------------|
| **Clozapine** | Most effective (treatment-resistant schizophrenia); **Agranulocytosis** (1-2%) — weekly WBC monitoring for 6 months, then biweekly; also seizures, myocarditis, hypersalivation, metabolic syndrome; NO EPS |
| **Olanzapine** | Most metabolic SE (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia) |
| **Risperidone** | Most EPS among atypicals; hyperprolactinemia |
| **Quetiapine** | Most sedating; used in bipolar depression; least EPS |
| **Aripiprazole** | Partial D2 agonist; weight neutral; least metabolic SE |
| **Ziprasidone** | QT prolongation; weight neutral |
| **Amisulpride** | D2/D3 selective; prolactinemia |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Clozapine — only drug effective in treatment-resistant schizophrenia AND reduces suicidality; but requires WBC monitoring due to agranulocytosis

**Hyperprolactinemia from antipsychotics** (D2 block in tuberoinfundibular pathway): Galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia, sexual dysfunction
- Most: risperidone, haloperidol
- Least: clozapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole (partial agonist actually ↓ prolactin)

### Antiepileptics

#### Drug of Choice by Seizure Type

| Seizure Type | First Choice | Alternatives |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Generalized tonic-clonic (GTC)** | Valproate (adults), Levetiracetam | Phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine |
| **Absence (petit mal)** | **Ethosuximide** (if pure absence), **Valproate** (if mixed) | Lamotrigine |
| **Myoclonic** | **Valproate**, Levetiracetam | Clonazepam |
| **Focal/Partial seizures** | Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine | Oxcarbazepine, levetiracetam, valproate |
| **Status epilepticus** | **IV Lorazepam** → if fails: **IV Phenytoin/fosphenytoin** → if fails: **IV Phenobarbitone** → RSI | — |
| **Febrile seizures** | Diazepam (rectal/IV, acute) | — |
| **Infantile spasms (West syndrome)** | **ACTH** / Vigabatrin (if tuberous sclerosis) | — |
| **Lennox-Gastaut** | Valproate, Rufinamide, Clobazam | — |
| **Trigeminal neuralgia** | **Carbamazepine** | Oxcarbazepine, gabapentin |

#### Sodium Valproate (Valproic Acid)
**Mechanism**: Multiple — ↑ GABA (↓ GABA transaminase), Na channel block, Ca channel block (T-type)
**Broad spectrum**: Effective for all seizure types
**SE**:
- **Teratogenicity** (Category D): **Neural tube defects** (spina bifida — 1-2%), craniofacial abnormalities
- Supplement **folic acid** if must use in pregnancy
- **Hepatotoxicity** (especially children <2 years, mitochondrial diseases)
- Weight gain, hair loss (alopecia), tremor
- **Pancreatitis** (rare but serious)
- **Drug interactions**: Inhibits metabolism of lamotrigine (↑ lamotrigine levels → toxic rash)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Valproate in pregnancy → NTDs → give folic acid (5 mg/day). Most teratogenic AED.

#### Carbamazepine
**Mechanism**: Na channel blockade (use-dependent — blocks rapidly firing neurons)
**Uses**: Partial + GTC seizures, **trigeminal neuralgia** (DOC), bipolar disorder (mood stabilizer), diabetic neuropathy
**SE**:
- **Diplopia, ataxia** (dose-related)
- **SIADH** (hyponatremia) — unique among AEDs
- **Aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis** (rare, idiosyncratic)
- **Stevens-Johnson syndrome** (HLA-B*1502 — Asian patients — screen before starting)
- **Teratogenicity**: Neural tube defects, craniofacial
- Enzyme inducer → reduces efficacy of OCPs, warfarin, other AEDs
- Autoinduction (induces own metabolism)

#### Phenytoin
**Mechanism**: Na channel blockade (stabilizes inactivated state)
**Zero-order kinetics** at therapeutic doses → small dose change → large plasma level change
**SE**:
- **Gingival hyperplasia** (most distinctive chronic SE)
- **Hirsutism**
- **Nystagmus, diplopia, ataxia** (dose-related toxicity)
- **Folate deficiency → megaloblastic anemia**
- **Peripheral neuropathy** (chronic)
- **Coarsening of facial features**
- Teratogenic: **Fetal hydantoin syndrome** (hypoplastic nails, IUGR, cleft lip/palate)
- Enzyme inducer
- **Purple glove syndrome** (IV extravasation)

#### Opioids

**Morphine**:
- **Mechanism**: μ (mu) opioid receptor agonist — Gi-coupled → ↓ cAMP, ↑ K⁺ conductance, ↓ Ca²⁺ conductance → hyperpolarization
- **Receptors**: μ (analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, constipation, miosis), κ (analgesia, dysphoria, sedation, miosis), δ (analgesia, mood modulation)

**Effects of Morphine**:
- **Analgesia** (most effective for severe pain)
- **Euphoria/Sedation**
- **Respiratory depression** (most dangerous effect — dose-limiting)
- **Miosis** (pinpoint pupils — pathognomonic of opioid toxicity; EXCEPT meperidine → pupil dilation due to anticholinergic effect)
- **Constipation** (does NOT develop tolerance)
- **Nausea/vomiting** (CTZ stimulation)
- **Urinary retention**
- **Bradycardia, hypotension**
- **Cough suppression** (codeine, dextromethorphan)

**Cholinergic excess (SLUDGE)** vs **Opioid effects**:
- SLUDGE = Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI cramps, Emesis (organophosphate/muscarinic excess)
- Opioid = miosis, respiratory depression, constipation, CNS depression

**Opioid Tolerance and Dependence**:
- **Tolerance develops to**: Analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, N/V, sedation
- **Tolerance does NOT develop to**: Constipation, miosis

**Naloxone**: Competitive opioid antagonist (all receptor types, μ > κ > δ), IV/IM/intranasal
- Short t1/2 (1-2 hrs) → may need repeat dosing for long-acting opioids
- **Naltrexone**: Oral, longer-acting; used in alcohol dependence + opioid dependence

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Morphine → constipation (does not develop tolerance) and miosis are persistent effects; respiratory depression has tolerance

**Meperidine (Pethidine)**: Opioid + anticholinergic → mydriasis (not miosis); normeperidine metabolite → seizures (avoid in renal failure)

## 5. ANTIMICROBIAL PHARMACOLOGY

### Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitors

#### Penicillins
**Mechanism**: Beta-lactam ring inhibits **transpeptidase** (PBP - penicillin binding protein) → prevents cross-linking of peptidoglycan → cell lysis

**Classification**:

| Class | Drugs | Coverage |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Natural | Penicillin G (IV), Penicillin V (oral) | Gram+, some Gram- (Neisseria), anaerobes above diaphragm |
| Antistaphylococcal | **Oxacillin, Nafcillin, Cloxacillin, Dicloxacillin** | MSSA (NOT MRSA), beta-lactamase stable |
| Aminopenicillins | **Ampicillin, Amoxicillin** | Extended Gram- (Haemophilus, E. coli, Proteus, Salmonella) + Gram+ |
| Extended spectrum | **Piperacillin, Ticarcillin** | Pseudomonas + above |
| Beta-lactam/inhibitor combos | Amoxicillin-clavulanate, Piperacillin-tazobactam | Beta-lactamase producing organisms |

**Allergy**: IgE-mediated (Type I) to beta-lactam ring
- Cross-reactivity: Penicillin ↔ Cephalosporin (~1-2%), Penicillin ↔ Carbapenem (<1%)
- **True penicillin allergy**: Use aztreonam (monobactam — no cross reactivity) or vancomycin

#### Cephalosporins — Generation Coverage

| Generation | Drugs | Coverage Highlights |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| **1st** | Cefazolin (IV), Cephalexin (oral) | Gram+ (skin flora), MSSA, limited Gram- |
| **2nd** | Cefuroxime, Cefaclor, Cefoxitin (cephamycin) | Extended Gram-; Cefoxitin: B. fragilis |
| **3rd** | Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Ceftazidime | ↑ Gram- (meningitis - CSF penetration); Ceftazidime: Pseudomonas |
| **4th** | Cefepime | Broad — Gram+/-, Pseudomonas |
| **5th** | Ceftaroline, Ceftolozane-tazobactam | **MRSA** coverage (ceftaroline) |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Ceftriaxone — DOC for meningococcal meningitis, gonorrhea (single dose), community-acquired pneumonia; causes biliary sludge/pseudolithiasis

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Ceftazidime — only 3rd gen cephalosporin with Pseudomonas coverage

#### Carbapenems
**Drugs**: Imipenem (+ cilastatin — prevents renal inactivation), Meropenem, Doripenem, Ertapenem

**Coverage**: Extremely broad — **ESKAPE** organisms, MRSA (NO), Stenotrophomonas (NO)
- **Imipenem**: Can cause seizures (avoid in meningitis → use Meropenem)
- **Ertapenem**: No Pseudomonas coverage

**Mechanisms of resistance**: Carbapenemase (KPC, NDM-1 — New Delhi Metallo-beta-lactamase), porin loss

#### Vancomycin
**Mechanism**: Glycopeptide — binds **D-Ala-D-Ala** terminus of peptidoglycan precursors → inhibits transglycosylation → cell wall synthesis blocked

**Uses**: MRSA, C. difficile (oral — not absorbed), serious Gram+ infections in penicillin allergy
**Monitoring**: **Trough levels** (AUC:MIC preferred now); check renal function

**SE**:
- **Red Man Syndrome**: Flushing, erythema of face/neck/trunk during rapid infusion — due to histamine release (NOT allergy) — slow infusion rate, pretreat with antihistamine
- **Nephrotoxicity** (especially with aminoglycosides)
- **Ototoxicity** (especially with loop diuretics)

**VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococci)**: Resistance due to modification of D-Ala-D-Ala to D-Ala-D-Lac
- Treatment: **Linezolid, Daptomycin, Tigecycline**

### Protein Synthesis Inhibitors

#### Aminoglycosides (30S ribosome — A site binding → misreading)
**Drugs**: Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Amikacin, Streptomycin, Neomycin

**Coverage**: Aerobic Gram-negative bacilli, synergistic with beta-lactams for Gram+

**Key SE**:
- **Nephrotoxicity**: Proximal tubule damage — tubular necrosis (reversible if caught early)
- **Ototoxicity**: Vestibular (streptomycin, gentamicin) and cochlear (amikacin, neomycin) — **may be irreversible**
- **Neuromuscular blockade**: Curare-like effect — avoid in myasthenia gravis
- **Teratogenicity**: Congenital deafness (avoid in pregnancy)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Once-daily aminoglycoside dosing — reduces nephrotoxicity (concentration-dependent killing, post-antibiotic effect allows drug-free interval for tubular recovery)

#### Tetracyclines (30S ribosome — A site blockade, reversible)
**Drugs**: Tetracycline, Doxycycline, Minocycline, Tigecycline (glycylcycline — IV, broader spectrum)

**Spectrum**: Broad — Gram+/-, Rickettsia, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Borrelia, Brucella

**Contraindications**:
- **Children <8 years**: Dental staining (yellow-brown), **enamel hypoplasia** (permanent)
- **Pregnancy**: Fetal bone/tooth discoloration, maternal hepatotoxicity

**SE**: GI (take with food but NOT dairy/antacids — chelation), photosensitivity, esophageal ulceration (take upright, with water), **Fanconi syndrome** (degraded tetracycline — obsolete formulations), pseudotumor cerebri (↑ ICP), vestibular (minocycline)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Doxycycline — DOC for Rickettsia, Chlamydia, Lyme disease (early), community-acquired pneumonia; can be taken with food (less GI SE than tetracycline); renal-safe (fecal excretion)

#### Macrolides (50S ribosome — peptide translocation inhibition)
**Drugs**: Erythromycin, Azithromycin (Z-pack), Clarithromycin, Roxithromycin

**Spectrum**: Atypical organisms (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella), Gram+, H. pylori (clarithromycin)

| Drug | Key Features |
|------|-------------|
| **Erythromycin** | Motilin receptor agonist (gastric motility) — used for gastroparesis; CYP3A4 inhibitor |
| **Azithromycin** | Longest t1/2 (68 hrs), tissue concentration >> plasma, OD dosing; less GI SE; NTM, MAC prophylaxis in HIV |
| **Clarithromycin** | H. pylori (triple therapy), strongest CYP3A4 inhibitor |

**SE**: GI (most common), **QT prolongation** (azithromycin — cardiac deaths controversy), cholestatic hepatitis (erythromycin estolate — avoid in pregnancy), **ototoxicity** (high-dose erythromycin)

#### Chloramphenicol (50S ribosome — peptidyl transferase inhibition)
**SE**:
- **Aplastic anemia** (idiosyncratic, rare, irreversible — most feared)
- **Reversible bone marrow suppression** (dose-related)
- **Gray Baby Syndrome**: Neonates lack glucuronosyltransferase → chloramphenicol accumulates → cardiovascular collapse, ashen-gray color, abdominal distension, vomiting
- **Optic neuritis** (rare)

**Uses**: Meningitis (penicillin/chloramphenicol historically), typhoid (alternative to fluoroquinolones), rickettsial disease in pregnancy (cannot use doxycycline)

#### Clindamycin (50S ribosome — same site as chloramphenicol)
**Spectrum**: Gram+, anaerobes (especially B. fragilis); NO Gram-

**Uses**: MRSA (skin/soft tissue — community acquired), aspiration pneumonia (anaerobes), pelvic inflammatory disease (BV), PCP (with primaquine), toxoplasmosis (with pyrimethamine), babesiosis

**SE**: **Pseudomembranous colitis** (C. difficile-associated diarrhea — CDAD) — most commonly associated with clindamycin (also ampicillin, cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones)

#### Linezolid (50S ribosome — 23S rRNA, prevents 70S initiation complex formation)
**Coverage**: Gram+ including **MRSA, VRE**
**SE**:
- **Serotonin syndrome** (MAO-A inhibitor) — avoid with SSRIs/SNRIs/MAOIs
- **Myelosuppression**: Thrombocytopenia (most common), anemia — monitor CBC weekly
- **Optic neuropathy** (prolonged use)
- **Lactic acidosis** (mitochondrial toxicity — prolonged use)
- **Peripheral neuropathy**

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Linezolid — MAO inhibitor → serotonin syndrome when combined with serotonergic agents; thrombocytopenia with prolonged use

### DNA Synthesis Inhibitors

#### Fluoroquinolones (DNA Gyrase/Topoisomerase IV Inhibitors)
**Mechanism**: Inhibit **DNA gyrase** (Gram-) and **topoisomerase IV** (Gram+) → DNA strand breaks

| Class | Drugs | Spectrum |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1st | Nalidixic acid | Gram- (UTI only) |
| 2nd | Ciprofloxacin, Norfloxacin, Ofloxacin | Gram-, Pseudomonas (ciprofloxacin) |
| 3rd | Levofloxacin | + Atypicals, better Gram+ |
| 4th | Moxifloxacin, Gemifloxacin | + Anaerobes, best Gram+, **NO Pseudomonas** |

**SE**:
- **Cartilage damage** → avoid in children <18, pregnancy (arthropathy in animal studies)
- **Tendinitis/Tendon rupture** (esp. Achilles, elderly, corticosteroids) — **BLACK BOX WARNING**
- **QT prolongation** (moxifloxacin > levofloxacin)
- **CNS**: Headache, dizziness, seizures (lower seizure threshold)
- **Phototoxicity**
- **Drug interactions**: Chelation with Mg²⁺/Al³⁺ antacids, Ca²⁺, Fe²⁺, Zn²⁺ (give 2 hours apart)
- **Peripheral neuropathy, aortic aneurysm/dissection**

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Ciprofloxacin — most active FQ against Pseudomonas; moxifloxacin — anaerobic coverage, NO Pseudomonas, DOC for MDR TB (group A)

#### Metronidazole
**Mechanism**: Prodrug activated by ferredoxin in anaerobic organisms → reactive intermediates → DNA strand breaks

**Spectrum**: **Anaerobes** (B. fragilis, Clostridium), Protozoa (E. histolytica, Giardia, Trichomonas)

**Uses**: BV (bacterial vaginosis — DOC), C. difficile (oral, mild-moderate), trichomoniasis, amebiasis, giardiasis, H. pylori (part of triple therapy), anaerobic coverage

**SE**:
- **Disulfiram-like reaction** with alcohol (acetaldehyde accumulation → flushing, nausea, vomiting) — **AVOID alcohol during and 48 hrs after**
- **Metallic taste**
- **Peripheral neuropathy** (prolonged use)
- **Seizures** (high doses)
- **Dark/reddish urine**

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Metronidazole + alcohol = disulfiram-like reaction (same as disulfiram/antabuse used in alcohol deaddiction)

#### Rifampicin (Rifampin)
**Mechanism**: Inhibits **bacterial DNA-dependent RNA polymerase** (beta-subunit) → ↓ mRNA synthesis

**Coverage**: Mycobacteria (TB, leprosy), Gram+ (MRSA add-on), Neisseria (prophylaxis)

**SE**:
- **Enzyme inducer** (major — CYP450): Reduces effectiveness of OCPs, warfarin, azoles, cyclosporine, HIV drugs
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Transaminase elevation (common), rarely fulminant
- **Reddish-orange discoloration** of all body fluids (urine, tears, sweat, saliva) — inform patients, stains contact lenses
- **Flu-like syndrome** (intermittent dosing)
- Thrombocytopenia, hemolytic anemia (intermittent dosing)

### Antifungals

#### Amphotericin B (AmB)
**Mechanism**: Binds **ergosterol** in fungal membrane → forms pores → cell death

**Spectrum**: Broad (most fungi) — Candida, Cryptococcus, Aspergillus, Mucor, Histoplasma, Coccidioides

**SE**:
- **Nephrotoxicity** (dose-limiting) — distal renal tubular acidosis, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia
- **Infusion reactions**: Fever, chills, rigors ("shake and bake") — premedicate with acetaminophen, diphenhydramine, hydrocortisone, meperidine
- **Anemia** (↓ erythropoietin)
- **Hypokalemia, Hypomagnesemia**

**Lipid formulations** (AmBisome, ABLC, ABCD): Reduced nephrotoxicity, higher doses possible, expensive; preferred in renal impairment

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Amphotericin B — "gold standard" for serious fungal infections; liposomal formulation reduces nephrotoxicity; ergosterol binding (vs cholesterol in humans — selective toxicity)

#### Azoles (Ergosterol Synthesis Inhibitors)
**Mechanism**: Inhibit **lanosterol 14-alpha-demethylase** (CYP51) → ↓ ergosterol synthesis → membrane disruption

| Drug | Route | Spectrum | Key Notes |
|------|-------|---------|-----------|
| **Fluconazole** | Oral/IV | Candida (not C. krusei/glabrata), Cryptococcus | Drug of choice: cryptococcal meningitis (maintenance), vaginal candidiasis; CYP2C9 inhibitor |
| **Itraconazole** | Oral | Histoplasma, Blastomyces, Aspergillus, dermatophytes | Negative inotrope (avoid in heart failure), poor oral bioavailability (needs acid) |
| **Voriconazole** | Oral/IV | Aspergillus (DOC), Candida, Fusarium | Visual disturbances (photopsia), hepatotoxicity, QT prolongation, photosensitivity, periostitis |
| **Posaconazole** | Oral/IV | Mucor (prophylaxis), Aspergillus, broad spectrum | Prophylaxis in HSCT/AML |
| **Isavuconazole** | Oral/IV | Aspergillus, Mucor | Less drug interactions |
| **Clotrimazole/Miconazole** | Topical | Dermatophytes, Candida | OTC antifungals |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Voriconazole — DOC for invasive aspergillosis; unique SE = visual disturbances (photopsia — colored halos)

**Azole drug interactions**: All inhibit CYP3A4 → ↑ statins (myopathy), ↑ warfarin (bleeding), ↑ cyclosporine/tacrolimus (nephrotoxicity), ↑ benzodiazepines

#### Echinocandins (Glucan Synthesis Inhibitors)
**Mechanism**: Inhibit **beta-1,3-glucan synthase** → disrupt fungal cell wall synthesis (no mammalian equivalent → low toxicity)

**Drugs**: Caspofungin, Micafungin, Anidulafungin

**Coverage**: Candida (including azole-resistant) and Aspergillus
- **NOT Cryptococcus** (no glucan in wall)

**Indications**: Invasive candidiasis (DOC including candidemia), empiric therapy in febrile neutropenia, salvage Aspergillus

**SE**: Generally well tolerated; ↑ liver enzymes, infusion reactions (histamine)

### Antivirals

#### Acyclovir (and related antiherpes drugs)
**Mechanism**:
1. Herpes TK (thymidine kinase) phosphorylates acyclovir → acyclovir monophosphate
2. Cellular kinases → acyclovir triphosphate
3. Inhibits viral DNA polymerase; also causes chain termination
4. **Requires viral TK** → selectivity (inactive in uninfected cells)

**Resistance**: Mutation in TK gene (most common) → use **foscarnet** or **cidofovir**

| Drug | Virus | Key Notes |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Acyclovir** | HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV | IV for encephalitis/neonatal HSV; oral for genital herpes, shingles; topical for labialis |
| **Valacyclovir** | HSV, VZV | Prodrug of acyclovir — better oral bioavailability |
| **Famciclovir** | HSV, VZV | Prodrug of penciclovir |
| **Ganciclovir** | **CMV** | Also activated by UL97 kinase (CMV); SE: **myelosuppression** (leukopenia, thrombocytopenia) |
| **Valganciclovir** | CMV | Oral prodrug of ganciclovir; myelosuppression |
| **Foscarnet** | HSV, CMV (acyclovir-resistant), HBV | Pyrophosphate analog; SE: **nephrotoxicity, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia** |
| **Cidofovir** | CMV, acyclovir-resistant HSV | SE: **nephrotoxicity** (give probenecid + IV fluids) |

#### Antiretrovirals (HIV)

| Class | Drugs | Mechanism | Key SE |
|-------|-------|-----------|--------|
| **NRTIs** | Zidovudine (AZT), Lamivudine (3TC), Tenofovir, Emtricitabine, Abacavir, Stavudine | Nucleoside/nucleotide RT inhibitors — chain termination | **Mitochondrial toxicity** (AZT: anemia/neuropathy; Stavudine: lipoatrophy/pancreatitis/neuropathy; Tenofovir: nephrotoxicity/bone density↓); Abacavir: **hypersensitivity (HLA-B*5701 screen before use)** |
| **NNRTIs** | Efavirenz, Nevirapine, Rilpivirine, Doravirine | Non-nucleoside RT inhibitors — allosteric binding | **Efavirenz: CNS SE** (vivid dreams, dizziness, hallucinations) — take at night; teratogenic; Nevirapine: hepatotoxicity, Stevens-Johnson; Rash (all) |
| **PIs** | Lopinavir, Atazanavir, Darunavir, Ritonavir | HIV protease inhibitors → immature non-infectious virions | **Metabolic syndrome** (lipodystrophy, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia), GI, nephrolithiasis (indinavir, atazanavir), **CYP3A4 inhibitors** (ritonavir boosting) |
| **Integrase inhibitors (INSTIs)** | Raltegravir, Elvitegravir, Dolutegravir, Bictegravir | HIV integrase inhibitors → no proviral DNA integration | Generally well tolerated; weight gain (dolutegravir); neural tube defects concern (dolutegravir — periconceptional) |
| **Entry inhibitors** | Enfuvirtide (T-20), Maraviroc | Enfuvirtide: gp41 fusion inhibitor; Maraviroc: CCR5 antagonist | Injection site reactions (enfuvirtide) |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Ritonavir — pharmacokinetic booster (CYP3A4 inhibitor) used with other PIs; dolutegravir — preferred first-line INSTI; abacavir — HLA-B*5701 testing mandatory before use

**Current preferred regimen (WHO)**: Tenofovir + Lamivudine + Dolutegravir (TLD)

#### Oseltamivir (Tamiflu)
**Mechanism**: Inhibits **neuraminidase** → prevents release of new virions from infected cells, prevents spread

**Indications**: Influenza A and B treatment (within 48 hrs of symptom onset), prophylaxis
**Resistance**: H275Y mutation in H1N1 (neuraminidase mutation)
**Alternative**: **Zanamivir** (inhaled), Baloxavir (cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor)

#### Hepatitis Drugs

| Hepatitis | Drug | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| **Hepatitis B** | Tenofovir (TDF/TAF), Entecavir, Lamivudine (resistance issues), Adefovir; **Pegylated IFN-α** | Tenofovir/Entecavir are DOC; Lamivudine — high resistance rate |
| **Hepatitis C** | **Direct-Acting Antivirals (DAAs)**: Sofosbuvir + Ledipasvir/Daclatasvir/Velpatasvir; Glecaprevir + Pibrentasvir | **Cure rates >95%**; Sofosbuvir: NS5B polymerase inhibitor; Daclatasvir: NS5A inhibitor; Simeprevir/Paritaprevir: NS3/4A protease inhibitors |
| **Hepatitis D** | **Pegylated IFN-α** (only effective treatment; bulevirtide — entry inhibitor — approved) | Co-infection requires HBsAg |
| **Hepatitis A/E** | Supportive (no antivirals) | Prevention: Vaccination (HAV) |

### Antitubercular Drugs

#### First-Line Drugs — HRZE (Mnemonic: **H**appy **R**iley **Z**ig **E**very)

| Drug | Mechanism | Key Side Effects |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Isoniazid (H)** | Inhibits InhA (enoyl-ACP reductase) → ↓ mycolic acid synthesis | **Peripheral neuropathy** (pyridoxine/B6 deficiency — supplement B6), **Hepatotoxicity** (most common serious SE), SLE-like syndrome, seizures, psychosis. **DOC for latent TB** |
| **Rifampicin (R)** | Inhibits RNA polymerase | **Enzyme inducer** (↑ OCP/warfarin/azole metabolism), **hepatotoxicity**, **reddish-orange discoloration** of body fluids, flu-like syndrome (intermittent), thrombocytopenia |
| **Pyrazinamide (Z)** | Converted to pyrazinoic acid → ↓ mycobacterial membrane energy | **Hyperuricemia** (gout — most common unique SE), hepatotoxicity, arthralgias (non-gouty), photosensitivity |
| **Ethambutol (E)** | Inhibits arabinosyl transferase → ↓ arabinogalactan synthesis | **Retrobulbar optic neuritis** → red-green color blindness, ↓ visual acuity (dose-related, monitor vision monthly); avoid in children <5 (cannot test vision) |
| **Streptomycin (S)** | 30S ribosome inhibition | **Ototoxicity** (vestibular > cochlear), **nephrotoxicity**; avoid in pregnancy (congenital deafness) |

**TB Treatment Regimens**:
- **Pulmonary TB**: 2HRZE + 4HR (intensive 2 months + continuation 4 months)
- **TB Meningitis/Miliary/Bone**: 2HRZE + 10HR (continuation 10 months); add steroids for meningitis
- **Latent TB (LTBI)**: Isoniazid 6-9 months; or Rifampicin 4 months; or 3HR

**Drug Resistance**:
- **MDR-TB**: Resistant to H + R → use second-line drugs (fluoroquinolones, injectable amikacin/kanamycin, bedaquiline, linezolid)
- **XDR-TB**: MDR + resistant to fluoroquinolones + injectable second-line agents

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Ethambutol → optic neuritis (check vision monthly; avoid in children <5); Pyrazinamide → hyperuricemia; Isoniazid → peripheral neuropathy (treat with pyridoxine)

#### MDR-TB Second-Line Drugs (Group Classification)

| Group | Drugs |
|-------|-------|
| **A (always include)** | Levofloxacin/Moxifloxacin, **Bedaquiline**, Linezolid |
| **B (add when needed)** | Clofazimine, Cycloserine/Terizidone |
| **C** | Ethambutol, Delamanid, Pyrazinamide, Imipenem-cilastatin, Amikacin, Streptomycin |

### Antimalarials

#### Chloroquine
**Mechanism**: Accumulates in parasite food vacuole → inhibits heme polymerase → toxic heme accumulates → parasite death

**Coverage**: P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, sensitive P. falciparum (most P. falciparum now resistant)

**SE**:
- **Retinopathy** (deposits in RPE → bull's eye maculopathy, irreversible) — monitor ophthalmically
- **Hemolysis in G6PD deficiency**
- Pruritus (common in Africans)
- QT prolongation
- Headache, GI

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Chloroquine retinopathy — irreversible; baseline and annual eye exams needed with long-term use

#### Artemisinin Derivatives (Artemisinins)
**Drugs**: Artesunate (IV/rectal), Artemether (IM), Arteether, Artemisinin

**Mechanism**: Endoperoxide bridge reacts with heme/iron → free radicals → parasite death

**Coverage**: All Plasmodium species including multidrug-resistant P. falciparum

**ACT (Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy)**: Used to prevent resistance
- **Artemether-lumefantrine** (Coartem): First-line for uncomplicated P. falciparum
- **Artesunate-mefloquine**: Southeast Asia
- **Artesunate** (IV): DOC for severe/cerebral malaria

#### Primaquine
**Mechanism**: Unknown — may form reactive oxygen species; kills liver stages (hypnozoites) and gametocytes

**Uses**:
- **Radical cure of P. vivax and P. ovale** (eliminates hypnozoites → prevents relapse)
- **Gametocytocidal** for P. falciparum
- PCP treatment (with clindamycin)

**SE**:
- **Hemolytic anemia in G6PD deficiency** — TEST G6PD BEFORE GIVING
- Methemoglobinemia

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Primaquine — only drug that eliminates hypnozoites (dormant liver stages); MUST check G6PD before use

#### Other Antimalarials

| Drug | Mechanism | Use | Key SE |
|------|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Mefloquine** | Similar to chloroquine | P. falciparum (prevention/treatment) | **Neuropsychiatric SE** (hallucinations, seizures, nightmares), QT prolongation |
| **Quinine** | Heme polymerase inhibition | Severe malaria (IV), multidrug resistant | **Cinchonism** (tinnitus, dizziness, headache), hypoglycemia (stimulates insulin), QT prolongation, blackwater fever in G6PD |
| **Atovaquone-proguanil** (Malarone) | ↓ mitochondrial electron transport | P. falciparum prevention/treatment | GI, expensive |
| **Doxycycline** | Protein synthesis inhibition | Prophylaxis (also treats) | Photosensitivity, avoid in pregnancy/children |
| **Pyrimethamine** | DHFR inhibitor | Toxoplasmosis (with sulfadiazine), malaria (with sulfadoxine — Fansidar) | Folate deficiency → rescue with folinic acid |

## 6. ENDOCRINE PHARMACOLOGY

### Insulin

#### Types of Insulin

| Type | Onset | Peak | Duration | Examples |
|------|-------|------|---------|---------|
| **Rapid-acting analogs** | 15 min | 1-2 hrs | 3-5 hrs | **Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine** ("LAG" — Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine) |
| **Short-acting (Regular)** | 30-60 min | 2-4 hrs | 6-8 hrs | **Regular insulin** (only type given IV) |
| **Intermediate-acting** | 1-3 hrs | 6-10 hrs | 12-18 hrs | **NPH (Neutral Protamine Hagedorn)** |
| **Long-acting analogs** | 1-2 hrs | No peak (flat) | 20-24 hrs | **Glargine** (U-100, U-300); **Detemir** |
| **Ultra-long-acting** | — | No peak | >40 hrs | **Degludec** |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Regular insulin — only insulin for IV use; Glargine — cannot be mixed with other insulins (pH 4 formulation precipitates in neutral pH)

**Insulin Complications**:
- **Hypoglycemia**: Most common, most dangerous
- **Lipodystrophy**: Lipoatrophy (older insulins) or lipohypertrophy (repeated injections same site)
- **Somogyi effect**: Rebound hyperglycemia after nocturnal hypoglycemia → morning high glucose
- **Dawn phenomenon**: Early morning hyperglycemia due to GH/cortisol surge → insulin resistance (NO rebound hypoglycemia prior)

### Oral Hypoglycemics

| Class | Drug | Mechanism | Key SE | Contraindications |
|-------|------|-----------|--------|------------------|
| **Biguanides** | **Metformin** | **Activates AMPK** → ↓ hepatic gluconeogenesis; ↑ peripheral glucose utilization | **Lactic acidosis** (rare — withhold in renal impairment, contrast media, surgery), GI (nausea/diarrhea — start low), vitamin B12 deficiency | eGFR <30, liver failure, alcohol, contrast, surgery |
| **Sulfonylureas** | Glibenclamide, Glipizide, Glimepiride, Gliclazide | Close **ATP-sensitive K⁺ channels** on β-cells → depolarization → Ca²⁺ influx → insulin release | **Hypoglycemia** (prolonged — especially glibenclamide), weight gain | Renal/hepatic failure, pregnancy |
| **Meglitinides** | Repaglinide, Nateglinide | Same as SU (K channel) but shorter acting, meal-related | Hypoglycemia (less than SU), weight gain | Pregnancy |
| **Alpha-glucosidase inhibitors** | Acarbose, Miglitol | ↓ intestinal carbohydrate digestion → ↓ postprandial glucose | **Flatulence, diarrhea** (most common — fermentation), no systemic SE | GI diseases, pregnancy, renal failure |
| **TZDs (Thiazolidinediones)** | Pioglitazone, Rosiglitazone | **PPAR-γ agonist** → ↑ insulin sensitivity | **Weight gain, edema, heart failure, bone fractures** (reduced density); Rosiglitazone: ↑ MI risk (withdrawn many places); Pioglitazone: ↑ bladder cancer risk | Heart failure, liver disease, bladder cancer |
| **DPP-4 inhibitors** | Sitagliptin, Vildagliptin, Saxagliptin | Inhibit DPP-4 → ↑ GLP-1 activity → ↑ insulin, ↓ glucagon (glucose-dependent) | **Pancreatitis**, nasopharyngitis, weight neutral; saxagliptin: HF exacerbation | Pancreatitis history |
| **GLP-1 receptor agonists** | Liraglutide, Semaglutide, Exenatide, Dulaglutide | GLP-1 receptor activation → ↑ glucose-dependent insulin secretion, ↓ glucagon, ↓ gastric emptying, ↑ satiety | **Nausea/vomiting** (most common), pancreatitis, **thyroid C-cell tumors** (animal data — avoid in MEN2/medullary thyroid CA), weight loss | MEN2, medullary thyroid CA |
| **SGLT2 inhibitors** | Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, Canagliflozin | Block **SGLT2** in PCT → ↑ urinary glucose excretion | **UTI, genital mycotic infections**, **euglycemic DKA**, **Fournier's gangrene**, polyuria, dehydration; **Canagliflozin: ↑ amputations** | eGFR <30-45 |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 agonists have cardiovascular mortality benefit beyond glucose lowering (EMPA-REG, LEADER trials)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Metformin — does NOT cause hypoglycemia; preferred first-line OAD; causes lactic acidosis (rare, mostly in contraindicated situations); reduces cardiovascular mortality (UKPDS)

### Thyroid Drugs

#### Hypothyroidism
- **Levothyroxine (T4)**: Treatment of choice; converted peripherally to T3; once daily (morning, fasting)
- **Liothyronine (T3)**: Short-acting, used in myxedema coma

#### Hyperthyroidism
| Drug | Mechanism | Key SE |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Propylthiouracil (PTU)** | Inhibits thyroid peroxidase → ↓ T3/T4 synthesis; **also inhibits T4→T3 conversion (peripherally)** | **Agranulocytosis** (most serious), hepatotoxicity (preferred in 1st trimester, thyroid storm) |
| **Methimazole (MMI)** | Inhibits thyroid peroxidase | **Agranulocytosis**, aplasia cutis (if given in 1st trimester — avoid), teratogenic; preferred in 2nd/3rd trimester and outside pregnancy |
| **Radioactive Iodine (I-131)** | Destroys thyroid tissue | Hypothyroidism (most common outcome long-term), avoid in pregnancy |
| **Potassium Iodide (Lugol's)** | **Wolff-Chaikoff effect**: High iodide → transient ↓ thyroid hormone synthesis | Used preoperatively (decreases vascularity, firmness — Plummer's iodine) and after nuclear accidents |
| **Beta-blockers** | ↓ sympathetic symptoms; propranolol also ↓ T4→T3 conversion | Symptomatic control only |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Thyroid storm treatment: PTU (also blocks T4→T3), beta-blocker, iodine (given AFTER PTU), steroids, supportive

### Corticosteroids

#### Potency Rankings (Relative Anti-inflammatory Potency)

| Drug | Anti-inflammatory | Mineralocorticoid | Duration |
|------|------------------|------------------|---------|
| **Cortisol (hydrocortisone)** | 1 (reference) | 1 | Short |
| **Prednisolone** | 4 | 0.8 | Intermediate |
| **Methylprednisolone** | 5 | 0.5 | Intermediate |
| **Triamcinolone** | 5 | 0 | Intermediate |
| **Dexamethasone** | **25-30** | **~0** | Long |
| **Betamethasone** | 25-30 | 0 | Long |
| **Fludrocortisone** | 10 | **125** | — |

**Mnemonic for potency**: "**Don't (Dexamethasone) Be (Betamethasone) Trivially (Triamcinolone) Mentally (Methylprednisolone) Primed (Prednisolone) Hydrostatically (Hydrocortisone)**" (decreasing potency)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Dexamethasone — most anti-inflammatory, least mineralocorticoid (used when fluid retention undesirable — brain edema, tumor, dexamethasone suppression test); NO mineralocorticoid activity → no Na retention

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Fludrocortisone — primarily mineralocorticoid activity; used in Addison's disease (replacement), orthostatic hypotension

**SE of Corticosteroids** (chronic use):
- **Metabolic**: Hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia, weight gain (central obesity), hypertension
- **Musculoskeletal**: **Osteoporosis** (most concerning chronic SE), myopathy, avascular necrosis (femoral head)
- **GI**: Peptic ulcer, pancreatitis
- **Infections**: ↑ susceptibility (especially TB, fungi, opportunistic)
- **Skin**: Thinning, striae, easy bruising, impaired wound healing
- **Eye**: **Catposterior subcapsular cataract**, glaucoma
- **Adrenal suppression**: HPA axis → taper slowly when stopping
- **Growth retardation** in children
- **Psychiatric**: Psychosis, euphoria, depression

### OCPs (Oral Contraceptive Pills)

#### Combined OCPs (Estrogen + Progestin)
**Mechanism**:
1. **Inhibit LH surge → prevent ovulation** (primary)
2. Thicken cervical mucus (impairs sperm penetration)
3. Thin endometrium (implantation less likely)
4. Alter tubal motility

**Contraindications** (ACHES):
- **A** — Abdominal pain (thrombosis)
- **C** — Chest pain (PE, MI)
- **H** — Headache (severe — stroke, HTN)
- **E** — Eye changes (vision loss — stroke)
- **S** — Severe leg pain (DVT)

Absolute contraindications: Pregnancy, undiagnosed vaginal bleeding, history of stroke/VTE/MI, hypertension (>160/100), migraine with aura, active liver disease, estrogen-sensitive cancers, age >35 + smoking >15 cigarettes/day

**Benefits of OCPs**:
- ↓ Ovarian cancer (50% reduction with long-term use)
- ↓ Endometrial cancer
- ↓ Dysmenorrhea, menorrhagia
- ↓ Ectopic pregnancy risk
- ↓ PID severity
- ↓ Benign breast disease
- Improvement of acne (anti-androgenic progestins)

**Risks of OCPs**:
- ↑ VTE (DVT/PE) — estrogen effect
- ↑ Cervical cancer (slight)
- ↑ Liver adenoma (rare)
- ↑ MI/stroke risk (smoking + >35 years especially)
- ↓ Folate levels

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Rifampicin (enzyme inducer) reduces OCP efficacy → use additional contraception

### Alkylating Agents

| Drug | Key SE | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Cyclophosphamide** | **Hemorrhagic cystitis** (acrolein metabolite — prevent with MESNA and hydration), myelosuppression, alopecia, SIADH | DOC for SLE nephritis, RA, NHL, breast cancer |
| **Ifosfamide** | **Hemorrhagic cystitis** (worse than cyclophosphamide), encephalopathy, nephrotoxicity | Always give MESNA |

| **Chlorambucil** | Myelosuppression, infertility | CLL |
| **Carmustine/Lomustine (nitrosoureas)** | **Delayed myelosuppression** (6 weeks!), pulmonary fibrosis | CNS tumors (BBB penetration) |
| **Cisplatin** | **Nephrotoxicity** (most common dose-limiting, prevent with hydration + amifostine + mannitol), **ototoxicity** (high-frequency hearing loss), peripheral neuropathy, N/V, hypomagnesemia | Testicular, ovarian, lung, bladder cancers |
| **Carboplatin** | **Myelosuppression** (unlike cisplatin — less nephro/neuro/ototoxicity) | Better tolerated, same cancers |
| **Oxaliplatin** | **Peripheral neuropathy** (cold-induced, acute; chronic cumulative) | Colorectal cancer |

### Antimetabolites

| Drug | Target | Mechanism | Key SE |
|------|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Methotrexate (MTX)** | **DHFR** (dihydrofolate reductase) | ↓ tetrahydrofolate → ↓ thymidylate synthesis → ↓ DNA synthesis | Myelosuppression, **mucositis**, hepatotoxicity (cirrhosis with chronic use), **pulmonary toxicity** (pneumonitis), nephrotoxicity (high dose); **RESCUE: Leucovorin (folinic acid)** |
| **5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)** | Thymidylate synthase | ↓ dTMP → ↓ DNA synthesis | Myelosuppression, mucositis, **hand-foot syndrome** (palmar-plantar erythrodysesthesia), diarrhea, **cerebellar ataxia** |
| **Capecitabine** | Same as 5-FU (oral prodrug) | Same | More hand-foot syndrome |
| **6-Mercaptopurine (6-MP)** | Purine synthesis | ↓ purine synthesis | Myelosuppression, hepatotoxicity; **allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase → ↓ 6-MP metabolism → TOXIC INTERACTION (reduce 6-MP dose to 25%)** |
| **Cytarabine (Ara-C)** | DNA polymerase | Chain termination | **Cerebellar toxicity** (high dose), myelosuppression, **Ara-C syndrome** (fever, rash) |
| **Gemcitabine** | DNA polymerase | Chain termination | Myelosuppression, pulmonary toxicity, **hemolytic uremic syndrome** |
| **Hydroxyurea** | Ribonucleotide reductase | ↓ DNA synthesis | Myelosuppression; USED for sickle cell (↑ HbF) |
| **Pemetrexed** | Multiple folate enzymes | ↓ purine + thymidylate synthesis | Myelosuppression, mucositis; **supplement folic acid + B12** |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Leucovorin (folinic acid) rescues from MTX toxicity — given 24 hrs after MTX; 6-MP + allopurinol = toxicity (reduce 6-MP dose to 25%)

### Vinca Alkaloids and Taxanes

| Drug | Mechanism | Key SE |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Vincristine** | Binds tubulin → inhibit microtubule polymerization (mitotic spindle arrest) | **Peripheral neuropathy** (dose-limiting), minimal myelosuppression, constipation, SIADH, alopecia |
| **Vinblastine** | Same | **Myelosuppression** (dose-limiting, unlike vincristine), peripheral neuropathy less |
| **Vinorelbine** | Same | Myelosuppression, peripheral neuropathy |
| **Paclitaxel (Taxol)** | Stabilizes polymerized microtubules → prevents depolymerization | **Myelosuppression**, **peripheral neuropathy**, hypersensitivity (vehicle Cremophor EL), alopecia, bradycardia |
| **Docetaxel** | Same as paclitaxel | **Fluid retention** (edema, pleural effusion), myelosuppression, hypersensitivity |
| **Cabazitaxel** | Same | Myelosuppression; used in prostate cancer (docetaxel-resistant) |

### Topoisomerase Inhibitors

| Drug | Target | Key SE |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Doxorubicin (Adriamycin)** | Topoisomerase II + free radical generation | **Dilated cardiomyopathy** (cumulative dose-dependent, irreversible — limit total dose to 550 mg/m²; dexrazoxane (iron chelator) for cardioprotection), myelosuppression, alopecia, mucositis, **red urine** |
| **Epirubicin** | Same | Less cardiotoxicity than doxorubicin |
| **Idarubicin, Daunorubicin** | Same | Cardiotoxicity |
| **Etoposide (VP-16)** | Topoisomerase II | Myelosuppression, **secondary AML/leukemia**, alopecia, hypotension (rapid infusion) |
| **Irinotecan (CPT-11)** | Topoisomerase I | **Diarrhea** (early: cholinergic — treat with atropine; late: secretory — treat with loperamide), myelosuppression |
| **Topotecan** | Topoisomerase I | Myelosuppression |

### Other Important Anticancer Drugs

| Drug | Mechanism | Key SE |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Bleomycin** | DNA strand breaks (free radicals) | **Pulmonary fibrosis/toxicity** (most serious, cumulative dose), minimal myelosuppression, **hypersensitivity**, cutaneous effects (Raynaud's, nail changes) |
| **L-Asparaginase** | Depletes asparagine (ALL blast cells cannot synthesize) | **Pancreatitis**, coagulopathy (↓ clotting factors), allergic reactions/anaphylaxis, hepatotoxicity |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Bleomycin — pulmonary fibrosis (avoid in >70 years or pre-existing lung disease; avoid high O2 during surgery — worsens fibrosis); doxorubicin — dose-dependent cardiomyopathy; cisplatin — nephrotoxicity/ototoxicity
