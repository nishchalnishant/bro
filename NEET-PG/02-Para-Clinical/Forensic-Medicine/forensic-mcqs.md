## THANATOLOGY (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A body is found at a crime scene. On examination, the jaw and neck muscles are stiff but the lower limbs are still flaccid. The estimated time since death is most likely:**
- A) 0–2 hours
- B) 2–6 hours
- C) 12–18 hours
- D) 24–48 hours

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Rigor mortis follows Nysten's law — begins in jaw and neck muscles first, progressing downward (face → neck → trunk → upper limbs → lower limbs); onset is 2–6 hours after death. The finding of stiff jaw/neck with flaccid lower limbs places time of death at 2–6 hours. It is complete by 12 hours and passes off in the same order (jaw first) by 24–48 hours. Cadaveric spasm (instantaneous rigor) differs and involves only one muscle group, without the flaccid stage.

</details>

**Q2. At autopsy, postmortem lividity (hypostasis) is noted to be fixed and does not shift when the body is repositioned. This finding indicates that death occurred at least:**
- A) 1–2 hours ago
- B) 2–4 hours ago
- C) 6–12 hours ago
- D) 24–36 hours ago

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Postmortem hypostasis appears 2–4 hours after death and becomes fixed (non-blanchable, non-shifting) at 6–12 hours due to hemolysis and tissue imbibition. Before fixation (2–6 hours), lividity shifts with body position — a forensically important point for determining body movement. After fixation, lividity remains at the original dependent sites even if the body is moved, indicating the body was repositioned post-mortem.

</details>

**Q3. A decomposing body shows greenish discoloration starting in the right iliac fossa. Which structure accounts for this location as the first site of putrefaction?**
- A) Sigmoid colon
- B) Cecum and ascending colon
- C) Liver
- D) Stomach

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The cecum and ascending colon (right iliac fossa) is the first site of putrefaction because it has the highest bacterial load of gut flora and the thinnest wall, leading to greenish discoloration from hydrogen sulfide + hemoglobin → sulfhemoglobin. The sequence: green discoloration (right iliac fossa, 24–36h in summer) → marbling → bloating → liquefaction → skeletonization. The liver putrefies fastest among solid organs due to its enzyme-rich content.

</details>

**Q4. A soldier is found dead gripping his rifle tightly. The attending doctor notes that the grip cannot be broken passively. This phenomenon is best explained by:**
- A) Rigor mortis
- B) Cadaveric spasm
- C) Heat stiffening
- D) Cold stiffening

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cadaveric spasm (instantaneous rigor) occurs at the moment of death, without the initial relaxation phase, typically in states of intense emotion, exhaustion, or violent death. It affects only one group of muscles (commonly hands) and is forensically important as it preserves the victim's last act — e.g., clutching grass, weapons, or a suicide note. Unlike rigor mortis, it cannot be artificially reproduced. It is seen in drowning, gunshot wounds, and electrocution.

</details>

**Q5. In a case of suspected alcohol poisoning, which sample from the body is most reliable for alcohol estimation at autopsy, as it is the last to decompose?**
- A) Blood from femoral vein
- B) Urine
- C) Vitreous humor
- D) Bile

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Vitreous humor is the sample of choice for postmortem alcohol estimation because it is the last body fluid to decompose (protected in the closed eyeball), least susceptible to putrefaction artifacts and redistribution, and gives accurate BAC correlation (vitreous alcohol × 0.8 = blood alcohol). Blood is affected by redistribution and decomposition. Urine gives only qualitative data. Vitreous potassium is also used to estimate time since death (rises ~0.14 mmol/L/hour).

</details>

## WOUNDS AND INJURIES (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 28-year-old man is brought to the emergency department with multiple cuts on the inner aspect of both forearms and the dorsum of hands. There are no injuries elsewhere. The most likely explanation for this injury pattern is:**
- A) Self-inflicted injuries
- B) Defense wounds
- C) Fabricated injuries
- D) Hesitation cuts

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Defense wounds are incised or contused wounds on the forearms (ulnar border), hands, and fingers sustained while a victim attempts to ward off a knife or blunt object attack. The inner forearm and dorsum of hands are classic sites. Hesitation cuts are multiple parallel, superficial, tentative cuts typically on the wrist or neck in suicidal attempts. Self-inflicted wounds are typically on accessible sites (left wrist in right-handed individuals) and are parallel and superficial.

</details>

**Q7. At autopsy of a gunshot victim, the entrance wound shows a round punched-out hole with inverted edges, an abrasion collar, and blackening/tattooing around the wound. The exit wound is expected to show:**
- A) Abrasion collar with inverted edges
- B) Blackening and stippling
- C) A larger, irregular, everted wound without abrasion collar
- D) A smaller wound than the entrance

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The exit wound of a gunshot is typically larger, irregular, everted (skin pushed outward), and lacks an abrasion collar, tattooing, or blackening because the bullet exits at lower velocity without gases or unburnt powder. The entrance wound has an abrasion collar (from bullet spinning), inverted edges, and in intermediate-range shots, tattooing (unburnt powder) and blackening (soot). Contact shots show a stellate entrance wound with muzzle contusion.

</details>

**Q8. A body recovered from a river shows a ligature mark that is pale, depressed, oblique, and incomplete (does not encircle the neck completely), located above the thyroid cartilage. The most likely manner of death is:**
- A) Strangulation by ligature
- B) Throttling
- C) Hanging
- D) Burking

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Hanging produces an oblique, incomplete, pale, grooved ligature mark situated above the thyroid cartilage (at the level of the larynx), directed upward toward the knot. Strangulation by ligature produces a horizontal, complete, lower-placed mark (at or below the thyroid cartilage). Throttling (manual strangulation) shows fingernail abrasions and fingertip bruising. The oblique, incomplete, high-placed nature of the mark here is pathognomonic of hanging.

</details>

**Q9. In a suspected drowning case, the gold standard investigation to confirm that the victim was alive when they entered the water is:**
- A) Hydrostatic test (Raschkow's test)
- B) Diatom test
- C) Gettler's chloride test
- D) Emphysema aquosum on autopsy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The Diatom test is the gold standard for confirming antemortem drowning — diatoms (siliceous algae) in water enter the lungs and, via the circulation, are found in the liver, kidney, bone marrow, and brain only if the heart was pumping at the time of immersion (i.e., victim was alive). Postmortem immersion does not distribute diatoms beyond the lungs. Emphysema aquosum (over-distended, waterlogged lungs) and Gettler's test are corroborative. The Hydrostatic test is for infanticide.

</details>

**Q10. A lacerated wound and an incised wound are compared. Which feature is characteristic of a lacerated wound that differentiates it from an incised wound?**
- A) Clean-cut margins
- B) Longer than deep
- C) Bridging of tissues at the wound base
- D) Absence of abrasion around the wound

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Lacerated wounds are caused by blunt force and show irregular, contused margins with bridging of blood vessels, nerves, and connective tissue at the wound base (because these structures are more elastic than skin and do not tear completely). Incised wounds are longer than deep with clean-cut margins, no bridging, and minimal surrounding abrasion. Bridging is pathognomonic of lacerated wounds and is the key feature in forensic differentiation.

</details>

## TOXICOLOGY (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A farmer is brought to the casualty with excessive sweating, lacrimation, salivation, urination, defecation, miosis, and bradycardia after working in a pesticide-sprayed field. The antidote of choice and the drug used to reactivate the inhibited enzyme are respectively:**
- A) Atropine and pralidoxime
- B) Naloxone and atropine
- C) Pralidoxime alone
- D) Diazepam and atropine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Organophosphate poisoning causes irreversible inhibition of acetylcholinesterase, leading to muscarinic (SLUD: salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation + miosis, bradycardia, bronchospasm) and nicotinic features. Atropine (large doses, titrated to dry secretions) blocks muscarinic receptors and is the primary antidote. Pralidoxime (2-PAM) reactivates the inhibited enzyme if given before "aging" (phosphorylation becomes irreversible within hours). Pralidoxime alone is insufficient; both must be given together.

</details>

**Q12. A 45-year-old chronic alcoholic is brought unconscious. His blood alcohol level is 350 mg/dL. Which formula is used to back-calculate blood alcohol concentration at a given time after the incident?**
- A) Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
- B) Widmark formula
- C) Beer-Lambert law
- D) Poiseuille's law

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The Widmark formula (BAC = A / r × W, where A = amount of alcohol consumed, r = Widmark factor [0.7 males, 0.6 females], W = body weight in kg) is used to estimate or back-calculate blood alcohol concentration. It is the standard medicolegal tool. The metabolism rate of alcohol is ~15 mg/dL/hour (zero-order kinetics). Breath analyzer (breathalyzer) measures expired air alcohol, with BAC = breath alcohol × 2100 (blood:breath partition ratio).

</details>

**Q13. A chronic arsenic poisoning case is suspected in a 35-year-old man who presents with Mees' lines on nails, hyperpigmentation, hyperkeratosis, and garlic-like odor from the body. The confirmatory investigation is:**
- A) Marsh test
- B) Reinsch test
- C) Harrison-Gilroy test
- D) Spot test with sodium rhodizonate

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

The Marsh test is the gold standard confirmatory test for arsenic poisoning — arsenic is converted to arsine gas (AsH₃) and deposited as a metallic mirror on a glass surface. The Reinsch test is a screening test (arsenic deposited on copper strip). Mees' lines (transverse white bands on nails, 1 mm = 1 month of exposure) are classic. Garlic odor is from dimethyl arsenide. Hair and nail analysis by AAS (atomic absorption spectroscopy) is used in chronic cases.

</details>

**Q14. A firefighter is rescued from a burning building and found unconscious. Postmortem examination shows cherry-red discoloration of lividity and tissues. The most likely poisoning is:**
- A) Cyanide
- B) Carbon monoxide
- C) Hydrogen sulfide
- D) Nitrobenzene

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Carbon monoxide (CO) binds hemoglobin with 200× affinity compared to oxygen, forming carboxyhemoglobin (COHb), which produces the pathognomonic cherry-red postmortem lividity and tissue discoloration. Diagnosis is by COHb levels (>50% = fatal). Cyanide also causes tissue hypoxia but skin is typically bright red from venous oxygenation; it smells of bitter almonds. Hydrogen sulfide causes blackish-green lividity. CO is the most common cause of poisoning death in closed-space fires.

</details>

**Q15. A 22-year-old male is brought unconscious with pin-point pupils, respiratory depression, and bradycardia. His companion says he was injecting something. The specific antidote and the duration of its action compared to the offending drug are:**
- A) Flumazenil; longer acting
- B) Naloxone; shorter acting than morphine
- C) Naltrexone; same duration as heroin
- D) Atropine; shorter acting

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Opioid (morphine/heroin) toxidrome: pin-point pupils (miosis), respiratory depression, CNS depression, bradycardia — the classic triad. Naloxone (IV/IM/intranasal) is the specific competitive opioid antagonist. Critically, naloxone has a shorter half-life (30–90 min) than morphine (4–6 hours) or heroin/methadone, requiring repeated doses or continuous infusion to prevent re-narcotization. Flumazenil reverses benzodiazepines. Pin-point pupils + respiratory depression = opiate overdose until proven otherwise.

</details>

## MEDICAL JURISPRUDENCE (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 32-year-old man is accused of murder. His defense counsel argues he was insane at the time of the act. Under the McNaughton rules as codified in Indian law, acquittal on grounds of insanity requires proof that the accused:**
- A) Had a mental disorder for more than 6 months
- B) Did not know the nature of the act or that it was wrong
- C) Was under the influence of alcohol at the time
- D) Had a history of previous acquittal on similar grounds

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The McNaughton rules (1843) form the basis of the insanity defense under Section 84 IPC (Indian Penal Code) — "Act of a person of unsound mind." The accused is not criminally responsible if, at the time of the act, due to a disease of the mind, they did not know the nature of the act, or did not know that what they were doing was wrong. Duration of illness, alcohol, and prior history are irrelevant to this test. The burden of proving insanity lies with the defense.

</details>

**Q17. A pregnant woman at 19 weeks gestation requests medical termination of pregnancy (MTP). Under the MTP Act 1971 (amended 2021), which of the following is correct regarding this request?**
- A) Requires opinion of two registered medical practitioners
- B) Requires a court order
- C) Can be performed by a single registered medical practitioner up to 20 weeks
- D) Is not permitted beyond 12 weeks

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Under the MTP Act 1971 amended in 2021: up to 20 weeks — opinion of one RMP (registered medical practitioner) is sufficient. Beyond 20 weeks up to 24 weeks — opinion of two RMPs required, for special categories (rape survivors, minors, differently-abled women, fetal anomalies). Beyond 24 weeks — requires state-level Medical Board approval for substantial fetal anomaly. This case at 19 weeks requires only one RMP's opinion. The 2021 amendment raised the upper limit from 20 to 24 weeks for specified categories.

**Q18. A 14-year-old girl is brought to a government hospital by a neighbor after alleged sexual assault. The doctor's obligations under POCSO Act 2012 include all of the following EXCEPT:**
- A) Providing emergency medical care
- B) Recording the statement of the child
- C) Mandatory reporting to the police or SJPU within 24 hours
- D) Maintaining confidentiality and NOT reporting to avoid secondary trauma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

The POCSO Act 2012 (Protection of Children from Sexual Offences) mandates that any person, including a doctor, who has knowledge of a sexual offence against a child (under 18 years) MUST report it to the Special Juvenile Police Unit (SJPU) or local police within 24 hours. Failure to report is a criminal offence (Section 21 POCSO). Confidentiality does NOT override mandatory reporting under POCSO. The age of sexual consent in India is 18 years. Emergency medical care must be provided without waiting for police formalities.

**Q19. A mortally wounded man gives a statement to the attending nurse identifying his assailant before dying. The admissibility of this Dying Declaration in court is BEST ensured if:**

- B) The declarant was conscious and understood he was dying
- C) It is corroborated by another witness

It need not be corroborated; a dying declaration alone can be the sole basis for conviction. ## SEXUAL OFFENCES AND IDENTIFICATION (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 20-year-old woman alleges rape and is brought to a government hospital 60 hours after the alleged incident. Regarding medicolegal examination, which statement is CORRECT?**
- A) Two-finger test must be performed to assess habituation
- B) Emergency contraception can still be offered as it is effective up to 72 hours
- C) DNA evidence collection is futile beyond 48 hours
- D) Medical examination should be deferred until police arrive

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Emergency contraception (levonorgestrel 1.5 mg) is effective up to 72 hours post-unprotected intercourse; this case at 60 hours is still within the window. The two-finger (per vaginum) test has been abolished by the Supreme Court of India (2022) and MCI guidelines — it is not a test of consent or habituation and is illegal. Medical examination should NOT be delayed for police — treatment and evidence collection proceed immediately.

</details>

**Q22. DNA fingerprinting is used to identify a suspect in a criminal case. Which technique is currently considered the most sensitive and is the standard for forensic DNA profiling in India?**
- A) RFLP (Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism)
- B) VNTR analysis
- C) STR (Short Tandem Repeat) analysis
- D) RAPD (Random Amplified Polymorphic DNA)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

STR (Short Tandem Repeat) analysis is the current gold standard for forensic DNA profiling — it requires minimal DNA (nanogram quantities), can be performed on degraded samples, and uses PCR amplification of 2–7 base pair repeat units at multiple loci for high discrimination power. RFLP requires large amounts of high-quality DNA and is slower. VNTR (Variable Number Tandem Repeats) was the original technique used by Jeffreys (1984). India's DNA Technology (Use and Application) Regulation Act 2019 mandates STR profiling for the national DNA database.

</details>

**Q23. In fingerprint classification, which pattern is the most common in the general population, and what percentage of fingers does it account for?**
- A) Whorl — 60%
- B) Loop — 65%
- C) Arch — 25%
- D) Composite — 10%

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Loop is the most common fingerprint pattern, found on approximately 65% of fingers; whorls account for ~30%, and arches ~5%. Loops require one delta and one core; they open toward the radial side (radial loop) or ulnar side (ulnar loop, more common). Whorls have two deltas. Arches have no delta. Fingerprints are permanent from the 6th fetal month, unique to each individual, and do not change throughout life — basis of dactylography (Galton, 1892; Henry classification system used in India).

</details>

**Q24. A forensic odontologist is asked to estimate the age of an unidentified skeletal remains using dental methods. The most comprehensive method using multiple dental features including attrition, secondary dentine, cementum apposition, root resorption, transparency, and periodontosis is:**
- A) Schour and Massler method
- B) Gustafson's method
- C) Demirjian's method
- D) London Atlas method

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Gustafson's method (1950) estimates age from six regressive dental changes: Attrition, Periodontosis, Secondary dentine, Cementum apposition, Root resorption, and Transparency of root (mnemonic: APSCRT). Each feature is scored 0–3, total score correlates with age (R² = 0.98). It is used for adult age estimation from teeth. Schour and Massler and Demirjian's methods are used for age estimation in children from tooth eruption/development patterns. The London Atlas is a newer reference standard for children.

</details>

**Q25. A 17-year-old male is presented before a court for age determination. On X-ray, the medial end of the clavicle shows a separate epiphysis that has not yet fused. This finding indicates the individual is most likely:**
- A) Below 18 years of age
- B) Between 18–25 years of age
- C) Above 25 years of age
- D) Below 14 years of age

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The medial epiphysis of the clavicle is the last epiphysis to fuse in the body, fusing between 21–25 years (appears at 18–20 years). Its presence as a separate epiphysis indicates the individual is between 18–25 years. This is forensically significant for determining whether an accused was a juvenile (<18 years) at the time of the offence under the Juvenile Justice Act. The iliac crest fuses at 21–25 years. **Q26. A magistrate orders an inquest in a case of suspected dowry death (death within 7 years of marriage under suspicious circumstances). This type of inquest in India is called:**
- A) Police inquest (Section 174 CrPC)
- B) Magistrate's inquest (Section 176 CrPC)
- C) Coroner's inquest
- D) Jury inquest

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

A Magistrate's inquest (Section 176 CrPC) is mandatory in cases of dowry death, death in police custody, death in prison, death in a psychiatric facility, and encounter deaths. The magistrate directly supervises the inquiry. A Police inquest (Section 174 CrPC) is conducted by a police officer (SHO) for all other unnatural/suspicious deaths and is the most common type. The Coroner's inquest (abolished in most Indian states except Maharashtra and West Bengal historically) was a quasi-judicial inquiry. Jury inquest does not exist in India.

</details>

**Q27. During autopsy of a suspected air embolism case (following an illegal abortion), the pathologist clamps the heart underwater before opening it. Air bubbling out confirms the diagnosis. This sign associated with scaphoid (sunken) abdomen seen in air embolism is called:**
- A) Tardieu's spots
- B) McEwen's sign
- C) Paltauf's hemorrhages
- D) Wischnewski spots

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

McEwen's sign is the scaphoid (sunken, boat-shaped) abdomen seen in air embolism — air in the peritoneal veins/mesenteric vessels causes the abdomen to appear sunken rather than distended. The submerged heart test (Raschkow-Brouardel) confirms air embolism at autopsy. Air embolism causes death when >100–150 mL of air enters the venous circulation. Tardieu's spots are petechial hemorrhages seen in asphyxia. Paltauf's hemorrhages are pale hemorrhages in the lungs in drowning. Wischnewski spots are gastric mucosal hemorrhages in hypothermia.

</details>

**Q28. A newborn is found dead. The police suspect infanticide. The hydrostatic (floatation) test is performed by placing the lungs in water. The lungs float. The correct interpretation is:**
- A) The baby was definitely murdered
- B) The baby had breathed air and was likely born alive
- C) The baby was stillborn
- D) The result is inconclusive and cannot be used in court

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The hydrostatic (floatation) test (Raschkow's test / Breslau's second life test): if the lungs float in water (specific gravity <1.0), it indicates they contain air and the baby had breathed, suggesting live birth. Sinking lungs indicate the baby was stillborn (never breathed). Limitations: artificial respiration, putrefaction with gas formation, or maceration can give false positive floatation. Infanticide = killing of a live-born child; foeticide = killing of a fetus in utero. The test is corroborative, not absolutely conclusive by itself.

</details>

**Q29. A 30-year-old man is assaulted with a blunt object on the head. CT scan shows a fracture at the site of impact that is depressed inward like a ping-pong ball, with no comminution. This type of skull fracture is:**
- A) Fissured fracture
- B) Pond (depressed) fracture
- C) Contre-coup fracture
- D) Ring fracture

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

A pond fracture (also called a depressed fracture) is a circular or oval depressed skull fracture resembling a pond — seen classically in infants (pliable skull) and from localized blunt force impact. It is inwardly depressed without comminution, resembling a ping-pong ball dent. A fissured fracture is a linear crack. A comminuted fracture has multiple fragments. A contre-coup fracture occurs at the site opposite to impact. A ring fracture encircles the foramen magnum and occurs in falls from height with axial loading.

</details>

**Q30. In a medico-legal autopsy, which type of autopsy is being performed, and who has the authority to order it?**
- A) Clinical autopsy — ordered by the treating physician
- B) Medico-legal autopsy — ordered by a magistrate or police authority
- C) Medico-legal autopsy — can be ordered by the family of the deceased
- D) Both types can be ordered by any doctor

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

A medico-legal (forensic) autopsy is ordered by a magistrate or police authority (Section 174 CrPC / Section 176 CrPC) in cases of unnatural, suspicious, or sudden unexplained death, and requires a qualified forensic medicine specialist (or designated government doctor). The family's consent is NOT required. A clinical (hospital/pathological) autopsy is performed to determine the cause of death for academic/quality-of-care purposes and requires the consent of the next of kin. The key distinction is legal authority vs. family consent.

</details>
