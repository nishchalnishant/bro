## BACTERIOLOGY BASICS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A sputum sample is processed and stained with carbol fuchsin, decolorized with 20% H₂SO₄, and counterstained with methylene blue. Pink bacilli are seen against a blue background. Which organism is most likely identified?**
- A) Nocardia asteroides
- B) Mycobacterium tuberculosis
- C) Legionella pneumophila
- D) Actinomyces israelii

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) stain uses carbol fuchsin with 20% H₂SO₄ as decolorizer; acid-fast organisms like Mycobacterium tuberculosis retain the red stain. Nocardia is weakly acid-fast (1% H₂SO₄ decolorizer). Legionella requires Dieterle silver stain. Actinomyces is gram-positive, not acid-fast.

</details>

**Q2. Loeffler's serum slope is the culture medium used to grow which organism, producing a characteristic "Chinese letter" arrangement?**
- A) Bordetella pertussis
- B) Corynebacterium diphtheriae
- C) Streptococcus pyogenes
- D) Haemophilus influenzae

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Corynebacterium diphtheriae grows on Loeffler's serum slope and shows metachromatic (Babes-Ernst) granules with Albert's stain, arranged in "Chinese letter" or "cuneiform" pattern. Bordetella grows on Bordet-Gengou medium. H. influenzae requires Chocolate agar (X and V factors). Streptococcus grows on blood agar.

</details>

**Q3. An autoclave set at 121°C for 15 minutes achieves sterilization by which mechanism?**
- A) Dry heat causing protein oxidation
- B) Moist heat causing protein coagulation and denaturation
- C) Radiation causing DNA strand breaks
- D) Ethylene oxide alkylation of nucleic acids

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Autoclaving (moist heat at 121°C, 15 psi, 15 minutes) sterilizes by protein coagulation and denaturation — the most efficient method of sterilization. Dry heat (160°C, 1 hour) works by protein oxidation. Gamma radiation causes DNA strand breaks. Ethylene oxide alkylates nucleic acids and is used for heat-labile equipment.

</details>

**Q4. India ink preparation of CSF from an immunocompromised patient shows encapsulated yeast cells with a clear halo. The organism and its diagnostic appearance are:**
- A) Candida albicans — pseudohyphae
- B) Cryptococcus neoformans — "halo" due to polysaccharide capsule
- C) Aspergillus fumigatus — septate hyphae
- D) Histoplasma capsulatum — intracellular yeast

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

India ink (negative staining) demonstrates Cryptococcus neoformans by revealing the polysaccharide capsule as a clear halo around the yeast cell, as the ink particles are excluded by the capsule. Candida shows pseudohyphae on culture. Aspergillus shows septate hyphae with acute angle branching. Histoplasma is an intracellular organism seen within macrophages.

</details>

**Q5. Which bacterial structure is responsible for conjugation (genetic transfer) and is NOT involved in motility?**
- A) Flagellum
- B) Common pilus (fimbria)
- C) Sex pilus (F pilus)
- D) Capsule

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Sex pilus (F pilus) encoded by the F-plasmid forms a conjugation tube for transfer of genetic material between bacteria. Flagella provide motility; their H antigen is important in Salmonella typing. Common pili/fimbriae mediate attachment to host cells (e.g., uropathogenic E. coli). Capsule prevents phagocytosis.

</details>

## IMMUNOLOGY (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 22-year-old woman develops urticaria, bronchospasm, and hypotension within minutes of receiving penicillin injection. The underlying immunological mechanism involves:**
- A) IgG-mediated complement activation
- B) IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation
- C) Immune complex deposition in vessel walls
- D) Cytotoxic T-lymphocyte activation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Anaphylaxis is a Type I (immediate) hypersensitivity reaction mediated by IgE bound to mast cells and basophils; re-exposure to antigen causes cross-linking of IgE → degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes, PGD2 release. Type II is IgG/IgM cytotoxic. Type III involves immune complexes. Type IV is T-cell mediated (delayed, 48–72 hours).

</details>

**Q7. A child with recurrent sinopulmonary infections has absent tonsils, undetectable serum immunoglobulins, and absent B cells on flow cytometry. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Common variable immunodeficiency (CVID)
- B) Selective IgA deficiency
- C) X-linked agammaglobulinemia (Bruton's disease)
- D) DiGeorge syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

X-linked agammaglobulinemia (Bruton's) presents after 6 months (maternal IgG wanes) with absent B cells, absent immunoglobulins, and absent tonsils/lymph nodes due to BTK gene mutation. CVID presents in adults with low Ig but normal B cell count. Selective IgA deficiency has low IgA only. DiGeorge affects T cells (thymic aplasia, tetany, conotruncal defects).

</details>

**Q8. The classical complement pathway is activated by which of the following?**
- A) LPS (lipopolysaccharide) on gram-negative bacteria
- B) Mannose residues on microbial surfaces
- C) Antigen-antibody (IgG or IgM) complexes
- D) Alternative pathway properdin stabilization

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Classical pathway is activated by IgG or IgM antigen-antibody complexes binding C1q → C1r → C1s → C4, C2, C3 activation. LPS activates the alternative pathway. Mannose residues activate the lectin (MBL) pathway. Properdin stabilizes C3bBb in the alternative pathway.

</details>

**Q9. A patient develops fever, arthralgia, and lymphadenopathy 7–10 days after receiving horse antiserum. Complement levels are low and immune complexes are detected in blood. This is an example of:**
- A) Type I hypersensitivity
- B) Type II hypersensitivity
- C) Type III hypersensitivity
- D) Type IV hypersensitivity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Serum sickness is the classic Type III (immune complex-mediated) hypersensitivity; immune complexes deposit in vessel walls, activate complement, and recruit neutrophils causing tissue damage. It occurs 7–10 days after antigen exposure. Type I is immediate IgE-mediated. Type II involves cell-surface antigens. Type IV (tuberculin test, contact dermatitis) is T-cell mediated, peaking at 48–72 h.

</details>

**Q10. BCG vaccine is given at birth in India under UIP. It protects primarily against:**
- A) Pulmonary tuberculosis in adults
- B) Miliary tuberculosis and tuberculous meningitis in children
- C) Latent TB reactivation
- D) Extrapulmonary TB in immunocompetent adults

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

BCG vaccine is most effective against severe disseminated disease in children — miliary TB and tuberculous meningitis — with 80% efficacy. Its protection against pulmonary TB in adults is variable (0–80% in different trials). It does not prevent latent infection or reactivation. It is given at birth or as early as possible under India's UIP.

</details>

## GRAM-POSITIVE ORGANISMS (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 3-year-old child presents with sudden-onset high fever, generalised erythematous rash sparing palms/soles, followed by desquamation. The toxin responsible acts as a superantigen. The causative organism is:**
- A) Streptococcus pyogenes — erythrogenic toxin A
- B) Staphylococcus aureus — exfoliatin toxin
- C) Staphylococcus aureus — TSST-1
- D) Clostridium perfringens — alpha toxin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Toxic shock syndrome toxin-1 (TSST-1) from Staphylococcus aureus is a superantigen causing massive T-cell activation → cytokine storm → TSS (fever, rash, hypotension, multi-organ involvement). Exfoliatin (A and B) causes staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome with cleavage at stratum granulosum. Erythrogenic toxin causes scarlet fever. Alpha toxin of C. perfringens is a lecithinase causing gas gangrene.

</details>

**Q12. A swab from a patient with necrotizing fasciitis grows beta-hemolytic colonies on blood agar. Bacitracin sensitivity test is positive. The organism produces which enzyme that degrades hyaluronic acid and aids tissue invasion?**
- A) Coagulase
- B) Hyaluronidase (spreading factor)
- C) Lecithinase
- D) Streptokinase only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Strep) is bacitracin-sensitive and produces hyaluronidase ("spreading factor") that degrades hyaluronic acid in connective tissue, facilitating spread. Coagulase is produced by S. aureus. Lecithinase (alpha toxin) is from C. perfringens. Streptokinase is also produced by S. pyogenes but does not degrade hyaluronate.

</details>

**Q13. Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) resistance is mediated by:**
- A) Beta-lactamase enzyme production
- B) Altered penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a) encoded by mecA gene
- C) Reduced outer membrane permeability
- D) Active efflux pump overexpression

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

MRSA resistance is due to mecA gene encoding PBP2a (also called PBP2'), an altered penicillin-binding protein with low affinity for all beta-lactams, making MRSA resistant to all penicillins and cephalosporins. Beta-lactamase only destroys certain penicillins. Reduced permeability and efflux pumps are mechanisms in gram-negative resistance. Vancomycin is the drug of choice for MRSA.

</details>

**Q14. A pregnant woman in her third trimester develops meningitis. CSF culture grows gram-positive rods that are motile at 22°C (tumbling motility) but non-motile at 37°C. The organism is:**
- A) Listeria monocytogenes
- B) Corynebacterium diphtheriae
- C) Bacillus anthracis
- D) Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Listeria monocytogenes is a gram-positive rod showing characteristic "tumbling motility" by end-over-end flagellar movement at 22°C (cold enrichment at 4°C is used for culture). It causes meningitis in neonates, pregnant women, elderly, and immunocompromised patients. Ampicillin is the drug of choice. B. anthracis is non-motile. C. diphtheriae is non-motile club-shaped. Erysipelothrix causes erysipeloid in fish handlers.

</details>

**Q15. A soldier develops gas gangrene after a shrapnel wound. The organism isolated is Clostridium perfringens. Its most important virulence factor causing myonecrosis is:**
- A) Tetanospasmin
- B) Alpha toxin (lecithinase/phospholipase C)
- C) Botulinum toxin
- D) Erythrogenic toxin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Alpha toxin (lecithinase/phospholipase C) of Clostridium perfringens is the major virulence factor for gas gangrene (myonecrosis); it hydrolyzes phospholipids in cell membranes causing hemolysis, necrosis, and vascular damage. Tetanospasmin causes tetanus (C. tetani). Botulinum toxin prevents ACh release. Erythrogenic toxin is from Streptococcus pyogenes.

</details>

## GRAM-NEGATIVE ORGANISMS (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 2-year-old child develops bloody diarrhea and hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS) after eating undercooked beef. The causative E. coli strain produces which toxin?**
- A) Heat-labile toxin (LT) — activates adenylyl cyclase
- B) Heat-stable toxin (ST) — activates guanylyl cyclase
- C) Shiga toxin (Stx) — inhibits 60S ribosome
- D) Hemolysin — lyses erythrocytes directly

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC, O157:H7) produces Shiga toxin (Stx1 and Stx2) that inhibits the 60S ribosomal subunit (cleaves 28S rRNA), causing HUS (microangiopathic hemolytic anemia + thrombocytopenia + AKI). LT (ETEC) activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → watery diarrhea. ST activates guanylyl cyclase → ↑cGMP. Antibiotics are contraindicated in EHEC as they may trigger HUS.

</details>

**Q17. A diabetic patient develops a rapidly progressive lobar pneumonia with "currant jelly" sputum (blood and mucus). Chest X-ray shows a bulging fissure. The most likely causative organism is:**
- A) Streptococcus pneumoniae
- B) Klebsiella pneumoniae
- C) Pseudomonas aeruginosa
- D) Legionella pneumophila

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Klebsiella pneumoniae pneumonia classically presents with "currant jelly" sputum (blood-stained mucinous sputum) and a "bulging fissure" on X-ray due to extensive consolidation with large mucoid exudate. It predominantly affects diabetics and alcoholics. Pneumococcal pneumonia has rusty sputum. Pseudomonas causes pneumonia in CF and ventilated patients. Legionella causes atypical pneumonia with hyponatremia.

</details>

**Q18. A 35-year-old presents with epigastric pain and is found to have a duodenal ulcer. CLO (Campylobacter-like organism) test is positive. The organism H. pylori evades gastric acid by producing:**
- A) Catalase only
- B) Urease — converts urea to ammonia, raising local pH
- C) Coagulase
- D) Hyaluronidase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

H. pylori produces urease (detected by CLO test, urea breath test, and rapid urease test), which hydrolyzes urea → ammonia + CO₂, raising the local pH and allowing survival in the gastric mucosa. It also produces catalase (protects against H₂O₂) and vacuolating cytotoxin (VacA). Triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin) is standard treatment in India.

</details>

**Q19. In typhoid fever caused by Salmonella typhi, which week of illness shows the highest diagnostic yield from blood culture?**
- A) Week 1 — bacteremia is highest
- B) Week 2 — Widal test becomes positive
- C) Week 3 — stool culture is positive
- D) Week 4 — urine culture is most useful

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Blood culture is positive in 80–90% of cases during Week 1 (bacteremia phase) and is the gold standard for typhoid diagnosis. Widal test (serological) becomes positive after Week 1 (O antibodies rise first). Stool and urine cultures are positive in Weeks 3–4 (excretion phase). Rose spots appear in Week 1–2. Intestinal perforation and hemorrhage occur in Week 3.

</details>

**Q20. Vibrio cholerae causes profuse "rice-water" diarrhea without blood or pus cells. The toxin mechanism involves:**
- A) Invasion of intestinal mucosa
- B) ADP-ribosylation of Gs protein → permanent activation of adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP
- C) Inhibition of guanylyl cyclase → ↑cGMP
- D) Cytotoxin-mediated destruction of enterocytes

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cholera toxin (CT) ADP-ribosylates the Gs alpha subunit, permanently activating adenylyl cyclase → massive ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion and Na⁺ absorption inhibition → isotonic fluid loss (rice-water stool). V. cholerae is non-invasive; no pus cells. E. coli LT has the same mechanism. E. coli ST activates guanylyl cyclase. ORS is the cornerstone of treatment; doxycycline reduces duration.

</details>

## MYCOBACTERIA AND SPECIAL ORGANISMS (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A sputum culture on Lowenstein-Jensen (LJ) medium at 37°C grows buff-colored, rough, raised colonies after 6–8 weeks. Which drug susceptibility test is used as the primary screen for isoniazid resistance in M. tuberculosis?**
- A) E-test (epsilometer test)
- B) Proportion method on LJ medium
- C) Disk diffusion (Kirby-Bauer)
- D) MIC by broth microdilution

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The proportion method on Lowenstein-Jensen medium is the gold standard DST for M. tuberculosis, including INH resistance testing. LJ medium (coagulated egg + glycerol + malachite green) is the standard culture medium; colonies appear in 6–8 weeks. E-test can be used for mycobacteria but is not the primary screen. Disk diffusion (Kirby-Bauer) is not reliable for slow-growing mycobacteria.

</details>

**Q22. A patient from a leprosy-endemic area presents with 4 hypopigmented anesthetic skin patches and 2 thickened peripheral nerves. Slit-skin smear shows no bacilli. According to Ridley-Jopling classification, this patient has:**
- A) Lepromatous leprosy (LL)
- B) Borderline lepromatous (BL)
- C) Borderline tuberculoid (BT)
- D) Tuberculoid leprosy (TT)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Borderline tuberculoid (BT) leprosy: 4–10 skin lesions, nerve involvement more prominent than TT, smear negative or scanty bacilli (BI 0–1+). TT leprosy: 1–5 lesions, smear negative. WHO PB (paucibacillary) classification covers TT and BT (≤5 lesions, smear negative). LL shows numerous lesions, smear strongly positive (BI 4–6+), no anesthesia initially, leonine facies. MDT-MB (12 months) for MB; MDT-PB (6 months) for PB.

</details>

**Q23. A 28-year-old sex worker is screened for syphilis. VDRL is reactive at 1:32 dilution. The confirmatory test is:**
- A) RPR (Rapid Plasma Reagin)
- B) TPHA (Treponema pallidum haemagglutination assay)
- C) Dark-field microscopy of blood
- D) Weil-Felix reaction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Weil-Felix tests for Rickettsia. FTA-ABS is the most sensitive test for all stages.

</details>

- A) OX-2 only
- B) OX-K only

Rocky Mountain spotted fever (R. rickettsii) gives OX-19 and OX-2 positive. Q fever (Coxiella burnetii) is Weil-Felix negative. Doxycycline is the treatment for all rickettsial diseases.

**Q25. A patient treated for multidrug-resistant TB is found to have resistance to isoniazid, rifampicin, fluoroquinolones, and one injectable agent. This is classified as:**
- A) Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB)
- B) Extensively drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB)
- C) Pre-XDR TB (as per 2021 WHO revised definition)
- D) Totally drug-resistant TB (TDR-TB)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Per the 2021 WHO revised definition: Pre-XDR TB = MDR/RR-TB + resistance to any fluoroquinolone. XDR-TB = MDR/RR-TB + resistance to any fluoroquinolone + at least one of bedaquiline or linezolid. The older definition of XDR (MDR + fluoroquinolone + injectable) is now pre-XDR. MDR-TB = resistance to INH + RIF only. NTEP uses bedaquiline-based regimens for MDR/XDR-TB in India.

</details>

## VIROLOGY AND PARASITOLOGY (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. A blood donor is tested and found to be HBsAg negative, anti-HBs negative, HBeAg negative, and anti-HBc IgM positive. This serological pattern is consistent with:**
- A) Successfully vaccinated individual
- B) Chronic hepatitis B carrier
- C) Window period of acute hepatitis B infection
- D) Resolved hepatitis B with immunity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The window period in acute HBV infection is characterized by: HBsAg negative (cleared), anti-HBs not yet detectable, and anti-HBc IgM positive (only marker present). Vaccination → only anti-HBs positive. Chronic carrier → HBsAg positive >6 months, anti-HBc IgG positive. Resolved infection → anti-HBs + anti-HBc IgG positive, HBsAg negative. Anti-HBc IgM is the best marker for acute infection.

</details>

**Q27. A 32-year-old man presents with oral candidiasis and weight loss. HIV ELISA is positive. CD4 count is 180 cells/μL. Antiretroviral therapy should be initiated:**
- A) Only when CD4 falls below 100 cells/μL
- B) Immediately, irrespective of CD4 count — "Test and Treat" strategy

**Q28. A patient from Odisha presents with high-grade fever with 48-hour periodicity, severe headache, and altered sensorium. Blood smear shows banana-shaped gametocytes. The Plasmodium species responsible and the drug of choice are:**
- A) P. vivax — chloroquine
- B) P. falciparum — artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT)
- C) P. malariae — primaquine
- D) P. ovale — chloroquine + primaquine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Plasmodium falciparum produces banana/crescent-shaped gametocytes (pathognomonic); causes tertian fever (48 h cycle), cerebral malaria, and highest mortality. ACT (Artesunate + SP or Artemether-lumefantrine) is the first-line treatment per NVBDCP guidelines. P. vivax shows Schüffner's dots and amoeboid trophozoites. P. malariae causes quartan fever (72 h). Primaquine is added for radical cure (P. vivax/ovale hypnozoites).

</details>

**Q29. Which hepatitis virus is transmitted by feco-oral route and causes fulminant hepatic failure with highest mortality in pregnant women (20–30% mortality in third trimester)?**
- A) Hepatitis A virus (HAV)
- B) Hepatitis B virus (HBV)
- C) Hepatitis C virus (HCV)
- D) Hepatitis E virus (HEV)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

Hepatitis E virus (HEV) is enterically transmitted (feco-oral, water-borne) and causes epidemic hepatitis with disproportionately high mortality in pregnant women (20–30% in third trimester) due to fulminant hepatic failure. HAV is also feco-oral but rarely causes fulminant disease in pregnancy. HBV and HCV are parenterally/sexually transmitted. No specific antiviral exists for HEV; ribavirin is used in immunocompromised patients.

</details>

**Q30. A child presents with perianal itching, worse at night. Scotch tape test reveals ova with one flattened side. The organism and treatment are:**
- A) Ascaris lumbricoides — albendazole
- B) Trichuris trichiura — mebendazole
- C) Enterobius vermicularis (pinworm) — mebendazole or albendazole
- D) Strongyloides stercoralis — ivermectin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Enterobius vermicularis (pinworm) causes nocturnal perianal pruritus; diagnosis by Scotch tape test (Graham's test) showing D-shaped/plano-convex ova. Treatment: single dose mebendazole or albendazole, repeated after 2 weeks; all household contacts treated simultaneously. Ascaris ova are bile-stained with mamillated coat. Trichuris (whipworm) causes rectal prolapse in heavy infections. Strongyloides is diagnosed by stool culture (Harada-Mori) or serology.

</details>
