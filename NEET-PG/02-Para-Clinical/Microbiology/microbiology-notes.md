## 1. BACTERIOLOGY BASICS

### Gram-Positive vs Gram-Negative Structure

| Feature | Gram-Positive | Gram-Negative |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| **Cell wall** | Thick peptidoglycan (20-80 nm), NO outer membrane | Thin peptidoglycan (2-7 nm) + **outer membrane (LPS)** |
| **Teichoic acids** | Present (lipoteichoic + wall teichoic) | Absent |
| **Outer membrane** | Absent | Present (LPS = lipid A + core + O antigen) |
| **Periplasmic space** | Narrow | Wide (beta-lactamases here) |
| **Gram stain** | Purple (retain crystal violet) | Pink (decolorized, counterstained with safranin) |
| **Antibiotic sensitivity** | Generally sensitive to penicillin | Less sensitive (outer membrane barrier) |
| **Endotoxin** | Absent | LPS (lipid A = toxic component) → fever, septic shock |
| **Exotoxin** | More commonly produce | Some produce |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: LPS (endotoxin) — only Gram-negative organisms; stimulates TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6 → fever, hypotension, DIC in septic shock

### Staining Methods

| Stain | Purpose/Organism | Result |
|-------|-----------------|--------|
| **Gram stain** | Bacteria classification | G+: purple; G-: pink |
| **Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN/AFB)** | **Mycobacteria** (M. tuberculosis, M. leprae), Nocardia | Acid-fast = red on blue/green background |
| **Modified ZN** | Cryptosporidium, Isospora, Cyclospora, Microsporidium | Weakly acid-fast |
| **India ink (negative stain)** | **Cryptococcus neoformans** (capsule) | Clear halo around organism |
| **Giemsa stain** | **Malaria** (thick/thin films), Borrelia, Leishmania, Rickettsia, Chlamydia (inclusions), Trypanosoma | — |
| **Periodic Acid-Schiff (PAS)** | Fungi (cell wall), Whipple's disease (T. whipplei), Glycogen | Magenta/pink |
| **Methenamine Silver (GMS/Silver stain)** | **Fungi** (Pneumocystis, Aspergillus, Histoplasma) | Black fungal walls |
| **Albert stain** | **Metachromatic granules** — Corynebacterium diphtheriae | Blue-black granules in yellow-green organisms |
| **Fontana-Masson** | Treponema, melanin | — |
| **Mucin stain (Mucicarmine)** | Cryptococcus capsule | Red capsule |
| **Toluidine blue** | Pneumocystis jirovecii (cysts) | Blue cysts |
| **Wayson stain** | Yersinia pestis (safety pin appearance) | — |
| **Acridine orange** | Fluorescent — all nucleic acids; Borrelia, Malaria | Fluorescent orange |
| **Auramine-rhodamine** | Mycobacteria (fluorescent ZN alternative) | Bright yellow-orange |
| **Calcofluor white** | Fungi (cell wall chitin) | Fluorescent blue-white |

### Special Culture Media

| Organism | Medium | Special Feature |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| **Mycobacterium tuberculosis** | **Lowenstein-Jensen (LJ)** (eggs, malachite green) | Colonies in 6-8 weeks; **Rough, dry, buff-colored colonies** |
| **C. diphtheriae** | **Loeffler's serum** (initial growth/metachromatic granules) + **Tellurite agar** (black colonies) | **Hoyle's medium** (selective) |
| **N. gonorrhoeae / N. meningitidis** | **Thayer-Martin (modified chocolate agar + VCN antibiotics)** | Selective for Neisseria |
| **Bordetella pertussis** | **Bordet-Gengou (potato-blood-glycerol)** and **Charcoal-blood (Regan-Lowe)** | Cough plate method |
| **H. pylori** | **Skirrow's medium**, BCYE (not preferred), CLO (Campylobacter-Like Organism test - rapid urease test) | Microaerophilic |
| **Haemophilus influenzae** | **Chocolate agar** (factors X and V); **Levinthal's agar** | X (hematin) + V (NAD) factors needed |
| **Legionella** | **BCYE (Buffered Charcoal Yeast Extract)** agar with L-cysteine | Cannot grow on blood agar |
| **Vibrio cholerae** | **TCBS (Thiosulfate Citrate Bile Salts Sucrose)** agar | Yellow colonies (sucrose fermenter) |
| **Salmonella/Shigella** | **MacConkey** (Salmonella: pale colonies; E. coli: pink); **XLD** (Xylose Lysine Deoxycholate); **SS agar** | — |
| **Streptococcus** | Blood agar — alpha/beta/gamma hemolysis | — |
| **Fungi** | **Sabouraud's dextrose agar** (SDA) | pH 5.6, cycloheximide selective |
| **Listeria** | Cold enrichment (4°C), PALCAM medium | Tumbling motility |
| **Brucella** | Castaneda's bottle; also BACT/Alert, blood culture | Slow grower |
| **Campylobacter** | CCDA (Charcoal Cefoperazone Desoxycholate); 42°C microaerophilic | — |

### Sterilization and Disinfection

#### Definitions
- **Sterilization**: Complete destruction of ALL microorganisms including spores
- **Disinfection**: Destruction of pathogens (not necessarily all organisms/spores)
- **Antiseptic**: Applied to living tissue
- **Germicide**: Kills all organisms
- **Bacteriostatic**: Inhibits growth (doesn't kill)
- **Bactericidal**: Kills bacteria

#### Methods of Sterilization

| Method | Temperature/Conditions | Items | Notes |
|--------|----------------------|-------|-------|
| **Autoclaving (moist heat)** | **121°C, 15 psi, 15-20 min** | Surgical instruments, media, rubber, fabrics | Most reliable — kills spores |
| **Dry Heat (Hot air oven)** | **160°C/1 hr or 170°C/30 min or 180°C/7.5 min** | Glassware, oils, powders, syringes (metal) | Cannot use on rubber/plastics |
| **Pasteurization** | **63°C/30 min (Holder) or 72°C/15 sec (HTST)** | Milk, dairy | NOT sterilization |
| **Boiling** | 100°C/10-30 min | Emergency instrument sterilization | Kills vegetative bacteria, not spores |
| **Incineration** | Red heat/flaming | Bacteriological loops, contaminated material | — |
| **Ethylene oxide gas** | 55°C, humid conditions | Heat-labile plastics, catheters, cardiac catheters, **endoscopes** | Leaves toxic residue — aerate after |
| **Gamma radiation** | Cobalt-60 source | Pre-packaged disposables, syringes, sutures | Commercial sterilization |
| **UV radiation** | 260 nm | Air, surfaces (OT, laminar flow) | No penetration — surface only |
| **Filtration** | Seitz filter (asbestos), membrane filter (0.22 μm), Berkefeld | Heat-labile liquids, vaccines, toxins, antibiotics | Does NOT remove viruses/toxins (EXCEPT Chamberland |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Autoclaving — method of choice for most materials; Ethylene oxide — for heat-labile items (endoscopes, plastic catheters); UV — no penetration, only surface/air

#### Disinfectants

| Agent | Mechanism | Uses | Notes |
|-------|-----------|------|-------|
| **Phenol (Carbolic acid)** | Protein denaturation | Reference standard | Rideal-Walker coefficient |
| **Chlorine/Hypochlorite** | Oxidizing agent | Water, surfaces, skin (wounds) | C. difficile requires 10% bleach |
| **Glutaraldehyde (2%)** | Cross-links proteins | **Endoscopes** (high-level disinfection), heat-labile equipment | Alkaline: sterilant at 4 hrs; disinfectant at 20 min |
| **Formaldehyde** | Cross-links proteins | Embalming, fumigation, formalin preservation | Carcinogenic |
| **Alcohol (70%)** | Protein denaturation, lipid dissolution | Skin antisepsis, thermometers | NOT sporicidal |
| **Povidone iodine** | Halogenation | Skin antisepsis, surgical site | — |
| **Hydrogen peroxide (3%)** | Oxidizing agent | Wound cleaning, endoscopes | Gas plasma sterilization (vaporized H2O2) |
| **Quaternary ammonium compounds** | Membrane disruption | Surfaces, utensils | NOT sporicidal, inactivated by organic matter |

## 2. IMMUNOLOGY

### Innate Immunity

#### Complement System

**Three Pathways of Activation**:

| Pathway | Activators | Initial Step |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Classical** | Antigen-antibody complexes (IgG, IgM) | C1q binds Fc region |
| **Lectin (MBL)** | Mannose-binding lectin on pathogen surfaces (mannose residues) | MBL-MASP complexes cleave C4, C2 |
| **Alternative** | Microbial surfaces (LPS, teichoic acid, fungal walls) — spontaneous C3 hydrolysis | C3b binds surface |

**All pathways converge at C3**:

```
Classical: C1→C4→C2→C3 convertase (C4b2a)
Lectin: MBL-MASP→C4→C2→C3 convertase (C4b2a)
Alternative: C3→Factor B→Factor D→C3 convertase (C3bBb)
```

**C3 Convertases**:
- **Classical/Lectin**: **C4b2a**
- **Alternative**: **C3bBb** (stabilized by properdin)

**C5 Convertases**:
- **Classical/Lectin**: C4b2a**3b**
- **Alternative**: C3bBb**3b**

**Terminal Complement (C5b-9) = MAC (Membrane Attack Complex)**:
- C5b + C6 + C7 + C8 + poly-C9 → pore formation → osmotic lysis

**Complement Functions**:
- **C3b**: Opsonization (most important)
- **C3a, C4a, C5a**: Anaphylatoxins (↑ vascular permeability, mast cell degranulation, chemotaxis)
- **C5a**: Most potent chemotaxin for neutrophils
- **C1q**: Activates classical pathway; binds apoptotic cells

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Deficiency of C5-C9 (MAC components) → recurrent Neisseria infections (meningitis, gonorrhea); C3 deficiency → most severe (recurrent pyogenic infections, SLE-like); C1q deficiency → SLE-like syndrome

**Complement Regulatory Proteins**:
- **DAF (CD55)**: Accelerates decay of C3 convertases
- **CD59 (Protectin)**: Inhibits MAC on host cells
- **C1-INH (C1 inhibitor)**: Inhibits C1r, C1s, MASP — deficiency → **Hereditary Angioedema** (HAE)
- **Factor H**: Inhibits alternative pathway
- **Factor I**: Cleaves C3b

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: PNH (Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria) — lack of GPI-anchored proteins (CD55, CD59) → uncontrolled MAC on RBCs → intravascular hemolysis; treatment: Eculizumab (anti-C5 antibody)

### Adaptive Immunity

#### T Cell Types and Functions

| T Cell Type | CD Markers | Cytokines | Function |
|------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Th1** | CD4+, CD45RO+ | **IFN-γ, TNF-β, IL-2** | Activates macrophages, CTLs; cell-mediated immunity; fights intracellular pathogens |
| **Th2** | CD4+, CD45RO+ | **IL-4, IL-5, IL-13** | B cell activation (IgG, IgE), eosinophil activation; fights helminths, allergies |
| **Th17** | CD4+ | **IL-17, IL-22** | Neutrophil recruitment; fights extracellular bacteria/fungi at mucosal surfaces |
| **Treg (Regulatory T)** | CD4+, CD25+, **FOXP3+** | **TGF-β, IL-10, IL-35** | Suppress immune responses; peripheral tolerance; deficiency → autoimmunity |
| **CTL (Cytotoxic T)** | **CD8+** | IFN-γ, perforin, granzymes | Kill virus-infected/tumor cells via MHC I presentation |
| **Th9** | CD4+ | IL-9 | Mast cell growth, allergy |
| **Tfh (follicular helper)** | CD4+, CXCR5+, PD-1+, ICOS+ | IL-21 | Germinal center formation, B cell help, affinity maturation |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: FOXP3 mutation → IPEX syndrome (Immune dysregulation, Polyendocrinopathy, Enteropathy, X-linked) = autoimmunity; Treg = CD4+CD25+FOXP3+

**MHC Restriction**:
- **CD4+ T cells** recognize antigen on **MHC II** (on APCs: dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells)
- **CD8+ T cells** recognize antigen on **MHC I** (on ALL nucleated cells)

#### B Cells and Antibodies

**B cell activation**: Antigen + CD4+ Th2 cell (CD40L-CD40, IL-4) → B cell activation → plasma cells → antibodies

**Immunoglobulin Classes**:

| Antibody | Structure | Functions | Notes |
|----------|----------|----------|-------|
| **IgM** | Pentamer (10 binding sites) | **First antibody produced** in primary response; best complement activator (classical); best agglutinator; on mature naïve B cells (monomer) | Cannot cross placenta; **Mac antibody = primary response marker** |
| **IgG** | Monomer (4 subclasses) | **Most abundant in serum**; only Ab that **crosses placenta** (maternal protection); opsonization (Fc receptor on phagocytes); neutralization; **secondary response** | IgG4: associated with IgG4-related disease |
| **IgA** | Dimer in secretions (secretory IgA = dimer + J chain + secretory component); monomer in serum | **Mucosal immunity** (breast milk, tears, saliva, respiratory/GI/GU secretions); prevents mucosal attachment | Most produced daily; secretory component prevents proteolysis |
| **IgE** | Monomer | **Allergy (Type I hypersensitivity)**: binds FcεRI on mast cells/basophils; **Parasitic infection (helminth)** defense; eosinophil activation | Lowest serum concentration; elevated in atopy, helminths |
| **IgD** | Monomer | **B cell surface receptor** (mature naïve B cells alongside IgM); function unclear; may activate basophils | Minimal serum concentration |

**Antibody Functions**:
- **Opsonization**: IgG (Fc receptor on phagocytes), C3b
- **ADCC**: NK cells, eosinophils (Fc receptors)
- **Neutralization**: Block toxin/viral binding
- **Complement activation**: IgM > IgG1/3 (classical pathway)
- **Neonatal protection**: IgG (placenta), IgA (breast milk)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: IgM — first antibody, pentamer, best complement activator, does NOT cross placenta; IgG — only Ab crossing placenta; IgA — secretory/mucosal; IgE — allergy and parasites

### Hypersensitivity Reactions

| Type | Name | Mechanism | Mediators | Examples | Timeline |
|------|------|-----------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Type I** | Immediate/Anaphylactic | IgE + mast cell/basophil degranulation | Histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, tryptase | Anaphylaxis, asthma (atopic), allergic rhinitis, urticaria, food allergy, **bee sting anaphylaxis** | Seconds-minutes |
| **Type II** | Cytotoxic/Antibody-mediated | IgG or IgM + complement or ADCC → cell destruction | Complement, ADCC | ABO hemolytic transfusion reaction, **Rh hemolytic disease**, autoimmune hemolytic anemia, Goodpasture's (anti-GBM), **Myasthenia gravis** (anti-AChR blocks), **Graves' disease** (anti-TSH-R stimulates), **ITP**, drug-induced hemolysis | Hours |
| **Type III** | Immune complex-mediated | Antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues → complement activation → neutrophil recruitment | Complement, PMNs | **SLE** (DNA-anti-DNA), **serum sickness**, post-streptococcal GN (anti-streptolysin), **Arthus reaction** (local), **farmer's lung** (hypersensitivity pneumonitis) | Hours-days (6-12 hrs for Arthus) |
| **Type IV** | Delayed-type/Cell-mediated | Sensitized T cells (Th1 + CD8 CTL) — NO antibody | Cytokines, CD8 cytotoxicity | **Tuberculin test (Mantoux)**, contact dermatitis (poison ivy, nickel), **graft rejection (acute)**, **type 1 DM**, **MS**, Hashimoto's, granuloma formation | 24-72 hours |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Type I — IgE; Type II — IgG/IgM + cell destruction; Type III — immune complexes + complement; Type IV — T cells, delayed, NO antibody, tuberculin/PPD is classic example

**Special Hypersensitivity Points**:
- **Arthus reaction**: Local type III — intradermal injection of antigen in sensitized individual → local edema, necrosis (6-12 hrs)
- **Serum sickness**: Systemic type III — 7-21 days after antigen exposure (horse serum, drugs) → fever, rash, arthralgias, lymphadenopathy
- **Anaphylaxis treatment**: Epinephrine (1:1000, IM deltoid) is drug of choice — first-line
- **Mast cell tryptase**: Biomarker for anaphylaxis (peaks 1-2 hrs post-reaction)

### Immunodeficiency Disorders

| Disorder | Defect | Manifestation | Diagnosis |
|---------|--------|--------------|----------|
| **X-linked agammaglobulinemia (Bruton's XLA)** | **BTK (Bruton's Tyrosine Kinase) mutation** → no B cell maturation past pre-B cell stage | Recurrent pyogenic infections (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae) after 6 months (maternal IgG wanes); no tonsils/lymph nodes; **Echovirus encephalitis**; Giardia infections | ↓ All Ig classes; absent B cells; normal T cells |
| **Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)** | B cell maturation failure (hypogammaglobulinemia) | Similar to Bruton's but presents in 2nd-3rd decade; both sexes; **↑ autoimmune disease**, malabsorption, Giardia | ↓ IgG, IgA, IgM; B cells present but no plasma cells |
| **Selective IgA Deficiency** | Most common primary immunodeficiency | Usually asymptomatic; recurrent sinopulmonary infections; **anaphylaxis to blood transfusion (anti-IgA IgG in recipient)** | ↓ IgA; normal other Ig |
| **DiGeorge Syndrome** | **22q11 deletion (TBX1 gene)** → thymic aplasia → T cell deficiency | CATCH-22: **C**ardiac defects, **A**bnormal facies, **T**hymic aplasia, **C**left palate, **H**ypocalcemia (parathyroid aplasia); T cell defects → fungal/viral/intracellular infections | ↓ T cells; normal/↑ B cells (no T cell help) |
| **SCID (Severe Combined Immunodeficiency)** | Multiple causes — ADA (adenosine deaminase) deficiency (most common AR), **JAK3**, common gamma chain, RAG1/2 | Severe infections within months; no B/T cells; **Omenn syndrome** (RAG1/2 missense — erythroderma, hepatosplenomegaly); fatal without BMT | Absent T and B cells |
| **Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome** | **WASp gene (Xp)** mutation | Triad: **Thrombocytopenia + Eczema + Immunodeficiency**; ↓ IgM, ↑ IgA/IgE; male | Abnormal lymphocytes, small platelets |
| **Hyper-IgM Syndrome** | **CD40L (CD154) deficiency** (X-linked) or AID/UNG | ↑ IgM, ↓ IgG/IgA/IgE (no class switching); Pneumocystis, Cryptosporidium, Toxoplasma infections | Elevated IgM; no somatic hypermutation |
| **Chediak-Higashi** | **LYST gene mutation** → defective phagosome-lysosome fusion | Recurrent pyogenic infections, **partial albinism**, neuropathy, giant lysosomal granules; **hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis** | Giant granules in neutrophils on blood smear |
| **Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD)** | **NADPH oxidase deficiency** (most common: gp91phox, X-linked) → no respiratory burst → no O2 radicals | Recurrent catalase-positive organism infections (**S. aureus, Klebsiella, Aspergillus, Candida, Nocardia, Pseudomonas**); granuloma formation; **positive DHR test** | DHR (dihydrorhodamine) flow cytometry; negative nitroblue tetrazolium (NBT) test |
| **Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (LAD)** | **CD18 (beta-2 integrin) deficiency** → neutrophils cannot exit blood | Recurrent bacterial infections without pus; **delayed umbilical cord separation**; periodontitis; neutrophilia | ↑↑ WBC (neutrophils stuck in blood); absent CD11/18 on flow |
| **Hereditary Angioedema (HAE)** | **C1-INH deficiency** (autosomal dominant) | Recurrent angioedema (non-pitting, non-pruritic) — face, extremities, intestines, **larynx (life-threatening)**; NO urticaria (not IgE-mediated) | ↓ C1-INH, ↓ C4 (best screening), normal C1q |

### Vaccines

#### Live Attenuated vs Killed Vaccines

| Feature | Live Attenuated | Killed (Inactivated) |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Immune response** | Both humoral + cellular (strong, long-lasting) | Mainly humoral (weaker, shorter) |
| **Boosters** | Usually single dose | Multiple doses usually needed |
| **Reversion** | Possible (rarely) | Impossible |
| **Contraindication** | Immunocompromised, pregnancy | Safer in immunocompromised/pregnancy |
| **Storage** | Cold chain essential | Less cold-chain sensitive |
| **Route** | Oral, SC, intranasal | IM, SC |

**Live Attenuated Vaccines**: BCG, OPV (Sabin polio), MMR (Measles-Mumps-Rubella), Varicella (VZV), Yellow fever, Typhoid (Ty21a oral), Rotavirus, Influenza (LAIV — intranasal), Dengue (CYD-TDV)

**Killed/Inactivated Vaccines**: IPV (Salk polio), Influenza (injected), Hepatitis A, Rabies (post-exposure), Typhoid (Vi polysaccharide), Cholera (OCV), Pertussis (whole-cell in DPT), Japanese encephalitis, Meningococcal (polysaccharide)

**Toxoid Vaccines**: Diphtheria (DT), Tetanus — inactivated exotoxins; produce antitoxin antibodies

**Subunit/Recombinant**: Hepatitis B (HBsAg), HPV (VLP — virus-like particles), Acellular pertussis (aP)

**Adjuvants**: Aluminum salts (alum — most common), Freund's complete/incomplete adjuvant (experimental), MF59, AS04 (MPL + alum)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: BCG — live attenuated M. bovis; given at birth; NOT given in HIV-positive infants (active disease risk); protects against miliary TB and TB meningitis; OPV — can cause vaccine-associated paralytic polio (VAPP) — 1/750,000 first dose recipients

## 3. KEY ORGANISMS AND ASSOCIATIONS

### Gram-Positive Cocci

#### Staphylococcus aureus
**Key Properties**: Coagulase-positive (distinguishes from CoNS), catalase-positive, beta-hemolytic, golden-yellow pigment, **Protein A** (binds Fc of IgG → complement evasion)

**Toxins**:

| Toxin | Mechanism | Disease |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **TSST-1 (Toxic Shock Syndrome Toxin-1)** | **Superantigen** — binds MHC II + TCR Vβ → massive T cell activation → cytokine storm | Toxic shock syndrome (fever, rash, hypotension, multiorgan) |
| **Exfoliatin (A & B)** | Serine protease — cleaves **desmoglein 1** (desmosome in epidermis) | **SSSS** (Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome) — Ritter's disease in neonates; bullous impetigo |
| **Alpha toxin (alpha-hemolysin)** | Pore-forming toxin | Cell lysis |
| **Panton-Valentine Leukocidin (PVL)** | Pore-forming — kills neutrophils/macrophages | Severe necrotizing pneumonia, furunculosis (community-MRSA) |
| **Enterotoxin (A-G)** | **Superantigen** — heat-stable, protease-resistant | Food poisoning (**PREFORMED toxin** — onset 1-6 hrs after ingestion, N/V/diarrhea, self-limiting) |
| **Coagulase** | Fibrin clot around bacteria → evade phagocytosis | Virulence factor |

**MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant S. aureus)**:
- **mecA gene** → altered PBP2a (low affinity for beta-lactams)
- Hospital-MRSA (HA-MRSA): multi-drug resistant
- Community-MRSA (CA-MRSA): often PVL+, skin/soft tissue, necrotizing pneumonia
- Treatment: **Vancomycin** (IV for serious), **Linezolid, Daptomycin, Tedizolid, Ceftaroline**; oral: TMP-SMX, doxycycline (for CA-MRSA skin)
- Decolonization: **Intranasal mupirocin** + chlorhexidine bathing

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: SSSS — exfoliatin cleaves desmoglein-1 (upper epidermis, granular layer); vs TEN (Stevens-Johnson — full thickness epidermal necrosis, immune-mediated); Nikolsky sign positive in both

#### Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus)
**Key Properties**: Beta-hemolytic, **Bacitracin sensitive** (PYR positive), Lancefield Group A

**Virulence Factors**:

| Factor | Function |
|--------|---------|
| **M protein** (most important) | Antiphagocytic; **antigenic — basis of rheumatic fever** (molecular mimicry with cardiac antigens); >80 serotypes |
| **Streptolysin O (SLO)** | Oxygen-labile hemolysin; antigen → **ASO titer** (serum marker of recent strep infection) |
| **Streptolysin S (SLS)** | Oxygen-stable; responsible for beta-hemolysis on blood agar plates |
| **Streptokinase (Fibrinolysin)** | Activates plasminogen → plasmin → dissolves fibrin; spreads infection |
| **Hyaluronidase (spreading factor)** | Breaks down hyaluronic acid in tissue |
| **DNase (Streptodornase)** | Degrades DNA in pus (reduces viscosity) |
| **Pyrogenic exotoxin A, B, C (SPE)** | Superantigens → **Scarlet fever** rash, streptococcal TSS |

**Diseases**:
- **Pharyngitis** (tonsillitis): Treat to prevent rheumatic fever (Penicillin V 10 days)
- **Impetigo** (skin): Can lead to post-streptococcal GN (NOT rheumatic fever)
- **Scarlet fever**: Sandpaper rash (blanching, starts neck/chest), strawberry tongue, pastia's lines; caused by SPE
- **Rheumatic fever**: Non-suppurative complication of pharyngitis (NOT skin infection)
  - **Jones Criteria**: Major (CARDIAC: Carditis, Arthritis (migratory polyarthritis), Rheum's chorea, Erythema marginatum, Subcutaneous nodules) + Minor + evidence of GAS (ASO titer +)
- **Post-streptococcal GN**: After pharyngitis OR impetigo (nephritogenic strains, Type M12, M49)
- **Necrotizing fasciitis** (flesh-eating bacteria)
- **Streptococcal TSS**

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Rheumatic fever follows THROAT (not skin) infection; post-streptococcal GN follows BOTH throat AND skin; ASO titer elevated in both but especially rheumatic fever

#### Streptococcus pneumoniae
**Key Properties**: Alpha-hemolytic (viridans on blood agar), **Optochin sensitive** (distinguishes from viridans strep — optochin resistant), **Bile soluble**, Lancefield NOT typeable (no Lancefield antigen), **Quellung reaction** positive (capsular swelling with specific antisera)

**Virulence**: **Polysaccharide capsule** (most important — antiphagocytic, IgG + complement needed for opsonization)

**Diseases**: Community-acquired pneumonia (most common bacterial cause), meningitis (most common in adults), otitis media (children), sinusitis, bacteremia

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Pneumococcal meningitis — classic CSF: ↑ WBC (PMN), ↑ protein, ↓ glucose; Gram stain: Gram+ lancet-shaped diplococci; Treatment: Ceftriaxone + Dexamethasone (reduces hearing loss)

**Vaccine**: PCV13 (conjugate — infants), PPSV23 (polysaccharide — adults ≥65, asplenic)

### Gram-Negative Cocci

#### Neisseria meningitidis
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative diplococci (kidney-bean), oxidase-positive, **ferments glucose AND maltose** (vs gonorrhoeae — glucose only), **polysaccharide capsule** (serogroups A, B, C, W135, X, Y)

**Serogroups**:
- **A**: Africa (meningitis belt, epidemic)
- **B**: Sporadic disease in developed world; **difficult to vaccinate** (capsule mimics human neural tissue)
- **C**: Most vaccine preventable disease in UK
- **W135, Y, X**: Emerging

**Waterhouse-Friderichsen Syndrome**: Fulminant meningococcemia → bilateral adrenal hemorrhage → adrenal insufficiency → death; DIC, purpuric/petechial rash
- Treatment: IV Penicillin G or Ceftriaxone IMMEDIATELY; Chemoprophylaxis: **Rifampicin** (2 days) or Ciprofloxacin (single dose) for close contacts

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Chemoprophylaxis for meningococcal contacts = Rifampicin 600 mg BD × 2 days (or ciprofloxacin 500 mg single dose or ceftriaxone 250 mg IM single dose)

#### Neisseria gonorrhoeae
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative diplococci, oxidase+, **ferments glucose ONLY** (not maltose), **NO capsule** (no polysaccharide), **intracellular diplococci** in PMNs on Gram stain (diagnostic)

**Culture**: **Thayer-Martin medium** (chocolate agar + VCN — Vancomycin, Colistin, Nystatin — to suppress other flora)

**Diseases**: Gonorrhea (urethritis, cervicitis, PID), **Ophthalmia neonatorum** (neonatal conjunctivitis — born through infected birth canal), **DGI (Disseminated Gonococcal Infection)** — septic arthritis + tenosynovitis + dermatitis

**Treatment**: **Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose** (dual therapy with doxycycline or azithromycin for concurrent chlamydia in some guidelines); resistance to fluoroquinolones now widespread

### Gram-Positive Bacilli

#### Corynebacterium diphtheriae
**Key Properties**: Gram-positive pleomorphic bacilli (Chinese letter/V/L shapes), non-motile, non-spore forming, **metachromatic granules** (Babes-Ernst granules, Albert stain), **Club-shaped bacilli**

**Toxin**: **Diphtheria toxin** (DT) — encoded by **tox gene on bacteriophage (beta phage)**
- **Mechanism**: **ADP-ribosylation of EF-2 (Elongation Factor 2)** → inhibits protein synthesis → cell death
- Fragment B: Binding, entry
- Fragment A: ADP-ribosylation

**Culture**:
- **Loeffler's coagulated serum**: Rapid growth (6-8 hrs) — metachromatic granules best seen
- **Potassium tellurite agar (Hoyle's medium)**: Black colonies (gray-black tellurite reduction)

**Elek's Test (Toxigenicity test)**: Immunodiffusion — filter paper soaked with antitoxin → precipitation line (positive = toxin producing)

**Diseases**:
- **Pharyngeal diphtheria**: Pseudomembrane (gray-white, bleeds on removal), bull neck (cervical lymphadenopathy), "barking" cough
- **Complications**: Myocarditis, **neuropathy** (palatine, oculomotor, peripheral), airway obstruction
- **Cutaneous diphtheria**: Skin ulcers (tropics)

**Treatment**: **Diphtheria antitoxin** (DAT — most important) + **Penicillin/Erythromycin**

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Diphtheria toxin = ADP-ribosylation of EF-2 (same as Pseudomonas exotoxin A); Gray pseudomembrane; Elek's test = toxigenicity; Tox gene on bacteriophage

#### Clostridium tetani
**Key Properties**: Gram-positive, terminal **round spore** ("drumstick" appearance), strictly anaerobic, motile

**Toxin**: **Tetanospasmin** (second most potent toxin after botulinum)
- **Mechanism**: Zinc metalloprotease — cleaves **VAMP/synaptobrevin** → inhibits release of **GABA and glycine** (inhibitory neurotransmitters) from Renshaw cells in spinal cord → **spastic paralysis**
- Retrograde axonal transport to CNS

**Diseases**:
- **Generalized tetanus**: Trismus (lockjaw), risus sardonicus (grimace), opisthotonus, convulsions, respiratory failure
- **Neonatal tetanus** (umbilicus portal of entry — unclean cord cutting)
- **Localized tetanus**: Muscle rigidity near wound

**Treatment**: Wound debridement, **TIG (Human Tetanus Immunoglobulin)** — neutralizes unbound toxin, IV Metronidazole (or Penicillin), diazepam (muscle relaxation), airway management

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Tetanus = SPASTIC (inhibitory NT blocked); Botulism = FLACCID (excitatory NT blocked); Both cleave SNARE proteins

#### Clostridium botulinum
**Key Properties**: Gram-positive, subterminal spore, strictly anaerobic, produces most potent biological toxin

**Toxin**: **Botulinum toxin** (7 types: A-G)
- **Mechanism**: Zinc metalloprotease — cleaves **SNARE complex proteins** (SNAP-25 or VAMP) → inhibits ACh release at **neuromuscular junction** → **flaccid paralysis**
- Type A, B, E most common in human disease

**Diseases**:
- **Food-borne botulism**: Preformed toxin in canned/preserved food → descending flaccid paralysis, diplopia, dysphagia, dysarthria; NO fever; NO sensory symptoms; bowel constipation
- **Infant botulism (Floppy baby syndrome)**: Ingested spores (honey!) → germinate in gut → toxin produced in vivo; **Most common form**; constipation → hypotonia → cranial nerve palsies
- **Wound botulism**: Contaminated wounds (IV drug users)

**Treatment**: **Trivalent antitoxin (ABE)** for food-borne; Supportive; **Human botulism immune globulin (BIG-IV) for infantile**; Penicillin/Metronidazole for wound

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Botulism floppy baby — from honey ingestion (spores); flaccid descending paralysis; ACh release blocked at NMJ; treat with BIG-IV (not antitoxin for infants)

#### Bacillus anthracis
**Key Properties**: Gram-positive, large bacilli, **square-ended**, **non-hemolytic**, non-motile, **spore-forming**, **Medusa head/comma colonies**, **India ink shows capsule** (polypeptide capsule = poly-D-glutamic acid)

**Anthrax Toxin Components**:
- **PA (Protective Antigen)** + **LF (Lethal Factor)** = **Lethal Toxin**: LF = zinc metalloprotease → cleaves MAPKK → cell death, cytokine dysregulation
- **PA + EF (Edema Factor)** = **Edema Toxin**: EF = adenylyl cyclase (calmodulin-dependent) → ↑ cAMP → edema

**Diseases**:
- **Cutaneous anthrax** (95%): Black eschar (malignant pustule) → painless
- **Pulmonary anthrax (Woolsorter's disease)**: Inhalation of spores → biphasic — initial flu-like → sudden deterioration → hemorrhagic mediastinitis, "hammered copper" chest X-ray (widened mediastinum)
- **GI anthrax**: Most severe; from eating infected meat

**Treatment**: **Ciprofloxacin** (DOC for bioterrorism), Doxycycline, Penicillin (sensitive strains)
Post-exposure prophylaxis: Ciprofloxacin 60 days + anthrax vaccine

#### Listeria monocytogenes
**Key Properties**: Gram-positive, short bacilli, **beta-hemolytic**, catalase-positive, oxidase-negative, **CAMP test positive** (like S. agalactiae), **tumbling motility** (4°C and 20-25°C — **cold enrichment** in pathology lab)

**Key Features**: **Actin rocket tails** (intracellular motility via ActA — polymerizes actin), facultative intracellular, survives and replicates in macrophages

**Diseases**:
- **Neonatal meningitis** (early onset: transplacental; late onset: perinatal)
- **Meningitis in immunocompromised adults, elderly, alcoholics**
- **Listeriosis in pregnancy**: Granulomatosis infantiseptica (disseminated granulomas in neonate)
- Food-borne: Contaminated deli meats, soft cheeses, raw produce

**Treatment**: **Ampicillin** (+ aminoglycoside for synergy in severe cases); Note: Listeria is **naturally resistant to cephalosporins** — ceftriaxone for meningitis must cover Listeria in neonates/elderly/pregnant → add ampicillin

#### Actinomyces israelii
**Key Properties**: Gram-positive, branching filamentous bacilli (NOT fungi), anaerobic/microaerophilic, non-acid fast, forms **sulfur granules** (yellow granules = filamentous bacteria aggregates)

**Diseases**: Actinomycosis — chronic suppurative granulomatous disease; **cervicofacial** (lumpy jaw — most common), thoracic, abdominal, pelvic (associated with IUDs)

**Treatment**: **Penicillin G** (long course, 6-12 months), followed by amoxicillin; drainage of abscesses

### Gram-Negative Bacilli

#### Escherichia coli — Pathotypes

| Pathotype | Disease | Mechanism |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **EPEC** (Enteropathogenic) | Watery diarrhea (infants) | A/E lesions (attaching/effacing); no toxin |
| **ETEC** | Traveler's diarrhea (Montezuma's revenge) | **Heat-labile (LT) toxin** → ↑ cAMP (like cholera); **Heat-stable (ST) toxin** → ↑ cGMP |
| **EIEC** | Dysentery-like | Invades colon like Shigella; Shiga-like |
| **EHEC (O157:H7)** | **Bloody diarrhea → HUS** | **Shiga toxin (Stx1/2)** → cleaves 28S rRNA → ↓ protein synthesis in endothelium → **HUS (Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome)**: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia + thrombocytopenia + acute renal failure |
| **EAEC** | Persistent diarrhea (children, HIV) | Aggregative adherence |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: EHEC O157:H7 → HUS → **DO NOT use antibiotics** (may precipitate HUS by lysing bacteria releasing more toxin); sorbitol NON-fermenter (key lab ID); no antibiotics or antimotility agents

#### Salmonella typhi (Typhoid Fever)
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, facultative intracellular, **oxidase-negative**, H2S positive, non-lactose fermenter, **O, H, and Vi antigens**

**Antigens**:
- **O antigen**: Somatic LPS (heat-stable)
- **H antigen**: Flagellar (heat-labile)
- **Vi antigen**: **Capsular polysaccharide** — virulence (anti-phagocytic); target of Vi polysaccharide vaccine

**Widal Test**: Agglutination test for H and O antibodies — positive after 2nd week; **O ≥1:160, H ≥1:160** suggestive; cross-reactions common (low specificity)

**Clinical**: Rose spots (abdominal trunk, Week 2), relative bradycardia (Faget's sign — pulse-temperature dissociation), splenomegaly, leukopenia with lymphocytosis

**Culture**: Blood (1st week), Urine + Stool (2nd-3rd week), Bone marrow (most sensitive, any time)

**Treatment**: **Ceftriaxone** (IV, severe), **Azithromycin** (oral, uncomplicated), Fluoroquinolones (resistance increasing), Chloramphenicol (historical)

#### Shigella
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, **non-motile** (unique among Enterobacteriaceae for causing diarrhea), non-H2S, **fermenter** vs Salmonella, **NEVER lactose fermenter**, most virulent: S. dysenteriae (produces Shiga toxin)

**Shiga Toxin**: A-B toxin — **cleaves 28S rRNA** (same as EHEC toxin) → ↓ protein synthesis → cell death in colonocytes; also has endotoxin activity

**Diseases**: Bacillary dysentery — small volume bloody/mucous stools (shigellosis), tenesmus, fever; **HUS** possible (especially S. dysenteriae)

**Most infective dose**: Shigella = **10-200 organisms** (lowest infective dose of enteric pathogens) → fecal-oral, very contagious

**Treatment**: Azithromycin or Fluoroquinolones (mild-moderate); Ceftriaxone (severe); no antimotility agents

#### Vibrio cholerae
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, comma/curved shaped (vibrio), oxidase-positive, **TCBS yellow colonies** (sucrose fermenter; V. parahaemolyticus = blue-green on TCBS), **El Tor biotype** (current pandemic strain), **O1 and O139** serogroups cause cholera, O1 = Ogawa/Inaba/Hikojima serotypes

**Cholera Toxin**:
- **Mechanism**: **ADP-ribosylation of Gs alpha subunit** → locks Gs in active state → **permanent activation of adenylyl cyclase** → ↑↑↑ cAMP → **CFTR Cl⁻ channel open** → Cl⁻/Na⁺/water secretion → **RICE WATER STOOLS** (massive isotonic secretory diarrhea)
- Similar mechanism to: **LT toxin** of ETEC, Pertussis toxin (Gs alpha also ADP-ribosylated — but inhibitory Gi blocked for pertussis)

**Clinical**: Abrupt watery diarrhea (rice-water), vomiting, rapid dehydration (10-20 L/day), **painless**, NO fever, **Washer woman's hands** (skin wrinkling), hypovolemic shock

**Treatment**: **ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution)** — cornerstone; Antibiotics shorten duration: **Doxycycline** (single dose, adults) or Azithromycin; IV Ringer's lactate for severe

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Cholera toxin = ADP-ribosylation of Gs-alpha (permanent activation) → ↑↑ cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion; same as ETEC LT toxin but ETEC reversible (heat-labile)

#### Helicobacter pylori
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, curved/spiral bacilli, microaerophilic, **urease-positive** (most important test), flagellated, CagA and VacA virulence factors

**Diagnosis**:
- **Non-invasive**: **13C or 14C Urea Breath Test** (gold standard for active infection), **Stool antigen test**, Serology (cannot distinguish active from past)
- **Invasive (endoscopy biopsy)**: **CLO test (rapid urease test)** — most rapid; Histology; Culture (most specific, but difficult)

**Associations**: Peptic ulcer disease (DU 95%, GU 70%), **MALT lymphoma** (eradication can cure low-grade), **gastric adenocarcinoma** (Class I carcinogen by WHO)

**Treatment (Triple/Quadruple therapy)**:
- **Standard triple**: PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (7-14 days)
- **Bismuth quadruple**: Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline + PPI (if clarithromycin resistant area)
- **Concomitant/Sequential** therapies used in high resistance areas

#### Klebsiella pneumoniae
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, lactose fermenter, **mucoid colonies** (prominent capsule), urease positive, non-motile

**Diseases**: Pneumonia in alcoholics/immunocompromised — **"currant jelly sputum"** (necrotic hemorrhage), lobar pneumonia with bulging fissure; UTI; liver abscess

#### Pseudomonas aeruginosa
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, non-fermenter, oxidase-positive, **grape/fruity odor** (pyocyanin), **blue-green pigment (pyocyanin + pyoverdin)**, motile, strict aerobe

**Virulence**: Multiple — **exotoxin A** (ADP-ribosylates EF-2, same as diphtheria), **pyocyanin** (generates free radicals, inhibits cilia), **biofilm** formation, alginate (mucoid strains in CF), **phospholipase C, proteases**

**Diseases**: Pneumonia (CF patients, ventilator-associated), **ecthyma gangrenosum** (skin necrosis with black eschar — pathognomonic in neutropenic patients), burn wound infections, UTI, hot tub folliculitis, otitis externa (swimmer's ear), endophthalmitis (penetrating eye trauma), osteomyelitis (IV drug users/diabetics — involving unusual sites like spine)

**Treatment**: **Antipseudomonal beta-lactams** (piperacillin-tazobactam, ceftazidime, cefepime, meropenem) ± aminoglycoside (gentamicin/tobramycin/amikacin); Ciprofloxacin (most active fluoroquinolone)

#### Brucella
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, small coccobacilli, **facultative intracellular**, slow-growing, **Castaneda's biphasic blood culture**, **aerosol precautions**

**Species**: B. melitensis (goats/sheep — most virulent), B. abortus (cattle), B. suis (pigs), B. canis (dogs)

**Diseases**: **Brucellosis = Undulant fever = Malta fever** (B. melitensis); fever with daily undulations, night sweats, arthralgias, hepatosplenomegaly, orchitis in men

**Treatment**: **Doxycycline + Rifampicin** (6 weeks); OR Doxycycline + Streptomycin (more effective for severe/neurobrucellosis)

#### Yersinia pestis (Plague)
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative, coccobacilli, **bipolar staining ("safety pin" appearance)** with Wayson/Giemsa stain, **cold-blooded insects (fleas)** vector, transmitted by **Xenopsylla cheopis** (rat flea)

**Clinical Forms**:
- **Bubonic plague**: Inguinal/axillary **bubo** (tender lymphadenopathy), bacteremia, sepsis; rat→flea→human
- **Septicemic plague**: Primary bacteremia; DIC, ecchymoses, DIC (Black Death)
- **Pneumonic plague**: Most dangerous; person-to-person droplet transmission; 100% fatal untreated

**Treatment**: **Streptomycin** (DOC), Gentamicin, Doxycycline, Ciprofloxacin

#### Bordetella pertussis (Whooping Cough)
**Key Properties**: Gram-negative coccobacilli, aerobic, **filamentous hemagglutinin (FHA)**, **pertussis toxin** (ADP-ribosylates Gi alpha → ↑ cAMP), **AC toxin** (adenylate cyclase toxin — ↑ cAMP in phagocytes → impairs killing)

**Pertussis Toxin**: ADP-ribosylates **Gi alpha subunit** → Gi locked in inactive state → loss of downregulation of adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP → **lymphocytosis** (inhibits lymphocyte recirculation), histamine sensitization

**Stages**:
1. **Catarrhal stage** (1-2 weeks): Coryza, mild cough — most infectious
2. **Paroxysmal stage** (2-4 weeks): **Paroxysmal whooping cough** (inspiratory whoop), **post-tussive vomiting**, **apnea** in infants; **lymphocytosis** (distinctive lab finding)
3. **Convalescent stage** (weeks-months): Gradual resolution

**Culture**: **Cough plate** (direct plating during coughing onto Bordet-Gengou medium); Nasopharyngeal swab → Regan-Lowe transport medium

**Treatment**: **Azithromycin** (DOC, reduces transmission); Clarithromycin, TMP-SMX; Erythromycin (historical; pyloric stenosis risk in neonates)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Pertussis toxin = Gi-alpha ADP-ribosylation (opposite of cholera — Gs); lymphocytosis is pathognomonic lab finding; whooping cough most dangerous in infants (apnea)

### Spirochetes

#### Treponema pallidum (Syphilis)
**Key Properties**: Thin, coiled spirochete, **NOT visible by Gram stain** (too thin), **NOT culturable in vitro** (obligate human pathogen), **Darkfield microscopy** (motile spirochetes — for primary chancre fluid)

**Stages**:

| Stage | Clinical | Serology |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Primary** | Painless, indurated ulcer (**chancre**) at inoculation site, regional lymphadenopathy; Heals in 3-6 weeks | RPR/VDRL may be negative initially; rises |
| **Secondary** | Systemic dissemination — **maculopapular rash (palms/soles — pathognomonic)**, condyloma lata (moist warts in genitalia), lymphadenopathy, fever, **alopecia**, hepatitis, uveitis, meningitis | RPR/VDRL positive (highest titers) |
| **Latent** | No symptoms; Early latent <1 yr, Late latent >1 yr | Positive treponemal and non-treponemal |
| **Tertiary (late)** | **Gummas** (granulomas), **Cardiovascular syphilis** (aortic root aneurysm/AR), **Neurosyphilis** (tabes dorsalis — dorsal column degeneration, general paresis) | VDRL may be negative; treponemal positive |
| **Congenital** | Early (<2yr): snuffles, pemphigus, pseudoparalysis, **saddle nose**, **saber shin**; Late: Hutchinson's triad (notched teeth, interstitial keratitis, CN8 deafness), rhagades | — |

**Serological Tests**:

| Test | Type | Principle | Use |
|------|------|-----------|-----|
| **VDRL** (Venereal Disease Research Laboratory) | Non-treponemal (reagin) | Anti-cardiolipin antibodies (cross-reacting) | Screening; **titres follow disease activity**; positive in BFP (biological false positive) |
| **RPR** (Rapid Plasma Reagin) | Non-treponemal | Similar to VDRL, more convenient | Screening |
| **TPHA** (T. pallidum Hemagglutination Assay) | Treponemal | Anti-T. pallidum antibodies | Confirmatory |
| **FTA-ABS** (Fluorescent Treponemal Antibody Absorbed) | Treponemal | **Most sensitive** (earliest positive), indirect immunofluorescence | Confirmatory; **first test positive in primary syphilis** |
| **TPPA, EIA (ELISA)** | Treponemal | — | Confirmatory |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Non-treponemal (VDRL/RPR) = quantitative, follow treatment response; Treponemal (FTA-ABS/TPHA) = qualitative, remain positive for life (cannot use to monitor treatment); VDRL BFP in SLE, malaria, pregnancy, infectious mononucleosis, antiphospholipid syndrome

**Biological False Positive (BFP) VDRL**: SLE, malaria, pregnancy, EBV/CMV, antiphospholipid syndrome, IV drug use, leprosy, old age

**Treatment**: **Benzathine Penicillin G** — primary/secondary/early latent: 2.4 MU IM single dose; late latent/tertiary: 2.4 MU IM × 3 doses (weekly); neurosyphilis: aqueous crystalline Penicillin G IV 14 days

#### Leptospira interrogans (Weil's Disease)
**Key Properties**: Spirochete, **hooked ends**, **darkfield microscopy** (corkscrew motility), host: rodents (urine), waterborne

**Weil's Disease (Icteric leptospirosis)**: Fever + **jaundice** + **renal failure** (acute tubular necrosis) + **hemorrhage** (thrombocytopenia); conjunctival suffusion (distinctive)

**Treatment**: **Doxycycline** (mild), Penicillin G IV or Ceftriaxone (severe)

#### Borrelia burgdorferi (Lyme Disease)
**Key Properties**: Spirochete, transmitted by **Ixodes tick** (Deer tick); reservoir: deer, white-footed mouse

**Clinical Stages**:
1. **Early localized**: **Erythema migrans** — expanding bull's eye rash (>5 cm), flu-like symptoms
2. **Early disseminated**: Multiple EM, **Bell's palsy** (cranial nerve VII — most common neurological feature), meningitis, heart block (Lyme carditis)
3. **Late/Chronic**: **Oligoarticular arthritis** (large joints — knee most common), neuroborreliosis, encephalopathy

**Treatment**: Early: **Doxycycline** (DOC, 14-21 days) or amoxicillin; Neurological/cardiac/arthritis: IV Ceftriaxone

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Ixodes tick also transmits Anaplasma, Ehrlichia, Babesia, RMSF (Dermacentor tick for RMSF)

### Mycobacteria

#### Mycobacterium tuberculosis
**Key Properties**: **Acid-fast bacilli (AFB)** — high lipid (mycolic acid) content in cell wall; **slow-growing** (doubling time 24 hrs); **obligate aerobe** (upper lobe predilection for pulmonary TB); **intracellular pathogen** (survives in macrophage phagosomes)

**Culture**: **Lowenstein-Jensen (LJ) medium** — 6-8 weeks; rough, buff-colored, dry, heaped colonies (**"cauliflower" morphology**); Middlebrook (broth — faster: 10-14 days)

**Virulence**:
- **Cord factor (trehalose 6,6'-dimycolate)**: Serpentine growth pattern; inhibits PMN migration; toxic to mitochondria
- **Sulfatides**: Inhibit phagosome-lysosome fusion (key to intracellular survival)
- **Lipoarabinomannan (LAM)**: Inhibits macrophage activation, scavenges free radicals

**Koch's Postulates**:
1. Organism present in all diseased individuals
2. Organism isolated and grown in pure culture
3. Pure culture causes disease in susceptible host
4. Organism re-isolated from experimental host

**Tuberculin Skin Test (TST/Mantoux)**:
- Intradermal injection of **PPD (Purified Protein Derivative)**
- Read at 48-72 hrs — measure **induration** (not erythema)
- **Type IV hypersensitivity** reaction

**Interpretation of TST Induration**:

| Induration | Considered Positive In |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **≥5 mm** | HIV+, recent TB contact, CXR with fibrotic changes, organ transplant, immunosuppressed |
| **≥10 mm** | Recent immigrant from high-prevalence country, IVDU, residents of high-risk settings (prisons, nursing homes), children <4 years, underlying medical conditions (DM, renal failure, malnutrition) |
| **≥15 mm** | All persons with no risk factors |

**False Positive TST**: BCG vaccination (especially recent), NTM infection
**False Negative TST (Anergy)**: Advanced TB (miliary), HIV, protein malnutrition, sarcoidosis, live viral infection/vaccines, steroid use

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: BCG vaccination → false positive TST (but NOT IGRA — Interferon Gamma Release Assay); IGRA (QuantiFERON/T-SPOT) preferred for BCG-vaccinated individuals and immunocompromised

#### Leprosy (Mycobacterium leprae)
**Key Properties**: **Cannot be cultured in vitro** (in vivo: **nine-banded armadillo** footpad, mouse footpad); stains by **Wade-Fite method** (modified ZN — used because leprae is weakly acid-fast); grows at cooler temperatures (skin, peripheral nerves, anterior eye, upper respiratory tract, testes)

**Ridley-Jopling Classification**:

| Type | Lepromatous (LL) | Tuberculoid (TT) | Borderline |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Immune response** | Poor (Th2 dominant) | Strong (Th1 dominant, cell-mediated) | Intermediate |
| **Bacterial load** | **Multibacillary (many AFB)** | **Paucibacillary (few/no AFB)** | Variable |
| **Skin lesions** | Multiple, **diffuse, symmetric**, poorly defined, **no sensation loss initially** | **Few, well-defined, raised, anaesthetic (loss of sensation)**, dry | Variable |
| **Nerves** | Late, symmetric | Early, asymmetric, palpable (thickened) | Variable |
| **Slit skin smear** | Positive (globi — masses of AFB) | Negative | Variable |
| **Lepromin test** | **Negative** | **Positive** | Variable |
| **Histology** | Foamy macrophages (Virchow cells), lepra cells | Epithelioid granulomas, Langhans giant cells | — |
| **Leonine facies** | Present (madarosis, saddle nose, punched-out ears) | Absent | — |

**WHO Classification for Treatment**:
- **Paucibacillary (PB)**: 1-5 lesions → Rifampicin monthly + Dapsone daily × 6 months
- **Multibacillary (MB)**: >5 lesions → Rifampicin + Dapsone + **Clofazimine** (monthly supervised + daily) × 12 months

**Leprosy Reactions**:
- **Type 1 (Reversal reaction)**: Upgrading toward TT — Th1 response; treat with **Prednisolone**
- **Type 2 (ENL — Erythema Nodosum Leprosum)**: Immune complex-mediated (Type III); tender red nodules, fever, iritis; treat with **Thalidomide** (DOC), prednisolone

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Lepromatous leprosy (LL) — weak CMI, many organisms, negative lepromin, Virchow cells (foamy macrophages with lepra cells); Tuberculoid (TT) — strong CMI, few organisms, positive lepromin, granulomas

### Rickettsia

**Key Properties**: **Obligate intracellular gram-negative organisms**, vector-borne (arthropods), cannot be grown on standard media — requires tissue culture/embryonated eggs

**Weil-Felix Reaction**: Agglutination test using Proteus OX strains (cross-reactive antigens)

| Disease | Organism | Vector | Weil-Felix | Reservoir |
|---------|---------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (RMSF)** | R. rickettsii | **Dermacentor tick** | **OX-19, OX-2 positive** | Dogs, ticks |
| **Epidemic typhus** | R. prowazekii | **Body louse** | OX-19 positive | Humans |
| **Endemic/Murine typhus** | R. typhi | **Rat flea** | OX-19, OX-2 positive | Rats |
| **Scrub typhus** | Orientia tsutsugamushi | **Trombiculid mite (chigger)** | **OX-K positive** (unique) | Rodents |
| **Q fever** | Coxiella burnetii | **NO arthropod vector** (inhalation of contaminated aerosols) | **Negative** (Weil-Felix) | Cattle, sheep, goats |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Scrub typhus → OX-K positive (unique to scrub); Q fever → no vector, no Weil-Felix; RMSF — rash starts on wrists/ankles, spreads centripetally (toward trunk), INCLUDES palms and soles; DOC for all rickettsial = **Doxycycline**

**RMSF**: Classic triad — **fever, headache, rash**; rash on day 2-4, starts wrists/ankles → trunk, involves palms/soles; **petechial/purpuric** in severe cases; **vasculitis** in endothelium; treat immediately with doxycycline (do not wait for confirmation)

## 4. VIROLOGY

### DNA Viruses vs RNA Viruses

**DNA Viruses** (Mnemonic: **"HHAPPPy"** — dsDNA except Parvo ssDNA):
- **Herpesviridae** (HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV, CMV, EBV, HHV-6,7,8)
- **Hepadnaviridae** (HBV) — partially dsDNA
- **Adenoviridae**
- **Papillomaviridae** (HPV)
- **Polyomaviridae** (JC, BK virus)
- **Poxviridae** (Smallpox, Molluscum contagiosum)
- **Parvoviridae** (Parvovirus B19) — **ssDNA**

**RNA Viruses**: Most RNA viruses; exceptions noted
- **Positive-sense ssRNA** (directly translated): Picornaviridae (Enteroviruses, Hepatitis A), Flaviviridae (HCV, Dengue, Zika, Yellow fever, JE, WNV), Togaviridae (Rubella, EEE), Coronaviridae, Caliciviridae (Norovirus, HEV)
- **Negative-sense ssRNA**: Orthomyxoviridae (Influenza), Paramyxoviridae (Measles, Mumps, RSV, Parainfluenza, HMPV), Rhabdoviridae (Rabies), Bunyaviridae, Filoviridae (Ebola)
- **dsRNA**: Reoviridae (Rotavirus, Colorado tick fever)
- **Retroviruses (ssRNA, DNA intermediate)**: HIV, HTLV

### Hepatitis Viruses

#### Hepatitis A (HAV)
- **ssRNA**, Picornavirus (Enterovirus-like), fecal-oral, incubation 2-6 weeks
- **NO chronic infection**, NO carrier state
- Vaccine: Killed (inactivated) — Havrix, Vaqta; 2 doses
- **Diagnosis**: **IgM anti-HAV** (acute); IgG anti-HAV (past infection/immunity)
- Fulminant hepatitis in: underlying chronic liver disease, pregnant women
- Treatment: Supportive

#### Hepatitis B (HBV)
- **Partially double-stranded DNA virus** (hepadnavirus), blood/sexual/perinatal transmission
- **Most common cause of HCC worldwide** (integrates into host genome)
- **Dane particle** = complete infective virion (42 nm)

**Serological Markers (must memorize timeline)**:

| Marker | Significance | When Present |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **HBsAg** (Surface antigen) | Infection (acute or chronic); FIRST to appear | Acute: 1-12 weeks post-infection to clinical recovery |
| **HBeAg** (e-antigen) | **Active viral replication, HIGH infectivity** | During acute infection; prolonged = chronic active |
| **HBcAg** (Core antigen) | Inside hepatocytes only; NOT in serum | Tissue only |
| **Anti-HBs** (Surface Ab) | **Immunity** (vaccination OR recovery); neutralizing | After HBsAg disappears; persists lifelong |
| **Anti-HBc IgM** | **Acute infection** (BEST marker of acute HBV) | Earliest antibody; during acute + early recovery |
| **Anti-HBc IgG** | Past infection OR chronic infection | Persists lifelong |
| **Anti-HBe** | ↓ Replication, lower infectivity | After HBeAg disappears |
| **HBV DNA** | Most sensitive marker of replication/viral load | Detectable in acute and chronic infection |

**Window Period**: HBsAg disappears but anti-HBs not yet appeared → **only anti-HBc IgM positive**

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Window period = anti-HBc IgM only positive; Vaccinated person = only Anti-HBs positive; Past infection = anti-HBs + anti-HBc IgG; Acute infection = HBsAg + anti-HBc IgM + HBeAg

**Chronic HBV** (HBsAg >6 months): Treatment with **Tenofovir** (TDF/TAF) or **Entecavir** (preferred first-line); Interferon alfa-2b (peg-IFN) — finite duration, 48 weeks

#### Hepatitis C (HCV)
- **ssRNA**, Flavivirus, **6 genotypes** (1a, 1b most common in developed world), blood-borne
- **Most common cause of chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, and HCC** in developed world
- **NO vaccine** (high mutation rate)
- **Diagnosis**: Anti-HCV antibody (screening) → **HCV RNA PCR** (confirm active infection)
- Spontaneous clearance: ~25% (rest develop chronic)
- **Treatment**: **Direct-Acting Antivirals (DAAs)** — >95% sustained virologic response (SVR = cure)
  - **Sofosbuvir + Ledipasvir** (genotype 1)
  - **Sofosbuvir + Velpatasvir** (pan-genotypic)
  - **Glecaprevir + Pibrentasvir** (pan-genotypic, shorter duration)

#### Hepatitis D (Delta Virus)
- **Defective RNA virus** — requires **HBsAg envelope** to form virions → only infects HBV+ individuals
- **Coinfection** (HBV + HDV simultaneously): Usually self-limiting, lower risk of chronic hepatitis
- **Superinfection** (HDV in chronic HBV carrier): **More severe**, 70-90% develop chronic hepatitis, higher risk of fulminant hepatitis

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: HDV requires HBsAg → only in HBV patients; Superinfection worse than coinfection; Prevention = HBV vaccination prevents HDV

#### Hepatitis E (HEV)
- **ssRNA**, Calicivirus-like, **fecal-oral** (waterborne outbreaks)
- **High mortality in pregnancy** (~20-25% in 3rd trimester — fulminant hepatic failure)
- Usually self-limiting, NO chronic infection (except in immunocompromised where it can become chronic)
- **No vaccine** approved widely (HEV239 in China)
- Treatment: Supportive; **Ribavirin** for chronic HEV in immunocompromised

### HIV

**Structure**:
- **ssRNA retrovirus** (two copies of RNA), enveloped
- **gp120** (binds CD4 + co-receptor) + **gp41** (fusion)
- **Reverse transcriptase** (RNA → DNA, error-prone = mutations), **Integrase**, **Protease**
- Genes: gag (core), pol (enzymes), env (envelope), + regulatory (tat, rev, nef, vif, vpr, vpu)

**Life Cycle**:
1. gp120 binds **CD4** + co-receptor (**CCR5** for macrophage-tropic R5 strains; **CXCR4** for T-cell tropic X4 strains)
2. gp41 fusion → virus entry
3. RT → ssDNA → dsDNA (provirus)
4. Integrase → integration into host chromosome
5. Transcription → viral RNA → proteins
6. Protease → protein processing → mature virion
7. Budding

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: CCR5 co-receptor — homozygous CCR5-Δ32 mutation → **natural resistance to HIV** (no CCR5 → R5 strains cannot enter); heterozygous → slower progression

**Natural History**:
- **Primary HIV infection** (acute retroviral syndrome): 2-4 weeks post-infection; flu-like (fever, lymphadenopathy, pharyngitis, rash, myalgia), high viral load, ↓ CD4
- **Clinical latency**: Years; CD4 gradually falls (~50-100 cells/μL/year)
- **AIDS**: CD4 <200 cells/μL OR AIDS-defining illness

**AIDS-Defining Illnesses (selected)**:
- CD4 <500: Oral candidiasis, hairy leukoplakia (EBV), herpes zoster (multi-dermatomal), recurrent bacterial infections
- CD4 <200: **PCP** (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia), toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis, **disseminated MAC**, CMV retinitis (CD4 <50), Kaposi's sarcoma (HHV-8), CNS lymphoma (EBV), progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (JC virus)

**Opportunistic Infections by CD4 Count**:

| CD4 Count | Opportunistic Infections |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| <500 | Herpes simplex, herpes zoster, oral candidiasis, HPV, TB |
| <200 | **PCP, toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis, cryptosporidiosis** |
| <100 | CMV, microsporidiosis, disseminated MAC, bartonellosis |
| <50 | CMV retinitis (most common), disseminated MAC, CNS lymphoma |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: PCP — most common AIDS-defining OI; CD4 <200 = prophylaxis with TMP-SMX; CMV retinitis (CD4 <50) — "pizza pie" or "ketchup and cheese" fundal appearance; MAC (CD4 <50) — treat with azithromycin + ethambutol; prophylaxis with azithromycin weekly

|-------|--------------|-------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **HSV-1** | Orolabial herpes (cold sores), herpetic gingivostomatitis, **herpes encephalitis** (temporal lobe) | Trigeminal ganglia | Cold sores, encephalitis | Yes |
| **HSV-2** | **Genital herpes**, neonatal herpes (most serious — brain/disseminated) | Sacral ganglia | Genital herpes, aseptic meningitis | Yes |

| **EBV** | **Infectious mononucleosis** (pharyngitis + lymphadenopathy + splenomegaly + atypical lymphocytes + heterophile antibodies/Monospot), **Burkitt's lymphoma**, **Nasopharyngeal carcinoma**, **CNS lymphoma** (AIDS), hairy leukoplakia (AIDS) | B cells | Lymphomas | No (no TK) |

**Herpes Encephalitis (HSV-1)**:
- Temporal lobe involvement — seizures, personality change, fever
- **CSF**: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, ↑ protein, normal glucose, **RBCs** (hemorrhagic)
- **PCR of CSF** (HSV DNA) = gold standard diagnosis
- Treatment: **Acyclovir IV** (10-15 mg/kg TID × 14-21 days)

### Influenza
**Key Properties**: Orthomyxovirus, ssRNA (negative sense, 8 segments), **segmented genome** (basis of **antigenic shift**), enveloped

**Hemagglutinin (HA)**: Binds to sialic acid receptors on host cells; **16 H types (H1-H16)** in animals; H1, H2, H3 in human influenza; **TARGET for antibody neutralization**

**Neuraminidase (NA)**: Cleaves sialic acid → **releases new virions** from cell surface; allows virus to escape respiratory mucus; **9 N types**; **TARGET of oseltamivir/zanamivir**

**Antigenic Drift vs Shift**:

| Feature | Antigenic Drift | Antigenic Shift |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Mechanism** | Point mutations in HA/NA genes | **Reassortment of RNA segments** between two strains co-infecting same cell |
| **Result** | Minor changes in HA/NA | **Major new HA/NA combination** (e.g., H1N1 pandemic 2009) |
| **Epidemics** | Yearly seasonal outbreaks | **Pandemics** |
| **Immunity** | Partial cross-protection | Little/no existing immunity |
| **Affected viruses** | A and B | **Only Influenza A** (animals + humans — aquatic birds are reservoir) |

**2009 H1N1 Pandemic**: Reassortment of human, avian, swine influenza segments

**Treatment**: **Oseltamivir** (within 48 hrs), Zanamivir (inhaled), Baloxavir (cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor — single dose)

### Dengue
**Key Properties**: Flavivirus, **4 serotypes (DENV-1 to 4)**, **Aedes aegypti** mosquito (day-biting), **no vaccine-induced cross-protection between serotypes** (partial immunity = ADE risk)

**Antibody-Dependent Enhancement (ADE)**: Secondary infection with DIFFERENT serotype → non-neutralizing antibodies from first infection → facilitate virus entry into monocytes → **severe dengue (DHF/DSS)**

**Clinical Phases**:
1. **Febrile phase** (days 1-3): High fever, myalgia ("breakbone fever"), flushing
2. **Critical phase** (days 4-6): **Defervescence** — plasma leakage (hematocrit ↑, pleural/abdominal effusion), **thrombocytopenia** (nadir ~day 5-7), bleeding; risk of dengue shock syndrome (DSS) here
3. **Recovery phase** (days 7-10): Reabsorption of plasma → fluid overload risk

**Diagnosis**:
- **NS1 antigen**: Days 1-5 (before antibody response) — **most useful early marker**
- **IgM anti-dengue**: Day 5-7, peaks day 14
- **IgG anti-dengue**: From day 7; **dengue IgG ratio >1.2** = secondary infection (ADE risk)
- PCR: Most sensitive in early days

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: NS1 antigen — earliest diagnostic marker; secondary dengue infection → ADE → higher risk of DHF/DSS; Aedes aegypti = day-biting mosquito; no specific antiviral treatment

**Treatment**: Supportive — fluid management (key), platelet transfusion only if <10,000 or bleeding; **NO aspirin/NSAIDs/steroids** (worsen bleeding)

### Rabies
**Key Properties**: Rhabdovirus (bullet-shaped), ssRNA (negative sense), **highly neurotropic**, transmitted via **infected saliva** (animal bite, scratch, mucous membrane)

**Pathogenesis**: Virus enters peripheral nerve endings → retrograde axonal transport (3 mm/hr) → spinal cord → brain (limbic system, hippocampus, brainstem) → anterograde transport to salivary glands, cornea, skin

**Pathological Features**:
- **Negri bodies**: Eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions in **hippocampal neurons** (Pyramidal cells of Sommer's sector) and **Purkinje cells of cerebellum** — **PATHOGNOMONIC of rabies**
- **Babes' nodules**: Perivascular aggregates of glial cells and lymphocytes (non-specific inflammation)

**Clinical**: Prodrome (pain/paresthesia at bite site) → Acute neurological phase (hydrophobia, aerophobia, agitation, autonomic instability — **encephalitic form**; OR ascending flaccid paralysis — **paralytic/dumb form**) → Coma → Death

**Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)**:
- **Wound cleaning** (soap + water + povidone-iodine) IMMEDIATELY (most critical step)
- **RIG (Rabies Immunoglobulin)**: Infiltrate wound (all around wound) + IM; **HRIG** (human) preferred over ERIG (equine); given with first vaccine dose
- **Rabies vaccine (HDCV or PCEC)**: Days 0, 3, 7, 14 (4-dose schedule); IM deltoid (NOT gluteal)
- **Previously vaccinated**: Only 2 doses vaccine (Day 0, 3) — NO RIG

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Negri bodies = hippocampal neurons (Sommer's sector) + Purkinje cells; Hydrophobia = classic sign; No antiviral treatment once clinical; Milwaukee Protocol (experimental coma induction)

## 5. PARASITOLOGY

### Malaria

#### Life Cycle

| Stage | Location | Cycle Duration |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| **Exoerythrocytic (Liver)** | Hepatocytes | P. vivax/ovale: dormant **hypnozoites** persist; P. falciparum/malariae: no hypnozoites |
| **Erythrocytic** | RBCs | P. falciparum: 48 hrs (tertian); P. malariae: **72 hrs** (quartan); P. vivax/ovale: 48 hrs |
| **Sexual (Gametocytes)** | RBCs → ingested by Anopheles | Gametes → zygote → oocyst → sporozoites → salivary gland |

**Thick Blood Film**: Species ID, detect parasitemia; **Thick film = diagnosis (concentrated)**; **Thin film = species ID (morphology)**

| Feature | P. falciparum | P. vivax | P. malariae | P. ovale |
|---------|--------------|----------|------------|---------|
| **RBC size** | Normal or small | **Enlarged** | Normal or small | **Enlarged, oval, fimbriated** |
| **Ring form** | Multiple rings/cell, **appliqué (accolé form)**, double chromatin dots (Maurer's) | Single ring | Single ring | Single ring |
| **Stippling** | **Maurer's clefts** | **Schüffner's dots** | **Ziemann's dots** | Schüffner's dots |
| **Gametocytes** | **Crescent/banana shaped** (pathognomonic) | Oval, fills RBC | Oval | Oval |
| **Hypnozoites** | **NO** | **YES** | NO | YES |

**P. falciparum Complications (Mnemonic: CALM)**:
- **Cerebral malaria** (ring hemorrhages, parasitized RBCs obstruct capillaries)
- **Anemia** (severe — hemolysis)
- **Lung** (ARDS — non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema)
- **Multi-organ**: Acute renal failure (acute tubular necrosis), hypoglycemia (quinine-induced), **Blackwater fever** (massive intravascular hemolysis → hemoglobinuria → dark urine → ARF — often in G6PD deficiency + quinine)
- Also: Splenomegaly, DIC, lactic acidosis

**Treatment**:
- **Uncomplicated P. falciparum**: **ACT (Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy)** — artemether-lumefantrine, or artesunate-mefloquine
- **Severe/Cerebral P. falciparum**: **IV Artesunate** (DOC), or IV quinine + doxycycline
- **P. vivax/ovale**: Chloroquine (blood stage) + **Primaquine** (radical cure of liver hypnozoites — check G6PD first)
- **P. malariae**: Chloroquine alone (no hypnozoites)
- **Chloroquine-resistant P. vivax**: Artemether-lumefantrine + primaquine

### Amoebiasis (Entamoeba histolytica)

**Key Properties**: Pathogenic species = E. histolytica; non-pathogenic = E. dispar (morphologically identical — differentiate by stool antigen test or PCR), E. moshkovskii

**Life Cycle**: Cyst (infective form, 4 nuclei) → swallowed → excystation → trophozoites (tissue invasive, ingests RBCs — **erythrophagocytosis = diagnostic**)

**Pathology**: Invades colon → **flask-shaped ulcer** (narrow neck, wide base) in cecum/ascending colon → may penetrate → liver abscess (anchovy sauce pus)

**Clinical**:
- **Intestinal amoebiasis**: Bloody diarrhea/dysentery (non-systemic), tenesmus, colicky pain
- **Amoebic liver abscess (ALA)**: Right lobe (85%), **"Anchovy sauce" or chocolate-colored** sterile pus (no organisms in pus usually), fever, RUQ pain, hepatomegaly; Rupture → pleuropulmonary (right side) or pericardial (left lobe ALA)

**Diagnosis**:
- **Stool microscopy**: Trophozoites with ingested RBCs (Hematophagous trophozoites) — pathognomonic
- **Stool antigen (ELISA)**: E. histolytica-specific
- **Serology (ELISA)**: Positive in ALA (90%+), intestinal (70-80%)
- **Aspiration of ALA**: Anchovy sauce (rarely needed for diagnosis)

**Treatment**:
- **Metronidazole** (tissue/invasive amoebiasis: 7-10 days)
- **Luminal amebicide** (kill cysts in gut): **Diloxanide furoate**, Paromomycin, Iodoquinol — used after metronidazole to eliminate cyst carriage

### Giardiasis (Giardia lamblia/intestinalis/duodenalis)

**Key Properties**: Binucleate pear-shaped trophozoite with **central "spectacles" (axostyles), 4 pairs of flagella**, **ventral sucking disc** (attaches to duodenal villi)

**Cyst**: Oval, 4 nuclei — infective form in feces

**Clinical**: "Mountain climber's/backpacker's diarrhea" — foul-smelling, greasy (fatty — malabsorption), watery diarrhea; **NO blood/mucus**; abdominal cramps, bloating, weight loss

**Diagnosis**: Stool O&P (ova and parasites) — cysts or trophozoites; **Stool ELISA antigen** (best); Duodenal aspirate/string test (Enterotest); Endoscopic biopsy (if other tests negative)

**Treatment**: **Metronidazole** (5-7 days); Tinidazole (single dose); Nitazoxanide; Albendazole

### Toxoplasmosis (Toxoplasma gondii)

**Key Properties**: **Definitive host = cat** (sexual cycle in cat intestine); **Intermediate host** = humans, birds, other animals; **Infective forms**: oocysts (from cat feces), bradyzoites (tissue cysts in undercooked meat), tachyzoites (active infection)

**Transmission to Humans**:
1. Ingestion of oocysts (cat feces contamination)
2. Ingestion of tissue cysts (undercooked meat — especially pork, lamb)
3. **Transplacental** (primary infection during pregnancy)

**Congenital Toxoplasmosis (TORCH)**:
- Classic triad (late manifestations): **Hydrocephalus + Chorioretinitis + Cerebral calcifications (diffuse, periventricular)**
- Fetal infection risk highest in 3rd trimester; severity greatest if 1st trimester
- Also: Hepatosplenomegaly, jaundice, microcephaly, seizures

**Diagnosis**: Serology (IgM = acute, IgG = past/chronic); PCR of amniotic fluid; Brain biopsy (AIDS)

**AIDS (CD4 <100)**: Toxoplasma encephalitis — **multiple ring-enhancing lesions in basal ganglia** on MRI; headache, fever, focal neurological deficits; **Empirical treatment with pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + leucovorin** (if no improvement → biopsy for CNS lymphoma)

**Treatment**: **Pyrimethamine + Sulfadiazine + Leucovorin** (folinic acid) → for active disease; Spiramycin (pregnancy — toxoplasma, does not cross placenta — reduces transmission); TMP-SMX for prophylaxis/secondary prophylaxis

#### Tapeworms (Cestodes)

| Organism | Definitive Host | Larval Stage/Intermediate | Disease | Treatment |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|---------|----------|
| **Taenia solium (Pork tapeworm)** | Human (adult worm) | Pig (Cysticercus cellulosae); **Humans can be accidental intermediate host** → **Neurocysticercosis** (most common cause of acquired epilepsy in endemic areas) | Intestinal: adult worm; Cysticercosis: subcutaneous, ocular, CNS (seizures, hydrocephalus) | Niclosamide/Praziquantel (adult worm); Albendazole + Dexamethasone (neurocysticercosis — note: praziquantel can worsen inflammation acutely) |

Intradermal injection of hydatid cyst fluid antigen; wheal and flare within 30 mins = positive; **hypersensitivity reaction**; low specificity (now replaced by serology + imaging)

## 6. MYCOLOGY

### Candida

**Key Properties**: **Dimorphic** yeast (yeast in 37°C/tissue; pseudohyphae/hyphae in certain conditions), gram-positive appearance on Gram stain, forms **pseudohyphae** (elongated budding yeast)

**Germ Tube Test (Reynolds-Braude test)**: Candida albicans + human serum at 37°C × 2-3 hrs → **germ tubes** (true hyphae without constriction at base) — **POSITIVE only for C. albicans** (and rarely C. dubliniensis)

**Key Lab Features**:
- **Germ tube test**: C. albicans (positive)
- **Chlamydoconidia (chlamydospores)**: C. albicans on cornmeal agar
- **Urease negative** (vs Cryptococcus which is urease positive)
- C. krusei: Fluconazole **intrinsically resistant**; C. glabrata: Reduced susceptibility to fluconazole; C. auris: Multidrug resistant — **hospital outbreak, difficult to treat**

**Diseases**:
- **Oropharyngeal candidiasis** (thrush): White plaques on red mucosa; scrape off (vs leukoplakia)
- **Esophageal candidiasis**: Dysphagia, odynophagia; AIDS-defining
- **Vulvovaginal candidiasis**: "Cottage cheese" discharge, pruritus
- **Candidemia/Invasive candidiasis**: Catheter-related in ICU patients, prolonged antibiotics, TPN, neutropenia
- **Chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis**: T cell defect (anti-IL-17 deficiency)

**Treatment**: **Fluconazole** (oral/IV candidiasis); **Echinocandins** (caspofungin/micafungin — DOC for invasive/candidemia, C. glabrata, critically ill); **Amphotericin B** (severe/resistant)

### Cryptococcus neoformans / C. gattii

**Key Properties**: Encapsulated yeast, **urease positive**, grows at 37°C (pathogen) unlike most fungi, **melanin pigment** (laccase enzyme → virulence), **polysaccharide capsule** (main virulence factor — antiphagocytic, inhibits complement, impairs T cell function)

**Diagnosis**:
- **India ink**: Capsule appears as **clear halo** around organism (negative stain — dye excluded by capsule)
- **Mucicarmine stain**: Red capsule
- **Latex agglutination for cryptococcal antigen (CrAg)**: Most sensitive, detects capsular polysaccharide; high titer = poor prognosis
- CSF: India ink, CrAg, culture (Sabouraud's medium)

**Diseases**:
- **Cryptococcal meningitis**: Most common in HIV (CD4 <100); **subacute** presentation (weeks); headache, meningismus, ↑ ICP; **opening pressure** often very high (>25 cmH2O)
- C. gattii: Can infect immunocompetent (Pacific Northwest US, Australia); more mass lesions (cryptococcomas)

**Treatment**:
- **Induction**: **Amphotericin B (liposomal) + Flucytosine** (5-FC) × 2 weeks
- **Consolidation**: **Fluconazole 400 mg/day** × 8 weeks
- **Maintenance/Secondary prophylaxis**: Fluconazole 200 mg/day (until CD4 >200 on ART × 6 months)
- **Serial lumbar punctures**: Management of elevated ICP (most important for survival)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: India ink = Cryptococcus; high opening pressure in cryptococcal meningitis requires LP drainage (serial LPs or lumbar drain); Flucytosine + AmB for induction (synergistic)

### Aspergillus

**Key Properties**: **Septate hyphae** with **acute angle branching (45°)**, green/gray-green colonies (A. fumigatus), uniseptate vesicle with flask-shaped phialides (stigmata), **conidiophore** with flask-shaped phialides bearing chains of conidia (uniserate = A. flavus; biserate = A. fumigatus)

**Diseases**:
- **Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)**: Asthma + hypersensitivity to Aspergillus; ↑ IgE, eosinophilia, central bronchiectasis; treat with prednisolone + itraconazole
- **Aspergilloma (Fungus ball)**: Grows in pre-existing cavity (old TB); **"Monod sign" or "crescent sign"** on CXR; hemoptysis; no invasion
- **Invasive Pulmonary Aspergillosis**: Neutropenic patients, organ transplant, high-dose steroids; **"halo sign" on CT** (ground-glass halo around nodule); rapidly progressive, angioinvasive → hemorrhagic infarcts, "air crescent sign" during recovery
- **Chronic necrotizing/Semi-invasive**: Debilitated patients, alcoholics, COPD

**Treatment**:
- **Invasive Aspergillosis**: **Voriconazole** (DOC); alternatives: liposomal AmB, isavuconazole, posaconazole (prophylaxis in at-risk)
- **Aspergilloma**: Surgical resection (if symptomatic); itraconazole/voriconazole
- **ABPA**: Prednisolone + itraconazole

### Mucor / Mucormycosis (Zygomycosis)

**Key Properties**: **Broad, non-septate (aseptate/pauci-septate) hyphae** with **wide-angle branching (~90° = right-angle branching)** — differentiate from Aspergillus (45°, septate)

**Organisms**: Mucor, Rhizopus (most common), Absidia, Cunninghamella, Lichtheimia

**Clinical Forms**:
- **Rhinocerebral/Rhinoorbitocerebral**: Most common form; **diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)** is classic risk factor; also hematological malignancy, neutropenia; black eschar on palate/nasal mucosa (necrosis); invades orbit → periorbital edema, proptosis, ophthalmoplegia; crosses into CNS via cribriform plate/orbital apex → cavernous sinus thrombosis
- **Pulmonary**: Immunocompromised, hematological malignancy
- **Disseminated**: Widespread, highest mortality
- **Cutaneous**: Trauma, burns, dressings

**Diagnosis**: Tissue biopsy (non-septate hyphae) — **cultures often negative** (fragile hyphae don't culture well); **DO NOT use serum galactomannan** (aspergillus-specific)

**Treatment**:
- **Surgery** (aggressive debridement) — cornerstone (non-negotiable)
- **Liposomal Amphotericin B** (DOC)
- **Isavuconazole or Posaconazole** (step-down or salvage)
- **No echinocandins, no voriconazole** (not effective against Mucorales)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: DKA → Rhinocerebral mucormycosis; non-septate right-angle branching hyphae; Aspergillus → septate acute-angle (45°) branching; Treatment = surgery + LipoAmB + reverse immunosuppression/control DKA

### Dermatophytes (Tinea)

**Key Properties**: Keratinophilic fungi (digest keratin), infect skin/hair/nails (NOT systemic), **septate hyphae** in KOH mount of skin scrapings, **arthrospores** in infected hair (endothrix vs ectothrix)

| Organism | Infection | Diagnosis | Treatment |
|---------|---------|----------|----------|
| T. rubrum | **Most common** overall dermatophyte; **Tinea pedis** (athlete's foot), onychomycosis (nail), tinea corporis | KOH + culture (SDA) | Topical azoles (mild); oral terbinafine/itraconazole (extensive/nail) |
| T. tonsurans | **Most common tinea capitis** (scalp ringworm) in USA/UK; **Endothrix** (spores inside hair shaft); does not fluoresce | Wood's lamp: **NO fluorescence** | Griseofulvin (historical), **Terbinafine** (oral DOC), Itraconazole |
| M. canis | Tinea capitis (tinea capitis in Europe); ectothrix; **Wood's lamp: green fluorescence** | Wood's lamp + KOH | Griseofulvin or Terbinafine |
| T. versicolor (Malassezia furfur) | **Pityriasis versicolor** (hypo- or hyperpigmented patches; spaghetti and meatballs on KOH) | KOH mount: **Spaghetti and meatballs** (hyphae + clusters of yeast) | **Selenium sulfide shampoo**, topical azoles; oral fluconazole/itraconazole |
| T. schoenleini | Favus (scalp tinea with scutula/crusts); ectothrix, fluorescent | — | Griseofulvin |

**KOH Mount**: Dissolves keratin → reveals hyphae/arthrospores; used for all dermatophyte diagnosis

**Wood's Lamp (UV)**: M. canis → green fluorescence; T. versicolor → golden/yellow fluorescence; T. tonsurans → NO fluorescence; Pseudomonas → blue-green; Porphyria → red/coral; Erythrasma (Corynebacterium minutissimum) → coral-red

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Malassezia furfur KOH = "spaghetti and meatballs" pattern; requires lipid-supplemented media (Sabouraud's + olive oil); Dermatophytes infect keratin-containing structures only (never mucous membranes/deep tissues)
