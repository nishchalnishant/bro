## CELL INJURY (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 55-year-old man dies of myocardial infarction. Autopsy performed 24 hours after onset of chest pain. Which of the following histological findings is most likely in the infarcted myocardium?**
- A) Ghost outlines of cells with loss of nuclei and cytoplasmic eosinophilia
- B) Liquefactive necrosis with neutrophilic infiltrate
- C) Caseous necrosis with granuloma formation
- D) Fat necrosis with saponification

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Coagulative necrosis is the hallmark of ischemic infarction in most solid organs including the heart. The key one-liner: cell architecture (ghost outlines) is preserved but nuclei are lost — this distinguishes coagulative from liquefactive necrosis. Liquefactive necrosis (B) occurs in brain infarction and bacterial abscesses. Caseous necrosis (C) is seen in tuberculosis. Fat necrosis (D) occurs in acute pancreatitis.

</details>

**Q2. A 30-year-old woman presents with a palpable neck mass. Fine needle aspiration shows cells with nuclear grooving, powdery "Orphan Annie eye" nuclei, and psammoma bodies. Which organelle is characteristically increased in these cells to produce ATP under hypoxic conditions?**
- A) Mitochondria
- B) Smooth endoplasmic reticulum
- C) Lysosomes
- D) Peroxisomes

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

This describes papillary thyroid carcinoma. However, the question pivots to cell injury physiology: under hypoxia, mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation fails, leading to compensatory mitochondrial swelling — the earliest ultrastructural change of reversible cell injury. Mitochondria are the primary site of ATP generation; their failure is the central event in hypoxic cell injury. The other organelles are not primary ATP producers.

</details>

**Q3. Dystrophic calcification differs from metastatic calcification in that:**
- A) Serum calcium levels are elevated in dystrophic calcification
- B) Dystrophic calcification occurs in dead or dying tissue with normal serum calcium
- C) Metastatic calcification occurs only in areas of necrosis
- D) Both require elevated serum phosphate levels

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The one-liner: dystrophic calcification = abnormal tissue + normal calcium; metastatic calcification = normal tissue + elevated calcium. Dystrophic calcification occurs in necrotic, damaged, or aging tissue (atherosclerotic plaques, TB foci, old infarcts) with normal serum calcium. Metastatic calcification occurs in viable tissues when serum calcium or phosphate is elevated (hyperparathyroidism, hypervitaminosis D).

</details>

**Q4. A 45-year-old alcoholic develops acute pancreatitis. Imaging shows areas of fat necrosis around the pancreas. The mechanism of cell death in fat necrosis is:**
- A) Enzymatic digestion by lipases releasing fatty acids that combine with calcium
- B) Ischemic coagulation of adipocyte cytoplasm
- C) Apoptosis triggered by TNF-alpha from macrophages
- D) Liquefactive digestion by neutrophil elastase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Fat necrosis in pancreatitis results from lipase (and phospholipase) released from damaged acinar cells acting on adipose tissue. Released fatty acids combine with calcium ions to form calcium soaps — a process called saponification, appearing as chalky-white deposits on imaging. This is the pathognomonic mechanism of enzymatic fat necrosis. Options B and D describe other necrosis types; option C is not the primary mechanism here.

</details>

**Q5. Which of the following is the earliest reversible change seen on electron microscopy in a cell undergoing hypoxic injury?**
- A) Pyknosis and karyorrhexis
- B) Membrane bleb formation and mitochondrial swelling
- C) Flocculent densities in mitochondria
- D) Lysosomal rupture and autodigestion

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The earliest ultrastructural changes in reversible hypoxic injury are mitochondrial swelling (due to loss of ionic homeostasis) and plasma membrane bleb formation. These changes are reversible if perfusion is restored. Flocculent densities (C) in mitochondria indicate irreversible injury. Pyknosis/karyorrhexis (A) and lysosomal rupture (D) are features of irreversible cell death/necrosis.

</details>

## INFLAMMATION (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A patient develops a localized skin infection with a central area of liquefied pus. The predominant cell type responsible for this process is:**
- A) Lymphocytes
- B) Plasma cells
- C) Neutrophils
- D) Eosinophils

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Neutrophils (PMNs) are the first responders in acute inflammation and are the dominant cell in purulent (suppurative) infections producing pus. The one-liner: pus = neutrophils + dead cells + bacteria + liquefied debris. Lymphocytes (A) and plasma cells (B) dominate chronic inflammation. Eosinophils (D) are characteristic of allergic reactions and parasitic infections.

</details>

**Q7. A 3-year-old child with recurrent bacterial infections is found to have a defect in the CD18 subunit of integrins. This results in failure of neutrophil:**
- A) Degranulation inside the phagolysosome
- B) Firm adhesion to endothelium and transmigration
- C) Recognition of opsonized bacteria via Fc receptors
- D) Production of reactive oxygen species via NADPH oxidase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

This describes Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (LAD) type I. CD18 (beta-2 integrin) pairs with CD11a/b/c to form LFA-1 and Mac-1, which mediate firm adhesion of neutrophils to ICAM-1 on endothelium — a prerequisite for transmigration. Without firm adhesion, neutrophils cannot reach sites of infection. The classic clinical feature is delayed umbilical cord separation and markedly elevated blood neutrophil count with absent pus formation.

</details>

**Q8. Granuloma formation in tuberculosis requires which of the following cell types as the central organizing element?**
- A) Neutrophils fused to form Langhans giant cells
- B) CD4+ T cells activating macrophages via IFN-gamma
- C) B cells producing anti-mycobacterial antibodies
- D) NK cells directly killing Mycobacterium tuberculosis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Granuloma is a type IV hypersensitivity (delayed-type) reaction. CD4+ Th1 cells recognize mycobacterial antigens presented by macrophages, then secrete IFN-gamma, which activates macrophages to become epithelioid cells and fuse into Langhans giant cells. The one-liner: granuloma = IFN-gamma–activated macrophages surrounded by CD4+ T lymphocytes. Antibodies (C) play no protective role against intracellular TB.

</details>

**Q9. A 25-year-old woman has an asthma attack after exposure to cat dander. The immediate bronchospasm (within minutes) is mediated by:**
- A) IgG complexes depositing on basement membrane
- B) Preformed IgE on mast cells cross-linked by allergen, releasing histamine
- C) CD8+ cytotoxic T cells destroying bronchial epithelium
- D) Complement activation via the classical pathway

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Asthma is a Type I (immediate) hypersensitivity reaction. On re-exposure to allergen, it cross-links IgE antibodies pre-bound to FcεRI receptors on mast cells, triggering immediate degranulation and release of histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins — causing bronchospasm within minutes. The one-liner: Type I = IgE on mast cells + allergen → histamine release → immediate response. IgG complexes (A) cause Type III; CTLs (C) cause Type IV.

</details>

- A) RB1
- B) BRCA1
- C) TP53

<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

TP53 is the most commonly mutated gene in human cancers overall, including lung adenocarcinoma. RB1 (A) is classic for retinoblastoma. BRCA1 (B) is associated with hereditary breast/ovarian cancer. APC (D) is the gatekeeper for colorectal cancer.

</details>

**Q12. Warburg effect, a key feature of cancer metabolism, refers to:**
- A) Increased oxidative phosphorylation in mitochondria of tumor cells
- B) Aerobic glycolysis producing lactate even in the presence of oxygen
- C) Enhanced fatty acid oxidation to fuel rapid cell division
- D) Upregulation of TCA cycle enzymes in hypoxic tumor centers

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The Warburg effect is aerobic glycolysis: tumor cells preferentially convert glucose to lactate even in the presence of oxygen. Despite being less efficient for ATP, this provides biosynthetic precursors (nucleotides, amino acids, lipids) for rapid proliferation. The one-liner: Warburg effect = aerobic glycolysis = high glucose uptake → lactate, even with O2 (basis for FDG-PET scanning in oncology).

</details>

## HEMATOLOGY (Q16–Q18)

**Q16. A 25-year-old woman presents with fatigue and pallor. CBC shows microcytic hypochromic anemia, low serum iron, low ferritin, elevated TIBC, and low transferrin saturation. The peripheral smear shows pencil cells (elliptocytes). The diagnosis is:**
- A) Anemia of chronic disease
- B) Thalassemia trait
- C) Iron deficiency anemia
- D) Sideroblastic anemia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Iron deficiency anemia (IDA) presents with low serum iron, low ferritin (depleted stores), elevated TIBC (the body upregulates transferrin receptors), and low transferrin saturation. Pencil cells/elliptocytes are characteristic on smear. The one-liner: IDA = low iron + low ferritin + HIGH TIBC (this distinguishes it from anemia of chronic disease, which has low TIBC). ACD has normal/elevated ferritin and low TIBC.

</details>

**Q17. A 65-year-old man is found to have a hemoglobin of 9 g/dL, MCV 105 fL, hypersegmented neutrophils on peripheral smear, and elevated serum homocysteine with normal methylmalonic acid. The most likely deficiency is:**
- A) Vitamin B12 (cobalamin)
- B) Folate
- C) Iron
- D) Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Both B12 and folate deficiency cause megaloblastic anemia with hypersegmented neutrophils and elevated homocysteine. The key differentiator: B12 deficiency ALSO elevates methylmalonic acid (MMA), while folate deficiency does not. Normal MMA with elevated homocysteine = folate deficiency. The one-liner: elevated MMA = B12 deficiency; normal MMA + elevated homocysteine = folate deficiency. B12 deficiency additionally causes subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.

</details>

**Q18. Hereditary spherocytosis is caused by a defect in which of the following red cell membrane proteins?**
- A) Glycophorin A
- B) Band 3 protein (anion exchanger 1)
- C) Spectrin or ankyrin
- D) Protein 4.1

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Hereditary spherocytosis most commonly results from mutations in spectrin (alpha or beta) or ankyrin, which connect the lipid bilayer to the cytoskeleton. Loss of this vertical linkage causes membrane loss via vesiculation, producing spherocytes that are osmotically fragile. The one-liner: HS = spectrin/ankyrin defect → spherocytes → positive osmotic fragility test + negative Coombs test. Band 3 defects can also cause HS but spectrin/ankyrin are the most frequently tested answer.

</details>

## LEUKEMIAS & LYMPHOMAS (Q19–Q22)

**Q19. A 5-year-old boy presents with bone pain, fever, and lymphadenopathy. Bone marrow biopsy shows >90% lymphoblasts. Flow cytometry reveals TdT+, CD10+, CD19+, CD20− cells. The most likely diagnosis and expected prognosis is:**
- A) AML-M3; poor prognosis requiring ATRA therapy
- B) Precursor B-cell ALL; favorable prognosis with standard chemotherapy
- C) Burkitt lymphoma/leukemia; intermediate prognosis
- D) CLL; good prognosis, watchful waiting appropriate

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

TdT (terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase) is a marker of lymphoid progenitors — it is positive in ALL but negative in mature lymphomas. CD10 (CALLA) + CD19 + TdT = precursor B-cell ALL. This is the most common childhood malignancy and has the best prognosis among leukemias with 5-year survival >85% in children. The one-liner: TdT+ CD10+ in a child = precursor B-ALL = most common childhood cancer with excellent prognosis.

</details>

## CARDIOVASCULAR PATHOLOGY (Q23–Q24)

**Q23. A 50-year-old hypertensive man dies suddenly. Autopsy reveals concentric left ventricular hypertrophy without chamber dilation. The most likely cause is:**
- A) Dilated cardiomyopathy from alcohol use
- B) Pressure overload hypertrophy from chronic hypertension
- C) Volume overload from chronic aortic regurgitation
- D) Hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy (HOCM)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Pressure overload (hypertension, aortic stenosis) causes concentric hypertrophy — sarcomeres are added in parallel, increasing wall thickness without chamber dilation. Volume overload (aortic/mitral regurgitation) causes eccentric hypertrophy — sarcomeres added in series, dilating the chamber. The one-liner: pressure overload = concentric hypertrophy (thick wall, normal or small cavity); volume overload = eccentric hypertrophy (dilated cavity). HOCM (D) shows asymmetric septal hypertrophy.

</details>

## RESPIRATORY PATHOLOGY (Q25–Q26)

**Q25. A 55-year-old man with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents with productive cough for 3 months per year for the past 3 years. Biopsy of a bronchus shows submucosal gland hypertrophy. The Reid index in this condition is:**
- A) Less than 0.4, indicating normal gland:wall ratio
- B) Greater than 0.4, indicating increased gland:wall ratio
- C) Equal to 1.0, indicating complete gland replacement of the wall
- D) Unmeasurable because goblet cells replace the glands entirely

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Chronic bronchitis is defined clinically (productive cough ≥3 months/year for ≥2 consecutive years). Histologically, it is characterized by submucosal gland hypertrophy. The one-liner: Reid index >0.4 = chronic bronchitis (submucosal gland hypertrophy).

</details>
