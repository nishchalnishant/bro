## ANATOMY (40 One-Liners)

Q: Nerve injured in fracture of shaft of humerus → A: Radial nerve (wrist drop)
Q: Nerve injured in fracture of surgical neck of humerus → A: Axillary nerve (loss of deltoid, sensory loss over regimental badge area)
Q: Nerve injured in fracture of medial epicondyle of humerus → A: Ulnar nerve (claw hand — ring and little fingers)
Q: Nerve injured in fracture of lateral epicondyle (supracondylar) → A: Anterior interosseous nerve (branch of median nerve) or median nerve proper
Q: Nerve injured in fracture of neck of fibula → A: Common peroneal nerve (foot drop)
Q: Nerve responsible for sensation on medial aspect of arm and forearm → A: Medial cutaneous nerve of arm and medial cutaneous nerve of forearm (from medial cord)
Q: Muscle that initiates abduction of shoulder (0–15°) → A: Supraspinatus
Q: Only muscle attached to lesser trochanter of femur → A: Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus)
Q: Most common site of ectopic pregnancy → A: Ampulla of fallopian tube (75%)
Q: Lymphatic drainage of testis → A: Para-aortic (lumbar) nodes (NOT inguinal)
Q: Lymphatic drainage of scrotum → A: Superficial inguinal nodes
Q: Lymphatic drainage of anal canal above pectinate line → A: Internal iliac nodes
Q: Lymphatic drainage of anal canal below pectinate line → A: Superficial inguinal nodes
Q: Blood supply of spinal cord — anterior 2/3 → A: Anterior spinal artery (from vertebral arteries, single midline vessel)
Q: Blood supply of posterior 1/3 of spinal cord → A: Two posterior spinal arteries
Q: Artery of Adamkiewicz (great anterior radicular artery) — typical level → A: T9–T11 on left side
Q: Dermatome of umbilicus → A: T10
Q: Dermatome of inguinal ligament → A: L1
Q: Dermatome of nipple → A: T4
Q: Spinal cord ends at in adults → A: Lower border of L1 (or upper L2)
Q: Spinal cord ends at in neonates → A: L3
Q: Circle of Willis — structure connecting two ACAs → A: Anterior communicating artery (ACoA)
Q: Circle of Willis — structure connecting ICA to PCA → A: Posterior communicating artery (PCoA)
Q: Artery supplying medial surface of frontal lobe → A: Anterior cerebral artery (ACA)
Q: Artery most commonly affected in hypertensive hemorrhage → A: Lenticulostriate arteries (branches of MCA)
Q: Contents of femoral canal → A: Lymph node of Cloquet/Rosenmuller, areolar tissue, lymphatics
Q: Hesselbach's triangle boundaries → A: Medial: rectus sheath; Lateral: inferior epigastric artery; Inferior: inguinal ligament
Q: Portal vein is formed by → A: Superior mesenteric vein + Splenic vein (behind neck of pancreas)
Q: Meckel's diverticulum — rule of 2s → A: 2% population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic)
Q: Opening through which femoral hernia passes → A: Femoral ring (femoral canal)
Q: Weakest point of inguinal canal (site of direct hernia) → A: Medial part of posterior wall (transversalis fascia) within Hesselbach's triangle
Q: Nerve that carries GVA from diaphragm → A: Phrenic nerve (C3,4,5) — referred pain to shoulder tip
Q: Most commonly fractured carpal bone → A: Scaphoid (at risk of AVN — blood supply from distal end)
Q: Nerve injured in "Saturday night palsy" → A: Radial nerve (compression in axilla/spiral groove)
Q: All intrinsic muscles of hand supplied by → A: Ulnar nerve, EXCEPT LOAF: Lateral two lumbricals, Opponens pollicis, Abductor pollicis brevis, Flexor pollicis brevis — supplied by Median nerve
Q: Short head of biceps — nerve supply → A: Musculocutaneous nerve
Q: Thoracodorsal nerve supplies → A: Latissimus dorsi
Q: Long thoracic nerve supplies → A: Serratus anterior; damage = winged scapula
Q: Nerve used for electromyography to diagnose ulnar nerve palsy → A: Flexor carpi ulnaris or hypothenar muscles
Q: Bone that articulates with base of skull to form atlanto-occipital joint → A: C1 (Atlas)

## PHYSIOLOGY (40 One-Liners)

Q: Normal tidal volume → A: 500 mL
Q: Normal functional residual capacity (FRC) → A: 2300 mL
Q: Lung volume that cannot be measured by spirometry → A: Residual volume (RV), FRC, TLC (all volumes containing RV require body plethysmography or helium dilution)
Q: Dead space volume (anatomical) → A: ~150 mL
Q: FEV1/FVC ratio in obstructive lung disease → A: <70% (<0.7)
Q: FEV1/FVC ratio in restrictive lung disease → A: Normal or increased (both FEV1 and FVC are reduced equally)
Q: P50 of oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve (normal) → A: 26 mmHg
Q: Hemoglobin type with highest O2 affinity (left shift) → A: Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) — 2,3-DPG binds less to γ-chains
Q: Substance used to measure GFR → A: Inulin (freely filtered, not reabsorbed or secreted)
Q: Substance used to measure renal plasma flow → A: Para-aminohippuric acid (PAH) — secreted completely in one pass
Q: Normal GFR → A: 125 mL/min (180 L/day filtered)
Q: Renal threshold for glucose → A: ~180 mg/dL plasma glucose
Q: Transport maximum (Tm) for glucose → A: ~375 mg/min
Q: Site of action of thiazide diuretics → A: Distal convoluted tubule (Na-Cl cotransporter)
Q: Site of action of furosemide → A: Thick ascending limb of loop of Henle (Na-K-2Cl cotransporter)
Q: Site of action of aldosterone → A: Late DCT and collecting duct (Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ excretion)
Q: Site of action of ADH (vasopressin) → A: Collecting duct (aquaporin-2 insertion)
Q: Cardiac output at rest → A: ~5 L/min
Q: Normal ejection fraction → A: 55–65%
Q: Normal stroke volume → A: ~70 mL
Q: Frank-Starling law → A: Increased preload (LVEDV) leads to increased stroke volume (within physiological limits)
Q: Laplace's law applied to heart → A: Wall stress = (Pressure × Radius) / (2 × Wall thickness)
Q: First heart sound (S1) corresponds to → A: Closure of mitral and tricuspid valves (AV valves) — start of systole
Q: Second heart sound (S2) corresponds to → A: Closure of aortic and pulmonic valves (semilunar valves) — start of diastole
Q: Hormone responsible for long-term blood pressure regulation → A: Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)
Q: pH of blood that defines acidosis → A: <7.35
Q: pH of blood that defines alkalosis → A: >7.45
Q: Normal bicarbonate → A: 22–26 mEq/L
Q: Normal pCO₂ → A: 35–45 mmHg
Q: Winter's formula (for expected pCO₂ in metabolic acidosis) → A: Expected pCO₂ = 1.5 × [HCO₃] + 8 ± 2
Q: Hormone that increases calcium reabsorption in DCT → A: Parathyroid hormone (PTH)
Q: Hormone that decreases phosphate reabsorption in PCT → A: PTH (phosphaturic)
Q: Phase of cardiac cycle in which coronary blood flow is maximum → A: Diastole (LV myocardium perfused during diastole)
Q: Mechanism of action of nitric oxide (NO) in vascular smooth muscle → A: Activates guanylyl cyclase → ↑cGMP → vasodilatation
Q: Receptor mediating baroreceptor reflex → A: Carotid sinus (CN IX) and aortic arch (CN X) baroreceptors
Q: Normal CVP → A: 0–8 mmHg
Q: Effect of right bundle branch block on QRS width → A: QRS >120 ms
Q: Muscle fiber type in marathon runners → A: Type I (slow-twitch, oxidative — high mitochondria, red, fatigue-resistant)
Q: Oxygen delivery formula → A: DO₂ = CO × CaO₂ = CO × (Hb × 1.34 × SaO₂ + 0.003 × PaO₂)
Q: Effect of increased 2,3-DPG on O₂ affinity → A: Decreased affinity (right shift) — occurs in anemia, high altitude, chronic hypoxia

## BIOCHEMISTRY (35 One-Liners)

Q: Only purely ketogenic amino acids → A: Leucine and Lysine

Q: Rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis → A: HMG-CoA reductase (inhibited by statins)
Q: Rate-limiting enzyme of urea cycle → A: Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase I (CPS-I) — mitochondrial
Q: ATP yield from complete oxidation of one glucose molecule → A: 30–32 ATP (net)
Q: Coenzyme form of vitamin B1 (thiamine) → A: Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP)
Q: Coenzyme form of vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) → A: Pyridoxal phosphate (PLP) — used in transamination
Q: Vitamin deficiency causing sideroblastic anemia → A: Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) — needed for ALA synthase in heme synthesis
Q: Vitamin whose deficiency causes neural tube defects → A: Folic acid (folate)
Q: Vitamin K-dependent clotting factors → A: II (prothrombin), VII, IX, X, Protein C, Protein S
Q: Enzyme deficient in phenylketonuria (PKU) → A: Phenylalanine hydroxylase (converts Phe → Tyr)
Q: Enzyme deficient in alkaptonuria → A: Homogentisate oxidase (homogentisic acid accumulates)
Q: Enzyme deficient in maple syrup urine disease → A: Branched-chain α-ketoacid dehydrogenase complex
Q: Most common glycogen storage disease → A: Type I (Von Gierke — glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency)
Q: Glycogen storage disease with cardiomegaly → A: Type II (Pompe disease — acid maltase/α-1,4-glucosidase deficiency)
Q: Glycogen storage disease showing no rise in lactate with exercise → A: Type V (McArdle — muscle phosphorylase deficiency)
Q: Staining method for amyloid → A: Congo red → apple-green birefringence under polarized light
Q: Amyloid type in chronic inflammatory conditions (TB, RA) → A: AA amyloid (serum amyloid A precursor)
Q: Amyloid type in multiple myeloma → A: AL amyloid (immunoglobulin light chain)
Q: Secondary structure of amyloid → A: β-pleated sheet configuration
Q: Product of reaction catalyzed by glucokinase vs hexokinase → A: Both produce glucose-6-phosphate; glucokinase has low affinity (high Km), not inhibited by product
Q: Substrate for one-carbon transfers in folate metabolism → A: Tetrahydrofolate (THF) — carries methyl, methylene, formyl groups
Q: Vitamin C role in collagen synthesis → A: Hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues (requires ascorbate as cofactor)
Q: Function of biotin → A: Cofactor for carboxylase reactions (pyruvate carboxylase, acetyl-CoA carboxylase, propionyl-CoA carboxylase)
Q: What avidin does in raw egg whites → A: Binds biotin tightly, preventing its absorption → biotin deficiency
Q: Orotic acid accumulation in blood → A: Indicates ornithine transcarbamylase (OTC) deficiency (urea cycle disorder)
Q: Homocysteine metabolism — enzyme deficiency causing homocystinuria → A: Cystathionine β-synthase
Q: Homocystinuria lens dislocation direction vs Marfan's → A: Homocystinuria = downward; Marfan's = upward (superotemporal)
Q: DNA repair defect in xeroderma pigmentosum → A: Nucleotide excision repair (NER) defect — cannot repair UV-induced thymine dimers
Q: Cancer predisposition in BRCA1/2 mutations → A: Breast + ovarian (also prostate, pancreatic in BRCA2)
Q: Pathway activated in complete starvation → A: Gluconeogenesis + fatty acid oxidation + ketogenesis
Q: Hormone activating hormone-sensitive lipase in adipose → A: Glucagon and catecholamines (via cAMP-PKA pathway)
Q: Substrate for gluconeogenesis in muscle → A: Alanine (alanine-glucose cycle) and glutamine

## PHARMACOLOGY (40 One-Liners)

Q: Drug of choice for status epilepticus (first line) → A: IV Lorazepam (then IV phenytoin/levetiracetam)
Q: Drug of choice for absence seizures → A: Ethosuximide (valproate if mixed or when ethosuximide fails)
Q: Drug of choice for trigeminal neuralgia → A: Carbamazepine
Q: Antiepileptic with highest risk of neural tube defects → A: Valproate (spina bifida risk ~1–2%)
Q: Antiepileptic causing gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, coarse facies → A: Phenytoin
Q: Mechanism of lithium toxicity marker → A: Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (blocks ADH action); also hypothyroidism
Q: Drug causing "red man syndrome" on rapid infusion → A: Vancomycin (mast cell histamine release)
Q: Drug causing irreversible ototoxicity → A: Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, amikacin — cochlear damage, high-frequency loss first)
Q: Drug causing optic neuritis → A: Ethambutol (color vision loss first; monitor with Ishihara plates)
Q: Drug causing retinopathy (macular degeneration) → A: Hydroxychloroquine and chloroquine (bull's eye maculopathy; monitor with OCT)
Q: Drug causing pulmonary fibrosis → A: Amiodarone, bleomycin, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, busulfan
Q: Drug causing serotonin syndrome → A: MAO inhibitors + SSRIs (or combinations increasing serotonin); presents with hyperthermia, clonus, agitation
Q: Drug causing neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) → A: Antipsychotics (haloperidol, clozapine); treat with dantrolene + bromocriptine
Q: Drug of choice for malignant hyperthermia → A: Dantrolene (blocks SR Ca²⁺ release via RYR1)
Q: Antidote for heparin overdose → A: Protamine sulfate
Q: Antidote for warfarin overdose (acute) → A: Fresh frozen plasma (FFP); vitamin K for subacute
Q: Antidote for acetaminophen (paracetamol) overdose → A: N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) — replenishes glutathione
Q: Antidote for organophosphate poisoning → A: Atropine + Pralidoxime (2-PAM within 24–48 hours before "aging")
Q: Antidote for opioid overdose → A: Naloxone
Q: Antidote for benzodiazepine overdose → A: Flumazenil
Q: Drug contraindicated in myasthenia gravis (worsens it) → A: Aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, D-penicillamine (avoid these NMJ blockers)
Q: ACE inhibitor-induced cough — mechanism → A: Bradykinin accumulation (ACE normally degrades bradykinin)
Q: First-line antihypertensive in diabetic nephropathy → A: ACE inhibitor or ARB
Q: First-line antihypertensive in pregnancy-induced hypertension → A: Labetalol or methyldopa (safe in pregnancy)
Q: Drug causing fetal renal dysgenesis/oligohydramnios if used in 2nd/3rd trimester → A: ACE inhibitors and ARBs
Q: Drug used for reversal of non-depolarizing NMJ blockade → A: Neostigmine (anticholinesterase)
Q: Drug used for reversal of rocuronium/vecuronium specifically → A: Sugammadex
Q: Beta-blocker used in acute management of AF with rapid ventricular response → A: Metoprolol IV or diltiazem/verapamil (IV)
Q: Drug of choice for Clostridium difficile diarrhea (first episode) → A: Oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin (metronidazole still used in mild cases)
Q: Mechanism of metformin's action → A: Activates AMPK → inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis + increases insulin sensitivity
Q: SGLT2 inhibitor class benefit beyond glycemia → A: Cardiorenal protection — reduces hospitalization for HF and CKD progression
Q: Drug causing hyperkalemia most commonly → A: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone)
Q: Drug causing hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia → A: Loop diuretics (furosemide)
Q: Drug requiring monitoring of uric acid levels → A: Thiazides and pyrazinamide (increase uric acid, can precipitate gout)
Q: Drug of choice for acute gout → A: NSAIDs (indomethacin or naproxen); colchicine if NSAIDs contraindicated
Q: Drug for long-term prevention of gout → A: Allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor; febuxostat alternative)
Q: Drug causing Stevens-Johnson Syndrome most commonly → A: Allopurinol, antiepileptics (carbamazepine, lamotrigine), sulfonamides, NSAIDs
Q: Drug of choice for H. pylori eradication (triple therapy) → A: PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin for 14 days
Q: Drug causing disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol → A: Metronidazole, tinidazole, cefoperazone, chlorpropamide
Q: Most potent antiplatelet drug combination → A: Aspirin + clopidogrel (DAPT) used post-ACS/PCI

Q: Quelling reaction used to identify → A: Streptococcus pneumoniae (capsular swelling with specific antiserum)
Q: Organism diagnosed by dark-field microscopy → A: Treponema pallidum (syphilis — not visible on Gram stain)

Q: Most common cause of viral encephalitis worldwide → A: Herpes simplex virus (HSV-1) — temporal lobe involvement; treat with acyclovir
Q: Organism causing epidemic typhus → A: Rickettsia prowazekii (louse-borne)
Q: Organism causing scrub typhus → A: Orientia tsutsugamushi (mite/chigger-borne); eschar at bite site
Q: VDRL positive in conditions other than syphilis (biological false positive) → A: SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome, leprosy, malaria, viral infections (infectious mononucleosis)

## FORENSIC MEDICINE (30 One-Liners)

Q: First sign of putrefaction → A: Greenish discoloration of right iliac fossa (cecal bacteria first)
Q: Time of onset of rigor mortis after death → A: 1–2 hours
Q: Time of maximum rigor mortis → A: 12 hours
Q: Time of resolution (secondary flaccidity) of rigor mortis → A: 24–48 hours
Q: Exception to gradual onset of rigor mortis → A: Cadaveric spasm — instantaneous rigor at moment of death
Q: Diatom test is positive in → A: Freshwater drowning (diatoms found in bone marrow/viscera)
Q: Color change in contusion — blue-black to green over time → A: Blue-black (2 days) → Green (4–5 days) → Yellow (7+ days)
Q: Entry vs exit wound in gunshot — skin appearance → A: Entry: smaller, clean, inverted margins; Exit: larger, ragged, everted
Q: Blackening around gunshot wound indicates → A: Contact/close range firing (carbon soot deposition)
Q: Tattooing (stippling) in gunshot wound indicates → A: Intermediate range (gunpowder particles embedded in skin)
Q: Hanging vs strangulation — ligature mark direction in hanging → A: Oblique, running upward toward knot; incomplete (not meeting at back)
Q: Hanging vs strangulation — manner of death usually → A: Hanging = suicidal; Strangulation = homicidal
Q: Hyoid bone most commonly fractured in → A: Manual strangulation
Q: Petechiae (Tardieu spots) most common in → A: Asphyxial deaths (strangulation, smothering)
Q: Death mask (face) in drowning — appearance → A: Pale; cutis anserina (goosebumps) may be present
Q: Saponification (adipocere) — condition required → A: Moist environment; warm + wet; long exposure
Q: Mummification — condition required → A: Dry, hot environment; low humidity; natural preservative
Q: Pugilistic attitude (boxer's position) in fire deaths — cause → A: Heat coagulation of flexor muscles (NOT a vital reaction, NOT life-related)
Q: Most common cause of death in hanging → A: Asphyxia (in incomplete hanging); fracture-dislocation at C2 with high drop (judicial hanging)
Q: Magistrate's inquest in India under CrPC → A: Section 176 CrPC — in cases of death in police custody
Q: Dying declaration — who can record it → A: Any magistrate, or doctor if magistrate not available; dying declaration is admissible evidence
Q: McNaghten's rules apply to → A: Criminal responsibility; person not guilty if they did not know nature of act or that it was wrong (due to mental disorder)
Q: "Doctor's dilemma" in consent → A: Emergency situations: implied consent applies when patient is unconscious and life-threatening situation
Q: Virginity — hymen type that cannot be torn even after intercourse → A: Fimbriated hymen (fleshy, irregular)
Q: Section of IPC for culpable homicide not amounting to murder → A: IPC Section 304
Q: Stab wound characteristics → A: Deeper than wide; sharp weapon; edges clean; direction indicates path of weapon
Q: Laceration — differentiating feature from incised wound → A: Irregular margins, tissue bridges, abraded/contused edges (blunt force)
Q: Sequence of death determination — triple cessation → A: Cessation of heartbeat, breathing, and brain function (but legally, brain death is accepted)
Q: Precipitated sulfur in injury — relevance → A: Not commonly tested; vitreous humor potassium more relevant for PMI estimation
Q: Vitreous potassium increase rate after death → A: ~0.17 mEq/L/hour (used to estimate time since death)

## SPM / COMMUNITY MEDICINE (40 One-Liners)

Q: WHO definition of health → A: "Complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity"
Q: Formula for sensitivity → A: Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN) × 100
Q: Formula for specificity → A: Specificity = TN / (TN + FP) × 100
Q: Formula for PPV → A: PPV = TP / (TP + FP) × 100
Q: Formula for NPV → A: NPV = TN / (TN + FN) × 100
Q: Sensitivity increases means → A: Fewer false negatives; better at ruling out disease (SnOut)
Q: Screening test ideal combination → A: High sensitivity (for screening) + high specificity (for confirmation)
Q: Odds ratio — used in which study design → A: Case-control study
Q: Relative risk — used in which study design → A: Cohort study

Q: Scabies — most common site of burrows → A: Web spaces of fingers; also wrists, areola, glans penis

## ANESTHESIA (25 One-Liners)

Q: MAC of nitrous oxide → A: 104% (cannot achieve general anesthesia with N₂O alone at 1 atmosphere)
Q: Most potent inhalational agent (lowest MAC) → A: Halothane (MAC 0.75%) — but largely replaced by sevoflurane and desflurane
Q: Inhalational agent of choice for inhalational induction (children) → A: Sevoflurane (non-pungent, smooth induction)
Q: Fastest inhalational agent (lowest blood-gas partition coefficient) → A: Desflurane (0.42), then N₂O (0.47)
Q: Malignant hyperthermia trigger agents → A: Succinylcholine + volatile anesthetics (halothane, sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane)
Q: Drug for malignant hyperthermia → A: Dantrolene (blocks RYR1 Ca²⁺ channel)
Q: RSI (Rapid Sequence Intubation) — agents used → A: Propofol (or ketamine/thiopental) + succinylcholine (or rocuronium)
Q: Succinylcholine — contraindications → A: Burns (>48h), crush injuries, denervation injuries, hyperkalemia, malignant hyperthermia susceptibility
Q: Neuromuscular blockade reversal — conventional → A: Neostigmine + glycopyrrolate (to block muscarinic effects of neostigmine)
Q: Selective reversal of rocuronium/vecuronium → A: Sugammadex
Q: Sellick's maneuver (cricoid pressure) — purpose → A: Compress esophagus against cervical spine to prevent regurgitation during RSI
Q: Airway assessment — Mallampati classification → A: I–IV based on visibility of oropharyngeal structures; Class III/IV = difficult airway anticipated
Q: Cormack-Lehane grade 3/4 → A: Cannot see most or any of glottis on direct laryngoscopy
Q: Normal end-tidal CO₂ (ETCO₂) → A: 35–40 mmHg (capnography)
Q: Sudden drop in ETCO₂ to zero → A: Esophageal intubation, cardiac arrest, or circuit disconnection
Q: Bier's block (intravenous regional anesthesia) → A: Used for forearm procedures; tourniquet essential; local anesthetic (lignocaine) injected IV after exsanguination
Q: Most common local anesthetic causing systemic toxicity → A: Bupivacaine (cardiac toxicity — avoid IV injection; treat with IV lipid emulsion)
Q: Maximum safe dose of lignocaine without epinephrine → A: 3–4 mg/kg
Q: Maximum safe dose of lignocaine with epinephrine → A: 7 mg/kg
Q: Spinal anesthesia level for cesarean section → A: T4 (nipple line) — sensory block needed to T4
Q: High spinal — complication → A: Hypotension + bradycardia (sympathectomy); if reaches C3–C5 → respiratory arrest
Q: Total spinal — treatment → A: Immediate intubation, IV fluids, vasopressors (ephedrine/phenylephrine)
Q: Epidural hematoma — presentation post-epidural → A: Back pain + neurological deficits (weakness, bowel/bladder); emergency — surgical decompression
Q: Opioid of choice for post-operative pain (intrathecal) → A: Morphine (preservative-free) — long duration
Q: Emergence agitation — most common after → A: Sevoflurane and desflurane anesthesia in children; also ENT/eye procedures

## RADIOLOGY (25 One-Liners)

Q: FAST exam — fluid location seen first in blunt abdominal trauma → A: Morrison's pouch (hepatorenal space) — right subhepatic
