## What Are PYQs and Why They Are Non-Negotiable

Previous Year Questions (PYQs) are the single most reliable predictor of what NEET PG will test. The exam is pattern-driven — examiners return to the same concepts year after year, sometimes with identical stems, sometimes with slight modifications. Solving and analyzing PYQs is not optional; it is the core of any serious preparation strategy.

## Year-Wise Topic Patterns and Shifts

### 2019
- Heavy emphasis on classical anatomy (nerve supply, muscle attachments)
- Pathology leaned on inflammatory and degenerative conditions
- Pharmacology: mechanisms of action and receptor pharmacology dominant
- Surgery: basic surgical principles, hernias, thyroid

### 2020
- First year with online CBT format fully standardized
- Increased clinical scenario-based questions in Medicine
- Microbiology saw increased emphasis on parasitology
- SPM: National health programs prominently tested

### 2021
- COVID-19-related questions appeared for the first time (PPE protocols, epidemiology basics)
- Pathology: molecular pathology and oncogenes trending upward
- Pharmacology: newer drug classes introduced (SGLT2 inhibitors, DOACs)
- OBG: increased focus on high-risk obstetrics

### 2022
- Paper restructured to 200 questions
- Applied/clinical questions increased across all subjects
- Radiology: imaging-based questions with brief clinical vignettes
- Surgery: emphasis shifted to laparoscopic and minimally invasive concepts

### 2023
- Genetics-based questions rose in Pathology and Biochemistry
- National programs (NHM, RNTCP/NTEP, NPCDCS) heavily tested in SPM
- Pediatrics: vaccination schedule updated per NIS featured prominently
- ENT: audiological tests and vertigo management increased

### 2024
- Integrative clinical vignette style dominant — single stem, 4 closely related options
- Psychiatry: ICD-11 terminology beginning to appear
- Pharmacology: adverse drug reactions and drug interactions prominent
- Dermatology: dermatoscopy and pigmentary disorders increased

## Low-Priority One-Time Appearance Topics

These appeared once in a 6-year window and have not recurred. Read if time permits; do not build study sessions around them.

- Specific embryological defects (e.g., prune belly syndrome mechanism)
- Detailed pharmacokinetics of obscure antimalarials
- Eponymous signs in clinical examination with no diagnostic utility
- Historical/deprecated surgical procedures
- Extremely rare genetic syndromes without clinical pattern relevance
- Detailed parasitology life cycles for uncommon parasites (e.g., Diphyllobothrium)
- Specific histological grading systems for rare tumors

## Strategy for Using PYQs Effectively

### First Reading Phase (Months 1–8)

Do NOT solve PYQs while reading a topic for the first time. At this stage:

1. **Read the standard source** for a topic completely.
2. **After finishing a chapter/topic**, open the subject-wise PYQ bank.
3. Solve PYQs for that specific chapter — this shows you what was asked, not just what exists.
4. For every question you get right: confirm whether you knew the concept or guessed.
5. For every question you get wrong: mark it, understand the concept, write a one-liner in your notebook.
6. Do not time yourself at this stage. The goal is pattern recognition, not speed.

### Revision Round Approach (Months 8–12)

1. **Round 1 (Full revision)**: Solve year-wise papers — aim to do complete 2022, 2023, 2024 papers under simulated exam conditions (3.5 hours, 200 questions).
2. **Round 2 (Error-focused)**: Go back only to marked/wrong questions. Re-read the concept from source.
3. **Round 3 (High-yield scan)**: In the last 4–6 weeks, only read questions you previously got wrong more than once. These are your true weak points.

### The 3x3 Rule for PYQs
- A topic appearing in 3 or more years across a 6-year window → **Mandatory**
- A topic appearing in 2 years → **Important**
- A topic appearing once → **Optional (solve if in your subject strength)**

## How to Analyze a Wrong Answer

Every wrong answer falls into one of three categories. Identifying the category is more important than just noting the right answer.

### Category 1: Concept Gap
**What it means**: You did not know the underlying concept at all.
**How to identify**: You had no idea what the question was asking even after reading the explanation.
**Action**: Go back to the source (textbook/notes), read the concept fresh, then make a brief note in your revision notebook. Re-test yourself in 48 hours.

### Category 2: Recall Gap
**What it means**: You knew the concept but could not retrieve the specific fact (number, name, classification) at the time of solving.
**How to identify**: After reading the explanation, you say "Oh yes, I had read this" — classic hindsight recognition.
**Action**: Add this fact to your one-liner revision list. Use spaced repetition. The issue is memory consolidation, not understanding.

### Category 3: Trick / Distractor
**What it means**: You knew the concept and the fact, but the question was deliberately worded to mislead — negative stems, "except" questions, overlapping answer choices.
**How to identify**: You read the explanation and realize you would have gotten it right if you had read more carefully.
**Action**: Practice reading question stems carefully. Underline the keyword (EXCEPT, MOST COMMON, BEST INITIAL). No content revision needed — this is a test-taking skill issue.

### Tracking System
Maintain an error notebook with columns:
| Q No. | Subject | Topic | Category (C/R/T) | Right Answer | Key Concept |

Review this notebook weekly. If a topic has 3+ errors in Category 1, schedule a dedicated re-read.

## Recommended Order of Solving PYQs

### Option A: Subject-Wise (Recommended for First Pass)
- Solve all PYQs for one subject before moving to the next
- Advantage: You see the entire pattern of that subject across years simultaneously
- Best done: Immediately after completing a subject's first read
- Use subject-wise MCQ books (ACROSS, Arvind Arora, Gobind Rai Garg)

### Option B: Year-Wise (Recommended for Mock/Revision Phase)
- Solve a complete exam year as a timed mock test
- Advantage: Simulates real exam, builds time management, crosses subjects
- Best done: After completing all subjects' first read, in the revision phase
- Use: Official NTA PYQ papers (2022, 2023, 2024) from NMC/PrepLadder/Marrow

### Recommended Sequence
1. Subject-wise during reading phase (months 1–8)
2. Year-wise papers in revision phase (months 9–12): do 2024 → 2023 → 2022 → 2021 → 2020 → 2019 (most recent first — highest predictive value)

## Integration of PYQs with Notes Revision

### The Annotation Method
When reading your notes during revision, keep the PYQ bank open alongside. Whenever a concept in your notes matches a PYQ:
- Put a small star (★) next to it in your notes
- If it was a frequently repeated question, put two stars (★★)
- This creates a visual priority layer in your notes — during the final week, you only read the starred items

### The Reverse Method
When solving a PYQ paper, for every question you get right or wrong:
- Locate the corresponding section in your notes
- Add a marginal note: "NEET 2023" next to the fact
- This reinforces the connection between content and exam context

### PYQ Integration Timeline

| Phase | PYQ Activity |
|---|---|
| Subject reading | Chapter-end PYQs, subject-wise bank |
| Post-first read | 1 full year-wise paper per month |
| Pre-final month | 2 year-wise papers per week + error review |
| Last week | Only error notebook + starred one-liners |
| Day before exam | No new PYQs. Only formulas, normal values, mnemonics |

## Common Mistakes in PYQ Analysis

1. **Solving without analyzing**: Checking the score and moving on without understanding why you got questions wrong is wasted effort.
2. **Only reading explanations for wrong answers**: Also read explanations for correct answers you guessed — guessing right is not the same as knowing.
3. **Ignoring paper pattern shifts**: 2022+ papers are more clinically applied — practicing only 2017–2019 papers creates a mismatch.
4. **Over-indexing on rare PYQ topics**: A question that appeared once in 2016 is not worth building new notes for.
5. **Solving PYQs too early**: In the first month of preparation, PYQs without foundation knowledge become memorization, not learning.
