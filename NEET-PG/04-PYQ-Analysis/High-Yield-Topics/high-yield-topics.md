# High-Yield Topics — NEET PG Master List

Topics in this file appear in NEET PG every year or with very high frequency. For each subject: must-know topics with key points, repeatedly tested one-liners, and critical numerical values.

---

## ANATOMY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Brachial Plexus**
- Roots C5–T1; trunks (upper/middle/lower); divisions; cords (lateral/medial/posterior); terminal branches
- Upper trunk (C5, C6) injury = Erb's palsy: waiter's tip position, loss of shoulder abduction/external rotation/elbow flexion
- Lower trunk (C8, T1) injury = Klumpke's palsy: claw hand, intrinsic muscle wasting, Horner's syndrome
- Long thoracic nerve (C5,6,7) → serratus anterior; injury → winged scapula
- Axillary nerve (C5,C6) → deltoid + teres minor; injury in surgical neck fracture of humerus

**2. Femoral Triangle**
- Boundaries: inguinal ligament (superior), sartorius (lateral), adductor longus (medial), iliopsoas + pectineus (floor)
- Contents (lateral to medial): NAVY — Nerve, Artery, Vein, Y-fronts (empty space → femoral canal)
- Femoral canal contains: lymph node of Cloquet (Rosenmuller), areolar tissue, lymphatics
- Femoral ring boundaries: inguinal ligament (anterior), lacunar ligament (medial), pectineal ligament (posterior), femoral vein (lateral)

**3. Inguinal Canal**
- Anterior wall: external oblique aponeurosis (entire), internal oblique (lateral 1/3)
- Posterior wall: transversalis fascia (entire), conjoint tendon (medial 1/3)
- Floor: inguinal ligament, lacunar ligament medially
- Roof: arching fibers of internal oblique and transversus abdominis
- MALT: Males = round ligament? No — Males: spermatic cord. Females: round ligament of uterus
- Hesselbach's triangle: inferior epigastric artery (lateral), rectus sheath (medial), inguinal ligament (inferior) — direct hernia site

**4. Circle of Willis**
- Anterior circulation: ICA → ACA + MCA
- Communicating arteries: ACoA (connects two ACAs), PCoA (connects ICA to PCA)
- PCA from basilar artery
- MCA occlusion → contralateral hemiplegia (face + arm > leg) + aphasia if dominant

**5. Portal Venous System and Portosystemic Anastomoses**
- Portal vein formed by: superior mesenteric vein + splenic vein (behind neck of pancreas)
- Sites of portosystemic anastomosis: lower esophagus (varices), rectum (hemorrhoids), paraumbilical (caput medusae), bare area of liver, retroperitoneum

**6. Lymphatic Drainage**
- Breast → mainly axillary (anterior/pectoral group → central → apical); also internal mammary, supraclavicular
- Testis → para-aortic (lumbar) nodes (NOT inguinal — only scrotal skin goes to inguinal)
- Lower limb → superficial inguinal (horizontal group: below inguinal ligament; vertical group: along great saphenous vein)

**7. Facial Nerve (CN VII)**
- Motor + parasympathetic + taste (anterior 2/3 tongue via chorda tympani)
- Upper motor neuron lesion: lower face only paralysis
- Lower motor neuron (Bell's palsy): entire ipsilateral face (including forehead)
- Branches: temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, cervical (Two Zebras Bit My Cat)

**8. Rotator Cuff**
- SITS: Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis
- Supraspinatus: initiates abduction (first 0–15°), most commonly torn
- Subscapularis: only internal rotator in the group
- Infraspinatus + Teres minor: external rotation

### Classic One-Liners
- Nerve injured in fracture of surgical neck of humerus → Axillary nerve
- Nerve injured in fracture of shaft of humerus → Radial nerve (Saturday night palsy)
- Nerve injured in fracture of medial epicondyle → Ulnar nerve
- Nerve injured in fracture of fibular neck → Common peroneal nerve
- Muscle supplied by all cervical motor roots → Levator scapulae
- Only muscle attached to lesser trochanter → Iliopsoas
- "Carrying angle" at elbow is formed at → Cubitus valgus (normal ~15° in females)
- Meckel's diverticulum: 2 inches long, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2% population

### Critical Numbers
- Femoral canal length: ~1.25 cm
- Inguinal canal length: ~4 cm
- Spinal cord ends at: L1–L2 in adults; L3 in neonates
- CSF total volume: ~150 mL; produced at ~0.35 mL/min; replaced 3–4x/day
- Thoracic duct: drains into junction of left subclavian + left internal jugular vein

## PHYSIOLOGY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Cardiac Cycle and Pressure-Volume Loop**
- Phases: isovolumetric contraction → rapid ejection → slow ejection → isovolumetric relaxation → rapid filling → slow filling → atrial systole
- Normal LVEDP: 8–12 mmHg; LVEDV: ~120 mL; SV: ~70 mL; EF: 55–65%
- Aortic valve opens when LV pressure > aortic diastolic pressure (~80 mmHg)
- Mitral valve opens when LV pressure < LA pressure (~12 mmHg)

**2. Lung Volumes and Capacities**
- TV: 500 mL; IRV: 3000 mL; ERV: 1100 mL; RV: 1200 mL
- TLC = 6000 mL; VC = 4800 mL; FRC = 2300 mL; IC = 3500 mL
- FRC = ERV + RV; IC = TV + IRV; VC = IRV + TV + ERV; TLC = VC + RV
- Dead space: anatomical (~150 mL); physiological = anatomical + alveolar
- FEV1/FVC: obstructive < 70%; restrictive: both reduced, ratio normal or increased

**3. Renal Transport**
- Glucose Tm (transport maximum): ~375 mg/min; splay begins at renal threshold (~180 mg/dL plasma)
- PAH: used to measure RPF; Tm-PAH = ~80 mg/min
- Inulin: used to measure GFR (freely filtered, not reabsorbed or secreted)
- Normal GFR: 125 mL/min; filtration fraction = GFR/RPF = 125/625 = 20%

**4. Acid-Base Compensation Formulas**
- Metabolic acidosis: expected pCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃ + 8 ± 2 (Winter's formula)
- Metabolic alkalosis: expected pCO₂ = 0.7 × HCO₃ + 21 ± 2
- Respiratory acidosis (acute): HCO₃ increases 1 mEq/L per 10 mmHg rise in pCO₂
- Respiratory acidosis (chronic): HCO₃ increases 3.5 mEq/L per 10 mmHg rise in pCO₂

**5. Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve**
- P50 (normal) = 26 mmHg (pO₂ at which Hb is 50% saturated)
- Right shift (↓ affinity, ↑ O₂ release): ↑ temperature, ↑ CO₂, ↑ H⁺ (↓pH), ↑ 2,3-DPG, sickle Hb
- Left shift (↑ affinity, ↓ O₂ release): ↓ temperature, ↓ CO₂, ↓ H⁺ (↑pH), ↓ 2,3-DPG, fetal Hb (HbF), CO poisoning, MetHb

### Classic One-Liners
- Pacemaker of the heart → SA node (intrinsic rate 60–100/min)
- AV node intrinsic rate → 40–60/min
- Purkinje fiber intrinsic rate → 20–40/min
- Frank-Starling law → increased preload → increased stroke volume (up to a point)
- Hormone that increases GFR → Angiotensin II (efferent arteriolar constriction)
- Oxygen carrying capacity of blood → 20 mL O₂/100 mL blood
- CO₂ transported mainly as → Bicarbonate (70%), carbamino compounds (23%), dissolved (7%)

### Critical Numbers
- Normal CVP: 0–8 mmHg; PCWP: 6–12 mmHg
- Normal arterial blood gas: pH 7.35–7.45; pCO₂ 35–45; pO₂ 80–100; HCO₃ 22–26
- Bowman's capsule pressure: ~18 mmHg; plasma oncotic: ~28 mmHg; GHP: ~60 mmHg
- Net filtration pressure = 60 − 18 − 28 = 14 mmHg
- Threshold for glucose in urine (renal threshold) = 180 mg/dL

## BIOCHEMISTRY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Essential Amino Acids (PVT TIM HaLL)**
- Phenylalanine, Valine, Tryptophan, Threonine, Isoleucine, Methionine, Histidine, arginine (semi-essential), Leucine, Lysine
- Glucogenic only: all except leucine and lysine (ketogenic only)
- Both glucogenic and ketogenic: Phe, Tyr, Trp, Ile

**2. Amino Acid Disorders**
- PKU (Phenylketonuria): phenylalanine hydroxylase deficiency; musty urine; fair skin/hair; intellectual disability
- Alkaptonuria: homogentisic acid oxidase deficiency; black urine; ochronosis; arthritis
- Homocystinuria: cystathionine β-synthase deficiency; lens dislocation downward (contrast Marfan: upward); DVT
- Maple Syrup Urine Disease: branched-chain ketoacid decarboxylase deficiency; sweet-smelling urine; BCAAs accumulate

**3. Vitamins**
- B1 (Thiamine): coenzyme in pyruvate/α-KG dehydrogenase, TKase; deficiency = beriberi, Wernicke's encephalopathy
- B2 (Riboflavin): FAD/FMN; angular stomatitis, corneal vascularization
- B3 (Niacin): NAD/NADP; deficiency = pellagra (4Ds: diarrhea, dermatitis, dementia, death); Hartnup disease
- B6 (Pyridoxine): transamination/decarboxylation; deficiency with INH (give prophylactic B6); sideroblastic anemia
- B12 (Cobalamin): methylmalonyl-CoA mutase + methionine synthase; megaloblastic anemia + subacute combined degeneration
- Folate: thymidylate synthesis; megaloblastic anemia; NTD prevention (periconceptional)
- Vitamin A: rhodopsin synthesis; deficiency = night blindness, Bitot's spots, keratomalacia; toxicity = pseudotumor cerebri
- Vitamin D: calcitriol (1,25-OH-D); 1α-hydroxylase in kidney; deficiency = rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults)
- Vitamin K: γ-carboxylation of II, VII, IX, X, C, S; deficiency → hemorrhagic disease of newborn

**4. Glycogen Storage Diseases**
- Type I (Von Gierke): G6Pase deficiency; liver + kidney; hypoglycemia, lactic acidosis, hyperuricemia
- Type II (Pompe): α-1,4-glucosidase (acid maltase); lysosomal; cardiomegaly; "heart" disease
- Type III (Cori): debranching enzyme; liver + muscle
- Type V (McArdle): muscle phosphorylase; exercise-induced cramps; myoglobinuria; no rise in lactate with exercise
- Type VI (Hers): liver phosphorylase; benign hepatomegaly

**5. Enzyme Kinetics**
- Michaelis-Menten equation: V = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S])
- Km = substrate concentration at ½ Vmax; low Km = high affinity
- Competitive inhibition: Km increases, Vmax unchanged
- Non-competitive inhibition: Vmax decreases, Km unchanged
- Uncompetitive inhibition: both Km and Vmax decrease

### Classic One-Liners
- Only urea cycle enzyme NOT in liver mitochondria → Argininosuccinate synthetase and lyase (cytoplasmic)
- Rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis → HMG-CoA reductase (inhibited by statins)
- Rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis → Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1)
- Rate-limiting enzyme of gluconeogenesis → Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase
- Biotin is cofactor for → Pyruvate carboxylase, Acetyl-CoA carboxylase, Propionyl-CoA carboxylase
- Vitamin C role in collagen → Hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues
- Respiratory chain final electron acceptor → Molecular oxygen (O₂) at Complex IV

### Critical Numbers
- ATP yield: glucose (aerobic) = 30–32 ATP; anaerobic glycolysis = 2 ATP
- Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD): maintains NADPH; protects RBCs from oxidative damage
- pH optimum: pepsin = 2; trypsin = 8; amylase = 7
- Normal serum albumin: 3.5–5 g/dL; half-life ~20 days
- Prealbumin (transthyretin): better marker of acute nutritional status; half-life ~2 days

**3. Amyloidosis**
- AA amyloid: serum amyloid A; chronic inflammation (TB, RA, osteomyelitis, Crohn's, FMF); kidney most affected
- AL amyloid: immunoglobulin light chains; multiple myeloma, primary amyloidosis; heart most affected
- Staining: Congo red → apple-green birefringence under polarized light
- β-pleated sheet secondary structure (non-branching fibrils)

**4. Cell Injury and Death**
- Apoptosis: caspase-dependent; programmed; no inflammation; cell shrinkage; chromatin condensation; DNA ladder pattern
- Necrosis: uncontrolled; inflammation; cell swelling; membrane disruption
- Coagulative necrosis: most organs (heart, kidney); ischemia; ghost outlines preserved
- Liquefactive necrosis: brain; pus (neutrophil enzymes); abscess
- Caseous necrosis: TB; cheese-like; surrounded by granuloma
- Fat necrosis: breast (trauma), pancreas (lipase); saponification (calcium soap)
- Gangrenous necrosis: limbs (dry = coagulative; wet = + liquefactive)

**5. Oncogenes and Tumor Suppressors**

| RB | Cell cycle checkpoint | Retinoblastoma, osteosarcoma |
| p53 | Genome guardian | Li-Fraumeni; most human cancers |

| APC | Wnt pathway | FAP, colon cancer |

### Classic One-Liners
- "Starry sky" pattern → Burkitt's lymphoma (tingible-body macrophages)
- Reed-Sternberg cells → Hodgkin's lymphoma (owl-eye appearance)
- Psammoma bodies → Papillary thyroid carcinoma, serous ovarian carcinoma, meningioma, mesothelioma
- Flame cells (Russell bodies) → Multiple myeloma
- Signet ring cells → Gastric carcinoma (linitis plastica)
- "Call-Exner bodies" → Granulosa cell tumor of ovary
- Schiller-Duval bodies → Yolk sac tumor
- Reinke crystals → Leydig cell tumor

### Critical Numbers
- Mitotic index threshold in melanoma: >1 mitosis/mm² = adverse prognosis
- Breslow depth: >4 mm = T4 melanoma
- Minimum Ki-67 for high-grade tumors varies by site but >20% generally high proliferation
- CRP half-life: ~19 hours; useful for monitoring acute inflammation

## PHARMACOLOGY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Antibiotics — Mechanisms and Key Side Effects**
| Class | Mechanism | Key Toxicity |
|---|---|---|
| Penicillins | Inhibit PBP → cell wall synthesis | Allergy, hemolytic anemia |
| Cephalosporins | Inhibit PBP (β-lactam) | Allergy (10% cross-reactivity with PCN) |
| Aminoglycosides | 30S ribosome → misreads mRNA | Nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity (irreversible) |
| Tetracyclines | 30S ribosome → tRNA binding | Teeth/bone deposition, photosensitivity, Fanconi |
| Macrolides | 50S ribosome → translocation | QT prolongation, CYP450 inhibition |
| Fluoroquinolones | DNA gyrase/topoisomerase IV | Tendinopathy, QT prolongation, avoid <18 yr |
| Metronidazole | DNA strand breaks | Disulfiram-like reaction, peripheral neuropathy |
| Rifampicin | RNA polymerase | Orange body fluids, CYP450 inducer |
| Vancomycin | Cell wall (peptidoglycan terminal D-Ala-D-Ala) | Red man syndrome, nephrotoxicity |
| Chloramphenicol | 50S → peptidyl transferase | Aplastic anemia (idiosyncratic), gray baby syndrome |

**2. Antiepileptics**
| Drug | Mechanism | Uses | Teratogenicity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phenytoin | Na⁺ channel blocker | Focal, generalized tonic-clonic | Neural tube defects, fetal hydantoin syndrome |
| Valproate | Multiple (Na⁺, Ca²⁺, GABA) | Broad spectrum | Spina bifida (highest NTD risk) |
| Carbamazepine | Na⁺ channel | Focal, trigeminal neuralgia | Neural tube defects, craniofacial |
| Lamotrigine | Na⁺ channel | Broad spectrum | Relatively safer in pregnancy |
| Ethosuximide | T-type Ca²⁺ channel | Absence seizures only | |
| Benzodiazepines | GABA-A (Cl⁻ channel frequency) | Status epilepticus (IV lorazepam/diazepam) | |
| Phenobarbitone | GABA-A (Cl⁻ channel duration) | Neonatal seizures, status | |

**3. Beta-Blockers**
- Cardioselective (β1): atenolol, metoprolol, bisoprolol, esmolol (A-MBE)
- Non-selective: propranolol, nadolol, timolol, carvedilol (also α1), labetalol (also α1)
- ISA: pindolol, acebutolol
- Contraindicated: asthma (non-selective), Prinzmetal angina, decompensated HF
- Sudden withdrawal → rebound tachycardia, angina, MI

**4. Organophosphate Poisoning and Management**
- Mechanism: irreversible inhibition of acetylcholinesterase → ACh accumulation
- Muscarinic effects (SLUDGE): Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis
- Also: DUMBELS (additional: Bradycardia, Bronchoconstriction, Eye miosis)
- Nicotinic effects: muscle fasciculations → paralysis (respiratory failure = cause of death)
- CNS: seizures, coma
- Management: Atropine (blocks muscarinic, titrate to dry secretions) + Pralidoxime (regenerates AChE, within 24–48 h before aging)

**5. Drug-Induced Conditions**
| Drug | Condition Caused |
|---|---|
| Amiodarone | Pulmonary fibrosis, thyroid dysfunction, corneal deposits, blue-gray skin |
| Methotrexate | Hepatotoxicity, megaloblastic anemia, pulmonary fibrosis |
| Clozapine | Agranulocytosis (mandatory WBC monitoring), metabolic syndrome |
| Succinylcholine | Malignant hyperthermia (with halothane); hyperkalemia |
| ACE inhibitors | Dry cough (bradykinin), angioedema, hyperkalemia, teratogenic |
| Statins | Myopathy, rhabdomyolysis (↑ CPK), hepatotoxicity |
| Lithium | Nephrogenic DI, hypothyroidism, Ebstein anomaly (in pregnancy) |
| Hydroxychloroquine | Retinopathy (macular degeneration) — screen with OCT |
| Ethambutol | Optic neuritis (color vision loss first) |
| Gentamicin | Ototoxicity (cochlear → high-frequency hearing loss) |

### Classic One-Liners
- Drug of choice for status epilepticus → IV Lorazepam (then phenytoin/levetiracetam)
- Drug of choice for absence seizures → Ethosuximide (valproate if mixed)
- Drug of choice for Pseudomonas aeruginosa → Anti-pseudomonal penicillin ± aminoglycoside (or ciprofloxacin)
- Drug causing gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, coarse facies → Phenytoin
- Antidote for heparin → Protamine sulfate
- Antidote for warfarin → Vitamin K (FFP for immediate reversal)
- Antidote for acetaminophen toxicity → N-acetylcysteine

### Critical Numbers
- Therapeutic index = TD50/ED50; narrow TI drugs: lithium, digoxin, warfarin, phenytoin, aminoglycosides, vancomycin
- Digoxin toxicity level: >2 ng/mL
- Lithium toxicity: >1.5 mEq/L (therapeutic 0.6–1.2 mEq/L)
- Warfarin target INR: atrial fibrillation/DVT/PE = 2–3; mechanical heart valve = 2.5–3.5

## MICROBIOLOGY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Hepatitis Serology**
| Marker | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| HBsAg | Active infection (acute or chronic) |
| Anti-HBs | Immunity (vaccination or recovery) |
| Anti-HBc IgM | Acute HBV infection; present during window period |
| Anti-HBc IgG | Past infection or chronic infection |
| HBeAg | High infectivity, active viral replication |
| Anti-HBe | Seroconversion, lower infectivity |
| HBV DNA | Most sensitive marker of replication |
| Window period | HBsAg cleared, anti-HBs not yet detectable; only anti-HBc IgM positive |

**2. HIV Immunology and Management**
- CD4 < 500: start ART (all patients regardless of CD4 count per current guidelines)
- CD4 < 200: PCP prophylaxis (cotrimoxazole)
- CD4 < 100: Toxoplasma prophylaxis (cotrimoxazole also covers); MAC prophylaxis (azithromycin)
- CD4 < 50: MAC prophylaxis (azithromycin/clarithromycin); CMV retinitis risk
- Preferred ART first line (India): TDF + 3TC + DTG (NACO 2023 guidelines)
- Western blot: confirmatory test

**3. Mycobacterium tuberculosis**
- Culture: Lowenstein-Jensen medium (growth in 4–8 weeks); Middlebrook 7H9 (liquid, faster)
- Staining: Ziehl-Neelsen (acid-fast; fuchsin-based); fluorescent: auramine-rhodamine
- Drug sensitivity: first-line = HRZE (isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol)
- MDR-TB: resistant to at least H + R
- XDR-TB: MDR + resistant to any fluoroquinolone + at least one injectable second-line drug
- Latent TB treatment: 6H (6 months INH) or 3HR or 1 month rifapentine + INH (3HP)

**4. Gram-Positive Organisms**
- Staph aureus: coagulase-positive; protein A (anti-phagocytic); toxins (TSST-1, exfoliatin); MRSA → vancomycin
- Staph epidermidis: coagulase-negative; slime layer (biofilm); catheter infections
- Strep pyogenes (Group A): streptolysin O + S; M protein; causes pharyngitis, rheumatic fever, post-strep GN
- Strep pneumoniae: diplococci; quellung reaction; pneumonia, meningitis, otitis media
- Clostridium tetani: drumstick spore; tetanospasmin (blocks glycine release at Renshaw cells → spastic paralysis)
- Clostridium botulinum: blocks ACh release at NMJ → flaccid paralysis; ABCDEF toxins; A and B most virulent

**5. Malaria Species Differences**
| Feature | P. falciparum | P. vivax | P. malariae | P. ovale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| RBC size | Normal/small | Enlarged | Normal | Enlarged |
| Schüffner's dots | Absent | Present | Absent | Present |
| Fever cycle | 36–48h (malignant tertian) | 48h (benign tertian) | 72h (quartan) | 48h (ovale tertian) |
| Hypnozoites | No | Yes | No | Yes |
| Multiple parasites/RBC | Common | Rare | Rare | Rare |
| Dangerous | Cerebral malaria | — | Nephrotic syndrome | — |
| Radical cure drug | Not needed | Primaquine | Not needed | Primaquine |

### Classic One-Liners
- Anthrax toxin components → Protective antigen + Lethal factor + Edema factor
- Organism causing "sulfur granules" in pus → Actinomyces israelii
- Organism growing on tellurite medium → Corynebacterium diphtheriae (black colonies)
- Organism growing on BCYE medium → Legionella pneumophila
- Organism associated with "school of fish" pattern → Haemophilus ducreyi (chancroid)
- Culture medium for N. gonorrhoeae → Thayer-Martin (chocolate agar + antibiotics)
- ELISA sensitivity; Western blot specificity → used together for HIV confirmation

### Critical Numbers
- Infective dose of Vibrio cholerae: 10⁶–10⁸ organisms
- BCG vaccination given at birth → protects against miliary TB and TB meningitis
- Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC): lowest concentration that inhibits visible growth
- Incubation period: Rabies = 20–60 days (range 10 days–7 years); Tetanus = 5–14 days; Cholera = 2–3 days

## FORENSIC MEDICINE

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Rigor Mortis**
- Definition: postmortem stiffening due to ATP depletion → actin-myosin cross-bridge lock
- Onset: 1–2 hours after death (starts face/neck → proceeds downward)
- Maximum: 12 hours
- Resolution (secondary flaccidity): 24–48 hours
- Exceptions to order of Nysten's law: cadaveric spasm (instantaneous rigor at time of death)
- Cold weather slows onset; hot weather accelerates

**2. Wound Types**
- Abrasion: epidermis only; direction of force identifiable by tissue tags
- Contusion (bruise): hemorrhage in tissue without skin break; color changes over days (red → blue → green → yellow)
- Laceration: tearing of skin from blunt force; irregular margins, tissue bridges
- Incised wound: clean-cut, sharp weapon; longer than deep; clean margins
- Stab wound: deeper than long; puncture type; depends on weapon

**3. Hanging vs Strangulation**
| Feature | Hanging | Strangulation |
|---|---|---|
| Ligature mark | Oblique, incomplete, high up (above thyroid cartilage), mark runs upward to knot | Horizontal, complete, low, transverse |
| Skin color | Face pale (typical), congested if incomplete | Face congested, cyanosed |
| Hyoid fracture | Less common (typical hanging) | More common (manual strangulation) |
| Petechiae | Uncommon in typical hanging | Common (conjunctival, facial) |
| Manner | Usually suicidal | Usually homicidal |
| Dribbling of saliva | Present (from angle of mouth) | Absent |

**4. Decomposition**
- Putrefaction begins: 2–3 days (warm climate), 3–5 days (cold)
- Greenish discoloration: right iliac fossa first (cecum area, highest bacteria)
- Bloating: gas production; skin slippage
- Adipocere: saponification of body fat; moist environment; provides evidence after months
- Mummification: dry, hot conditions; body preserves in desiccated state

**5. Age Estimation**
- Neonates (0–2 years): ossification centers (radiological)
- Children/Adolescents: dental eruption, long bone growth plates, Risser's sign (iliac crest)
- Adults: skull sutures, pubic symphysis changes, dental wear (Gustafson's method)
- Gustafson's criteria: Attrition, Periodontosis, Secondary dentine, Cementum apposition, Root resorption, Transparency of root

### Classic One-Liners
- Cadaveric spasm → instantaneous rigor; indicates position at time of death
- Tardieu spots → petechiae (ecchymoses) in asphyxia deaths
- Hyoid bone most often fractured in → manual strangulation
- Contact range gunshot: blackening (soot), tattooing, burning around wound
- Entry wound in gunshot: smaller, clean, inverted margins; exit: larger, everted, ragged
- Pugilistic attitude in fire deaths: due to heat coagulation of muscles (NOT vital reaction)
- Diatom test: positive in drowning (fresh water); diatoms found in bone marrow

### Critical Numbers
- Rigor mortis maximum at: 12 hours
- Time for secondary flaccidity: 24–48 hours
- Stomach contents digestion time: ~4–6 hours (useful for time since last meal)
- Vitreous potassium: increases ~0.17 mEq/L/hour postmortem (used to estimate time of death)

## SPM / COMMUNITY MEDICINE

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Epidemiological Measures**
- Incidence: new cases / population at risk / time period
- Prevalence = Incidence × Duration of disease
- Case fatality rate (CFR) = Deaths from disease / Cases × 100
- Attack rate = Cases / Population exposed × 100 (used in epidemics)
- RR (Relative Risk) = Incidence in exposed / Incidence in unexposed (used in cohort studies)
- OR (Odds Ratio) = (a×d) / (b×c) from 2×2 table (used in case-control studies)
- PAR (Population Attributable Risk) = Incidence total − Incidence unexposed

**2. Screening Test Calculations**
- Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN) — ability to detect disease (rules OUT if negative — SnOut)
- Specificity = TN / (TN + FP) — ability to rule out disease (rules IN if positive — SpIn)
- PPV = TP / (TP + FP) — depends on prevalence
- NPV = TN / (TN + FN) — depends on prevalence
- High prevalence → PPV rises, NPV falls
- Likelihood ratio+ = Sensitivity / (1 − Specificity)

**3. National Health Programs (Key Facts)**
- RNTCP → renamed NTEP (National Tuberculosis Elimination Programme); target TB elimination by 2025 (India)

## GENERAL MEDICINE

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Diabetes Mellitus**
- Diagnosis: FPG ≥126 mg/dL; 2h OGTT ≥200 mg/dL; HbA1c ≥6.5%; Random PG ≥200 + symptoms
- Targets: HbA1c <7% (general); <6.5% if young, newly diagnosed; <8% if elderly/comorbid
- First-line: Metformin (unless eGFR <30); then add SGLT2i (if CKD/HF) or GLP-1 agonist (if CVD)
- DKA: pH <7.3, HCO3 <18, ketones positive; management = IV fluids + insulin infusion + K⁺ replacement
- HHS: glucose >600 mg/dL; osmolality >320 mOsm/kg; minimal ketones; elderly T2DM

**2. Thyroid Disorders**
- Hypothyroid: ↑TSH, ↓T4; treatment = levothyroxine
- Hyperthyroid: ↓TSH, ↑T4/T3; Graves = TSH-R antibodies (TRAB)
- Subclinical hypothyroid: ↑TSH, normal T4 — treat if TSH >10 or symptomatic
- Thyroid storm: high fever, tachycardia, confusion; treat with PTU + beta-blocker + iodine (Lugol's) + steroids
- Myxedema coma: severe hypothyroid; IV T3/T4 + supportive care

**3. Cardiac Conditions**
- MI diagnosis: troponin rise + fall; ST elevation in ≥2 contiguous leads (STEMI)
- STEMI territories: II, III, aVF = inferior (RCA); V1–V4 = anterior (LAD); I, aVL, V5, V6 = lateral (LCx)
- LBBB: broad notched R in V5/V6; deep S in V1; QRS >120 ms
- RBBB: rSR' (M pattern) in V1; wide S in V5/V6; QRS >120 ms
- Heart failure: HFrEF (EF <40%) → ACEi/ARB + BB + MRA + SGLT2i; HFpEF = diuretics + control comorbidities

## GENERAL SURGERY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Hernia**
- Inguinal hernia: indirect (lateral to inferior epigastric, through deep ring, most common overall, more in men) vs direct (medial to inferior epigastric, through Hesselbach's triangle, Nuck's canal in women)
- Femoral hernia: through femoral ring; more common in women; high strangulation risk
- Richter's hernia: only part of bowel wall in sac (antimesenteric border); can strangulate without obstruction
- Littre's hernia: Meckel's diverticulum in hernia sac
- Amyand's hernia: appendix in inguinal hernia sac

**2. Burns**
- Rule of 9s (adults): head+neck = 9%; each arm = 9%; anterior trunk = 18%; posterior trunk = 18%; each leg = 18%; perineum = 1%
- Children: Lund-Browder chart (head relatively larger in children)
- Parkland formula: 4 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA; give half in first 8 hours, rest in next 16 hours; use Ringer's lactate
- Second degree (partial thickness): blistering; painful; heals spontaneously
- Third degree (full thickness): no pain (nerve destruction); needs grafting

**3. Shock Classification**
| Class | Blood Loss (mL) | HR | BP | RR | Mental Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | <750 (<15%) | <100 | Normal | 14–20 | Normal |
| II | 750–1500 (15–30%) | 100–120 | Normal | 20–30 | Anxious |
| III | 1500–2000 (30–40%) | 120–140 | ↓ | 30–40 | Confused |
| IV | >2000 (>40%) | >140 | ↓↓ | >35 | Lethargic/unconscious |

### Critical Numbers
- Appendix: McBurney's point = junction of lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of umbilicus-to-ASIS line
- Alvarado score: ≥7 = appendicitis; migration of pain, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, RIF tenderness, rebound tenderness, elevated temp, leukocytosis, shift to left
- Bowen's disease: squamous cell carcinoma in situ of skin

## OBG

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Antepartum Hemorrhage (APH)**
| Feature | Placenta Previa | Abruptio Placentae |
|---|---|---|
| Pain | Painless | Painful |
| Bleeding | Revealed, bright red | Concealed or revealed, dark red |
| Uterus | Soft, non-tender | Hard ("woody"), tender |
| Fetal heart | Usually audible | May be absent (if severe) |
| Coagulopathy | Rare | Common (DIC) |
| Diagnosis | USG | Clinical (USG may be normal) |

**2. Pre-eclampsia and Eclampsia**
- Diagnosis: BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks + proteinuria (≥300 mg/24h or PCR ≥0.3)
- Severe features: BP ≥160/110; proteinuria ≥5g/24h; thrombocytopenia <100k; elevated creatinine; pulmonary edema; visual/cerebral symptoms
- HELLP: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelet count (<100k)
- Management: antihypertensives (labetalol, nifedipine); seizure prophylaxis = MgSO4
- MgSO4 toxicity: loss of deep tendon reflexes (first sign); respiratory depression; antidote = calcium gluconate

**3. Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) — 4Ts**
- Tone (uterine atony) — 80% of PPH
- Trauma (lacerations, uterine rupture, inversion)
- Tissue (retained placenta, products)
- Thrombin (coagulopathy — DIC)
- Active management: Oxytocin 10 IU IM at delivery of anterior shoulder; controlled cord traction; uterine massage

**4. Stages of Labor**
- Stage 1: onset of labor to full dilation (10 cm); latent phase (0–4 cm); active phase (4–10 cm)
- Stage 2: full dilation to delivery of baby
- Stage 3: delivery of baby to delivery of placenta (up to 30 min)
- Bishop Score: used for cervical ripening assessment; >8 = favorable (components: dilation, effacement, station, consistency, position)

### Critical Numbers
- Term pregnancy: 37–42 weeks
- Preterm: <37 weeks; post-term: >42 weeks
- Normal blood loss at delivery: <500 mL vaginal; <1000 mL cesarean
- PPH threshold: >500 mL after vaginal, >1000 mL after CS
- MgSO4 therapeutic serum level: 4–8 mEq/L

- Exchange transfusion threshold: varies by gestational age and hours of life (use phototherapy charts)

## ENT

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Tuning Fork Tests**
| Test | Normal | CHL | SNHL |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rinne | AC > BC (positive) | BC > AC (negative Rinne) | AC > BC (positive Rinne — false positive in SNHL) |
| Weber | Midline | Lateralizes to affected (worse) ear | Lateralizes to unaffected (better) ear |
| Schwabach | Equal to examiner | Prolonged (Schwabach increased) | Reduced (Schwabach decreased) |
- AC = air conduction; BC = bone conduction
- False negative Rinne: when SNHL is severe — BC>AC appears but it's actually the other ear hearing by bone

**2. Cholesteatoma**
- Squamous epithelium in middle ear (not its normal location)
- Types: congenital (behind intact TM), acquired primary (retraction pocket), acquired secondary (migration through perforation)
- Dangerous otitis media: associated with posterosuperior attic/pars flaccida perforation
- Complications: meningitis, brain abscess, facial nerve palsy, labyrinthitis, sigmoid sinus thrombosis
- Treatment: surgery (mastoidectomy)

### Critical Numbers
- Normal hearing threshold: 0–25 dB HL
- Mild hearing loss: 26–40 dB; moderate: 41–60 dB; severe: 61–80 dB; profound: >80 dB
- Pure tone average (PTA): average of 500, 1000, 2000, 4000 Hz thresholds

## PSYCHIATRY

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Schizophrenia**
- Schneider's First Rank Symptoms: auditory hallucinations (3rd person, running commentary), thought insertion/withdrawal/broadcasting, somatic passivity, made feelings/impulses/actions, delusional perception
- Positive symptoms: hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder
- Negative symptoms: flat affect, alogia, anhedonia, avolition, asociality
- Treatment: atypical antipsychotics first line (risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole)
- Clozapine: treatment-resistant schizophrenia; agranulocytosis risk

**2. Antipsychotic Side Effects**
| Side Effect | Drug(s) | Management |
|---|---|---|
| EPS (acute dystonia) | Haloperidol, FGAs | Anticholinergics (benztropine) |
| Akathisia | FGAs | Beta-blockers, benzodiazepines |
| Tardive dyskinesia | Chronic FGA use | Reduce/stop FGA; consider valbenazine |
| Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome | Any antipsychotic | Stop drug; dantrolene, bromocriptine |
| Metabolic syndrome | Olanzapine, clozapine | Lifestyle; consider switch |
| Agranulocytosis | Clozapine | Weekly CBC |
| Hyperprolactinemia | Risperidone, FGAs | Switch to aripiprazole |

### Critical Numbers
- Duration criteria: schizophrenia ≥6 months (DSM-5); active symptoms ≥1 month
- Major depressive episode: ≥2 weeks, ≥5 symptoms including depressed mood or anhedonia
- Bipolar I: manic episode ≥7 days (or any if hospitalization); depressive episode common but not required for diagnosis
- ECT: typically 6–12 sessions; most effective in severe/treatment-resistant depression, catatonia

## ORTHOPEDICS

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Fracture Classifications**
- Garden classification (neck of femur): I = incomplete/impacted; II = complete undisplaced; III = complete partially displaced; IV = complete fully displaced
- Neer classification (proximal humerus): 2, 3, or 4-part fractures based on displacement of surgical/anatomical neck, greater/lesser tuberosity
- Salter-Harris (physeal fractures): I–V; II = most common; V = crush (worst prognosis)
- Gustilo-Anderson (open fractures): I = wound <1 cm clean; II = 1–10 cm; III = >10 cm or contaminated

**2. Compartment Syndrome — 6 Ps**
- Pain (disproportionate, on passive stretch)
- Pressure (high compartment pressure >30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of diastolic)
- Paresthesia
- Paralysis (late)
- Pallor
- Pulselessness (very late)
- Treatment: immediate fasciotomy

### Critical Numbers
- Compartment pressure for fasciotomy: >30 mmHg, or within 30 mmHg of diastolic blood pressure
- Bone healing time (approximate): upper limb ~3 months; lower limb ~6 months; spine varies

## ANESTHESIA

### Must-Know Topics

**1. Minimum Alveolar Concentration (MAC)**
| Agent | MAC (%) |
|---|---|
| Nitrous oxide (N₂O) | 104 (cannot achieve general anesthesia alone at 1 atm) |
| Halothane | 0.75 |
| Isoflurane | 1.15 |
| Sevoflurane | 2.0 |
| Desflurane | 6.0 |
- Lower MAC = more potent; MAC decreases with age, hypothermia, opioids, pregnancy
- MAC increases with: hyperthermia, CNS stimulants, chronic alcohol

**2. Neuromuscular Blocking Agents**
| Feature | Succinylcholine | Non-depolarizing (rocuronium, etc.) |
|---|---|---|
| Type | Depolarizing | Non-depolarizing |
| Onset | Fastest (60 seconds) | Slower (rocuronium ~60s, others 2–5 min) |
| Duration | Ultrashort (5–10 min) | Intermediate/long |
| Mechanism | ACh receptor agonist → persistent depolarization | Competitive ACh antagonism |
| Fasciculations | Yes | No |
| Reversal | Spontaneous (pseudocholinesterase) | Neostigmine/sugammadex (for rocuronium) |
| Contraindications | Burns, crush, denervation, hyperkalemia risk | — |
| Malignant hyperthermia | Trigger (with halothane) | No |

**3. Malignant Hyperthermia**
- Trigger: succinylcholine + volatile anesthetics (halothane, sevoflurane)
- Mechanism: RYR1 gene mutation → uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release from SR
- Signs: hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, hypercarbia, metabolic acidosis, ↑CPK
- Treatment: stop trigger + IV dantrolene; cooling measures

### Critical Numbers
- Normal ET-CO₂: 35–40 mmHg (capnography)
- Sellick's maneuver (cricoid pressure): applied during rapid sequence intubation to prevent aspiration
- Intubation at: laryngoscopy grade I = easy; grade IV = cannot intubate (Cormack-Lehane)

| Air bronchogram | Air-filled bronchi visible within opacity | Consolidation (lobar pneumonia, pulmonary edema) |
| Kerley B lines | Horizontal lines at lung periphery | Pulmonary edema (interstitial), lymphangitis |
| Bat-wing/butterfly | Central bilateral opacity | Pulmonary edema |
| Honeycomb | Thick-walled cystic spaces | UIP/IPF, end-stage fibrosis |
| Ground glass opacity | Hazy increased density; vessels visible | Early infection, hemorrhage, edema |
| Pleural effusion | Blunting of costophrenic angle (>250 mL) | Multiple causes |
| Pneumothorax | Absent lung markings at periphery; visceral pleural line | Spontaneous, traumatic |
| Widened mediastinum | >8 cm on PA CXR | Aortic dissection, lymphoma, mediastinal mass |

**2. CT Brain**
- Hyperdense (white): acute blood (extradural/subdural/SAH), calcification, melanoma
- Hypodense (dark): infarct, edema, abscess, old blood
- Extradural hematoma: biconvex (lenticular) shape; arterial (middle meningeal artery)
- Subdural hematoma: crescent shape; venous (bridging veins); can cross sutures
- SAH: blood in basal cisterns; Fisher grade; aneurysm most common cause

### Critical Numbers
- Normal aorta on CT: ascending <4 cm, descending <3 cm; aneurysm = >4.5 cm (ascending) or >3 cm (descending)
- Hounsfield Units: water = 0; fat = −100; blood (acute) = 40–80; bone = +400 to +1000; air = −1000
- USG: cystic lesion = anechoic; liver is reference echogenicity; spleen > liver > kidney (normal echogenicity)
