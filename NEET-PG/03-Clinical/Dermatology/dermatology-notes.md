## MORPHOLOGY OF SKIN LESIONS

### Primary Lesions

| Lesion | Definition | Size | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Macule** | Flat, non-palpable color change | <1 cm | Freckle, café-au-lait |
| **Patch** | Flat, non-palpable color change | >1 cm | Vitiligo, Mongolian spot |
| **Papule** | Raised, palpable, solid | <1 cm | Acne, wart |
| **Plaque** | Raised, palpable, flat-topped, solid | >1 cm | Psoriasis, lichen planus |
| **Nodule** | Deeper solid papule, palpable into dermis/subcutis | 1-2 cm | Rheumatoid nodule, melanoma |
| **Vesicle** | Fluid-filled blister | <1 cm | HSV, varicella |
| **Bulla** | Fluid-filled blister | >1 cm | Pemphigus, bullous pemphigoid, burns |
| **Pustule** | Pus-filled blister | — | Acne, folliculitis, psoriasis (pustular) |
| **Wheal (Urticaria)** | Transient, edematous, pruritic | Variable | Hives |
| **Telangiectasia** | Dilated superficial blood vessels | — | Rosacea, hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia |
| **Comedone** | Dilated follicle filled with keratin/sebum | — | Acne |

### Secondary Lesions

| Lesion | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Scale** | Heaped-up dead keratinocytes (dry/greasy) |
| **Crust** | Dried exudate (serum/blood/pus) — honey-colored in impetigo |
| **Erosion** | Loss of epidermis only (heals without scar) |
| **Ulcer** | Loss of epidermis + dermis (heals with scar) |
| **Fissure** | Linear crack through epidermis into dermis |
| **Lichenification** | Thickened skin with accentuated markings (from chronic rubbing) |
| **Scar** | Fibrous tissue replacing normal dermis |
| **Atrophy** | Thinning of epidermis/dermis/subcutis |
| **Excoriation** | Linear erosion from scratching |
| **Milia** | Small white cysts (keratin-filled) |

### Configuration/Distribution
- **Annular**: Ring-shaped (tinea, erythema multiforme, granuloma annulare)
- **Linear**: Along a line (dermatitis artefacta, linear lichen planus, Blaschko lines)
- **Grouped (herpetiform)**: Clustered (HSV, DH)
- **Dermatomal**: Along a dermatome (herpes zoster)
- **Targetoid**: Central blister/necrosis + pale middle ring + red outer ring (erythema multiforme)

## ECZEMA/DERMATITIS

### Atopic Dermatitis (AD)

#### Hanifin-Rajka Criteria (3 major + 3 minor)
**Major (must have ≥3)**:
1. Pruritus
2. Typical morphology and distribution (flexural)
3. Chronic/relapsing course
4. Personal/family history of atopy

**Minor (≥3 of 23)**:
- Elevated serum IgE, immediate skin test reactivity, early age of onset, Dennie-Morgan infra-orbital folds, keratosis pilaris, ichthyosis, periorbital darkening, pityriasis alba, nipple eczema, white dermographism, food intolerance, etc.

#### Distribution by Age
- **Infants (<2 years)**: Cheeks, extensor surfaces (face, scalp, trunk)
- **Children (2-12 years)**: **Flexures** (antecubital fossae, popliteal fossae, neck)
- **Adults**: **Flexures**, lichenification, nummular lesions, hands

#### Key Features
- **Elevated total IgE** (and specific IgE to food/environmental allergens)
- Pruritus = cardinal feature ("itch that rashes")
- **White dermographism**: Scratch → white line (vasoconstriction, opposite to normal red)
- **Koebner phenomenon**: May occur (lesions at sites of trauma)
- **Complications**: Secondary infection (S. aureus, eczema herpeticum — Kaposi varicelliform eruption)
- **Eczema herpeticum**: HSV superinfection → punched-out monomorphic erosions; **IV acyclovir**

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Eczema herpeticum = HSV + atopic eczema → widespread vesicles/punched-out erosions. Treat with IV acyclovir**

#### AD Treatment
- Emollients (mainstay, barrier restoration)
- **Topical corticosteroids** (first-line for flares)
- **Topical calcineurin inhibitors** (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus — steroid-sparing, safe for face)
- Systemic: Cyclosporine, methotrexate, azathioprine, MMF
- **Dupilumab** (anti-IL-4Rα, blocks IL-4 + IL-13) — biologic for moderate-severe; most effective currently
- **Antihistamines**: Sedating (hydroxyzine) for nocturnal pruritus; not for itch itself

### Contact Dermatitis

| Feature | Allergic CD (ACD) | Irritant CD (ICD) |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | **Type IV hypersensitivity** (delayed, T-cell) | Direct chemical/physical damage (no immune sensitization) |
| Onset | **24-48h after contact** (requires prior sensitization) | **Immediate or hours** |
| Sensitization | Required | Not required |
| First exposure | No reaction (sensitization phase) | Can react on first exposure |
| Distribution | Corresponds to contact area but may spread | Precisely at contact area |
| Patch test | **Positive** (diagnosis) | Negative |
| Common causes | **Nickel** (most common — jewelry), fragrances, preservatives, rubber, chromate | Soaps, detergents, solvents, acids |
| Treatment | Avoid allergen, TCS, tacrolimus | Avoid irritant, emollients, TCS |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Patch test = gold standard for ACD. Type IV (delayed) hypersensitivity. Most common allergen = nickel**

### Seborrheic Dermatitis
- **Malassezia (Pityrosporum) species** — commensal yeast, pathogenic role
- Greasy yellowish scales on erythematous base, **sebaceous areas** (scalp, face — nasolabial folds, eyebrows, retroauricular, chest)
- **Dandruff** = mild scalp seborrheic dermatitis
- Associated with **HIV/AIDS** (severe, refractory), Parkinson's disease (neurological form)
- Treatment: **Antifungal** (ketoconazole shampoo/cream), TCS (for inflammation), selenium sulfide, zinc pyrithione, ciclopirox

### Nummular Dermatitis
- Coin-shaped (discoid) pruritic eczematous plaques
- Lower limbs and trunk in adults
- Treatment: TCS, emollients, tar preparations

### Pompholyx (Dyshidrotic Eczema)
- Deep-seated vesicles on **palms, lateral fingers, soles**
- Intense pruritus, often triggered by heat/sweat/metals
- Treatment: Potent TCS, soaks; tacrolimus; phototherapy for chronic

## PAPULOSQUAMOUS DISORDERS

### Psoriasis

#### Clinical Features
- **Plaque psoriasis** (most common, 90%): Well-defined erythematous plaques with **silvery-white scales**
- Sites: **Extensor surfaces** (elbows, knees), scalp, lower back, nails
- **Auspitz sign**: Removal of scale → pinpoint bleeding (dilated dermal capillaries)
- **Koebner (isomorphic) phenomenon**: Lesions at sites of trauma
- **Candle grease sign**: Scratching → scales fall like candle grease
- **Woronoff ring**: Pale halo around resolving psoriatic plaque

#### Nail Changes (NEET PG Hotspot)
- **Pitting** (most common — large, irregular; distinguishes from fungal nail)
- **Oil drop sign** (salmon patch): Yellow-brown discoloration under nail plate
- **Onycholysis**: Separation of nail from nail bed
- **Subungual hyperkeratosis**
- **Beau's lines, leukonychia** (less specific)

#### Histopathology
- **Munro microabscesses**: Neutrophilic collections in stratum corneum
- **Kogoj spongiform pustules**: Neutrophilic collections in spinous layer (especially pustular psoriasis)
- Parakeratosis, acanthosis, elongated rete ridges (club-shaped)

#### PASI Score (Psoriasis Area and Severity Index)
- Body divided into head (10%), trunk (30%), upper limbs (20%), lower limbs (40%)
- Erythema + Induration + Scaling scored 0-4
- Area involvement scored 0-6
- **PASI 75** = 75% reduction from baseline = clinical response target
- **PASI ≥10** or DLQI ≥10 = moderate-severe disease (systemic treatment indicated)

#### Psoriasis Treatment

| Severity | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Mild (PASI <10, DLQI <10) | Topical: Corticosteroids, **calcipotriol (Vitamin D3 analogue)** — first-line topical; coal tar, dithranol |
| Moderate-Severe | **Methotrexate** (first-line systemic), cyclosporine (rapid response), acitretin (retinoid), PUVA/nbUVB phototherapy |
| Biologics | Anti-TNF: Adalimumab, infliximab, etanercept; **Anti-IL-12/23: Ustekinumab**; **Anti-IL-17: Secukinumab, ixekizumab** (most effective); **Anti-IL-23: Guselkumab, risankizumab** |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab) = most effective biologics for psoriasis. Contraindicated in IBD (worsen Crohn's)**

#### Special Forms of Psoriasis
- **Guttate**: Small drop-like lesions, post-streptococcal throat infection, young adults
- **Pustular**: von Zumbusch (generalized, fever, systemic) → **medical emergency**; Palmoplantar pustulosis
- **Erythrodermic**: >90% BSA erythema, risk of high-output HF, hypothermia, infection
- **Inverse psoriasis**: Flexures, smooth (no scale from moisture), differential from intertrigo

### Lichen Planus

#### 6 P's Mnemonic
**P**ruritic, **P**lanar, **P**olygonal, **P**urple, **P**apules, **P**laques

- **Wickham's striae**: White lace-like pattern on surface (pathognomonic)
- Sites: Flexor aspects of wrists and forearms, lower legs, lumbar area, genitalia
- **Koebner phenomenon** positive
- **Oral LP**: White reticulate lesions on buccal mucosa — **cannot be rubbed off** (unlike candidiasis)
- **Nail LP**: Pterygium formation (pathognomonic), nail loss, longitudinal ridging

#### Histopathology
- **Band-like lymphocytic infiltrate** hugging the DEJ (liquefactive degeneration of basal layer)
- **Civatte bodies (colloid/hyaline bodies)**: Apoptotic keratinocytes (eosinophilic, PAS-positive)
- "Sawtooth" rete ridges

#### Drug-Induced LP (Lichenoid Reactions)
- Gold, antimalarials (hydroxychloroquine), ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, thiazides, NSAIDs, penicillamine

**PYQ HOTSPOT: LP = 6 P's + Wickham's striae. Oral LP = cannot be rubbed off. Civatte bodies on histology. Drug-induced LP = gold, antimalarials**

#### LP Treatment
- **Topical/intralesional corticosteroids** (first-line)
- Hydroxychloroquine, acitretin, cyclosporine for extensive disease
- Oral LP: TCS (tacrolimus), hydroxychloroquine

## SKIN INFECTIONS

### Bacterial Infections

#### Impetigo
- **Non-bullous (70%)**: S. aureus or S. pyogenes (GAS); honey-colored crusted lesions on face/hands
- **Bullous**: S. aureus (phage type 71, exfoliative toxin); superficial flaccid bullae
- **Ecthyma**: Ulcerated form of impetigo, deeper
- Treatment: **Topical mupirocin** (first-line for localized); oral **flucloxacillin/cefalexin** for widespread

#### Folliculitis, Furuncle, Carbuncle
- **Folliculitis**: Superficial infection of hair follicle (S. aureus); perifollicular pustule
- **Furuncle (boil)**: Deep folliculitis with surrounding cellulitis; painful nodule
- **Carbuncle**: Multiple interconnected furuncles; nape of neck and back; systemic upset
- Treatment: I&D (furuncle/carbuncle), oral antibiotics (flucloxacillin/clindamycin)

#### Erysipelas vs Cellulitis

| Feature | Erysipelas | Cellulitis |
|---|---|---|
| Depth | **Superficial dermis + lymphatics** | **Deeper dermis + subcutaneous fat** |
| Borders | **Sharply demarcated, raised edge** | Poorly defined margins |
| Causative organism | **S. pyogenes (GAS)** | S. aureus, S. pyogenes |
| Surface | **Shiny, tense, red plaque** with peau d'orange | Warm, red, tender |
| Face involvement | More common (butterfly distribution) | Any area, especially legs |

#### Hidradenitis Suppurativa (HS)
- Chronic inflammatory disease of **apocrine gland-bearing areas** (axillae, groins, perineum, inframammary)
- **Hurley staging**: I (abscess formation), II (recurrence + sinus tracts), III (diffuse involvement)
- Treatment: Topical/systemic antibiotics (tetracyclines), intralesional corticosteroids, **adalimumab** (anti-TNF, FDA-approved), surgical drainage/wide excision

### Viral Infections

#### Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)
- HSV-1: Orolabial (cold sores), encephalitis
- HSV-2: Genital herpes
- Lesions: **Grouped vesicles on erythematous base** → rupture → crusted erosions
- **Tzanck smear**: Multinucleated giant cells (Tzanck cells), intranuclear inclusions (Cowdry A bodies); **not specific (VZV also positive)**
- Primary gingivostomatitis (HSV-1 in children): Painful, extensive oral ulcers, fever
- **Eczema herpeticum**: HSV + atopic eczema → emergency
- Diagnosis: Viral culture (gold standard), **PCR** (most sensitive and specific), DFA
- Treatment: **Acyclovir** (nucleoside analogue, needs HSV thymidine kinase for phosphorylation), valacyclovir (oral, better bioavailability), famciclovir

#### Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)
- **Primary (Chickenpox)**: Pruritic, generalized, **pleomorphic rash** (all stages simultaneously: macules, papules, vesicles, crusts)
- **Herpes Zoster**: Reactivation; **dermatomal** (most common T3-L3, trigeminal — ophthalmic branch)
- **Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN)**: Most common complication; pain persists >3 months; Risk increases with age
- **Ramsay Hunt syndrome**: VZV + geniculate ganglion → facial palsy + ear vesicles ± hearing loss
- Treatment: **Valacyclovir/famciclovir** (preferred oral); IV acyclovir for severe/immunocompromised
- PHN treatment: **Gabapentin/pregabalin**, lidocaine patches, capsaicin, TCAs (amitriptyline)
- **Vaccine**: Shingrix (recombinant zoster vaccine) for ≥50 years — prevents zoster and PHN

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Herpes zoster = dermatomal distribution. PHN = pain >3 months after rash heals. Ramsay Hunt = zoster + ear + facial palsy**

#### Molluscum Contagiosum
- **Poxvirus**; autoinoculation; common in children and immunocompromised
- **Pearly, flesh-colored, umbilicated papules** (central dell)
- Diagnosis: **Henderson-Paterson bodies** (molluscum bodies) = large intracytoplasmic inclusions on Tzanck/biopsy
- Self-limiting in immunocompetent; persistent in HIV
- Treatment: Cryotherapy, curettage, topical (imiquimod, podophyllin, cantharidins)

#### Warts (HPV)
| HPV Type | Wart Type |
|---|---|
| **1, 2, 4** | Common warts (verruca vulgaris) |
| **1** | Plantar warts (verruca plantaris) — deep, painful, endophytic |
| **3, 10** | Flat warts (verruca plana) |
| **6, 11** | **Anogenital warts** (condylomata acuminata) — benign |
| **16, 18** | **Cervical cancer, penile/anal/oropharyngeal SCC** — oncogenic |
| **5, 8** | Epidermodysplasia verruciformis (EV) — SCC risk |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: HPV 6, 11 = condyloma acuminata (benign). HPV 16, 18 = high-risk oncogenic (cervical cancer)**

Treatment: Cryotherapy, TCA, imiquimod, podophyllin (not in pregnancy), electrocautery

### Fungal Infections

#### Dermatophytes (Tinea)

| Type | Location | Causative Species | Wood's Lamp | KOH |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tinea capitis | Scalp | T. tonsurans (endothrix), M. canis (ectothrix) | Bright green (Microsporum) | Hyphae + spores |
| Tinea corporis | Body | T. rubrum, T. mentagrophytes | — | Hyphae |
| Tinea pedis | Feet | T. rubrum | — | Hyphae |
| Tinea unguium (onychomycosis) | Nails | T. rubrum | — | Hyphae in nail |
| Tinea cruris | Groin | T. rubrum, E. floccosum | — | Hyphae |
| Tinea manuum | Hands | T. rubrum | — | Hyphae |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: T. rubrum = most common dermatophyte. Microsporum = bright green fluorescence on Wood's lamp. T. tonsurans = NO fluorescence (most common tinea capitis in India)**

**Treatment**: Topical imidazoles (clotrimazole, miconazole) for limited disease; **Oral terbinafine** (most effective, fungicidal) or **itraconazole** for nail/scalp/extensive disease. Griseofulvin (older, fungistatic, still used for tinea capitis in children in some areas)

**Kerion**: Inflammatory tinea capitis with boggy, pus-filled mass — treat with systemic antifungal + short course oral prednisolone

#### Candidiasis
- **Candida albicans** (most common)
- KOH: **Pseudohyphae + budding yeast** ("hyphae with sausage-link blastospores")
- **Satellite pustules** (characteristic) around main erythematous area
- Types: Oral thrush, genital (vulvovaginitis — thick curd-like discharge, erythema), intertriginous, paronychia (chronic)
- **Chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis (CMC)**: T-cell deficiency (AIRE gene mutation in APECED)
- Treatment: Topical imidazoles (clotrimazole, econazole); oral fluconazole for severe/systemic

#### Tinea Versicolor (Pityriasis Versicolor)
- **Malassezia furfur** (Pityrosporum orbiculare)
- **Hypopigmented** (most common in dark skin), or hyperpigmented, or pinkish macules with **fine scales** on trunk, shoulders, neck
- **KOH**: **"Spaghetti and meatballs"** = short hyphae (spaghetti) + clusters of yeast cells (meatballs)
- **Wood's lamp**: **Yellow-green** or orange fluorescence (porphyrins)
- Non-inflammatory; pruritus mild or absent
- Recurrence common
- Treatment: Topical antifungals (selenium sulfide, ketoconazole, ciclopirox); oral itraconazole/fluconazole for extensive/recurrent

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Tinea versicolor = Malassezia + hypopigmented macules + spaghetti and meatballs on KOH + yellow-green on Wood's lamp**

### Leprosy

#### Ridley-Jopling Classification (Immunological Spectrum)

| Type | Immunity | Bacillary Load | Skin Lesions | Nerve | Lepromin Test |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **TT (Tuberculoid)** | **High** | **Low (paucibacillary)** | 1-2 well-defined, hypopigmented, **anesthetic**, dry, hairless | Single thickened nerve; severe damage | **Strongly positive** |
| **BT (Borderline tuberculoid)** | High-moderate | Low-moderate | Few lesions, satellite lesions | Several nerves | Weakly positive |
| **BB (Mid-borderline)** | Intermediate | Moderate | Multiple irregular "punched-out" lesions | Moderate | Negative |
| **BL (Borderline lepromatous)** | Low-moderate | High | Numerous lesions, bilateral | Multiple | Negative |
| **LL (Lepromatous)** | **Low** | **High (multibacillary)** | **Diffuse infiltration**, nodules (lepromas), **loss of eyebrows (madarosis)**, leonine facies | Symmetrical; **late damage** | **Negative (anergy)** |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: TT = strong immunity, paucibacillary, few lesions, early nerve damage. LL = poor immunity, multibacillary, diffuse lesions, late nerve damage**

#### WHO Classification (Clinical/Treatment)
| Type | Skin Patches | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Paucibacillary (PB)** | 1-5 lesions | **Rifampicin 600mg (monthly supervised) + Dapsone 100mg (daily) × 6 months** |
| **Multibacillary (MB)** | >5 lesions | **Rifampicin 600mg + Clofazimine 300mg (monthly supervised) + Dapsone 100mg + Clofazimine 50mg (daily) × 12 months** |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Single skin lesion = Rifampicin + Ofloxacin + Minocycline (ROM single dose — for single lesion PB leprosy)**

#### Leprosy Reactions

| Feature | Type 1 (Reversal) | Type 2 (ENL — Erythema Nodosum Leprosum) |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | **Cell-mediated (Type IV)** | **Immune complex (Type III)** |
| Type | Borderline (BT, BB, BL) | **Multibacillary (BL, LL)** |
| Timing | During/after treatment | Can occur before, during, or after treatment |
| Lesions | **Existing lesions become erythematous, swollen** | **New tender erythematous nodules** + systemic features |
| Nerve | **Acute nerve function impairment** (pain, loss of function) | Neuritis (less severe) |
| Systemic | No | **Yes** (fever, arthritis, iritis, orchitis, glomerulonephritis) |
| Treatment | **Prednisolone** (mainstay) | **Thalidomide** (specific, most effective); clofazimine; prednisolone |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Type 1 = borderline spectrum, nerve damage, treat with prednisolone. Type 2 (ENL) = LL/BL, systemic features, treat with thalidomide**

#### Nerve Thickening in Leprosy (Commonly Tested)
- **Greater auricular nerve** (most commonly enlarged in leprosy overall)
- **Ulnar nerve** (most commonly damaged — causing claw hand)
- Common peroneal nerve (foot drop)
- Radial cutaneous nerve, posterior tibial nerve, sural nerve

#### Deformity Grading (WHO)
| Grade | Eyes | Hands/Feet |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 0 | No problem | No anesthesia, no deformity |
| Grade 1 | — | Anesthesia present (insensate palm/sole) |
| Grade 2 | **Visible deformity** (lagophthalmos, iritis) | **Visible deformity/damage** (ulcers, clawing, absorption) |

## ACNE VULGARIS

### Pathogenesis (4 Factors)
1. **Sebaceous gland hypersecretion** (androgens drive sebum production)
2. **Abnormal keratinization** (comedone formation — retention hyperkeratosis)
3. **Propionibacterium acnes (Cutibacterium acnes)** colonization
4. **Inflammation** (cytokines, lipase from C. acnes → fatty acids → inflammation)

### Grading
- **Grade I**: Comedones (open = blackhead; closed = whitehead); non-inflammatory
- **Grade II**: Comedones + papules and pustules; mild-moderate
- **Grade III**: Nodulocystic lesions; moderate-severe
- **Grade IV**: Acne conglobata (severe nodules, sinuses, scarring)

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Comedone = primary lesion of acne. Open = blackhead (oxidized melanin). Closed = whitehead**

### Treatment Ladder

| Severity | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Comedonal (Grade I) | **Topical retinoids** (tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene) — first-line, prevents/treats comedones |
| Mild-moderate (Grades I-II) | Topical retinoid + **Topical antibiotic** (clindamycin, erythromycin) ± benzoyl peroxide (BP) |
| Moderate (Grade II-III) | Add **oral antibiotics** (doxycycline, minocycline for 3-6 months); avoid monotherapy antibiotics |
| Severe/nodulocystic (Grade III-IV) | **Oral isotretinoin** |
| Hormonal (women) | Combined OCP (ethinyl estradiol + cyproterone acetate), spironolactone |

#### Isotretinoin (13-cis-Retinoic Acid)
- **Only treatment that targets all 4 pathogenic factors**
- Dose: 0.5-1 mg/kg/day for 16-24 weeks (cumulative dose 120-150 mg/kg)
- **Teratogenic** (Category X — extremely teratogenic; causes retinoid embryopathy): **iPLEDGE program** (USA) — two negative pregnancy tests before and monthly during treatment
- **Monitoring**: LFTs, fasting lipids (hypertriglyceridemia — most common side effect), CBCs
- Side effects: Cheilitis (most common, universal), xerosis, photosensitivity, epistaxis, musculoskeletal, IBD risk (controversial), **depression/suicidality** (black box warning)

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Isotretinoin = teratogenic. Cheilitis = most common/expected SE. Hyperlipidemia (especially triglycerides) must be monitored. Contraception mandatory**

## VESICULOBULLOUS DISORDERS

### Comparison Table

| Feature | Pemphigus Vulgaris (PV) | Bullous Pemphigoid (BP) | Dermatitis Herpetiformis (DH) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cleavage level | **Intraepidermal (suprabasal)** | **Subepidermal (dermal-epidermal junction)** | **Subepidermal (dermal papillae)** |
| Nikolsky sign | **Positive** | **Negative** | Negative |
| Blister characteristics | **Flaccid, fragile** (easily rupture) | **Tense, intact** | Small grouped vesicles (herpetiform) |
| Age | Middle-aged (40-60) | **Elderly** (>60 years) | Young adults |
| Mucosal involvement | **Yes (oral in 90%, may precede skin)** | Less common | Rare |
| Target antigen | **Desmoglein 3** (mucosa), Desmoglein 1 (skin) | **BP180 (collagen XVII) + BP230** | **Epidermal transglutaminase (TG3)** |
| Antibody type | **IgG** | IgG + C3 | **IgA** |
| DIF (Direct Immunofluorescence) | **IgG on keratinocyte cell surface** (chicken-wire/fishnet pattern) | **Linear IgG + C3 at DEJ (basement membrane)** | **Granular IgA in dermal papillae** |
| Association | — | Internal malignancy (some) | **Celiac disease (gluten sensitivity)** |
| Treatment | **Prednisolone** ± immunosuppressants; rituximab (refractory) | Prednisolone, doxycycline + nicotinamide | **Gluten-free diet + Dapsone** |

**PYQ HOTSPOT:**
- **PV**: Nikolsky positive, suprabasal, desmoglein 3, oral mucosa first, IgG fishnet on DIF
- **BP**: Nikolsky negative, subepidermal, BP180, tense bullae in elderly, linear IgG at BMZ on DIF
- **DH**: IgA granular at dermal papillae, gluten/celiac association, dapsone treatment

#### Nikolsky Sign (Positive = Pemphigus)
- Lateral pressure on normal/perilesional skin → epidermal shearing

#### Pemphigus Foliaceus
- **Desmoglein 1** only (superficial, no mucosal involvement)
- Superficial erosions, scaling; **No oral lesions**
- Endemic in Brazil (**Fogo Selvagem**)

#### Rituximab in Pemphigus
- **Anti-CD20** (eliminates B cells → reduces autoantibody production)
- Increasingly used as first-line in moderate-severe PV, or rituximab + corticosteroids

## PIGMENTARY DISORDERS

### Vitiligo
- **Autoimmune** destruction of melanocytes
- **Depigmented macules/patches** (NOT hypopigmented — complete loss of pigment)
- **Wood's lamp**: **Bright chalk-white fluorescence** (accentuates depigmentation)
- **Koebner phenomenon** positive
- Associated autoimmune diseases: Thyroid disorders (**most common** — Hashimoto's, Graves'), DM type 1, Addison's, pernicious anemia, RA, SLE
- **Types**: Segmental (unilateral, stable, dermatomal — early age onset), Non-segmental/generalized (most common, symmetrical, progressive), Universal (>80% depigmentation)
- **Halo nevus (Sutton's nevus)**: Depigmented halo around pigmented nevus (associated with vitiligo)

#### Vitiligo Treatment
- **NBUVB (narrowband UVB)** phototherapy — first-line for extensive disease; also PUVA
- **Topical tacrolimus/pimecrolimus** — face, anogenital (calcineurin inhibitors; avoid steroids on face long-term)
- Topical corticosteroids — localized disease
- **Ruxolitinib cream** (JAK1/2 inhibitor) — FDA-approved, newer
- Depigmentation (monobenzone) for >80% involvement (render remaining skin depigmented)
- Surgical: Blister grafting, melanocyte transplantation (stable vitiligo)

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Vitiligo = depigmented (not hypopigmented). Wood's lamp = chalk-white. Most common association = thyroid disease**

### Melasma (Chloasma)
- **Acquired hyperpigmentation**, symmetrical, brown patches on sun-exposed face (cheeks, forehead, upper lip)
- Triggered by **sunlight + female hormones** (pregnancy, OCPs)
- Types: Epidermal (superficial, responds well to treatment), dermal (deeper, responds poorly), mixed
- **Wood's lamp**: Enhances epidermal melasma (accentuates), no enhancement in dermal
- Treatment: **Strict sunscreen** (essential, cornerstone), **Hydroquinone** (most effective depigmenting agent, 4%), topical retinoids, azelaic acid, kojic acid, chemical peels
- **Triple combination cream** (Kligman's formula): Hydroquinone + tretinoin + corticosteroid

### Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation/Hypopigmentation
- **PIH**: Melanin deposition after skin injury/inflammation (acne, burns, atopic eczema)
- Treatment: Sunscreen, hydroquinone, topical retinoids, chemical peels
- **Pityriasis alba**: Post-inflammatory hypopigmentation on cheeks in children (atopic tendency); self-resolving

## DRUG REACTIONS

### Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS), TEN, DRESS Comparison

| Feature | SJS | TEN | DRESS (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms) |
|---|---|---|---|
| BSA involved | **<10%** | **>30%** (SJS-TEN overlap 10-30%) | Not blistering primarily |
| Mechanism | **Type IV hypersensitivity** (CTL-mediated keratinocyte apoptosis) | Same, more severe | T-cell + eosinophil activation |
| Onset | 1-3 weeks after drug | 1-3 weeks | **2-8 weeks** (longer latency) |
| Causative drugs | Allopurinol (#1), carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin, **sulfonamides**, oxicam NSAIDs | Same, especially allopurinol | Allopurinol, carbamazepine, phenobarbital, dapsone, lamotrigine, minocycline |
| Skin | Erythematous macules → blisters + erosions, target lesions, Nikolsky positive | **Extensive epidermal detachment**, "scalded skin" appearance | Morbilliform rash, facial edema |
| Mucous membranes | **≥2 mucous membranes** involved | Yes | Less prominent |
| Systemic involvement | Fever, malaise | Sepsis risk, fluid/electrolyte loss, multi-organ failure | **Fever + lymphadenopathy + organ involvement (liver most common), eosinophilia** |
| Mortality | ~10% | **25-35%** | 10% |
| Treatment | **Stop culprit drug**, supportive (burn unit), cyclosporine (emerging evidence), IVIG (controversial) | Same as SJS but more intensive | Stop drug, **systemic corticosteroids** (unlike SJS/TEN where steroids controversial) |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: SJS <10% BSA. TEN >30%. DRESS = eosinophilia + lymphadenopathy + internal organ involvement (liver). Allopurinol = most common cause of SJS/TEN**

#### SCORTEN (TEN severity score)
- Age >40, HR >120, cancer/malignancy, BSA >10%, BUN >10 mmol/L, serum bicarbonate <20, glucose >14 mmol/L → each = 1 point; Score ≥5 = mortality >90%

### Drug-Induced Lupus
- **Anti-histone antibodies** (most specific)
- Drugs: **Procainamide** (most common), **hydralazine** (most studied), isoniazid, quinidine, minocycline, anti-TNF agents
- Features: Arthralgia, serositis, rash; **Renal and CNS involvement uncommon** (unlike idiopathic SLE)
- **Anti-dsDNA usually negative** (unlike idiopathic SLE)
- Resolves on drug discontinuation

### Fixed Drug Eruption (FDE)
- **Same site** each time upon drug rechallenge
- Well-defined, violaceous/dusky red round/oval plaque → hyperpigmented patch (post-inflammatory)
- Common sites: Lips, genitalia, perianal
- **Residual hyperpigmentation** (hallmark)
- Causative drugs: **NSAIDs** (most common — tetracyclines, cotrimoxazole, barbiturates, antimalarials)
- Treatment: Avoid offending drug; TCS for acute phase

## SEXUALLY TRANSMITTED INFECTIONS

### Comparison Table

| STI | Organism | Ulcer | Lymph Nodes | Other Features | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Syphilis (primary)** | Treponema pallidum | **Painless, indurated, clean base** (chancre) | **Non-tender, firm, bilateral** inguinal LAP | Heals spontaneously | VDRL/RPR (screening); TPHA/FTA-ABS (confirmatory) |
| **Chancroid** | Haemophilus ducreyi | **Painful, soft, purulent, ragged/undermined edges** | **Tender, fluctuant bubo** (inguinal; may rupture) | Multiple ulcers | Culture on selective media; clinical diagnosis |
| **LGV** (Lymphogranuloma venereum) | Chlamydia trachomatis L1-L3 | **Small, painless, transient** (often missed) | **Tender matted LAP** (groove sign of Greenblatt — groove between femoral and inguinal nodes) | Genital elephantiasis (late), rectal strictures, proctitis | Serology (microimmunofluorescence), PCR |
| **Granuloma Inguinale (Donovanosis)** | Klebsiella granulomatis (formerly Calymmatobacterium granulomatis) | **Painless, beefy red, friable, progressive** ulcer (NO lymphadenopathy) | **No buboes** (may simulate as pseudobuboes — subcutaneous granulomas) | Progressive tissue destruction, **Donovan bodies** on smear | **Tissue smear — Donovan bodies** (intracellular organisms in macrophages, safety-pin appearance); Wright-Giemsa stain |
| **Genital Herpes** | HSV-2 (primarily), HSV-1 | **Painful grouped vesicles** → erosions | Tender inguinal LAP | Recurrent; prodrome | Tzanck smear, PCR, DFA |

**PYQ HOTSPOT:**
- Painless ulcer = syphilis/granuloma inguinale
- Painful ulcer = chancroid/herpes
- Groove sign = LGV
- Donovan bodies = granuloma inguinale
- Beefy red ulcer, no nodes = granuloma inguinale

#### Syphilis — Full Details

**Primary**: Painless indurated chancre; heals 3-6 weeks (±)

**Secondary** (3-6 weeks after chancre):
- **Generalized maculopapular rash** including **palms and soles** (highly characteristic)
- **Condylomata lata**: Flat, moist, gray warty lesions on genitalia/perianal (highly infectious)
- Oral patches (snail track ulcers), moth-eaten alopecia, generalized LAP, fever, malaise
- **The great imitator**

**Latent**: No symptoms; early latent (<1 year since infection) vs late latent

**Tertiary** (years later):
- **Gumma**: Granulomatous lesion in skin, bone, liver
- **Cardiovascular syphilis**: Aortic root aneurysm, aortic regurgitation
- **Neurosyphilis**: Meningitis, tabes dorsalis (dorsal column destruction — Romberg's, Charcot's joint, lancinating pains, Argyll Robertson pupils), general paresis of insane (dementia)

#### Syphilis Serology
- **VDRL/RPR**: Non-treponemal (reagin antibody); used for **screening** and monitoring treatment response; **false positives** (pregnancy, SLE, leprosy, malaria, viral infections — "biological false positive")
- **TPHA/FTA-ABS/MHA-TP**: Treponemal specific; **confirmatory**; remain positive lifelong

**PYQ HOTSPOT: VDRL becomes negative after successful treatment (useful for monitoring). FTA-ABS remains positive lifelong — not useful for monitoring**

**Treatment**: **Benzathine penicillin G** (single IM dose for primary/secondary/early latent; 3 doses for late latent/tertiary). Doxycycline (penicillin allergy, non-pregnant)

#### Gonorrhea
- **Neisseria gonorrhoeae**: Gram-negative intracellular diplococci
- Urethral discharge (purulent, profuse), dysuria in men; women often asymptomatic (cervicitis)
- **PID** (pelvic inflammatory disease): Chlamydia + gonorrhea; Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome (perihepatitis)
- **Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI)**: Tenosynovitis + dermatitis + polyarthritis; **septic arthritis** (most common cause in sexually active young adults)
- Gram smear: **Intracellular Gram-negative diplococci in PMNs** (diagnostic)
- Culture: Thayer-Martin (chocolate agar) medium
- Treatment: **Ceftriaxone 500mg IM** (single dose; increased due to resistance) + azithromycin/doxycycline (cover Chlamydia co-infection)

## NAIL DISORDERS

### Nail Anatomy
- **Nail plate**: Dead keratinized cells
- **Nail matrix**: Produces nail plate (lunula = distal visible matrix)
- **Nail bed**: Vascularized, gives pink color
- **Hyponychium**: Under free edge
- **Cuticle (Eponychium)**: Protective barrier

### Nail Changes and Associations

| Finding | Association |
|---|---|
| **Pitting** | **Psoriasis** (large, irregular), alopecia areata (small, geometric), eczema |
| **Oil drop sign (salmon patch)** | **Psoriasis** |
| **Onycholysis** | Psoriasis, thyrotoxicosis, trauma, Plummer's nail |
| **Koilonychia** (spoon-shaped) | **Iron deficiency anemia** |
| **Leukonychia** (white nails) | Trauma, chronic disease |
| **Terry's nails** (white, pink distal band) | Cirrhosis, CCF, DM |
| **Lindsay's nails (half-and-half)** | **Chronic kidney disease** |
| **Yellow nail syndrome** | Lymphedema + pleural effusion + bronchiectasis + yellow nails |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Koilonychia = iron deficiency. Mees' lines = arsenic. Beau's lines = systemic illness/growth arrest. Terry's = cirrhosis**

## HAIR DISORDERS

### Alopecia Areata (AA)
- **Autoimmune** T-cell attack on hair follicle bulb
- **Well-defined, round/oval patches** of hair loss; **no scarring**
- **Exclamation mark hairs**: Short hairs with narrowed base near scalp (pathognomonic)
- **Nail changes**: **Fine uniform pitting** (most common; like stippled); leukonychia, trachyonychia
- **Types**: Patchy AA (most common), alopecia totalis (all scalp), alopecia universalis (scalp + body)
- Associated: Atopy, autoimmune thyroid, Down syndrome, Addison's
- **Prognosis**: Patchy AA resolves in 80% within 1 year; universalis has poor prognosis

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Exclamation mark hairs = pathognomonic of alopecia areata. Fine pitting of nails (geometric, small) = alopecia areata. Large irregular pitting = psoriasis**

#### AA Treatment
- **Intralesional corticosteroids** (triamcinolone) — first-line for limited patchy AA
- Potent topical corticosteroids, topical minoxidil
- **Contact sensitization/immunotherapy** (DPCP/SADBE) for extensive
- **JAK inhibitors** (baricitinib, ruxolitinib) — now FDA-approved for severe AA; highly effective
- Systemic steroids (short-term pulse)

### Androgenetic Alopecia (AGA)

**Male pattern baldness (Hamilton-Norwood classification)**:
- Begins at temples and vertex; Hamilton-Norwood scale I-VII
- **DHT (Dihydrotestosterone)** — 5-alpha reductase converts testosterone to DHT; DHT miniaturizes follicles

**Female pattern hair loss**:
- Ludwig scale I-III
- Frontal hairline preserved; diffuse thinning over crown

#### AGA Treatment
- **Minoxidil** (topical 2%/5%, oral low-dose): Potassium channel opener; vasodilator; **suitable for both sexes**; maintenance required indefinitely
- **Finasteride** (oral 1mg/day): **5-alpha reductase inhibitor**; reduces DHT; **males only** (teratogenic); 3-6 months for response
- **Dutasteride** (5-alpha reductase inhibitor — types 1+2); more potent than finasteride
- **Hair transplant** (follicular unit extraction/transplantation)

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Finasteride = contraindicated in women of childbearing age (teratogenic — feminization of male fetus). Minoxidil = safe in both sexes**

### Telogen Effluvium (TE)
- Diffuse, non-scarring hair shedding
- **Trigger 2-3 months prior** to hair loss (telogen phase delay): Post-partum (most common), major surgery, fever, crash diet, iron deficiency, thyroid disorders, psychosocial stress
- **Hair pull test**: >6 hairs in anagen-predominant scalp = increased shedding; most are telogen hairs (club-shaped roots)
- Self-limiting (resolves in 3-6 months); treat underlying cause
- **Chronic TE**: Fluctuating diffuse shedding >6 months; often middle-aged women

### Scarring vs Non-Scarring Alopecia

| Type | Causes | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-Scarring (Reversible)** | Alopecia areata, AGA, TE, tinea capitis, traction | **Follicular ostia preserved** on scalp |
| **Scarring (Irreversible)** | **Lichen planopilaris, DLE (discoid lupus), CCLE, frontal fibrosing alopecia, folliculitis decalvans, pseudopelade** | **Follicular ostia absent**, atrophic skin, cannot regrow |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Scarring alopecia = follicle permanently destroyed. No treatment restores hair. DLE on scalp = scarring. AA = non-scarring (follicle preserved)**

#### Lichen Planopilaris (LPP)
- Scalp lichen planus → scarring alopecia
- Perifollicular erythema + scale → atrophic patches with absent follicles
- **Frontal Fibrosing Alopecia (FFA)**: Variant of LPP in post-menopausal women; band of frontal recession with perifollicular erythema

#### Pseudopelade of Brocq
- "Footprints in the snow" — small oval atrophic areas, no inflammation
- End-stage of various scarring alopecias

## MISCELLANEOUS HIGH-YIELD DERMATOLOGY

### Erythema Multiforme (EM)
- **Target lesions** (3 zones: central blister/erosion + pale edematous ring + erythematous outer ring)
- Typically **acral distribution** (palms, soles), may involve mucosa
- Most common cause: **HSV** (EM minor); **Mycoplasma** (EM major)
- **NOT the same as SJS** (EM = distinct entity; target lesions; usually mild; recurrent)
- Treatment: Acyclovir (HSV-associated); TCS; if recurrent — **prophylactic acyclovir**

### Psoriasiform vs Eczematous vs Lichenoid Reactions
- **Psoriasiform**: Epidermal hyperplasia + parakeratosis + neutrophilic collections (psoriasis-like)
- **Eczematous (Spongiotic)**: Intercellular edema (spongiosis) in epidermis
- **Lichenoid**: Band-like lymphocytic infiltrate at DEJ, Civatte bodies (lichen planus-like)

### WOOD'S LAMP Summary Table (Frequently Tested)

| Condition | Wood's Lamp Finding |
|---|---|
| **Tinea versicolor** | **Yellow-green fluorescence** |
| **Tinea capitis (Microsporum)** | **Bright green (yellow-green) fluorescence** |
| **Erythrasma** (Corynebacterium) | **Coral-red fluorescence** |
| **Vitiligo** | **Chalk-white fluorescence** |
| **Melasma (epidermal)** | Enhanced (accentuated) |
| **Porphyria** | Pink-red fluorescence (urine/teeth) |
| **Pseudomonas** | Green fluorescence |

**PYQ HOTSPOT: Erythrasma = coral-red fluorescence = Corynebacterium minutissimum = interdigital/groin macerated plaques. Treat with erythromycin**

### Darier's Disease (Keratosis Follicularis)
- **ATP2A2 gene** (SERCA2 pump), autosomal dominant
- Greasy, warty, crusted papules in seborrheic distribution
- **Koebner phenomenon** positive
- Nail: **V-shaped notching at free margin**, alternating red/white longitudinal bands
- Treatment: Retinoids (acitretin, isotretinoin)
