## ECZEMA AND INFLAMMATORY DERMATOSES (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 6-year-old boy presents with intensely pruritic, erythematous, scaly plaques over the antecubital and popliteal fossae, neck, and face since infancy. His mother has bronchial asthma. Serum IgE is markedly elevated. The most likely diagnosis and primary immunological mechanism are:**
- A) Contact dermatitis — Type IV hypersensitivity
- B) Atopic dermatitis — Type I hypersensitivity (IgE-mediated)
- C) Psoriasis — T-cell mediated (Type IV)
- D) Seborrheic dermatitis — Pityrosporum overgrowth

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a Type I hypersensitivity (IgE-mediated) inflammatory skin condition associated with the atopic triad (asthma, allergic rhinitis, eczema). Key features: elevated IgE, positive family history, flexural involvement in children (infants: face and extensor surfaces), intense pruritus ("the itch that rashes"). Filaggrin gene mutations (skin barrier defect) are the primary genetic risk factor. First-line: emollients + topical corticosteroids; second-line: tacrolimus/pimecrolimus. Dupilumab (anti-IL-4/IL-13) for severe refractory AD.

</details>

**Q2. A 30-year-old nurse develops a pruritic, erythematous, vesicular rash on the dorsum of both hands after wearing latex gloves. Patch testing is positive for latex at 48–72 hours. The type of hypersensitivity reaction involved is:**
- A) Type I (IgE-mediated)
- B) Type II (cytotoxic)
- C) Type III (immune complex)
- D) Type IV (delayed-type/cell-mediated)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

Allergic contact dermatitis (ACD) is a Type IV (delayed-type) hypersensitivity reaction — T-lymphocyte mediated, with a 48–72 hour delay between antigen exposure and skin reaction. The reaction peaks at 48–72 hours on patch testing. Classic allergens: nickel (jewellery), chromates (cement — "cement dermatitis"), hair dye (paraphenylenediamine — PPD), latex, and rubber chemicals. Irritant contact dermatitis (most common form — no prior sensitization) occurs due to direct chemical injury, not immunological mechanism.

</details>

**Q3. A 3-month-old infant has greasy, yellowish scales over the scalp ("cradle cap"), eyebrows, nasolabial folds, and behind the ears. The organism implicated in the pathogenesis is:**
- A) Staphylococcus aureus
- B) Malassezia furfur (Pityrosporum ovale)
- C) Candida albicans
- D) Dermatophytes (Trichophyton species)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Seborrheic dermatitis (SD) is a chronic inflammatory condition of sebaceous gland-rich areas (scalp, face — nasolabial folds, eyebrows, post-auricular regions, central chest). In infants: "cradle cap." The yeast Malassezia furfur (Pityrosporum ovale) plays a pathogenic role by metabolizing sebum triglycerides into irritating free fatty acids. Treatment: antifungal shampoos (ketoconazole, selenium sulfide), zinc pyrithione, or low-potency topical steroids. In HIV patients, SD is a common and severe manifestation.

</details>

**Q4. A 35-year-old woman presents with itchy coin-shaped, erythematous, weeping plaques on her lower legs, 3–5 cm in diameter, with well-defined borders. She has no history of atopy. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Atopic dermatitis
- B) Psoriasis
- C) Nummular (discoid) eczema
- D) Tinea corporis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Nummular (discoid) eczema presents as coin-shaped (nummular = coin), well-demarcated, pruritic, oozing plaques typically on the lower extremities of middle-aged adults, often without an atopic history. It can be confused with tinea corporis, but KOH mount is negative in nummular eczema. Triggers: dry skin, local trauma, stress. Treatment: mid-to-high potency topical steroids + emollients. Psoriasis has silvery scales with Auspitz sign; tinea has an active scaly border with central clearing (KOH positive).

</details>

**Q5. A 28-year-old woman develops intensely pruritic, deep-seated, clear vesicles on the lateral aspects of her fingers and palms during summer, associated with hyperhidrosis. The vesicles do not rupture easily. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Bullous pemphigoid
- B) Pompholyx (dyshidrotic eczema)
- C) Epidermolysis bullosa
- D) Pemphigus vulgaris

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Pompholyx (dyshidrotic eczema / cheiropompholyx) presents as tapioca-grain-like, deep-seated vesicles on the palms, soles, and lateral aspects of fingers. Despite the name, it is NOT due to blocked sweat ducts. Triggers: heat, stress, nickel sensitivity, fungal infections (Id reaction). The vesicles are tense, deeply seated, and do not rupture easily. Treatment: potent topical corticosteroids, cool soaks; botulinum toxin for severe hyperhidrosis-associated cases. Bullous pemphigoid presents with large tense bullae on urticarial base in elderly patients.

</details>

## PAPULOSQUAMOUS DISORDERS (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 35-year-old man has sharply demarcated, erythematous plaques with thick silvery scales on his elbows, knees, and scalp. When the scale is scraped, pinpoint bleeding occurs. He also has onycholysis and subungual hyperkeratosis. Which of the following signs describes the pinpoint bleeding on scale removal?**
- A) Nikolsky sign
- B) Auspitz sign
- C) Koebner phenomenon
- D) Carpet tack sign

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Auspitz sign (pinpoint bleeding on removal of psoriatic scale) occurs because psoriatic epidermis thins over the tips of dermal papillae (suprapapillary thinning), and scraping exposes the dilated capillaries in the papillary dermis. Koebner phenomenon: psoriatic lesions appear at sites of trauma/injury. Nikolsky sign: lateral pressure causes skin to shear off (seen in pemphigus vulgaris, SSSS). Carpet tack sign: follicular plugging in discoid lupus — when scales are removed, they show spines like a carpet tack.

</details>

**Q7. A psoriasis patient with extensive skin involvement requires systemic therapy. Which of the following is the most important monitoring parameter for methotrexate therapy?**
- A) Serum creatinine and urine analysis for nephrotoxicity
- B) Liver function tests and liver biopsy (cumulative dose monitoring) for hepatotoxicity
- C) CBC for thrombocytopenia and ECG for cardiac toxicity
- D) Thyroid function tests and pulmonary function tests

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Methotrexate (MTX) is the most widely used systemic agent for moderate-to-severe psoriasis in India. The major concern is hepatotoxicity (hepatic fibrosis and cirrhosis with cumulative doses >1.5–2 g). Liver biopsy is recommended after every 1.5–2 g cumulative dose in high-risk patients. Regular LFT monitoring is mandatory. MTX is teratogenic (Category X), and folate supplementation (5 mg/week) reduces mucositis and hematological toxicity without reducing efficacy. CBC monitoring for myelosuppression is also required (not the primary concern compared to hepatotoxicity).

</details>

**Q8. A 25-year-old woman has flat-topped, violaceous, polygonal papules on the flexor aspect of her wrists and ankles with fine white lines on the surface. She also has white lacy lesions on the buccal mucosa. The hepatitis C antibody test is positive. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Psoriasis
- B) Lichen planus
- C) Secondary syphilis
- D) Discoid lupus erythematosus

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Lichen planus (LP) is characterized by the "4 Ps": Pruritic, Purple (violaceous), Polygonal, Papules — with Wickham's striae (fine white reticulate lines on the surface, representing focal hypergranulosis). Oral LP (Wickham's striae on buccal mucosa) is present in 70%. LP is strongly associated with Hepatitis C virus (HCV). Koebner phenomenon is also seen in LP. Treatment: potent topical corticosteroids; systemic steroids for severe/generalized disease. The association with HCV is a classic exam point.

</details>

**Q9. A 22-year-old woman presents with an oval, salmon-pink, scaly patch (4 cm × 3 cm) on her trunk that appeared 10 days before multiple smaller oval lesions erupted along the skin cleavage lines in a "Christmas tree" pattern. The large initial lesion is called:**
- A) Mother spot
- B) Herald patch
- C) Primary plaque
- D) Nummular patch

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Pityriasis rosea presents with a herald patch (primary lesion — a single, large, oval, salmon-colored patch with a fine collarette scale trailing edge, occurring 1–2 weeks before the secondary eruption). Secondary lesions: smaller oval lesions with long axis following Langer's lines (cleavage lines of skin), creating a "Christmas tree" distribution on the back. The disease is self-limiting (6–8 weeks). Causative agent: HHV-6 or HHV-7 (probable). Treatment: symptomatic — antihistamines, acyclovir may reduce duration if started early.

</details>

**Q10. Which of the following is the most common extra-articular manifestation of psoriasis?**
- A) Psoriatic arthritis
- B) Uveitis
- C) Cardiovascular disease
- D) Inflammatory bowel disease

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) occurs in approximately 30% of psoriasis patients and is the most common extra-articular manifestation. Patterns: (1) oligoarticular asymmetric (most common), (2) DIP joint predominant (with nail changes — "sausage digits" dactylitis), (3) rheumatoid-like symmetric polyarthritis, (4) axial spondyloarthropathy, (5) arthritis mutilans (pencil-in-cup deformity — most severe). PsA is seronegative (negative RF). HLA-B27 is associated with axial involvement. Treatment: NSAIDs, DMARDs (methotrexate), biologics (TNF-α inhibitors).

</details>

## BLISTERING DISORDERS (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 55-year-old woman presents with flaccid bullae arising on normal-appearing skin. The bullae rupture easily, leaving raw, painful erosions. Nikolsky sign is positive. Direct immunofluorescence shows IgG and C3 deposits in an intercellular pattern throughout the epidermis ("fishnet/chicken wire" pattern). The diagnosis is:**
- A) Bullous pemphigoid
- B) Pemphigus vulgaris
- C) Dermatitis herpetiformis
- D) Linear IgA disease

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Pemphigus vulgaris (PV) is an autoimmune intraepidermal blistering disorder caused by IgG antibodies against desmoglein 3 (mucosal) and desmoglein 1 (skin) — desmosomal cadherins holding keratinocytes together. Acantholysis (loss of cell-cell adhesion) produces flaccid blisters on normal skin + positive Nikolsky sign. DIF: intercellular IgG + C3 ("fishnet" pattern). Nikolsky sign: lateral pressure on normal skin → sheet-like peeling. Treatment: systemic corticosteroids + azathioprine/mycophenolate mofetil; rituximab for refractory cases.

</details>

**Q12. An 80-year-old man develops large, tense bullae on an urticarial base involving the inner thighs, abdomen, and flexures. Mucosal involvement is absent. Nikolsky sign is NEGATIVE. The diagnosis and autoantibody target are:**
- A) Pemphigus vulgaris — anti-desmoglein 3
- B) Bullous pemphigoid — anti-BP180 (type XVII collagen) and anti-BP230
- C) Epidermolysis bullosa acquisita — anti-type VII collagen
- D) Dermatitis herpetiformis — anti-endomysial antibody

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bullous pemphigoid (BP) is the most common autoimmune blistering disease in the elderly. IgG antibodies target hemidesmosomal proteins BP180 (BPAG2 — type XVII collagen) and BP230 (BPAG1) at the dermoepidermal junction — a subepidermal blister. Key contrast with PV: tense bullae (subepidermal, therefore more intact roof) on urticarial base, NO mucosal involvement, NEGATIVE Nikolsky sign, and elderly patients. DIF: linear IgG + C3 at the basement membrane zone. Treatment: superpotent topical corticosteroids (clobetasol); oral prednisolone; doxycycline + niacinamide.

</details>

**Q13. A 5-year-old child develops sudden onset fever followed by widespread superficial blistering, and the skin peels off in large sheets with the slightest touch. The area around the mouth shows honey-colored crusting. The causative toxin and diagnosis are:**
- A) Streptococcus pyogenes exotoxin — Toxic shock syndrome
- B) Staphylococcus aureus exfoliative toxin — SSSS (Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome)
- C) S. aureus Panton-Valentine leukocidin — Necrotizing fasciitis
- D) Varicella zoster virus — Eczema herpeticum

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

SSSS (Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome) is caused by exfoliative toxins (ET-A, ET-B) from phage group II Staphylococcus aureus, which specifically cleave desmoglein 1 in the superficial epidermis. The child appears to be "scalded." Perioral crusting is characteristic (the primary infection site). Nikolsky sign is positive. The split is subcorneal (upper epidermis) — biopsy shows no inflammatory cells at the split (unlike TEN, where full-thickness epidermal necrosis occurs with dermal lymphocyte infiltrate). Treatment: IV antistaphylococcal antibiotics (cloxacillin/nafcillin).

</details>

**Q14. A patient on trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole develops a widespread blistering eruption with painful mucosal erosions (mouth, eyes, genitals). Skin detachment involves 35% of the body surface area. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS)
- B) Toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)
- C) SSSS
- D) Pemphigus vulgaris

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

SJS-TEN spectrum is classified by BSA of epidermal detachment: SJS (<10% BSA), SJS-TEN overlap (10–30%), TEN (>30% BSA). Both involve drug-induced full-thickness epidermal necrosis with mucosal involvement. Commonest culprit drugs: sulfonamides (TMP-SMX), allopurinol, anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin), NSAIDs. Nikolsky sign positive. Management: immediate drug withdrawal, ICU burns unit care, supportive. SCORTEN score predicts mortality. Cyclosporine and IV immunoglobulin have been used. SSSS lacks mucosal involvement and is superficial (subcorneal split).

</details>

**Q15. Which of the following correctly pairs the blistering disorder with its immunofluorescence finding and antibody target?**
- A) Pemphigus vulgaris — linear IgG at BMZ — anti-BP180
- B) Bullous pemphigoid — intercellular IgG — anti-desmoglein 3
- C) Dermatitis herpetiformis — granular IgA at dermal papillae tips — anti-tissue transglutaminase
- D) Linear IgA disease — granular IgA at BMZ — anti-endomysium

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Dermatitis herpetiformis (DH) is associated with celiac disease (gluten-sensitive enteropathy). DIF shows granular IgA deposits at the tips of dermal papillae — pathognomonic. ## SKIN INFECTIONS (Q16–Q20)

**Q17. A patient in India is diagnosed with multibacillary (MB) leprosy (5 skin lesions + 2 nerve trunks involved, slit-skin smear positive). According to the NLEP (National Leprosy Eradication Programme), the correct treatment regimen is:**
- A) Rifampicin 600 mg monthly + Dapsone 100 mg daily for 6 months
- B) Rifampicin 600 mg monthly + Clofazimine 300 mg monthly + Dapsone 100 mg daily + Clofazimine 50 mg daily for 12 months
- C) Rifampicin + Dapsone + Ofloxacin for 6 months
- D) Dapsone monotherapy for 24 months

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Under India's NLEP (WHO/NLEP MDT): **Multibacillary (MB) leprosy** (>5 lesions or smear positive): Rifampicin 600 mg once monthly (supervised) + Clofazimine 300 mg once monthly + Dapsone 100 mg daily + Clofazimine 50 mg daily × **12 months**. **Paucibacillary (PB) leprosy** (1–5 lesions): Rifampicin 600 mg monthly + Dapsone 100 mg daily × **6 months**. Single-lesion PB: ROM (Rifampicin + Ofloxacin + Minocycline) single dose. Clofazimine causes skin hyperpigmentation (reddish-brown) and is the distinguishing MB drug.

</details>

**Q16. A leprosy patient on MDT develops painful red nodules on the face and limbs, high fever, and burning sensation in the hands and feet. Nerve function impairment is noted. This reaction is classified as:**
- A) Type 1 reaction (reversal reaction)
- B) Type 2 reaction (Erythema nodosum leprosum — ENL)
- C) Lucio phenomenon
- D) Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Type 2 reaction (ENL — Erythema Nodosum Leprosum) occurs in MB leprosy (LL/BL types), is immune-complex mediated (Type III hypersensitivity), and presents with crops of painful erythematous nodules, fever, malaise, and systemic features (iritis, orchitis, nephritis). Treatment: thalidomide (drug of choice for ENL in non-pregnant adults) or prednisolone; clofazimine for recurrent ENL. Type 1 (reversal) reaction: delayed hypersensitivity (Type IV), occurs in borderline leprosy (BT/BB/BL), presents with skin lesion upgrading and acute nerve function impairment — treated with prednisolone.

</details>

- A) Erythrasma — topical erythromycin

<summary>Answer</summary>

Tinea cruris (ringworm of the groin) is a dermatophyte infection (Trichophyton rubrum most common) presenting as itchy, erythematous, annular lesions with central clearing and a raised, scaly, active border — sparing the scrotum (unlike candidal intertrigo which involves the scrotum). **Q19. A 65-year-old diabetic woman develops an acute painful vesicular rash over the left cheek, ear canal, and external ear, with ipsilateral facial nerve palsy (LMN type) and sensorineural hearing loss. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Bell's palsy
- B) Ramsay Hunt syndrome (Herpes zoster oticus)
- C) Otitis media with facial nerve palsy
- D) Trigeminal neuralgia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ramsay Hunt syndrome is caused by VZV (varicella-zoster virus) reactivation in the geniculate ganglion of the facial nerve (CN VII). **Q20. A 10-year-old child has multiple, skin-colored, dome-shaped papules with central umbilication on the trunk and axillae. The lesions are painless and non-inflammatory. The causative organism is:**
- A) Human papillomavirus (HPV)
- B) Molluscum contagiosum virus (MCV — poxvirus)
- C) Herpes simplex virus
- D) Varicella-zoster virus

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

## PIGMENTATION AND SKIN TUMORS (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 25-year-old woman has well-defined, chalk-white patches on her face and hands that fluoresce bright white under Wood's lamp examination. She has a personal history of hypothyroidism. The diagnosis and treatment are:**
- A) Pityriasis versicolor — antifungal (ketoconazole)
- B) Vitiligo — narrow-band UVB phototherapy + topical tacrolimus
- C) Tuberous sclerosis — no treatment required
- D) Leprosy — MDT

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Vitiligo is an autoimmune depigmentation disorder caused by destruction of melanocytes (T-cell mediated). Wood's lamp: bright chalk-white fluorescence (enhances depigmentation in fair-skinned patients). Associated with autoimmune conditions: Hashimoto's thyroiditis, type 1 DM, Addison's disease, pernicious anemia. Management: (1) Repigmentation: narrow-band UVB (NB-UVB) — most effective; topical corticosteroids; topical tacrolimus (face, genitals); (2) Camouflage; (3) Surgical: melanocyte transplantation for stable vitiligo. Pityriasis versicolor: copper-orange fluorescence under Wood's lamp; KOH shows "spaghetti and meatballs."

</details>

**Q22. A 35-year-old woman develops symmetrical brown hyperpigmentation on her forehead, cheeks, and upper lip. It worsened during her recent pregnancy and with OCP use. Wood's lamp examination enhances the pigmentation. The diagnosis and first-line treatment are:**
- A) Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation — hydroquinone
- B) Melasma (chloasma) — sun protection + triple combination cream (hydroquinone + tretinoin + mometasone)
- C) Addison's disease — corticosteroid replacement
- D) Acanthosis nigricans — treat underlying insulin resistance

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Melasma (chloasma — "mask of pregnancy") is epidermal/mixed hyperpigmentation triggered by UV radiation, pregnancy, OCPs, and hormonal influences. Symmetrical distribution over the centrofacial (most common), malar, and mandibular areas. Wood's lamp enhances epidermal melasma (not dermal). First-line treatment: strict photoprotection (broad-spectrum SPF ≥30) + triple combination cream (Kligman's formula: 4% hydroquinone + 0.05% tretinoin + 0.01% fluocinolone acetonide). Hydroquinone inhibits tyrosinase; tretinoin increases epidermal cell turnover; steroid reduces irritation.

</details>

**Q23. A 60-year-old man presents with a slowly enlarging, pearly, translucent papule with raised rolled borders and telangiectasias on his nose. The center has a central ulcer. He has a history of chronic sun exposure. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Squamous cell carcinoma
- B) Merkel cell carcinoma
- C) Basal cell carcinoma
- D) Keratoacanthoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common skin cancer (and most common cancer) in light-skinned individuals. It arises from basal cells of the epidermis (NOT from hair follicles as previously thought — now from pluripotent stem cells). Classic nodular BCC: pearly/translucent papule with raised "rolled" borders, telangiectasias, and central ulceration ("rodent ulcer"). Most common site: nose (most common), face. Virtually never metastasizes despite local invasion. Treatment: surgical excision (Mohs micrographic surgery for facial lesions). The PTCH1 (hedgehog pathway) gene is mutated in sporadic and syndromic BCC.

</details>

**Q24. A 70-year-old farmer with extensive sun exposure has a rough, hyperkeratotic, scaly lesion on his lower lip that is firm on palpation. Biopsy shows malignant squamous cells with keratin pearl formation. Regional lymph nodes are palpable. The precursor lesion and diagnosis are:**
- A) Seborrheic keratosis → BCC
- B) Actinic keratosis → Squamous cell carcinoma
- C) Dysplastic nevus → Melanoma
- D) Bowen's disease → Merkel cell carcinoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) is the second most common skin cancer, arising from keratinocytes. Precursor lesions: actinic keratosis (most important), Bowen's disease (SCC in situ), leukoplakia (oral SCC), Marjolin's ulcer (SCC in chronic scar/burn), xeroderma pigmentosum. Unlike BCC, SCC can metastasize to regional lymph nodes (5–10% of cases). High-risk SCC: lip, ear, anogenital region. Keratin pearls (squamous eddies) on histology are characteristic. Treatment: wide local excision; sentinel lymph node biopsy for high-risk lesions. SCC lip is commonest in lower lip (sun exposure).

</details>

**Q25. A 45-year-old man presents with a changing mole on his back — asymmetric, with irregular borders, mixed colors (black, brown, pink), and 8 mm in diameter. A biopsy reveals melanoma with a Breslow thickness of 2.5 mm. The most important prognostic factor for melanoma is:**
- A) Diameter of the lesion
- B) Breslow thickness (depth of invasion)
- C) Number of color variations
- D) Anatomical site

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Breslow thickness (depth of tumor invasion in mm from the granular layer of the epidermis to the deepest tumor cell) is the single most important prognostic factor in melanoma. ABCDE criteria for clinical screening: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6 mm, Evolution. Breslow thickness guides surgical margins and sentinel lymph node biopsy decisions: ≤1 mm (thin, excellent prognosis), 1–4 mm (intermediate), >4 mm (poor prognosis). Clark level (anatomical layer of invasion) is less reproducible and less prognostically significant than Breslow. BRAF V600E mutation: targeted therapy (vemurafenib).

</details>

## DRUG REACTIONS AND MISCELLANEOUS (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. A 45-year-old epileptic man on carbamazepine for 6 weeks develops fever, facial edema, lymphadenopathy, maculopapular rash covering >50% BSA, and elevated eosinophil count (1800/mm³) with deranged liver function. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Stevens-Johnson syndrome
- B) DRESS syndrome (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms)
- C) Serum sickness
- D) Morbilliform drug eruption

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

DRESS syndrome (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms) — also called Drug-induced Hypersensitivity Syndrome (DiHS) — is a severe, potentially life-threatening drug reaction with: delayed onset (2–8 weeks after drug initiation), extensive skin rash (>50% BSA maculopapular), facial edema (characteristic), fever, lymphadenopathy, eosinophilia (>1500/mm³), atypical lymphocytosis, and multi-organ involvement (liver, kidneys, lungs, heart). Common culprits: anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine), allopurinol, sulfonamides, dapsone. Mortality 10%. Immediate drug cessation + systemic corticosteroids.

</details>

**Q27. A 30-year-old man with a history of taking naproxen for joint pain develops a sharply defined, dusky-red, round erythematous patch at the same location on his right forearm each time he takes the drug. After resolution, a hyperpigmented macule remains. The diagnosis is:**
- A) DRESS syndrome
- B) Fixed drug eruption (FDE)
- C) Urticaria
- D) Erythema multiforme

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Fixed drug eruption (FDE) is characterized by a solitary (or few) well-defined, round/oval, erythematous-to-violaceous plaque that recurs at the SAME anatomical site with each exposure to the offending drug. After resolution, a residual hyperpigmented macule persists. Common sites: lips, genitalia (glans penis — "balanitis fixed"), perianal area, hands. Common culprits: NSAIDs (naproxen, aspirin), tetracyclines, cotrimoxazole, paracetamol, barbiturates. Mechanism: Type IV hypersensitivity with CD8+ memory T cells resident in the skin. The "fixed" nature at the same site is pathognomonic.

</details>

**Q28. A 17-year-old girl presents with open and closed comedones on her forehead and cheeks, inflammatory papules, and nodules. She is planning a pregnancy in 6 months. She is prescribed isotretinoin. The most critical counseling point is:**
- A) Isotretinoin causes permanent hair loss
- B) Isotretinoin is highly teratogenic — two forms of contraception mandatory, pregnancy test before and during therapy
- C) Isotretinoin commonly causes acute pancreatitis
- D) Isotretinoin must be taken on an empty stomach for optimal absorption

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Isotretinoin (13-cis retinoic acid) is the most effective treatment for severe noduloulystic/refractory acne and works by reducing sebaceous gland size and sebum production, normalizing follicular keratinization, reducing P. acnes colonization, and having anti-inflammatory effects. CRITICAL RISK: Category X teratogen — causes severe craniofacial, cardiac, and CNS defects. iPLEDGE program (USA) / mandatory pregnancy prevention: negative pregnancy test before initiation, two contraceptive methods, monthly monitoring. Other side effects: cheilitis (most common), dry skin, elevated triglycerides, hepatotoxicity, myalgia, IBD risk. Isotretinoin should be taken WITH food (fat-soluble).

**Q29. A 35-year-old woman presents with persistent central facial erythema, flushing triggered by hot drinks and sun, and papulopustules on the nose and cheeks. There are no comedones. The diagnosis and first-line treatment are:**
- A) Acne vulgaris — benzoyl peroxide
- B) Rosacea — topical metronidazole or azelaic acid
- C) Perioral dermatitis — topical clindamycin
- D) Seborrheic dermatitis — ketoconazole

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the face characterized by: (1) flushing/persistent central facial erythema, (2) papulopustules WITHOUT comedones (distinguishes from acne), (3) telangiectasias, and (4) rhinophyma (sebaceous gland hypertrophy of the nose — more common in men). Triggers: heat, alcohol, spicy food, sun, Demodex folliculorum mites. First-line topical treatment: metronidazole 0.75–1% or azelaic acid 15–20%. For papulopustular rosacea: doxycycline 40 mg/day (sub-antimicrobial dose). Brimonidine gel for persistent erythema (alpha-2 agonist causing vasoconstriction).

</details>

**Q30. A 28-year-old woman develops widespread urticaria (wheals with surrounding erythema, intensely pruritic, transient <24 hours) after taking ibuprofen. Each wheal resolves within 2 hours without leaving any mark. The first-line treatment is:**
- A) Systemic corticosteroids
- B) Second-generation non-sedating antihistamines (cetirizine/fexofenadine)
- C) Adrenaline (epinephrine) IM
- D) IV diphenhydramine (first-generation antihistamine)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Urticaria (hives) is characterized by transient (typically <24 hours), pruritic wheals with surrounding erythema that resolve completely without residual marks. It results from mast cell degranulation and histamine release. Drug-induced urticaria from NSAIDs occurs via COX-1 inhibition (not IgE-mediated). First-line treatment: second-generation non-sedating H1 antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine, levocetirizine) — preferred due to better tolerability and no sedation. Adrenaline IM is reserved for anaphylaxis (airway involvement, hypotension). Systemic corticosteroids are used for severe/refractory acute urticaria — not routine first-line.

</details>
