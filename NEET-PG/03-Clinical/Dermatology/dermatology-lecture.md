# Dermatology — Lecture Notes for NEET PG
### Written in the voice of a clinician teaching at the dermatology clinic

## Skin as an Organ — First Principles

### The Epidermis — A Living Wall

The skin is the largest organ in the body — approximately 1.5–2 m² in surface area, weighing 3–5 kg. Before we look at any disease, we must understand what the skin is for, because every major skin disease is a failure of one or more of these functions. The skin serves as a physical and chemical barrier preventing water loss (transepidermal water loss, TEWL) and blocking the entry of pathogens, allergens, and toxins. It regulates temperature through eccrine sweat glands and control of cutaneous blood flow. It is a sensory organ — packed with mechanoreceptors (Meissner's, Merkel's, Pacinian, Ruffini's corpuscles), thermoreceptors, nociceptors. It performs immune surveillance through Langerhans cells (dendritic antigen-presenting cells of the epidermis). And it synthesizes vitamin D — UVB radiation converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in keratinocytes to previtamin D3, which is then isomerized to vitamin D3.

The epidermis is the outer cellular layer, composed predominantly of keratinocytes that undergo a continuous, orderly process of differentiation from the bottom upward. The basal layer (stratum germinativum) sits on the basement membrane and is the proliferative layer — basal cells divide, and one daughter cell remains in the basal layer while the other begins its journey upward. As keratinocytes move up, they progressively differentiate: they accumulate keratin filaments, form cornified envelopes, and eventually lose their nuclei and organelles to become anucleate, protein-filled envelopes (corneocytes). The stratum spinosum (spinous, prickle-cell layer) derives its name from the spine-like intercellular bridges — desmosomes — that connect adjacent keratinocytes; these are the mechanical anchors of the epidermis. The stratum granulosum is where cells accumulate keratohyalin granules (containing profilaggrin, loricrin, and other proteins) and lamellar bodies (membrane-coating granules that will release lipids — ceramides, free fatty acids, cholesterol — into the intercellular spaces, forming the "mortar" of the corneal barrier). The stratum corneum is the final product: 15–20 layers of flat, dead corneocytes embedded in a lipid matrix — the "bricks and mortar" model. The corneocytes are the bricks; the intercellular lipids are the mortar.

**Analogy:** The epidermis is like a brick wall being continuously rebuilt from the ground up. The basal cells are the bricklayers. The corneocytes are the finished bricks. The lipid lamellae are the mortar. The strength of the wall depends on the integrity of both bricks and mortar. Psoriasis is a wall built too fast — the bricks are poorly formed and the mortar is incomplete. Ichthyosis is a wall where the old bricks stick together and won't shed — the corneocytes don't desquamate properly.

Normal keratinocyte transit time from basal layer to desquamation is approximately 28 days (2 weeks in the epidermis, 2 weeks in the stratum corneum). In psoriasis, this turnover is dramatically accelerated to approximately 4 days — the keratinocytes have no time to differentiate properly, the cornified envelope is incomplete, and the cells retain their nuclei (parakeratosis). This is why psoriatic plaques are covered in silvery-white scales: the accelerated, poorly differentiated keratinocytes shed in visible clumps rather than invisibly as we normally shed our skin (we shed approximately 30,000–40,000 skin cells per hour — entirely invisibly because each corneocyte is a single, flat, transparent cell).

The dermis lies beneath the epidermis, separated by the basement membrane zone (BMZ). It is composed of collagen (mainly Type I — tensile strength, and Type III — elastic properties), elastin (provides recoil), and ground substance (glycosaminoglycans — hyaluronic acid, dermatan sulphate — which provide hydration and volume). The dermis houses the hair follicles, sebaceous glands, eccrine and apocrine sweat glands, blood vessels, lymphatics, and nerves. The dermal papillae project upward into the epidermis, interlocking with the rete ridges (downward projections of the epidermis) — this interlocking increases the surface area of the dermo-epidermal junction (DEJ) and resists shearing forces. In blistering diseases that affect the DEJ, this interlocking is disrupted.

The hypodermis (subcutis) is the deepest layer — adipose tissue organized into lobules by fibrous septa, providing thermal insulation, mechanical cushioning, and energy storage.

> **Key exam point:** Stratum spinosum = site of desmosomes (targeted in pemphigus vulgaris). Stratum granulosum = site of lamellar bodies (lipid barrier assembly). Stratum corneum = barrier (disrupted in atopic dermatitis — filaggrin deficiency, ichthyosis). Basement membrane zone = site of hemidesmosomes (targeted in bullous pemphigoid). Know all epidermal layers in order: basale → spinosum → granulosum → lucidum (only on palms/soles) → corneum.

## Inflammatory Skin Diseases

### Atopic Dermatitis — The Leaky Skin Model

Atopic dermatitis (AD, eczema) is the most common inflammatory skin disease, affecting 15–20% of children and 1–3% of adults worldwide. It is the first manifestation of what allergists call the "atopic march" — the natural history by which atopic disease progresses through different organ systems over a person's lifetime. Understanding AD from first principles requires understanding the skin barrier, because the disease begins there.

The skin barrier defect in AD centers on filaggrin. Filaggrin (filament aggregating protein) is encoded by the FLG gene on chromosome 1q21 — a gene within the "epidermal differentiation complex," a cluster of genes involved in terminal keratinocyte differentiation. Filaggrin performs critical functions: it aggregates keratin intermediate filaments into tight bundles in the stratum granulosum (contributing to the mechanical strength of corneocytes), and its proteolytic breakdown products form natural moisturizing factor (NMF) — amino acids, pyrrolidone carboxylic acid, and urocanic acid — which are hygroscopic and maintain stratum corneum hydration. Loss-of-function mutations in FLG are found in approximately 30–40% of patients with moderate-to-severe AD (and in 10% of the general population in some ethnic groups). These mutations weaken the skin barrier: the stratum corneum is more permeable, TEWL is increased (the skin is chronically dry), and the gap-ridden barrier allows allergens, irritants, and microorganisms to penetrate.

Once allergens penetrate the disrupted barrier, keratinocytes at the skin surface release "danger signals" — TSLP (thymic stromal lymphopoietin), IL-25, and IL-33 — which activate dendritic cells (particularly plasmacytoid DCs and inflammatory dendritic epidermal cells, IDECs). These DCs present allergen to T-cells and drive a Th2 polarization — Th2 cells produce IL-4, IL-5, IL-13. IL-4 and IL-13 upregulate IgE production from B cells → IgE binds to mast cells and basophils in the skin → on allergen re-exposure, mast cell degranulation → histamine release → itch. IL-4 and IL-13 also suppress filaggrin and loricrin expression, further weakening the barrier in a vicious cycle. IL-5 recruits eosinophils. The result is a chronic pruritic dermatitis with an underlying Th2-dominated immune response.

**Analogy:** The leaky skin model: imagine the skin as a brick wall with crumbling mortar (filaggrin deficiency). Rain (allergens) seeps through the cracks. Inside the wall, the immune cells (soldiers) become sensitized and mount an excessive response every time rain seeps in. The soldiers also damage the mortar in their fight, making the wall even leakier. This is the self-reinforcing cycle of atopic dermatitis.

The clinical presentation follows a characteristic age-related distribution: in infants (< 2 years), eczema affects the face (cheeks, forehead) and extensor surfaces of the limbs. In children and adolescents, it preferentially involves the flexural creases — antecubital fossae (inside of the elbows), popliteal fossae (behind the knees), wrists, ankles, neck. This flexural distribution is almost pathognomonic. The skin is erythematous, dry, with excoriations (from scratching), and in chronic disease, lichenification (thickening and accentuation of skin markings, from repeated rubbing — the skin literally toughens in response to mechanical trauma). In adults, the pattern may be more diffuse or involve the hands.

The atopic march is a longitudinal epidemiological phenomenon: children with AD have a significantly elevated risk of developing asthma (30–50%) and allergic rhinitis (35–66%). The current understanding is that the sensitization begins at the skin — allergens (house dust mite, cat dander, peanut) penetrate the disrupted skin barrier, are presented to the immune system, and generate a Th2 systemic response. Subsequently, when the same allergens are inhaled, the pre-primed immune system mounts an airway allergic response. This "skin first" sensitization model has important therapeutic implications: early, aggressive emollient therapy to repair the skin barrier in high-risk infants may prevent the development of asthma — the LEAP trial showed early peanut introduction prevents peanut allergy; similar trials with barrier emollients are ongoing.

**Clinical connection:** The cornerstone of AD treatment is barrier repair — twice-daily emollients to the whole body, avoiding irritants (harsh soaps, hot water, synthetic fabrics). Topical corticosteroids remain the mainstay of flare treatment. Tacrolimus and pimecrolimus (topical calcineurin inhibitors) are useful for maintenance, particularly on the face and eyelids where topical steroids cause complications (skin atrophy, glaucoma). Dupilumab — a monoclonal antibody against the IL-4 receptor alpha subunit (blocking both IL-4 and IL-13 signaling) — is the first biologic approved for moderate-to-severe AD, and it represents a major advance for patients who fail topical therapy.

> **Key exam point:** AD is associated with filaggrin mutations (barrier defect), Th2 skewing (elevated IgE, eosinophilia), and Staph aureus superinfection (colonizes 90% of AD skin — Staph releases toxins that act as superantigens, driving further inflammation). The Hanifin-Rajka criteria for diagnosis: major criteria (pruritus, typical morphology and distribution, chronic relapsing course, personal or family history of atopy) plus minor criteria (elevated IgE, early age of onset, dry skin/ichthyosis, Dennie-Morgan lines under the eyes, keratosis pilaris, pityriasis alba).

### Psoriasis — The Immunological Blueprint

Psoriasis is a chronic, immune-mediated inflammatory skin disease affecting approximately 2–3% of the global population. The hallmark lesion — a well-demarcated, erythematous plaque covered with silvery-white scales — is the visible consequence of a specific immunological cascade that drives keratinocyte hyperproliferation and angiogenesis. Understanding the immunology is now essential because it directly informs the most effective available treatments.

The initiating event in psoriasis is not fully understood, but genetic predisposition (HLA-Cw6 is the strongest associated allele) combined with environmental triggers (streptococcal throat infection — particularly in guttate psoriasis, Koebner phenomenon — lesions at sites of trauma, stress, drugs — beta-blockers, lithium, antimalarials, NSAIDs) converges on activation of plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs). These pDCs produce type I interferons and IL-12, which drive naive T-cells toward a Th1/Th17 phenotype. The pivotal cytokines in psoriatic plaques are IL-17 (from Th17 cells) and TNF-α. IL-17 (principally IL-17A) acts on keratinocytes to drive hyperproliferation, produce antimicrobial peptides (defensins, cathelicidins), and generate further inflammatory cytokines. IL-22 (from Th17 cells) drives epidermal hyperplasia and impairs differentiation. TNF-α amplifies the inflammation and maintains the cycle.

The result at the tissue level is dramatic: keratinocyte transit time drops from 28 days to approximately 4 days (a seven-fold acceleration). The epidermis thickens enormously (acanthosis — thickening of the spinous layer). The granular layer is absent or reduced (because there is no time for proper granular differentiation). Parakeratosis is present (retention of nuclei in the stratum corneum). Below the epidermis, blood vessels in the dermal papillae become dilated and tortuous (angiogenesis driven by VEGF, which is also upregulated by IL-17). These dilated vessels are prominent (the "red" of the plaque), and when the overlying scale is removed, the vessels are exposed — the Auspitz sign: pinpoint bleeding when scales are removed, because the thinned epidermis over the elongated dermal papillae is easily torn.

**Analogy:** A psoriatic plaque is like a city building roads in emergency mode — the concrete isn't set before trucks are driving on it, pipes are leaking everywhere, and the scaffolding (normal epidermal architecture) is never properly assembled. The result is a chaotic, incomplete structure that is simultaneously too thick and too fragile.

The Koebner (isomorphic) phenomenon is the appearance of psoriatic lesions at sites of skin trauma — scratches, surgical wounds, tattoos, sunburn. This reflects the fact that local skin trauma releases damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) that activate the innate immune response, which in a genetically predisposed individual gets amplified into the full psoriatic inflammatory cascade.

Clinical variants of psoriasis reveal the immune pathways involved. **Plaque psoriasis** (psoriasis vulgaris, ~90%): classic well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, predominating on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, and sacrum. **Guttate psoriasis**: small (<1 cm), teardrop-shaped papules erupting suddenly, often triggered by group A streptococcal pharyngitis — particularly in children and young adults, and often the first presentation of psoriasis; it is thought that streptococcal M protein cross-reacts with keratinocyte proteins (molecular mimicry), activating T-cells. **Pustular psoriasis**: non-infectious pustules driven by IL-36 (rather than IL-17) — generalized (von Zumbusch) type is acute and life-threatening (fever, malaise, sheets of sterile pustules) — triggered by stopping systemic corticosteroids (never stop steroids abruptly in psoriasis). **Erythrodermic psoriasis**: >90% body surface area involvement, erythroderma with generalized exfoliation — a dermatological emergency (impaired thermoregulation, fluid loss, high-output cardiac failure risk, infection risk). **Psoriatic arthritis**: occurs in 20–30% of psoriasis patients — HLA-B27 associated, affects DIP joints, causes sausage digits (dactylitis), enthesitis, sacroiliitis, asymmetric oligoarthritis.

> **Key exam point:** The cytokine axis in psoriasis: Th17 → IL-17 → keratinocyte hyperproliferation. Treatment ladder: topicals (corticosteroids, vitamin D analogues — calcipotriol, retinoids, tar) → phototherapy (narrowband UVB, PUVA) → systemic (methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin) → biologics (anti-TNF: adalimumab, infliximab; anti-IL-12/23 p40: ustekinumab; anti-IL-17: secukinumab, ixekizumab; anti-IL-23 p19: guselkumab, risankizumab). Biologics targeting IL-17 and IL-23 have the highest efficacy (PASI 90 and PASI 100 rates).

### Contact Dermatitis — Irritant vs Allergic

Contact dermatitis is inflammation of the skin caused by contact with an external substance, and it comes in two fundamentally different forms that must not be confused. **Irritant contact dermatitis (ICD)** is a non-immune-mediated reaction — it occurs when a substance directly damages keratinocytes, disrupts the skin barrier, or overwhelms the skin's repair capacity. It can occur in anyone given sufficient exposure (it is dose- and time-dependent). Common irritants: water and detergents (dishwasher's hands), alkalis (cement, cleaning products), acids, solvents. Clinically: erythema, dryness, fissuring at the site of contact.

**Allergic contact dermatitis (ACD)** is a Type IV (delayed-type) hypersensitivity reaction — T-cell-mediated. It occurs only in sensitized individuals. The sensitization phase: a hapten (a small chemical molecule that cannot alone trigger an immune response) penetrates the skin, binds to a skin protein to form a hapten-carrier conjugate, which is taken up and processed by Langerhans cells (the skin's resident antigen-presenting cells), which migrate to regional lymph nodes and present the hapten to naïve T-cells → T-cell clonal expansion → memory T-cells return to the skin. The elicitation phase: on re-exposure to the same hapten, memory T-cells in the skin are activated within 24–72 hours → release IFN-γ and TNF-α → inflammation → pruritic, vesicular dermatitis at the site of contact. The distribution mirrors the contact pattern — a classic presentation is a linear streak of vesicles from poison ivy, or a pattern of the watchstrap for nickel allergy, or the pattern of footwear straps for chromate allergy.

Patch testing is the diagnostic gold standard for ACD. The standard battery of allergens (European Baseline Series or Indian Standard Series for the Indian context) is applied to the back under occlusion for 48 hours, then read at 48 hours and 96 hours. A positive patch test shows erythema, induration, and vesicles at the tested site — this is a reproducible T-cell-mediated reaction. The most common contact allergens: nickel (costume jewelry, jeans buttons), fragrance mix, balsam of Peru, rubber chemicals (gloves, footwear), chromate (cement, leather), preservatives (parabens, methylisothiazolinone in cosmetics).

## Blistering (Vesiculobullous) Diseases

### Pemphigus Vulgaris — The Blister that Breaks

To understand the blistering diseases, you must think vertically through the skin: where exactly does the blister form, and why? The answer depends entirely on which adhesion structures are targeted by the autoimmune attack. The position of the blister — within the epidermis (intraepidermal) or below the epidermis (subepidermal) — determines the clinical behavior completely.

**Pemphigus vulgaris (PV)** is an autoimmune intraepidermal blistering disease. The target antigens are desmoglein 3 (Dsg3) and, in patients with mucocutaneous involvement, also desmoglein 1 (Dsg1). Desmogleins are transmembrane cadherins — the adhesion molecules that form the core of desmosomes, the intercellular junctions that bind keratinocytes together in the spinous layer. The antibodies are IgG4 subclass, targeting the adhesion domain of Dsg3. When these antibodies bind desmoglein, they do not merely block the adhesion — they trigger downstream intracellular signaling (phosphorylation of desmosomal proteins, activation of proteases) that actively disrupts desmosomal integrity. The keratinocytes separate from each other (acantholysis) — like tiles falling off a mosaic when the grout dissolves. The result is a "tombstone" pattern on histology: a single layer of basal keratinocytes remaining attached to the basement membrane (like tombstones) while the overlying keratinocytes have separated and rounded up into the blister cavity.

The blister in PV is intraepidermal — which has critical clinical consequences. The roof of the blister is only the uppermost few layers of the epidermis (the thin, fragile suprabasal layers). This roof ruptures with the slightest pressure. In fact, in many patients, you rarely see intact blisters at all — what you see are painful erosions and crusts, because the blisters have already broken. **Nikolsky's sign** is positive: lateral pressure on normal-appearing skin adjacent to a lesion causes the epidermis to slide off, because the desmosomal adhesion is compromised diffusely. The Asboe-Hansen sign (pressure on the top of a blister causes it to spread laterally) is also positive, for the same reason.

PV predominantly affects mucous membranes — oral mucosa, oropharynx, esophagus, genitalia — because Dsg3 is the predominant desmoglein in mucosal epithelium (Dsg1 predominates in the superficial epidermis). Patients typically present first with painful oral erosions — often mistaken for aphthous ulcers for months — before the skin blisters appear. Painful oral erosions that do not heal, preventing eating and drinking, is the alarm symptom. The disease carries significant morbidity: extensive skin erosions lead to fluid and protein loss (like a burn), secondary infection (Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia, Candida), and before the steroid era had a >90% mortality. Even now, with treatment, PV has significant morbidity from the disease and its treatment.

**Clinical connection:** Diagnosis is confirmed by skin biopsy (two biopsies needed: one perilesional skin for routine histology showing suprabasal acantholysis, one perilesional for direct immunofluorescence showing IgG and C3 in a "fish-net" or "chicken-wire" pattern throughout the epidermis — IgG deposited on all keratinocyte surfaces). Serology: circulating anti-Dsg3 (and anti-Dsg1) antibodies by ELISA — titers correlate with disease activity (useful for monitoring). Treatment: systemic corticosteroids (prednisolone 1 mg/kg) plus a steroid-sparing agent (azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil). Rituximab (anti-CD20, depleting B cells that produce the pathogenic antibodies) has dramatically improved outcomes and is now often used early in severe disease.

### Bullous Pemphigoid — The Blister that Holds

**Bullous pemphigoid (BP)** is the most common autoimmune blistering disease, predominantly affecting the elderly (>70 years). The pathogenesis is fundamentally different from PV: the target antigens are at the basement membrane zone — specifically BP180 (type XVII collagen, a transmembrane component of hemidesmosomes) and BP230 (a plakin protein at the cytoplasmic face of hemidesmosomes). Hemidesmosomes anchor basal keratinocytes to the basement membrane via interaction with Laminin 332 and type IV collagen in the lamina lucida and lamina densa.

When IgG antibodies bind to BP180 and BP230, they activate complement (C3 deposition at the BMZ) and recruit neutrophils and eosinophils (by binding to Fc receptors on eosinophils — BP is characteristically associated with marked blood and tissue eosinophilia). These effector cells release proteases (elastase, metalloproteinases) that cleave the anchoring proteins, separating the epidermis from the dermis at the lamina lucida level. The blister is therefore subepidermal — the roof is the full thickness of the epidermis.

This anatomical difference has immediate clinical consequences. The roof of the blister is thick, strong, and resilient. The blisters in BP are **tense** — large, turgid, fluid-filled, sitting on an erythematous or urticarial base. They do not rupture easily. Nikolsky's sign is **negative** (the BMZ adhesion in non-lesional skin is intact — only the immunologically active areas are separated). This contrasts sharply with PV. The eruption is typically preceded by a pruritic urticarial phase (weeks to months of itching with urticarial plaques but no blisters) — this prodromal phase occurs while anti-BMZ antibodies accumulate but the proteolytic destruction has not yet created blisters.

**Analogy:** Compare PV and BP to two different scenarios of a building losing its floors. PV is like removing the screws from the floor boards — the boards come apart easily (the roof of the blister — just a few layers of cells — falls apart at the slightest provocation). BP is like blasting the entire floor-to-ceiling joint (basement membrane) — the floor itself (full epidermis) stays intact, but it separates from the wall (dermis) as a strong, intact unit. That is why BP blisters are tense and hold their integrity.

BP is typically less life-threatening than PV because: (1) mucous membrane involvement is much less common (oral BP occurs in only ~20% of cases, unlike near-universal oral involvement in PV); (2) the skin erosions are less extensive since blisters don't break as easily; (3) protein and fluid loss is less severe. However, BP occurs in an elderly population with multiple comorbidities, and the disease and its treatment (systemic corticosteroids) carry their own risks. Interestingly, recent studies show an association between BP and neurological disease (dementia, stroke, Parkinson's) — because neuronal isoforms of BP180 may be the primary target, with the skin involvement being secondary.

**Clinical connection:** Direct immunofluorescence of perilesional skin shows linear IgG and C3 at the BMZ (like a ribbon along the basement membrane) — distinguishing it from PV where deposition is throughout the epidermis. Salt-split skin preparation (skin treated with NaCl splits through the lamina lucida): in BP, the immune deposits are on the roof (epidermal) side — because the BMZ proteins targeted (BP180 is in the hemidesmosomes, which is in the basal keratinocyte cytoplasm/outer leaflet, above the split). Treatment: potent topical clobetasol propionate (can control mild-moderate BP without systemic steroids — important in elderly patients); systemic prednisolone + doxycycline (doxycycline has anti-inflammatory, anti-eosinophil properties independent of its antibiotic activity); azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, or rituximab for refractory cases.

> **Key exam point:** The blistering diseases table — know where the blister is and why. Nikolsky positive = intraepidermal (PV, pemphigus foliaceus, TEN, SSSS). Nikolsky negative = subepidermal (BP, DH, EBA, PCT). Acantholysis = PV (suprabasal), Hailey-Hailey (throughout). Eosinophils in blister = BP. Neutrophils in blister = DH, linear IgA disease. DIF patterns: fish-net (IgG throughout epidermis) = PV; linear IgG at BMZ = BP; granular IgA at dermal papillae = DH (dermatitis herpetiformis — associated with celiac disease, gluten-sensitive enteropathy).

## Infections — Viral, Bacterial, and Fungal

### Viral Skin Infections

**Herpes zoster (shingles)** is the reactivation of varicella-zoster virus (VZV) that has been latent in sensory dorsal root ganglia (or cranial nerve ganglia) since the primary infection (chickenpox). The virus reactivates (typically when cell-mediated immunity wanes — with aging, immunosuppression, stress) and travels down the sensory nerve to the skin of the corresponding dermatome, causing a painful vesicular eruption strictly confined to that dermatome. The dermatomal distribution — unilateral, does not cross the midline — is pathognomonic. The thoracic dermatomes are most commonly affected.

The prodrome is diagnostically challenging: severe burning, shooting pain in the dermatomal distribution appears 2–5 days before the rash. This pain may be misdiagnosed as pleuritis, MI, appendicitis, or renal colic, depending on the dermatome. The rash begins as erythematous macules, rapidly progresses to grouped vesicles on an erythematous base (the classic "dew drops on a rose petal" appearance — identical to primary chickenpox but dermatomal), then pustulates, crusts, and heals over 2–4 weeks. Complications: post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN — pain persisting after rash resolution, most feared complication, particularly in the elderly — caused by nerve fiber damage and central sensitization), herpes zoster ophthalmicus (HZO — V1 involvement, risk of corneal dendritic ulcers, uveitis, sight-threatening), Ramsay Hunt syndrome (geniculate ganglion of CN VII — ipsilateral facial palsy + vesicles in the ear + sensorineural hearing loss + vertigo), bacterial superinfection.

**Clinical connection:** Hutchinson's sign in HZO: vesicles on the tip or side of the nose (nasociliary nerve branch of V1) predict ocular involvement — urgent ophthalmology referral. Treatment: antiviral (acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir) within 72 hours of rash onset reduces severity, duration, and risk of PHN. PHN treatment: tricyclics (amitriptyline), gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin), topical lidocaine, capsaicin.

### Fungal Skin Infections

The superficial fungal infections (dermatophytoses) are caused by dermatophytes — a group of keratinophilic fungi (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton) that infect only keratinized tissue (skin, hair, nails — they cannot penetrate the living cells of the skin). They elaborate keratinases that digest keratin, using it as their nutrient source while producing inflammation through their metabolic products and cell wall components. The clinical presentation depends entirely on the anatomical site infected, and this is why we name them by location (tinea) with a Latin suffix.

**Tinea capitis** (scalp) occurs predominantly in children; in the post-puberty sebum-rich scalp environment, dermatophytes cannot survive. Microsporum canis (from infected cats/dogs) produces an endothrix infection with ectothrix spores — fluoresces green under Wood's lamp. Trichophyton violaceum and T. tonsurans produce endothrix infection (spores inside the hair shaft) — do not fluoresce. Kerion is an inflammatory, pustular mass on the scalp — a hypersensitivity reaction to the fungus — can cause scarring alopecia if untreated.

**Tinea unguium (onychomycosis)** is the most common nail disease in adults — affects toenails much more than fingernails (the warm, moist, occlusive shoe environment). Trichophyton rubrum is the dominant pathogen (>90% of cases). The fungus typically invades from the distal lateral edge → distal-lateral subungual onychomycosis (DLSO) — yellowish-white discoloration, subungual hyperkeratosis, onycholysis (nail separation from nail bed), crumbling, thickened nail. Diagnosis: fungal culture or nail clipping for PCR. Treatment: oral terbinafine (for toenails: 250 mg daily for 12 weeks — the most effective) or itraconazole (pulse therapy: 200 mg twice daily for 1 week per month, for 3 pulses for toenails).

## Dermatophyte (Tinea) Infections — Keratin as the Battlefield

### Why Dermatophytes Stay Superficial

Dermatophytes — the group of fungi comprising Trichophyton, Microsporum, and Epidermophyton — are **keratinophilic** (keratin-loving) and **keratinolytic** (keratin-digesting). They cannot survive in living, immunologically active tissue. They elaborate keratinases that break down the keratin of the stratum corneum, hair shaft, and nail plate, digesting it as their sole carbon and nitrogen source. This exquisite metabolic specialization means they live in the outermost dead layer of skin — the stratum corneum — without ever penetrating the living epidermis below. The infection is, by definition, superficial.

This creates a paradox worth understanding: if the fungus stays in the dead keratin layer and never enters living tissue, why does the skin become red, scaly, and inflamed? The answer is that the inflammation is not caused by direct fungal invasion of living cells — it is an **immune-mediated hypersensitivity reaction** to fungal metabolic products (keratinase enzymes, cell wall mannans, and other antigens) that diffuse downward from the stratum corneum into the living epidermis and dermis below. On first contact with a new host, there is often little inflammation (no prior sensitization). On re-exposure or chronic infection, the immune system — particularly Th1-mediated, cell-mediated immunity — mounts an increasingly vigorous response. This is why tinea infections vary so much in their inflammatory intensity: a highly cell-mediated immune response (a person mounting a vigorous Th1 response) causes intense inflammation (kerion, tinea incognito dramatically flares when steroids suppress the response), while an immunosuppressed patient may have widespread infection with minimal inflammation.

**Analogy:** Think of dermatophytes as squatters in the outer shell of a building (the stratum corneum). They never break through into the occupied floors (the living epidermis). But their noise and waste (fungal antigens) seep through the floor and irritate the occupants below (the immune cells in the living epidermis and dermis), who call the police (mount an inflammatory response). The eviction attempt — the immune inflammation — is what you see clinically as the red, itchy, scaling ring.

The **active border with central clearing** of most tinea infections is explained by this same immunology. At the advancing edge, the fungus is newest and the immune response is maximal — hence erythema, scaling, and vesiculation at the periphery. As the fungus advances, it leaves older, already-inflamed skin behind where the immune response has been active longest and the fungus has been partially controlled — the center heals and clears. The result: an annular (ring-shaped) lesion expanding centrifugally.

**Wood's lamp** (ultraviolet light at 365 nm) is a clinical tool that exploits the fact that certain dermatophytes and fungi cause fluorescence of infected hair or skin — not because the fungus itself fluoresces, but because some species produce **pteridine compounds and porphyrins** within their cell wall as metabolic byproducts, and these compounds absorb the UV light and re-emit it at visible wavelengths. **Microsporum canis** and **M. audouinii** (agents of tinea capitis in children) produce bright **green fluorescence** of infected hair — the porphyrins in the fungal cell wall fluoresce green. **Trichophyton** species — which cause the majority of adult tinea infections and most nail infections — do not produce these porphyrins and therefore do **not fluoresce**. Erythrasma (caused by *Corynebacterium minutissimum*, a bacterial not fungal infection that occupies a similar superficial niche) produces a characteristic **coral-pink** fluorescence under Wood's lamp — from coproporphyrin III.

> **Key pearl:** Wood's lamp: Microsporum tinea capitis = green fluorescence. Erythrasma = coral-pink. Pityriasis versicolor (*Malassezia furfur*) = yellow-green or pale fluorescence. Normal skin, Trichophyton infections, bacterial infections = no specific fluorescence. Practical point: Trichophyton tonsurans (now the dominant cause of tinea capitis in urban settings globally, replacing Microsporum) does not fluoresce — so a negative Wood's lamp does not exclude tinea capitis.

## Acne Vulgaris — The Pathogenesis Ladder in Detail

### From Follicle to Nodule: A Cascade

Acne is not a single event — it is a cascade, and each step in the cascade builds on and amplifies the previous one. Understanding the cascade means that every treatment can be logically positioned and every combination therapy justified.

**Step 1 — Follicular hyperkeratosis (microcomedone formation).** The pilosebaceous unit (the hair follicle + sebaceous gland) is the anatomical stage for acne. Under androgenic stimulation (which begins at puberty — adrenarche drives DHEAS production, which is converted peripherally to testosterone and DHT), the sebaceous gland enlarges and produces more sebum. Simultaneously, the follicular infundibulum (the upper part of the hair follicle, lined by keratinocytes) undergoes abnormal keratinization — instead of desquamating normally (corneocytes shedding loosely into the follicular lumen), the corneocytes become cohesive and sticky. They accumulate, compacting into a plug of keratin that obstructs the follicular opening. This is the microcomedone — invisible to the naked eye, the initiating lesion of all acne.

**Step 2 — Comedone formation.** As sebum continues to be produced by the sebaceous gland below the keratin plug, it accumulates in the follicular lumen (which cannot drain). The follicle dilates. The result is a comedone — either an open comedone (blackhead, where the follicular ostium is still open enough for the keratin/sebum plug to oxidize at the surface — the black colour is oxidized melanin and lipid, not dirt) or a closed comedone (whitehead — the ostium is completely closed).

**Step 3 — Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) proliferation.** The obstructed follicle, filled with sebum (an excellent bacterial growth medium) and depleted of oxygen (the sebum plug excludes air, creating an anaerobic microenvironment), is colonized by C. acnes — a gram-positive, anaerobic rod that is a normal skin commensal in low numbers. Under the oxygen-depleted, sebum-rich conditions of the comedone, C. acnes proliferates exponentially.

**Step 4 — Lipase activity and free fatty acid production.** C. acnes expresses lipases that hydrolyze the triglycerides of sebum into free fatty acids (FFAs). These FFAs are potent irritants and pro-inflammatory molecules. They also act as ligands for pattern-recognition receptors (Toll-like receptor 2, TLR-2) on keratinocytes and the surrounding immune cells.

**Step 5 — Innate immune activation → inflammation.** TLR-2 activation on keratinocytes triggers NF-κB → production of IL-1α, IL-8, and other cytokines → neutrophil recruitment into the follicular wall → neutrophils release proteases and reactive oxygen species → the follicular wall weakens and ruptures → the contents of the comedone (keratin, sebum, FFAs, bacteria) spill into the surrounding dermis → intense dermal inflammatory response → papule, pustule, nodule, or cyst. The nodulo-cystic lesion is the result of a ruptured comedone contents causing a foreign body granulomatous reaction in the dermis — not, as is commonly assumed, a simple "infected pimple." This is why it heals with potential scarring (dermal damage from the foreign body reaction and subsequent fibrosis).

**Analogy:** The acne cascade is like a factory fire caused by improper waste disposal. The factory (sebaceous gland) produces waste (sebum). The waste disposal system (normal follicular desquamation) breaks down (hyperkeratosis). Waste accumulates in the storage room (comedone). Bacteria (C. acnes) thrive in the waste. The bacteria produce toxic byproducts (FFAs). The toxic byproducts set off the sprinkler system (innate immune activation). The sprinklers, combined with the fire (bacterial products), destroy the storage room walls (follicular rupture) — and now the fire spreads to the whole building (surrounding dermis) — that is the nodule.

**Why isotretinoin works at every step** of this cascade: (1) It dramatically reduces sebaceous gland size and sebum output (by ~90%) — eliminating the substrate for C. acnes and the material for comedone formation. (2) It normalizes follicular keratinocyte differentiation — reducing cohesiveness of corneocytes, preventing the initial keratin plug. (3) The reduction in sebum starves C. acnes — it cannot proliferate without its nutrient substrate. (4) Isotretinoin has direct anti-inflammatory effects on keratinocytes and macrophages. The result is that isotretinoin is the only treatment that can achieve complete, prolonged remission of severe acne — because it addresses all four levels of the cascade simultaneously.

> **Key pearl:** Isotretinoin is a vitamin A analogue (retinoid) — teratogenic (causes retinoic acid embryopathy: craniofacial defects, cardiac malformations, CNS abnormalities) → mandatory pregnancy prevention programme (two forms of contraception, monthly pregnancy tests). Monitoring: LFTs, fasting lipids (isotretinoin raises triglycerides, rarely to dangerous levels), and FBC. Side effects: dryness of all mucous membranes (cheilitis, epistaxis, dry eyes), myalgia, photosensitivity, possible mood change (still debated but monitor). Dose: 0.5–1 mg/kg/day, cumulative dose target 120–150 mg/kg (determines relapse rate).

## STIs in Dermatology — Syphilis as the Master Mimic

### Treponema Pallidum and the Three Acts of Syphilis

Syphilis, caused by the spirochete *Treponema pallidum*, is one of medicine's great mimics — it has been called "the great imitator" for more than a century, because it can present with almost any skin finding and affect virtually every organ system. Understanding why it presents the way it does requires understanding the biology of the organism and its interaction with the human immune system over time.

*T. pallidum* has no lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in its outer membrane — unlike most bacteria, it does not trigger the immediate, powerful innate immune response that LPS normally provokes. Its outer membrane is sparse in surface proteins, making it difficult for antibodies to target. The organism therefore evades the immune system with remarkable effectiveness during its early dissemination — it spreads widely before an effective immune response is mounted. This immune evasion is the biological foundation of syphilis's multi-stage clinical course.

**Primary syphilis:** The organism enters through a mucosal surface or skin abrasion. At the site of inoculation, the spirochetes multiply locally and trigger a local immune response — producing the **chancre**: a clean-based, indurated (firm, cartilaginous edge — the induration is the fibrous tissue response to spirochetal infiltration), non-tender ulcer. The non-tenderness is crucial and biologically explained: the organism evades the innate immune recognition that normally triggers the acute inflammatory cascade (prostaglandins, cytokines) responsible for pain. There is no pus, no intense neutrophilic response, and minimal pain. This painlessness is why primary syphilis is so commonly missed — the patient does not notice or does not seek care for a "painless sore." The chancre heals spontaneously in 3–6 weeks, even without treatment — because the local immune response eventually clears the local infection, but the organism has already disseminated haematogenously.

**Secondary syphilis:** 6–12 weeks after the primary chancre (often before it has completely healed), the haematogenously disseminated spirochetes trigger a systemic immune response. This is the phase of immune complex deposition and T-cell-mediated reactions in multiple organs. The skin rash of secondary syphilis is the result of immune complex deposition in dermal capillaries and a lymphocytic infiltrate around spirochetes in the dermis. The rash is characteristically **maculopapular**, involving the trunk, and — most diagnostically important — **the palms and soles**. The palms and soles are sites that almost no other exanthem involves (the rare exceptions include Rocky Mountain spotted fever, hand-foot-mouth disease, and drug reactions). The reason for palmar and plantar involvement in syphilis: the secondary rash is immune-mediated, disseminated via the bloodstream, and spirochetes seed the entire skin including glabrous (non-hair-bearing) skin of the palms and soles, triggering the immune response there. The rash is not pruritic (important differential from drug reactions and viral exanthems). Other features of secondary syphilis: condylomata lata (flat, moist, highly infectious papules in moist genital and perianal skin), mucous patches (painless white erosions on oral mucosa), alopecia ("moth-eaten alopecia" — patchy hair loss from immune infiltrate around hair follicles in the scalp), generalized lymphadenopathy, fever, malaise.

**Clinical connection:** A patient with a non-itchy maculopapular rash involving the palms and soles, with or without lymphadenopathy, has secondary syphilis until proven otherwise. Screen with RPR or VDRL (non-treponemal tests — sensitive, good for monitoring treatment response, titers fall with successful treatment) confirmed by TPPA or FTA-ABS (treponemal tests — specific, remain positive for life). Treatment: benzathine penicillin G IM — single dose for primary/secondary, three doses for latent/tertiary.

**Tertiary syphilis:** After years to decades of untreated or inadequately treated infection, the immune response in some patients mounts a destructive, delayed-hypersensitivity-like reaction against residual spirochetes or spirochetal antigens in various tissues. The **gumma** is the characteristic lesion of tertiary syphilis — a granulomatous reaction (organized chronic inflammatory infiltrate with central necrosis, surrounded by epithelioid macrophages, plasma cells, and giant cells) that can form in any tissue: skin (produces a painless, necrotic ulcer with a "wash-leather" slough), bone (osteitis), liver (hepar lobatum — gummas throughout the liver), testis, palate (can destroy the palate, producing a characteristic "saddle-nose" deformity when the nasal cartilage is involved). Cardiovascular syphilis: aortitis → aortic root dilatation → aortic regurgitation, coronary ostial stenosis. Neurosyphilis: meningitis, general paralysis of the insane (GPI — frontal lobe atrophy, dementia), tabes dorsalis (degeneration of the dorsal columns → loss of proprioception, sensory ataxia, lightning pains, Argyll Robertson pupils — pupils that accommodate but do not react to light).

> **Key exam point:** Primary = painless chancre, non-tender inguinal lymphadenopathy. Secondary = maculopapular rash including palms and soles (non-itchy), condylomata lata, mucous patches — highly infectious. Tertiary = gummas, cardiovascular syphilis, neurosyphilis. Congenital syphilis: Hutchinson's triad (Hutchinson's teeth — notched, barrel-shaped incisors, interstitial keratitis, sensorineural deafness). Saddle-nose, saber tibia, rhagades (perioral fissures). Test: RPR/VDRL (non-specific, quantitative — good for monitoring) + TPPA/FTA-ABS (specific, qualitative — remains positive for life). Treat: benzathine penicillin G.

> **Final clinical pearl:** The morphology of a skin lesion tells you the depth and nature of the pathological process. A macule or patch = epidermal pigment change or superficial vasodilation. A papule = solid elevation < 1 cm (dermal or epidermal). A vesicle = fluid-filled < 1 cm (intraepidermal). A bulla = fluid-filled > 1 cm. A pustule = pus-filled. A plaque = flat-topped elevation > 1 cm. A nodule = solid, > 1 cm, extends into dermis. Learn morphological terms before attempting to diagnose skin diseases — the morphological description IS the diagnosis until histology is available.
