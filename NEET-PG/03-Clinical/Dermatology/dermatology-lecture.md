## Skin Structure and Function

The skin is the largest organ of the body and serves as the primary barrier between the
internal milieu and the external environment. To understand dermatological disease you
must understand normal skin architecture from inside out — where in the skin a process
occurs determines almost everything about its clinical presentation.

The epidermis is a stratified squamous epithelium derived from ectoderm. Crucially, it
is completely avascular; it depends on the underlying dermis for nutrients and oxygen
delivered by diffusion across the basement membrane. Under normal conditions the transit
time from basal layer to surface is approximately 28 days — a number that becomes
diagnostically important when psoriasis is discussed.

The stratum basale (stratum germinativum) is a single layer of columnar-to-cuboidal
cells anchored to the basement membrane via hemidesmosomes. These cells are mitotically
active; here the epidermis renews itself. Interspersed among the keratinocytes at a
ratio of approximately 1:10 are melanocytes, which originate from neural crest cells.
Melanocytes extend dendritic processes and transfer melanin granules (melanosomes) into
surrounding keratinocytes, protecting the nucleus from UV-induced DNA damage. Also
present are Merkel cells — mechanoreceptors for fine touch.

The stratum spinosum (prickle cell layer) is several cells thick. The "spines" visible
on histology are desmosomal connections between cells made visible by shrinkage artifact.
Desmosomes are built around desmosomal cadherins (desmoglein and desmocollin), and
disruption of these molecules is the central event in pemphigus. Within this layer
reside Langerhans cells — bone-marrow–derived dendritic cells that are the skin's
professional antigen-presenting cells. When activated they migrate to regional lymph
nodes to present antigen to T cells, forming the cellular basis of allergic contact
dermatitis.

The stratum granulosum contains keratohyalin granules (profilaggrin, the precursor of
filaggrin) and lamellar bodies (Odland bodies). Lamellar bodies discharge lipid bilayers
into the intercellular space, forming the water-impermeable barrier. Loss-of-function
mutations in filaggrin — the chief genetic predisposition to atopic dermatitis — disrupt
both the mechanical scaffold and the lipid barrier arrangement.

The stratum lucidum is a thin pale eosinophilic layer present only in thick skin (palms,
soles). The stratum corneum is the outermost layer: dead anucleate corneocytes arranged
in a basket-weave pattern and embedded in a lamellar lipid matrix — the "bricks and
mortar" model. This layer provides the definitive barrier against transepidermal water
loss (TEWL) and percutaneous entry of allergens and microbes.

Below the epidermis lies the basement membrane zone (BMZ), comprising the lamina lucida,
lamina densa, and sub-lamina densa fibrillar zone. Key molecules include laminin-332,
type IV collagen, and type VII collagen anchoring fibrils. Hemidesmosomes on the basal
keratinocytes contain BP180 (type XVII collagen) and BP230 — the antigens targeted in
bullous pemphigoid, explaining why BP produces a subepidermal split.

The dermis (mesoderm-derived) has two zones. The papillary dermis is loose connective
tissue with capillary loops, lymphatics, and free nerve endings. The reticular dermis is
dense irregular connective tissue of type I collagen and elastic fibers providing tensile
strength and elasticity. The ground substance — proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans —
binds water and provides turgor. The dermis houses pilosebaceous units (hair follicle +
sebaceous gland), eccrine glands (thermoregulation, open directly to skin surface),
apocrine glands (axilla, groin, open into follicle), and Merkel disc receptors and
encapsulated corpuscles. Below lies the hypodermis (subcutaneous fat), providing
insulation, mechanical padding, and energy storage; infections reaching this layer cause
panniculitis, and fascial spread produces necrotizing fasciitis.

Skin functions extend beyond barrier protection: immune surveillance via Langerhans and
dermal dendritic cells; vitamin D3 synthesis from 7-dehydrocholesterol under UVB;
thermoregulation via eccrine sweating and cutaneous vasomotion; sensory reception via
Meissner corpuscles (light touch, papillary dermis), Pacinian corpuscles (vibration,
deep dermis/hypodermis), Ruffini endings, and free nerve endings.

## Inflammatory Dermatoses

Inflammation is the common thread running through psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, and
contact dermatitis. Their immunological mechanisms, however, differ fundamentally:
psoriasis is a Th1/Th17 disorder; atopic dermatitis is a Th2 disorder; allergic contact
dermatitis is a Th1-driven delayed hypersensitivity. Understanding these differences
explains why the treatments work.

**Psoriasis** is a T-lymphocyte–driven disorder in which visible keratinocyte
hyperproliferation is the downstream consequence of immune activation, not the primary
defect. The sequence begins with plasmacytoid dendritic cells releasing IFN-alpha in
response to a trigger — physical trauma, streptococcal pharyngitis, stress, or drugs
(lithium, beta-blockers, NSAIDs, chloroquine). This activates myeloid dendritic cells to
produce IL-12 and IL-23. IL-12 drives Th1 differentiation (IFN-gamma, TNF-alpha); IL-23
drives Th17 differentiation (IL-17, IL-22). IL-17 and IL-22 acting on keratinocytes
cause accelerated proliferation — epidermal transit time drops from 28 days to 3–4 days.
Neutrophils are recruited and migrate into the epidermis.

Histologically: regular acanthosis (elongated rete ridges), thinned suprapapillary plate,
dilated tortuous papillary dermal capillaries, absent granular layer (too little time for
keratohyalin formation), parakeratosis (nuclei retained in stratum corneum), and Munro's
microabscesses (neutrophil collections in the parakeratotic corneum). These findings
directly reflect the immunological chaos.

Clinical features: sharply demarcated salmon-red plaques with thick silvery-white
micaceous scale on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees, scalp, lumbosacral area). Auspitz
sign — punctate bleeding when scale is forcibly removed — exposes the thinned
suprapapillary epidermis over dilated capillaries. Koebner (isomorphic) phenomenon:
new lesions at trauma sites. Nail involvement (pitting, onycholysis, oil-drop sign) and
psoriatic arthritis are extra-cutaneous manifestations.

Treatment ladder: mild — topical corticosteroids and calcipotriol (vitamin D analogue).
Moderate — narrow-band UVB or PUVA phototherapy. Severe — methotrexate (inhibits
rapidly proliferating cells; monitor for hepatotoxicity and myelosuppression), cyclosporine
(calcineurin inhibitor), or acitretin (systemic retinoid for pustular/erythrodermic forms).
Biologics: anti-TNF (adalimumab, etanercept), anti-IL-17 (secukinumab, ixekizumab —
extremely effective), anti-IL-23 (guselkumab, risankizumab).

**Atopic dermatitis** is a disease of two co-existing defects: impaired epidermal barrier
and Th2 immune skewing, which amplify each other in a vicious cycle. Filaggrin (FLG)
loss-of-function mutations are the strongest genetic risk factor. Without functional
filaggrin, TEWL is elevated, skin pH rises, and the barrier is permeable to allergens,
irritants, and Staphylococcus aureus — which colonizes atopic skin in up to 90% of
patients and worsens inflammation via superantigens. Th2 cytokines (IL-4, IL-13) drive
IgE class switching and mast cell differentiation; importantly, IL-4 and IL-13 directly
suppress filaggrin expression, creating a feedback loop where inflammation worsens the
barrier defect. IL-31, produced by Th2 cells, is the primary mediator of itch, acting on
IL-31 receptors on sensory neurons.

Histology: spongiosis (intercellular edema) in acute phase, progressing to vesiculation;
lichenification and fibrosis in chronic phase from repeated rubbing. Distribution is
age-dependent: cheeks and extensors in infants; flexural fossae (antecubital, popliteal,
neck) in older children and adults. Management: bland emollients (barrier restoration,
applied liberally to damp skin); topical corticosteroids for flares (potency calibrated
to site); calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) for steroid-sparing and
sensitive sites; dupilumab (anti-IL-4Ra, blocking IL-4 and IL-13) for moderate-severe
disease — has transformed management.

**Contact dermatitis** has two mechanistically distinct forms. Irritant contact dermatitis
(ICD) requires no sensitization — it is direct chemical injury to keratinocytes, causing
innate immune activation via released IL-1, IL-6, and TNF-alpha. Strong alkalis (soaps,
detergents) disrupt the lipid barrier; acids denature proteins. Anyone exposed to
sufficient concentration for sufficient duration develops ICD.

Allergic contact dermatitis (ACD) is type IV (delayed-type, Th1-mediated) hypersensitivity.
During sensitization, a hapten penetrates skin, binds carrier proteins, and is processed
by Langerhans cells that migrate to lymph nodes and generate antigen-specific memory T
cells. On re-exposure 24–72 hours later, memory T cells release IFN-gamma, recruiting
macrophages and producing a spongiotic dermatitis. Common allergens: nickel (most common
in women), chromate (cement), paraphenylenediamine (hair dyes), fragrance mix, rubber
accelerators. Diagnosis is by patch testing (48 h occlusive application, read at 48 and
96 h) — not prick testing (which detects IgE/type I reactions).

## Blistering Disorders

The level of the skin split determines blister morphology. Deeper split = more epidermis
forming the roof = more intact (tense) blister. Shallower split = thinner roof = more
fragile (flaccid) blister. This single principle unlocks the entire blistering disorders
chapter.

**Pemphigus vulgaris (PV)** is caused by IgG autoantibodies against desmoglein-3 (DSG3),
the desmosomal cadherin mediating keratinocyte adhesion in the stratum spinosum. IgG
binding blocks DSG3 and triggers intracellular signaling causing desmosomes to
disassemble — acantholysis (loss of epidermal cell-cell cohesion). The split is
suprabasal: basal cells separate from spinosum cells above but remain anchored to the
BMZ. Histologically: "row of tombstones" — intact basal cells standing alone on the BMZ,
blister cavity above, acantholytic cells (Tzanck cells) floating in the blister fluid.

The blister roof is thin (upper epidermis only), so bullae are flaccid and rupture
easily, leaving extensive, painful erosions. Denuding of large areas carries the fluid
and electrolyte consequences of a severe burn — the mechanism of pre-steroid mortality.
Oral mucosa is almost invariably involved (DSG3 is predominantly mucosal), often
preceding skin lesions by months.

Nikolsky sign is positive: tangential pressure on normal-appearing adjacent skin causes
it to slip, because circulating antibodies compromise DSG3 globally. DIF of perilesional
skin: IgG and C3 in an intercellular (chicken-wire / fishnet) pattern throughout the
epidermis. IIF uses monkey esophagus substrate to detect circulating anti-desmoglein
antibodies; titers correlate with disease activity. Treatment: high-dose systemic
corticosteroids (prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day); rituximab (anti-CD20, depleting B cells) is
now first-line for severe or refractory PV; steroid-sparing agents: azathioprine or
mycophenolate mofetil.

**Bullous pemphigoid (BP)** is the most common autoimmune blistering disorder, peaking
after age 70. IgG (and IgE — contributing to the prominent eosinophilia) autoantibodies
target BP180 (type XVII collagen, COL17A1) and BP230, both components of hemidesmosomes.
Hemidesmosomal disruption causes a subepidermal split — below the basal cell layer. The
entire epidermis forms the blister roof, producing tense, large, intact bullae that do
not rupture easily under lateral pressure. Nikolsky sign is negative.

Histology: subepidermal blister with eosinophilic infiltrate. DIF: linear IgG and C3
along the BMZ — a linear pattern contrasting with PV's intercellular pattern. On salt-
split skin IIF, BP antibodies bind to the epidermal roof (BP180/BP230 are on the
keratinocyte side of the split). Treatment: clobetasol propionate (superpotent topical
steroid) for localized disease; systemic prednisolone for extensive disease; doxycycline
+ nicotinamide as steroid-sparing combination; rituximab for refractory cases.

Key comparison: PV — suprabasal — acantholysis — flaccid — Nikolsky positive — oral
mucosa always involved — IgG anti-DSG3 — intercellular DIF. BP — subepidermal — no
acantholysis — tense — Nikolsky negative — mucosa usually spared — IgG/IgE anti-
BP180/BP230 — linear BMZ DIF.

## Infections

**Bacterial infections.** Impetigo is the most superficial bacterial skin infection
(epidermis only), caused by S. aureus and/or S. pyogenes. Non-bullous impetigo evolves
through vesicles and pustules to the characteristic honey-colored (golden) crusts of
dried serum and neutrophilic debris. Bullous impetigo is caused exclusively by S. aureus
phage group II producing exfoliatin toxin — a serine protease cleaving desmoglein-1
specifically in the superficial epidermis (same target as pemphigus foliaceus), producing
flaccid, fragile bullae. Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) is the systemic
toxin-mediated form, affecting neonates and immunocompromised children. Treatment: topical
mupirocin for localized disease; oral flucloxacillin or cefalexin for widespread disease.

Erysipelas involves the upper dermis and superficial lymphatics; caused almost exclusively
by Group A Streptococcus. The lesion is bright red, fiery, warm, tender, with a sharply
demarcated, raised, palpable border — you can feel the edge of the plaque. The sharp
border reflects lymphatic confinement. Systemic features (fever, rigors) precede or
accompany skin signs. Cellulitis involves the deep dermis and subcutaneous tissue (S.
aureus and S. pyogenes), presenting similarly but with an indistinct, diffuse border
because deeper inflammation is not lymphatically contained. Both are treated with
penicillin-based antibiotics; flucloxacillin or amoxicillin-clavulanate covers
staphylococci in cellulitis.

**Fungal infections (tinea/dermatophytoses)** are caused by Trichophyton, Microsporum,
and Epidermophyton — keratinophilic fungi invading only keratinized tissues. Named by
site: tinea capitis (scalp), corporis (body), cruris (groin), pedis (feet), unguium
(nails = onychomycosis). Pathophysiology: the fungus colonizes stratum corneum, secretes
keratinases, and advances centrifugally while the immune response clears the center —
producing the annular morphology with an active, scaly, raised peripheral border.
KOH preparation shows branching septate hyphae and arthrospores. Wood's lamp (365 nm
UV-A): Microsporum species fluoresce green (ectothrix); most Trichophyton species do not
fluoresce. Treatment: topical azoles or terbinafine for superficial tinea; oral
terbinafine (drug of choice for onychomycosis, fungicidal against dermatophytes) or
itraconazole for nail and scalp infections.

**Viral infections.** HSV (HSV-1 orolabial, HSV-2 genital) establishes latency in dorsal
root ganglia and reactivates under triggers (UV, fever, stress, immunosuppression).
Reactivation produces grouped vesicles on an erythematous base — the grouped arrangement
is the morphological signature. Tzanck smear shows multinucleated giant cells but cannot
distinguish HSV from VZV. Treatment: acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir.

Molluscum contagiosum (poxvirus) produces dome-shaped, umbilicated, flesh-colored papules
with a central dimple. Umbilication is caused by the large intracytoplasmic inclusion
body (Henderson-Patterson / molluscum body) that fills and eventually protrudes through
the cell. Self-limiting in immunocompetent hosts; numerous, large, and widespread in
HIV/AIDS — molluscum burden mirrors degree of immunosuppression.

**Leprosy** (Mycobacterium leprae, Hansen's disease) cannot be cultured in vitro. It
preferentially infects cooler tissues (skin, peripheral nerves, anterior chamber, upper
respiratory mucosa, testes) at 27–30°C. The entire clinical spectrum is determined by
the host's cell-mediated immunity (CMI).

Tuberculoid leprosy (TT, high CMI): vigorous granulomatous response nearly eliminates
bacilli but damages nerves within the granuloma. Lesions are few (1–5), well-defined,
hypopigmented, with a raised indurated border and anesthetic, anhidrotic center. Lepromin
(Mitsuda) test strongly positive. Paucibacillary on slit-skin smear.

Lepromatous leprosy (LL, absent CMI): unchecked M. leprae multiplication in macrophages
throughout skin and nerves. Lesions are numerous, symmetric, diffuse. Leonine facies
(diffuse facial skin thickening), madarosis (loss of lateral eyebrows/eyelashes),
saddle-nose deformity (anterior nasal spine destruction by mucosal lesions),
gynecomastia and testicular atrophy from nerve infiltration. Lepromin test negative.
Multibacillary — globi (clumps of bacilli) visible in macrophages on slit-skin smear.

Lepra reactions: Type 1 (reversal reaction, borderline cases): CMI suddenly improves,
causing inflammation in existing lesions and acute nerve function deterioration. Treatment:
systemic corticosteroids. Type 2 (ENL, BL/LL): immune complex (type III) deposition in
skin and vessels — tender erythematous nodules, fever, systemic illness. Treatment:
thalidomide (contraindicated in pregnancy) or prednisolone; clofazimine has
anti-inflammatory properties useful here.

WHO MDT: Paucibacillary (TT, BT) — rifampicin 600 mg monthly + dapsone 100 mg daily
for 6 months. Multibacillary (BB, BL, LL) — rifampicin 600 mg monthly + dapsone 100 mg
daily + clofazimine 300 mg monthly (supervised) and 50 mg daily for 12 months.
Rifampicin is the only bactericidal drug; dapsone is the bacteriostatic backbone;
clofazimine is bacteriostatic, anti-inflammatory, and causes reddish-brown skin
discoloration.

## Pigmentation Disorders

Skin color is determined by the amount and type of melanin, not by the number of
melanocytes (which is remarkably constant across races). Pigmentary disorders result
from altered melanocyte function or survival, or from changes in melanin transfer and
distribution.

**Vitiligo** results from selective autoimmune destruction of melanocytes by autoreactive
CD8+ cytotoxic T cells targeting melanocyte antigens (MART-1, tyrosinase, gp100). The
strong association with other autoimmune diseases (Hashimoto's thyroiditis, Graves',
type 1 DM, Addison's disease, pernicious anaemia) reflects a shared autoimmune diathesis.
Lesions are well-defined depigmented macules with convex borders and chalky-white
fluorescence under Wood's lamp (bright enhancement because complete absence of melanin
removes all light absorption). Leukotrichia (white hair in patches) indicates follicular
melanocyte loss — follicular melanocytes are the reservoir for repigmentation, so
leukotrichia predicts a poorer response to phototherapy. Treatment: narrow-band UVB
(most effective for widespread vitiligo); topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors
for limited disease; ruxolitinib cream (JAK inhibitor) — the first topical approved for
vitiligo; surgical transplantation for stable vitiligo.

**Melasma** is acquired symmetric hyperpigmentation of sun-exposed areas (cheeks,
forehead, upper lip, chin) driven by UV radiation, estrogen, and genetic predisposition.
Predominantly affects women: pregnancy (chloasma gravidarum), oral contraceptive pills.
UV stimulates melanocyte activity directly (via p53-POMC-MSH axis) and indirectly
(keratinocyte-derived SCF, ET-1, bFGF). Estrogen upregulates melanocortin receptors,
amplifying the UV signal. Wood's lamp accentuates epidermal melasma (superficial melanin)
but not dermal melasma (deep melanophage deposits) — dermal melasma responds poorly to
topical treatments. Treatment: strict broad-spectrum sunscreen (without this, no treatment
works); Kligman's formula (hydroquinone 4% + tretinoin 0.05% + fluocinolone acetonide
0.01%) — hydroquinone inhibits tyrosinase (rate-limiting enzyme of melanin synthesis),
tretinoin accelerates epidermal turnover and increases hydroquinone penetration, the
steroid reduces irritation. Alternatives: azelaic acid, kojic acid, tranexamic acid.

**Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)** follows cutaneous inflammation via two
mechanisms: (1) increased epidermal melanin production by activated melanocytes in
response to prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and cytokines; (2) inflammation disrupting
the basal layer causes melanin to "drop" into the dermis where macrophages phagocytose
it as melanophages — this dermal melanin is resistant to topical bleaching and may
persist for years. PIH disproportionately affects Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin types because
higher baseline melanin synthesis amplifies the response. Prevention (treating underlying
inflammation) is more effective than treating established PIH.

## Skin Tumors

**Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)** is the most common cancer in humans, arising from basal
keratinocytes or follicular outer root sheath. UV radiation (chronic cumulative exposure)
drives constitutive activation of the Hedgehog (Hh) signaling pathway via UV-induced
PTCH1 mutations (PTCH1 normally inhibits SMO; loss of PTCH1 releases SMO, activating
GLI1/2 transcription factors). Gorlin-Goltz syndrome: germline PTCH1 mutation causing
multiple early-onset BCCs, jaw keratocysts, bifid ribs, calcified falx cerebri.

Classic nodular BCC: pearly, flesh-colored papule/nodule with rolled telangiectatic
border; central ulceration ("rodent ulcer") as it enlarges. Histology: basaloid cell
nests with peripheral palisading and a retraction cleft between tumor islands and
fibromyxoid stroma. BCC almost never metastasizes but invades bone, cartilage, and
periorbital structures if neglected. Treatment: surgical excision; Mohs micrographic
surgery for high-risk sites and recurrence; vismodegib or sonidegib (SMO inhibitors)
for locally advanced/metastatic BCC.

**Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)** arises from keratinocytes and has genuine metastatic
potential, especially SCCs on non-sun-exposed sites, lip, ear, or in immunosuppressed
patients. UV causes signature C→T and CC→TT mutations at dipyrimidine sites, affecting
TP53 and CDKN2A. Actinic keratosis (AK) is the obligate precursor — a rough, gritty,
erythematous, scaly patch on sun-damaged skin representing keratinocyte dysplasia in
the lower epidermis. AK "feels like sandpaper" — tactile diagnosis is sometimes more
sensitive than visual. Full-thickness dysplasia = Bowen's disease (SCC in situ). Other
causes: HPV-16/18, Marjolin's ulcer (SCC in chronic burn scars — particularly aggressive),
arsenic ingestion, ionizing radiation. Treatment: surgical excision; Mohs for high-risk
sites; topical 5-fluorouracil or imiquimod for AK/Bowen's; cemiplimab (PD-1 inhibitor)
for metastatic SCC.

**Melanoma** arises from melanocytes and is responsible for most skin cancer deaths.
UV radiation is the primary carcinogen (UVB causing CPD lesions and 6-4 photoproducts;
UVA causing oxidative damage). Clinical screening: ABCDE — Asymmetry, Border irregularity
(notched, scalloped, indistinct), Color variegation (brown, black, red, white, blue),
Diameter greater than 6 mm, Evolution (any change in size, shape, color, or new symptoms).

Most important prognostic factor: Breslow thickness (depth of invasion in mm from
granular layer to deepest tumor cell). Ulceration and mitotic rate are additional
independent prognostic factors in AJCC staging. BRAF V600E mutation (~50% of cutaneous
melanomas, especially superficial spreading melanoma): constitutively activates
MAPK/ERK signaling. Treatment: BRAF + MEK inhibitors (dabrafenib + trametinib,
vemurafenib + cobimetinib) for BRAF-mutated disease; PD-1 checkpoint inhibitors
(pembrolizumab, nivolumab) have transformed prognosis in advanced melanoma.

## Papulosquamous Disorders

**Lichen planus (LP)** is a CD8+ T-cell–mediated lichenoid interface dermatitis. Cytotoxic
T cells attack basal keratinocytes expressing altered self-antigens (triggers: drugs,
viruses, idiopathic). Basal keratinocyte apoptosis produces Civatte bodies (colloid /
hyaline / cytoid bodies) at the dermal-epidermal junction — PAS-positive dead
keratinocytes. The 4 Ps: Purple, Polygonal, Pruritic, Papules. Wickham's striae — fine
white lacy lines on the surface of papules, pathognomonic for LP — result from focal
hypergranulosis (the granular layer is increased, the opposite of psoriasis). Oral LP:
white reticulate pattern on buccal mucosa; erosive oral LP carries a low (<1%) risk of
SCC transformation.

Histology: saw-toothed rete ridges, band-like lymphocytic infiltrate hugging the basal
layer, hypergranulosis, and Civatte bodies. Compare with psoriasis: regular (not saw-
toothed) rete ridges, absent granular layer, Munro's microabscesses, dilated papillary
capillaries. These histological contrasts are high-yield for MCQs. Koebner phenomenon
occurs. Treatment: potent topical corticosteroids; systemic corticosteroids for
widespread disease; retinoids for hypertrophic LP; phototherapy.

**Pityriasis rosea** is a self-limiting exanthem, most common at ages 10–35, with
probable reactivation of HHV-6 or HHV-7 as the trigger. Begins with a herald patch
(mother patch) — a single oval, salmon-pink, well-defined plaque with a collarette of
scale (free edge pointing inward), typically 2–4 cm, on the trunk. After 1–2 weeks, a
generalized secondary eruption of smaller, similarly morphological lesions appears on
the trunk and proximal limbs, oriented along skin cleavage lines (Langer's lines) — the
"Christmas tree" pattern on the back. Face, distal limbs, and palms/soles are typically
spared.

Secondary syphilis mimics pityriasis rosea almost exactly — the single most important
distinguishing feature is palms and soles involvement in secondary syphilis (PR spares
them). Any sexually active adult with PR-like morphology must have RPR/VDRL performed.
PR is self-limiting, resolving in 6–12 weeks; treatment is symptomatic.

**Secondary syphilis** results from hematogenous dissemination of Treponema pallidum
2–8 weeks after primary infection. The maculopapular rash is generalized and
copper-red in color; the classical teaching point is involvement of the palms and soles.
Condylomata lata (moist, broad-based, flat papules in anogenital areas) are teeming with
treponemes and highly infectious. Moth-eaten alopecia, mucous patches (grey-white oral
erosions), and systemic features (fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy) are additional
findings. Diagnosis: RPR/VDRL reactive (titers parallel disease activity); confirmed by
FTA-ABS or TPHA. Treatment: benzathine penicillin G 2.4 million units IM single dose.
The Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction (fever and rash flare within 2–8 hours of first dose) is
a cytokine-release phenomenon from rapid treponemal killing — self-limiting, not an
allergic reaction.

## Acne and Rosacea

**Acne vulgaris** is modeled on four converging pathogenic factors within the pilosebaceous
follicle. First: androgen-driven sebum overproduction. DHT (from testosterone via 5-alpha-
reductase in sebaceous glands) is the principal stimulant, explaining why acne surges at
puberty and worsens in hyperandrogenic states (PCOS, CAH). Second: abnormal follicular
keratinization. Infundibular keratinocytes become cohesive and fail to desquamate, forming
a microcomedone that expands into an open comedone (blackhead — oxidized melanin colors
the plug) or closed comedone (whitehead). Third: Cutibacterium acnes (gram-positive
anaerobe) metabolizes sebum triglycerides to free fatty acids, which are comedogenic and
irritating, and activates TLR-2 on keratinocytes and macrophages, driving IL-1beta, IL-8,
and TNF-alpha — innate immune inflammation. Fourth: follicular rupture releases keratin,
sebum, FFAs, and bacteria into the dermis, triggering a foreign-body inflammatory response
producing papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts.

Treatment by severity: comedonal acne — topical retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene; normalize
follicular keratinocyte differentiation, the only treatment addressing comedonal obstruction)
or benzoyl peroxide (bactericidal, no documented resistance). Mild-to-moderate papulopustular
— add topical clindamycin or erythromycin, always combined with benzoyl peroxide to prevent
resistance, alongside a retinoid. Moderate-to-severe — systemic tetracyclines (doxycycline
100 mg daily for maximum 3 months); hormonal therapy in women (combined OCP, spironolactone).
Severe nodulo-cystic or scarring acne — oral isotretinoin (13-cis retinoic acid), the only
treatment addressing all four pathogenic factors simultaneously (reduces sebaceous gland
size and sebum output; normalizes follicular keratinization; reduces C. acnes colonization
indirectly; anti-inflammatory). Isotretinoin is teratogenic (category X) — two forms of
contraception and a negative pregnancy test mandatory; monitor lipids and liver function;
causes mucocutaneous dryness (cheilitis, xerosis, epistaxis) universally.

**Rosacea** affects the central face and is pathophysiologically distinct from acne.
Two fundamental defects: (1) neurovascular dysregulation — flushing reflects exaggerated
vasodilatory responses; chronically dilated vessels produce persistent erythema and
telangiectasia; (2) innate immune hyperactivation — kallikrein-5 (elevated in rosacea
skin) abnormally processes cathelicidin (LL-37) into pro-inflammatory peptide fragments
that activate TLR-2, trigger mast cell degranulation, stimulate angiogenesis, and attract
neutrophils. Demodex folliculorum mites are found in higher density in rosacea skin and
may trigger these innate immune pathways.

Four subtypes: erythematotelangiectatic (flushing, persistent erythema, telangiectasia);
papulopustular (papules and pustules without comedones — absence of comedones is the
key clinical distinction from acne); phymatous (tissue hypertrophy, rhinophyma);
ocular (blepharitis, conjunctivitis, corneal involvement). Triggers: UV, heat, alcohol,
spicy food, stress. Treatment: topical metronidazole or azelaic acid for papulopustular
lesions; ivermectin 1% cream (targets Demodex) for PPR; oral doxycycline 40 mg (sub-
antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory dose) for moderate-to-severe PPR; laser/IPL for
telangiectasia and erythema; isotretinoin for phymatous subtype.
