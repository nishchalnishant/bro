## FRACTURE PRINCIPLES (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 10-year-old boy falls off a bicycle and sustains an injury to the distal radius. X-ray shows a fracture line extending through the physis (growth plate) only, without involving the metaphysis or epiphysis. This is classified as:**
- A) Salter-Harris Type I
- B) Salter-Harris Type II
- C) Salter-Harris Type III
- D) Salter-Harris Type IV

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Salter-Harris Type I fractures pass through the physis (growth plate) only, separating the epiphysis from the metaphysis. The mnemonic SALTR: Type I = Straight across (physis only); Type II = Above (physis + metaphysis — most common, 75%); Type III = Lower (physis + epiphysis); Type IV = Through (all three — physis, metaphysis, epiphysis); Type V = Rammed/Crush (compression of physis — worst prognosis for growth disturbance). Type I has an excellent prognosis.

</details>

---

**Q2. Which Salter-Harris type carries the worst prognosis for subsequent growth disturbance?**
- A) Type I
- B) Type II
- C) Type IV
- D) Type V

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

Salter-Harris Type V is a crush/compression injury of the physis itself, with no visible fracture line on initial X-ray (often diagnosed retrospectively when growth arrest occurs). It carries the worst prognosis because the germinal layer of the physis is directly damaged, leading to premature physeal closure and limb length discrepancy or angular deformity. Type I and II have the best prognosis; Types III and IV involve the epiphysis/joint surface and risk growth disturbance plus joint incongruity.

</details>

**Q3. Correct order of fracture healing phases is:**
- A) Inflammatory → Soft callus → Hard callus → Remodeling
- B) Soft callus → Inflammatory → Hard callus → Remodeling
- C) Hard callus → Soft callus → Inflammatory → Remodeling
- D) Inflammatory → Hard callus → Soft callus → Remodeling

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Fracture healing occurs in 4 phases: (1) Inflammatory (days 1–5): hematoma, cytokine release, osteoclast recruitment; (2) Soft callus (weeks 1–3): fibrocartilage bridges gap, periosteal response, visible on X-ray at 2–3 weeks; (3) Hard callus (weeks 3–12): endochondral ossification converts cartilage to woven bone; (4) Remodeling (months to years): Wolff's law shapes bone along stress lines, lamellar bone replaces woven bone. The callus first appears on X-ray at 2–3 weeks.

</details>

**Q4. A 25-year-old sustains a closed tibial fracture following a road traffic accident. Six hours later, he develops severe pain on passive stretch of the toes, tense swelling of the leg, and paraesthesia in the first web space. The most appropriate immediate management is:**
- A) Elevation and ice application
- B) Urgent fasciotomy
- C) Intravenous mannitol
- D) Reassess after 4 hours

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency. The "6 Ps" are Pain (out of proportion, on passive stretch — earliest and most reliable sign), Pressure (tense compartment), Paraesthesia, Paralysis, Pallor, Pulselessness (late sign). Compartment pressure >30 mmHg (or within 30 mmHg of diastolic BP) requires urgent fasciotomy. Delay leads to irreversible Volkmann's ischemic contracture. Elevation is contraindicated (reduces perfusion pressure). Any circumferential dressing/cast must be split immediately.

</details>

**Q5. Avascular necrosis (AVN) is a recognized complication of fractures at which of the following sites?**
- A) Midshaft radius fractures
- B) Femoral neck and scaphoid fractures
- C) Colles' fracture and ankle fractures
- D) Tibial plateau fractures

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

AVN occurs when the blood supply to bone is disrupted. High-risk sites for post-fracture AVN: (1) Femoral head — blood supply via medial and lateral femoral circumflex arteries is disrupted in femoral neck fractures; (2) Scaphoid waist — blood supply enters distally, so proximal pole undergoes AVN after waist fractures; (3) Talus (neck fractures); (4) Humeral head (surgical neck fractures). Colles', tibial plateau, and midshaft radius fractures are not classically associated with AVN.

</details>

## UPPER LIMB FRACTURES (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 55-year-old woman slips and falls on an outstretched hand (FOOSH). X-ray shows a distal radius fracture with dorsal displacement and dorsal tilt of the distal fragment ("dinner fork deformity"). The nerve most at risk of acute injury in this fracture is:**
- A) Radial nerve
- B) Anterior interosseous nerve
- C) Median nerve (carpal tunnel)
- D) Ulnar nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Colles' fracture (most common wrist fracture in adults >40 years, FOOSH mechanism) features: fracture within 2.5 cm of the wrist with dorsal displacement, dorsal tilt, radial shortening, and radial deviation — "dinner fork deformity." The median nerve is at risk acutely due to carpal tunnel compression; chronic median nerve symptoms can develop from post-reduction swelling. Smith's fracture (reverse Colles', volar displacement, "garden spade deformity") occurs from a fall on the back of the hand. Radial nerve injury occurs in humeral shaft fractures.

</details>

**Q7. A 28-year-old cricketer presents with pain in the anatomical snuffbox after a fall on an outstretched hand. Initial X-rays appear normal. The most appropriate next step is:**
- A) Reassure and discharge — normal X-ray excludes fracture
- B) Apply thumb spica cast and reassess at 2 weeks (repeat X-ray or MRI)
- C) Surgical exploration immediately
- D) CT of the wrist only if clinical examination is positive at 6 weeks

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Scaphoid fracture presents with anatomical snuffbox tenderness after FOOSH. It is the most commonly fractured carpal bone. Initial X-rays are negative in up to 20% of cases. The scaphoid has a retrograde blood supply (enters distally), making the proximal pole vulnerable to AVN — particularly with untreated waist fractures. Clinical rule: snuffbox tenderness = treat as scaphoid fracture until proven otherwise. Apply thumb spica cast and reimage at 2 weeks (X-ray may become positive as bone resorption occurs) or obtain MRI/CT immediately if high suspicion.

</details>

**Q8. A 6-year-old boy falls off a swing injuring his elbow. X-ray shows a supracondylar fracture of the humerus (Gartland Type III, completely displaced). Which nerve is most commonly injured in supracondylar fractures?**
- A) Ulnar nerve
- B) Radial nerve
- C) Anterior interosseous nerve (branch of median nerve)
- D) Musculocutaneous nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Supracondylar fractures of the humerus are the most common elbow fractures in children (peak age 5–8 years). The anterior interosseous nerve (AIN — a pure motor branch of the median nerve) is the most commonly injured nerve, causing inability to flex the distal phalanx of the thumb (flexor pollicis longus) and index finger (FDP) — "pinch sign" or "OK sign" loss. Volkmann's ischemic contracture results from anterior interosseous vessel injury and compartment syndrome. Radial nerve injury is associated with humeral shaft fractures at the spiral groove.

</details>

**Q9. A 35-year-old man sustains a humeral shaft fracture following a direct blow to the arm. On examination, he is unable to extend the wrist and fingers, and has numbness over the first dorsal web space. The nerve injured is:**
- A) Musculocutaneous nerve
- B) Axillary nerve
- C) Radial nerve (in the spiral groove)
- D) Posterior interosseous nerve at the elbow

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The radial nerve is injured in humeral shaft fractures at the spiral groove (middle third of humerus), causing wrist drop (loss of wrist extension — ECRL, ECRB), finger drop (loss of finger extension), and numbness over the first dorsal web space (superficial radial nerve cutaneous supply). The axillary nerve is injured in shoulder dislocations/surgical neck fractures (loss of deltoid + patch of lateral arm numbness). Posterior interosseous nerve (deep branch of radial nerve at the elbow) causes finger drop without wrist drop (ECRL preserved — supplied before the bifurcation).

</details>

**Q10. A 25-year-old rugby player sustains a clavicle fracture at the junction of the middle and outer thirds. Which of the following is the most common complication of a displaced midshaft clavicle fracture?**
- A) Subclavian artery injury
- B) Malunion
- C) Brachial plexus injury
- D) Pneumothorax

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Clavicle fractures occur most commonly at the middle third (80%). Malunion (bony union in an abnormal position) is the most common complication of conservatively managed displaced midshaft clavicle fractures, causing shoulder shortening and cosmetic deformity. Indications for surgical fixation include: open fractures, neurovascular compromise, skin tenting, shortening >2 cm, significant displacement, and floating shoulder. Subclavian artery injury and pneumothorax are rare but recognized complications, more common with high-energy injuries.

</details>

## LOWER LIMB FRACTURES (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 75-year-old woman with osteoporosis falls at home. X-ray shows a femoral neck fracture with complete displacement of the femoral head — the head is fully separated and rotated. This is Garden classification:**
- A) Garden I
- B) Garden II
- C) Garden III
- D) Garden IV

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

Garden classification of femoral neck fractures: Grade I — incomplete/impacted (valgus impacted, least risk of AVN); Grade II — complete, non-displaced; Grade III — complete, partially displaced; Grade IV — complete, fully displaced (no trabecular alignment — highest risk of AVN ~33%). In elderly patients with Garden III/IV fractures, hemiarthroplasty is preferred over fixation due to high AVN and re-operation risk. Younger patients (<55 years) undergo urgent reduction and internal fixation to preserve the femoral head.

</details>

**Q12. A 70-year-old man with a displaced intertrochanteric fracture of the femur is medically fit. The preferred surgical management is:**
- A) Total hip replacement
- B) Dynamic hip screw (DHS) fixation
- C) Conservative management with traction
- D) Hemiarthroplasty

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Intertrochanteric fractures (extracapsular, below the lesser trochanter attachment) have an intact blood supply to the femoral head, making fixation (Dynamic Hip Screw) the treatment of choice. The DHS allows controlled collapse along the screw axis, promoting union. AVN risk is low (blood supply intact). Hemiarthroplasty is reserved for femoral neck (intracapsular) fractures in the elderly. Subtrochanteric fractures are treated with intramedullary nailing (cephalomedullary nail — e.g., PFN) due to high bending forces.

</details>

**Q13. A 55-year-old woman is brought after a road traffic accident. X-ray pelvis shows a tibial plateau fracture on the lateral side. The structure most at risk of associated injury is:**
- A) Posterior cruciate ligament
- B) Lateral collateral ligament and common peroneal nerve
- C) Medial collateral ligament and saphenous nerve
- D) Posterior tibial nerve and artery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Lateral tibial plateau fractures (most common — Schatzker I–III) result from valgus force on the knee (e.g., pedestrian struck by car bumper — "bumper fracture"). The lateral compartment structures are at risk: lateral collateral ligament, anterior cruciate ligament, popliteal artery, and the common peroneal nerve (as it winds around the fibular neck). Common peroneal nerve injury causes foot drop (loss of dorsiflexion and eversion). Medial plateau fractures (Schatzker IV–VI) involve high-energy injuries with greater neurovascular risk.

</details>

**Q14. A 30-year-old athlete is assessed after an ankle inversion injury. X-ray shows a fracture of the lateral malleolus at the level of the ankle mortise, with an intact medial side. This is a:**
- A) Pott's fracture (bimalleolar)
- B) Weber A fracture
- C) Weber B fracture
- D) Weber C fracture

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Weber classification of ankle fractures is based on the level of fibular fracture relative to the ankle mortise (syndesmosis): Weber A — below the mortise (supination-adduction, syndesmosis intact, stable); Weber B — at the level of the mortise (syndesmosis possibly injured, variable stability — most common); Weber C — above the mortise (syndesmosis disrupted, unstable, requires surgical fixation). Pott's fracture is a bimalleolar fracture. Stability is the key determinant of surgical vs. conservative management.

</details>

**Q15. In a fracture neck of femur, AVN of the femoral head occurs due to disruption of the primary blood supply. The main artery supplying the femoral head in adults is:**
- A) Obturator artery via the ligamentum teres
- B) Medial femoral circumflex artery (retinacular branches)
- C) Lateral femoral circumflex artery
- D) Superior gluteal artery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

In adults, the primary blood supply to the femoral head is from the medial femoral circumflex artery (MFCA), which gives off retinacular branches running along the femoral neck under the joint capsule. These are disrupted in displaced femoral neck fractures, causing AVN. The ligamentum teres (obturator artery branch) provides a negligible blood supply in adults (significant only in children). This vascular anatomy explains why displaced intracapsular fractures (Garden III/IV) have high AVN rates, while extracapsular (intertrochanteric) fractures do not.

</details>

## BONE INFECTIONS AND TUMORS (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 10-year-old boy presents with 6 weeks of pain, swelling, and redness around the metaphysis of the distal femur. He had a boil on his leg 8 weeks ago. X-ray shows a lytic lesion with a surrounding sclerotic rim in the metaphysis. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Ewing's sarcoma
- B) Osteosarcoma
- C) Brodie's abscess (chronic osteomyelitis)
- D) Giant cell tumor

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Brodie's abscess is a form of subacute/chronic osteomyelitis, most commonly caused by Staphylococcus aureus (most common organism in all age groups of osteomyelitis). It appears as a lytic lesion with a sclerotic (reactive bone) rim in the metaphysis of long bones — "target sign" on MRI. Preceded by a skin infection (hematogenous spread). Ewing's sarcoma shows "onion skin" periosteal reaction in the diaphysis; osteosarcoma shows "sunburst" pattern + Codman's triangle; GCT shows "soap bubble" appearance at the epiphysis.

</details>

**Q17. A 6-month-old infant presents with fever, pseudoparalysis of the right hip (not moving the leg), and a swollen, tender right hip joint. WBC is elevated. The most likely causative organism of septic arthritis in this age group is:**
- A) Haemophilus influenzae
- B) Staphylococcus aureus
- C) Group B Streptococcus
- D) Gram-negative organisms

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Staphylococcus aureus is the most common causative organism of septic arthritis in all age groups beyond the neonatal period. In neonates (<3 months), Group B Streptococcus and S. aureus are co-predominant. Septic arthritis is a true orthopedic emergency — synovial fluid pus destroys articular cartilage within hours via proteolytic enzymes. Management: urgent joint aspiration (hip — image-guided) and surgical washout + IV antibiotics. Kocher criteria help differentiate septic arthritis from transient synovitis (fever, ESR, CRP, non-weight-bearing).

</details>

**Q18. A 15-year-old boy presents with a 3-month history of progressive pain and swelling around his right knee. X-ray shows a destructive lesion in the distal femoral metaphysis with spiculated ("sunburst") periosteal reaction and a Codman's triangle. Serum alkaline phosphatase is elevated. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Ewing's sarcoma
- B) Giant cell tumor
- C) Osteosarcoma
- D) Chondrosarcoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Osteosarcoma is the most common primary malignant bone tumor in children and adolescents (peak age 10–20 years). Classic location: metaphysis of distal femur, proximal tibia, proximal humerus (around the knee most common — "I hate you below my knee"). X-ray: sunburst periosteal reaction + Codman's triangle (elevated periosteum forming a triangle). Raised ALP reflects osteoblastic activity. Treatment: neoadjuvant chemotherapy (MAP — methotrexate, doxorubicin, cisplatin) + limb-salvage surgery. Ewing's sarcoma affects the diaphysis with onion-skin reaction.

</details>

**Q19. A 20-year-old man presents with bone pain in his right fibula diaphysis. X-ray shows a permeative lytic lesion with a lamellated "onion skin" periosteal reaction. Biopsy shows small round blue cells. The most likely diagnosis and characteristic chromosomal translocation are:**
- A) Osteosarcoma — t(11;22)
- B) Ewing's sarcoma — t(11;22) [EWS-FLI1]
- C) Lymphoma — t(14;18)
- D) Neuroblastoma — MYCN amplification

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ewing's sarcoma is the second most common primary malignant bone tumor in children/adolescents (peak 10–20 years), arising in the diaphysis of long bones (femur, tibia, fibula, pelvis). Histology: small round blue cells (like lymphoma, neuroblastoma, rhabdomyosarcoma — the "small blue round cell tumors"). The defining molecular feature: t(11;22)(q24;q12) translocation creating the EWS-FLI1 fusion gene. Radiology: "onion skin" periosteal layering. Treatment: multiagent chemotherapy + local control (surgery/radiotherapy).

</details>

**Q20. A 35-year-old woman presents with a slowly progressive painless swelling at the distal femoral epiphysis. X-ray shows a lytic lesion with a "soap bubble" appearance extending to the articular surface. There is no periosteal reaction. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Osteosarcoma
- B) Ewing's sarcoma
- C) Giant cell tumor (GCT)
- D) Unicameral bone cyst

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Giant cell tumor (GCT / osteoclastoma) is characterized by: age 20–40 years (skeletally mature), epiphyseal location (extends to articular surface), "soap bubble" or "blow-out" lytic appearance on X-ray, NO periosteal reaction. Histology: mononuclear stromal cells + multinucleated osteoclast-like giant cells. Location: distal femur, proximal tibia, distal radius (around the knee). GCT is locally aggressive and can recur; 5–10% undergo malignant transformation. Treatment: curettage + bone grafting ± denosumab (anti-RANKL). Unicameral bone cysts occur in the metaphysis of children.

</details>

## JOINT DISORDERS AND SPINE (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 55-year-old obese woman presents with bilateral knee pain worsening on activity, morning stiffness lasting 15 minutes, and bony hard swellings around the knee. X-ray shows joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, osteophytes, and subchondral cysts. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Rheumatoid arthritis
- B) Gout
- C) Osteoarthritis
- D) Septic arthritis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Osteoarthritis (OA) X-ray features: the mnemonic LOSS — Loss of joint space (asymmetric, weight-bearing area), Osteophytes (marginal), Subchondral sclerosis, Subchondral cysts. Morning stiffness <30 minutes (vs. RA >1 hour). OA knees: medial compartment most affected → varus deformity. RA shows symmetric joint space loss, periarticular osteoporosis, marginal erosions, and soft tissue swelling — no osteophytes. Gout presents with asymmetric joint involvement, tophi, and punched-out erosions with overhanging edges.

</details>

**Q22. A 40-year-old man presents with acute severe pain, redness, and swelling of the first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra) with a fever. Synovial fluid analysis shows negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals under polarized light. The diagnosis and crystals are:**
- A) Pseudogout — calcium pyrophosphate (positively birefringent, rhomboid)
- B) Gout — monosodium urate (negatively birefringent, needle-shaped)
- C) Septic arthritis — no crystals
- D) Reactive arthritis — gram-negative bacilli

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Gout is caused by monosodium urate (MSU) crystal deposition. Synovial fluid: negatively birefringent (yellow when parallel to polarizer axis), needle-shaped crystals. Classic presentation: podagra (first MTP joint). Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate crystal disease — CPPD) presents with positively birefringent (blue when parallel), rhomboid-shaped crystals — typically affecting the knee (chondrocalcinosis visible on X-ray). Treatment of acute gout: NSAIDs, colchicine, or corticosteroids. Prophylaxis: allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor) or febuxostat.

</details>

**Q23. A 45-year-old woman has hip pain worsening on walking. On examination, when she stands on her right leg, her left hip drops (pelvis tilts toward the unaffected side). This is a positive:**
- A) Thomas test
- B) Trendelenburg test
- C) FABER test
- D) McMurray test

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Trendelenburg test assesses hip abductor (gluteus medius) function. Positive test: when weight is borne on the affected side, the pelvis sags/drops toward the unaffected side (gluteus medius cannot stabilize the pelvis). In OA hip, the abductors are weakened. The patient leans toward the affected side when walking (Trendelenburg gait = antalgic lurch). Thomas test: assesses fixed flexion deformity of the hip. FABER (Flexion, ABduction, External Rotation): sacroiliac joint and hip pathology. McMurray: meniscal tears of the knee.

</details>

**Q24. A 35-year-old man presents with progressive back pain, low-grade fever, night sweats, and weight loss. MRI shows destruction of the L2-L3 vertebral bodies with disc space involvement and a paraspinal abscess (psoas abscess). The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Pyogenic osteomyelitis
- B) Multiple myeloma
- C) Pott's disease (spinal tuberculosis)
- D) Metastatic carcinoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Pott's disease (TB spine) is the most common extra-pulmonary skeletal TB. Key features: anterior vertebral body destruction, disc space involvement (unlike metastases which spare the disc), paraspinal "cold abscess" (psoas abscess tracks to groin), and gibbous deformity (sharp kyphosis from anterior collapse). Most common site: thoracolumbar junction (T10–L2). Treatment: 4-drug ATT for 9–12 months ± surgery for cord compression. Pyogenic osteomyelitis also involves the disc but presents more acutely with higher fever.

</details>

**Q25. A 45-year-old man has L5-S1 disc prolapse confirmed on MRI. Which is the correct neurology for L5-S1 level disc herniation?**
- A) L5-S1 compresses L5 nerve root — loss of great toe dorsiflexion, sensory loss dorsum foot
- B) L5-S1 compresses S1 nerve root — loss of ankle plantar flexion, diminished ankle jerk, sensory loss lateral foot
- C) L5-S1 compresses L4 nerve root — loss of ankle dorsiflexion, diminished knee jerk
- D) L5-S1 compresses S2 nerve root — bladder and bowel dysfunction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

L5-S1 disc prolapse compresses the S1 nerve root (disc herniates postero-laterally compressing the nerve root exiting at the level below). S1 compression: weakness of plantar flexion (gastrocnemius/soleus — "standing on tiptoe"), diminished ankle jerk reflex, and sensory loss over the lateral foot and sole. L4-L5 disc prolapse compresses L5: weakness of great toe dorsiflexion (EHL), sensory loss over dorsum of foot/first web space, ankle jerk typically preserved. L4 compression from L3-L4 disc: knee jerk diminished, quadriceps weakness.

</details>

## METABOLIC AND PEDIATRIC ORTHOPEDICS (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. A 65-year-old postmenopausal woman's DEXA scan reports a T-score of −2.8 at the lumbar spine. She has no prior fractures. The diagnosis and first-line treatment are:**
- A) Osteopenia — calcium and vitamin D supplementation only
- B) Osteoporosis — bisphosphonates (alendronate) + calcium and vitamin D
- C) Osteomalacia — vitamin D supplementation alone
- D) Osteoporosis — calcitonin as first-line

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

WHO T-score criteria: Normal ≥ −1.0; Osteopenia −1.0 to −2.5; Osteoporosis ≤ −2.5. This patient has osteoporosis (T-score −2.8). First-line treatment: bisphosphonates (alendronate weekly, risedronate, zoledronic acid annually) + calcium 1000–1200 mg/day + Vitamin D 800–1000 IU/day. Bisphosphonates inhibit osteoclast-mediated bone resorption (farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase inhibition). Calcitonin (nasal/injectable) is a second-line agent. Osteomalacia is a mineralization defect with raised ALP and Looser's zones.

</details>

**Q27. A 60-year-old man with malabsorption presents with bone pain, proximal muscle weakness, and waddling gait. X-ray shows pseudofractures (Looser's zones) in the medial femoral neck. Serum calcium is low, ALP is markedly elevated, and phosphate is low. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Osteoporosis
- B) Paget's disease
- C) Osteomalacia
- D) Hyperparathyroidism

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Osteomalacia (adult form of rickets) is a defect in bone mineralization due to vitamin D deficiency (malabsorption, inadequate sun exposure) or phosphate deficiency. Biochemistry: ↓ Ca²⁺, ↓ PO₄, ↑ ALP, ↑ PTH, ↓ 25-OH Vitamin D. Looser's zones (pseudofractures) are pathognomonic — linear radiolucent bands perpendicular to the cortex at areas of high mechanical stress (medial femoral neck, pubic rami, lateral scapular border). Osteoporosis has normal biochemistry; Paget's disease has isolated ↑↑ ALP with normal Ca²⁺/PO₄ and alkaline phosphatase elevation.

</details>

**Q28. A newborn is assessed and found to have limited abduction of the left hip with a "clunk" on performing the Ortolani maneuver. Ultrasound confirms the diagnosis. The most appropriate initial management for a 2-week-old infant is:**
- A) Observation — most cases resolve spontaneously
- B) Pavlik harness
- C) Open surgical reduction
- D) Hip spica cast

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH): Ortolani test — relocates the dislocated hip (clunk = reduction); Barlow test — dislocates a reducible hip (clunk = dislocation). Ultrasound is the imaging modality of choice in infants <6 months (cartilaginous femoral head not visible on X-ray). First-line treatment in infants <6 months: Pavlik harness (keeps hip in flexion + abduction, allowing acetabular remodeling). Failure of harness (>6 months) → closed/open reduction + spica cast. The Pavlik harness is the gold standard for neonatal DDH.

</details>

**Q29. A 13-year-old obese male presents with a 3-week history of right hip pain radiating to the knee, and a limp. On examination, the hip has restricted internal rotation and the leg lies in external rotation. X-ray shows posterior slippage of the femoral epiphysis (ice cream falling off the cone). The diagnosis is:**
- A) Perthes disease
- B) Transient synovitis of the hip
- C) Slipped upper femoral epiphysis (SUFE)
- D) Developmental dysplasia of the hip

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

SUFE (Slipped Upper Femoral Epiphysis) occurs in obese adolescent males (peak 10–16 years) due to weakness of the perichondrial ring around the physis under shear stress from body weight. The femoral epiphysis slips posteriorly and medially relative to the femoral neck — "ice cream falling off the cone" on AP X-ray (Klein's line no longer intersects the femoral head). The leg lies in external rotation. Treatment: urgent surgical fixation with a single cannulated screw in situ. Bilateral in 25%. Must screen the contralateral hip.

</details>

**Q30. A 6-year-old boy has a limp and mild hip pain for 3 weeks. X-ray of the hip shows increased density and flattening of the femoral head ("crescent sign"). Technetium bone scan shows reduced uptake in the femoral head. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) SUFE
- B) Perthes disease (Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease)
- C) Septic arthritis
- D) Transient synovitis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Perthes disease is idiopathic AVN of the femoral head in children, typically affecting boys aged 4–8 years (peak 5–7 years). X-ray progresses from: normal → increased density (avascular phase) → fragmentation → re-ossification → healed (may have residual deformity). The "crescent sign" represents a subchondral fracture. Bone scan: cold spot (reduced uptake) in the early avascular phase. MRI is the most sensitive early imaging modality. Prognosis depends on age (worse if >8 years), extent of head involvement, and containment treatment (abduction orthosis/surgery). SUFE occurs in older, obese adolescents.

</details>
