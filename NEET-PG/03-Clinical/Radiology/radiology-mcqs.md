## CHEST X-RAY SIGNS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 4-year-old child is brought with cyanosis since birth. Chest X-ray shows a boot-shaped heart with decreased pulmonary vascular markings. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Transposition of great arteries
- B) Tetralogy of Fallot
- C) Total anomalous pulmonary venous connection
- D) Pulmonary arterial hypertension

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Boot-shaped (coeur en sabot) heart on CXR = Tetralogy of Fallot — caused by right ventricular hypertrophy (upturned cardiac apex) + pulmonary oligemia (small pulmonary artery). TOF is the most common cyanotic CHD after 1 year. TGA gives an "egg-on-side" heart with narrow mediastinum and increased pulmonary vascularity. Pulmonary oligemia differentiates TOF from TGA.

</details>

**Q2. A 60-year-old man with known malignancy presents with progressive dyspnea. Chest X-ray shows a large globular/flask-shaped cardiac silhouette. What is the minimum volume of pericardial fluid required to produce this appearance on CXR?**
- A) 50 mL
- B) 100 mL
- C) 200 mL
- D) 500 mL

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Flask-shaped/globular cardiac silhouette on CXR indicates pericardial effusion — requires at least 200 mL of fluid to produce radiological cardiomegaly. Below 200 mL, the pericardial fluid is not visible on plain X-ray. Echocardiography detects as little as 15–50 mL. The "water bottle" silhouette is the classic description. CT/Echo are far more sensitive than CXR for effusion quantification.

</details>

**Q3. A 65-year-old hypertensive patient presents with acute dyspnea and orthopnea. CXR shows horizontal lines 1–2 cm long at the lung bases in the costophrenic angles. What do these lines represent, and what PCWP do they indicate?**
- A) Pleural plaques — indicate asbestos exposure
- B) Kerley B lines — interstitial pulmonary edema, PCWP >20 mmHg
- C) Kerley A lines — alveolar edema, PCWP >30 mmHg
- D) Atelectasis — indicate postoperative collapse

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Kerley B lines = horizontal, 1–2 cm lines perpendicular to the pleural surface at lung bases — represent dilated interlobular lymphatics in interstitial pulmonary edema. They appear when PCWP (pulmonary capillary wedge pressure) exceeds 20 mmHg. Kerley A lines are longer, oblique lines in upper/mid zones. Kerley B lines are the earliest radiological sign of left heart failure on CXR.

</details>

**Q4. A neonate with cyanosis at birth has a CXR showing an "egg-on-its-side" cardiac silhouette with a narrow mediastinum and increased pulmonary vascularity. What is the diagnosis and why is the mediastinum narrow?**
- A) TOF — right ventricular hypertrophy narrows the mediastinum
- B) TGA — the great arteries lie one behind the other (AP relationship) narrowing the vascular pedicle
- C) TAPVC — vertical vein creates a wide mediastinum
- D) Truncus arteriosus — single great vessel narrows the shadow

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA) — "egg-on-side" heart: in TGA, the aorta lies anterior and the pulmonary artery posterior (instead of normal side-by-side relationship), so the vascular pedicle appears narrow on AP CXR. Increased pulmonary vascularity is due to parallel circulation with mixing. TGA is the most common cyanotic CHD presenting in the neonatal period (not TOF, which presents later).

</details>

**Q5. A CXR is done for a 45-year-old man in an emergency department. The heart appears enlarged. Before diagnosing cardiomegaly, which technical factor must be confirmed?**
- A) The X-ray must be taken in expiration
- B) The cardiothoracic ratio must be >0.5, measured on a PA (posteroanterior) film
- C) The CTR must be >0.45 on any view
- D) Cardiomegaly is diagnosed only on AP films

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cardiomegaly is defined as cardiothoracic ratio (CTR) >0.5, measured ONLY on a PA (posteroanterior) erect film. AP films magnify the cardiac shadow due to beam divergence and the heart being farther from the film — AP films always overestimate heart size, so cardiomegaly cannot be diagnosed on AP films. Standard CXR: PA view, full inspiration, erect posture.

</details>

## ABDOMINAL X-RAY SIGNS (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 70-year-old presents with sudden onset massive abdominal distension and constipation for 5 days. Plain X-ray abdomen shows an inverted U-shaped loop of bowel with its apex pointing toward the right upper quadrant. What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment?**
- A) Caecal volvulus — Rx right hemicolectomy
- B) Sigmoid volvulus — Rx sigmoidoscopic/colonoscopic decompression
- C) Small bowel obstruction — Rx nasogastric tube decompression
- D) Paralytic ileus — Rx conservative management

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Coffee bean sign = sigmoid volvulus — the inverted U-shaped distended sigmoid loop with its apex pointing toward the right upper quadrant (or right hypochondrium). It is the most common colonic volvulus. First-line treatment: sigmoidoscopic or colonoscopic decompression (followed by elective sigmoid colectomy). If gangrenous sigmoid: emergency Hartmann's procedure. Caecal volvulus apex points to left upper quadrant.

</details>

**Q7. A neonate born to a 32-year-old woman (antenatal ultrasound showed polyhydramnios) presents with bilious vomiting on day 1. Plain X-ray abdomen shows gas in the stomach and proximal duodenum only (double bubble sign). What is the most likely diagnosis and associated condition?**
- A) Jejunal atresia — associated with cystic fibrosis
- B) Duodenal atresia — associated with Down syndrome (trisomy 21)
- C) Pyloric stenosis — associated with Turner syndrome
- D) Malrotation with volvulus — associated with heterotaxy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Double bubble sign = duodenal atresia — gas in stomach + gas in proximal duodenum (dilated first part), with no gas beyond. Associated with Down syndrome (trisomy 21) in 30% of cases, also polyhydramnios (failure to swallow amniotic fluid). Surgical treatment: duodenoduodenostomy. Triple bubble sign suggests jejunal atresia. Pyloric stenosis appears at 2–6 weeks (not day 1) with non-bilious vomiting.

</details>

**Q8. A 35-year-old with recurrent right lower quadrant pain and chronic diarrhea undergoes barium meal follow-through. It shows a fixed, narrow "string-like" segment of terminal ileum. What is this sign called and what does it indicate?**
- A) Bird's beak sign — achalasia cardia
- B) String sign of Kantor — Crohn's disease of terminal ileum
- C) Rat-tail sign — carcinoma of esophagus
- D) Apple core sign — carcinoma of colon

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

String sign of Kantor on barium meal = Crohn's disease — fixed, elongated narrowing of the terminal ileum due to transmural inflammation and fibrosis, creating a "string" of contrast. Associated with cobblestone mucosa, skip lesions, and fistulae. Apple core sign = colonic carcinoma on barium enema. Rat-tail sign = carcinoma esophagus on barium swallow. Bird's beak = achalasia on barium swallow.

</details>

**Q9. A 45-year-old woman with progressive dysphagia to solids and liquids for 2 years undergoes barium swallow. It shows smooth, tapering narrowing at the gastroesophageal junction with proximal dilatation. What is the diagnosis and gold standard investigation?**
- A) Carcinoma esophagus — gold standard: CT chest
- B) Achalasia cardia — gold standard: esophageal manometry
- C) Diffuse esophageal spasm — gold standard: barium swallow
- D) Peptic stricture — gold standard: endoscopy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bird's beak/rat-tail sign on barium swallow = achalasia cardia — smooth, symmetric tapering at the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) due to failure of LES relaxation (loss of Auerbach's plexus), with proximal mega-esophagus. Gold standard investigation: esophageal manometry (shows absent peristalsis + incomplete LES relaxation + elevated LES pressure). Barium swallow is the best initial investigation.

</details>

**Q10. An elderly patient on NSAIDs presents with sudden onset severe abdominal pain. Erect CXR shows air under the right hemidiaphragm. On supine abdominal X-ray, both walls of the bowel are clearly visible. What is this sign called?**
- A) Rigler's sign (double wall sign) — pneumoperitoneum
- B) Cupola sign — indicates pleural effusion
- C) Football sign — indicates normal bowel gas
- D) Psoas shadow sign — retroperitoneal air

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Rigler's sign (double wall sign) = pneumoperitoneum on supine X-ray — normally only the inner bowel wall is visible; with free air on both sides, BOTH walls of the bowel become visible. The erect CXR finding of air under the right hemidiaphragm is the most common and classic sign of perforation. Causes: perforated peptic ulcer (most common), perforated appendix, bowel perforation.

</details>

## BRAIN AND SPINE IMAGING (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 25-year-old motorcyclist is brought to the emergency after a head injury. He was conscious initially but lost consciousness 30 minutes later. CT head without contrast shows a biconvex (lenticular) hyperdense lesion. What is the diagnosis and which vessel is typically involved?**
- A) Subdural hematoma — bridging veins
- B) Epidural hematoma — middle meningeal artery
- C) Contusion — cortical vessels
- D) Subarachnoid hemorrhage — Berry aneurysm

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Epidural hematoma = biconvex/lenticular hyperdense lesion on CT that does NOT cross suture lines (dura is attached at sutures, limiting spread). The classic history: lucid interval followed by rapid deterioration. Arterial source: middle meningeal artery (temporal bone fracture). Subdural hematoma is crescent-shaped, crosses suture lines (bridging veins, low-pressure venous bleeding), common in elderly/alcoholics.

</details>

**Q12. An 80-year-old alcoholic presents with progressive cognitive decline over 3 weeks. CT head without contrast shows a crescent-shaped hypodense lesion over the right cerebral hemisphere. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Acute epidural hematoma
- B) Chronic subdural hematoma
- C) Cerebral contusion
- D) Subarachnoid hemorrhage

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Chronic subdural hematoma appears hypodense (dark) on CT — blood breakdown products over 3 weeks reduce density to near-CSF levels. Acute SDH (< 3 days) is hyperdense (bright). SDH is crescent-shaped and crosses suture lines (unlike EDH). Bridging vein rupture in elderly/alcoholics (cerebral atrophy causes stretching). Subacute SDH (1–3 weeks) is isodense to brain — can be missed on CT.

</details>

**Q13. A 50-year-old woman presents with the "worst headache of her life" with sudden onset. CT head without contrast is done within 6 hours — it is reported as normal. What is the next appropriate investigation, and what finding confirms the diagnosis?**
- A) MRI brain with gadolinium — ring-enhancing lesion
- B) Lumbar puncture — xanthochromia (yellow CSF)
- C) CT angiography — Berry aneurysm location
- D) EEG — epileptiform discharges

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) — thunderclap headache ("worst ever"). CT without contrast within 6 hours detects SAH in >95% of cases (hyperdense basal cisterns). If CT is negative but clinical suspicion high: LP after 12 hours shows xanthochromia (yellow discoloration from bilirubin, oxyhemoglobin degradation). Most common cause: Berry aneurysm (saccular) at Circle of Willis bifurcations.

</details>

**Q14. Which of the following statements about CT versus MRI in acute intracranial hemorrhage is correct?**
- A) MRI with gadolinium is the first investigation of choice for acute hemorrhage
- B) CT without contrast is the investigation of choice — acute blood is hyperdense
- C) MRI T2 is most sensitive for acute hemorrhage within the first hour
- D) CT with contrast is done first to detect bleeding source

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

CT head WITHOUT contrast is the first investigation of choice for any suspected intracranial hemorrhage — acute blood (oxyhemoglobin) is hyperdense (bright) on CT. Contrast is NOT given initially as it can obscure hemorrhage (isodense artifact). MRI is superior for subacute/chronic hemorrhage, posterior fossa lesions, and ischemic stroke. MRI DWI detects ischemic stroke within 30 minutes (CT detects only after 6–24 hours).

</details>

**Q15. A 40-year-old presents with L4-L5 disc prolapse causing sciatica. MRI lumbar spine is performed. On which MRI sequence does the herniated nucleus pulposus appear as a bright (hyperintense) signal?**
- A) T1-weighted sequence
- B) T2-weighted sequence
- C) FLAIR sequence
- D) Diffusion-weighted sequence

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

T2-weighted MRI: fluid appears bright (hyperintense) — the nucleus pulposus (water-rich gelatinous core) appears bright on T2. A degenerated disc loses water content and becomes hypointense ("dark disc") on T2. T1 shows anatomy (fat bright, CSF dark). T2 shows pathology (fluid/edema/inflammation bright). Key rule: T1 = anatomy, T2 = pathology (water/fluid).

</details>

## BONE AND MUSCULOSKELETAL RADIOLOGY (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 15-year-old boy presents with pain and swelling around the knee. X-ray shows a destructive lesion of the distal femoral metaphysis with sunburst periosteal reaction and Codman's triangle. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Ewing's sarcoma
- B) Giant cell tumor
- C) Osteosarcoma
- D) Chondrosarcoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Sunburst pattern + Codman's triangle (periosteal elevation at the lesion margin) = osteosarcoma. It arises in the metaphysis of long bones (distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus) in adolescents. Most common primary malignant bone tumor in children. Ewing's sarcoma shows onion-skin periosteal reaction in the diaphysis. Giant cell tumor shows soap-bubble appearance in the epiphysis.

</details>

**Q17. A 10-year-old boy has mid-shaft femur pain. X-ray shows a diaphyseal lesion with laminated "onion-skin" periosteal reaction. Biopsy shows small round blue cells. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Osteosarcoma
- B) Ewing's sarcoma
- C) Osteoblastoma
- D) Lymphoma of bone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Onion-skin (laminated) periosteal reaction + diaphyseal location + small round blue cells on biopsy = Ewing's sarcoma. It shows the t(11;22) chromosomal translocation (EWS-FLI1 fusion). Second most common malignant bone tumor in children. Unlike osteosarcoma (metaphyseal), Ewing's is diaphyseal. Treatment: chemotherapy + surgery/radiotherapy.

</details>

**Q18. A 35-year-old woman presents with a lytic epiphyseal lesion at the distal femur. X-ray shows a "soap bubble" appearance extending to the articular surface. There is no periosteal reaction. What is the diagnosis and treatment?**
- A) Aneurysmal bone cyst — Rx: embolization
- B) Chondrosarcoma — Rx: wide excision
- C) Giant cell tumor (osteoclastoma) — Rx: curettage + bone grafting
- D) Simple bone cyst — Rx: aspiration + steroid injection

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Soap bubble appearance + epiphyseal + distal femur/proximal tibia + extends to articular surface = Giant cell tumor (GCT/osteoclastoma). It occurs in skeletally mature adults (20–40 years), after epiphyseal plate closure. 10–15% are locally malignant. Treatment: extended curettage + bone cement/grafting; wide excision for recurrent cases. Aneurysmal bone cyst is metaphyseal, occurs in younger patients.

</details>

**Q19. A 50-year-old vegetarian woman presents with bone pain and proximal muscle weakness. X-ray pelvis shows bilateral symmetric radiolucent lines perpendicular to the cortex of the pubic rami. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Multiple myeloma — punched-out lytic lesions
- B) Osteomalacia — Looser's zones (pseudofractures)
- C) Paget's disease — cotton wool appearance
- D) Hyperparathyroidism — subperiosteal resorption

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Looser's zones (pseudofractures/Milkman fractures) = osteomalacia — bilateral symmetric radiolucent lines perpendicular to the bone cortex, representing stress fractures filled with unmineralized osteoid. Common sites: pubic rami, medial femoral neck, scapulae lateral border, ribs. Caused by vitamin D deficiency. Osteoporosis shows fractures (not pseudofractures). Hyperparathyroidism shows subperiosteal resorption at radial border of middle phalanges.

</details>

**Q20. A 30-year-old HLA-B27 positive male with low back pain and morning stiffness has X-ray lumbar spine showing "bamboo spine." What are the earliest radiological changes seen in this condition?**
- A) Bamboo spine and bridging syndesmophytes — earliest changes
- B) Sacroiliitis (blurring of sacroiliac joint margins) — earliest change
- C) Squaring of vertebral bodies — appears before sacroiliitis
- D) Calcification of anterior longitudinal ligament — earliest sign

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ankylosing spondylitis radiological sequence: earliest = sacroiliitis (bilateral symmetric SI joint blurring/erosions/sclerosis) → squaring of vertebral bodies (erosion of anterior vertebral corners = Romanus lesion) → syndesmophytes (vertical calcifications bridging vertebrae) → bamboo spine (complete fusion). Bamboo spine is the LATE/end-stage change, not early. X-ray changes lag 7–10 years behind; MRI detects early sacroiliitis.

</details>

## PEDIATRIC AND EMERGENCY RADIOLOGY (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 2-year-old presents with a barking cough, stridor, and low-grade fever. AP X-ray of the neck shows a "steeple sign." What is the diagnosis and most common causative organism?**
- A) Epiglottitis — Haemophilus influenzae type B
- B) Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis) — Parainfluenza virus
- C) Bacterial tracheitis — Staphylococcus aureus
- D) Retropharyngeal abscess — Streptococcus pyogenes

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Steeple sign = croup (LTB) — subglottic tracheal narrowing on AP neck X-ray, giving the appearance of a church steeple. Caused by parainfluenza virus (type 1 most common). Presents in children 6 months–3 years. Thumb sign on lateral neck X-ray = epiglottitis (swollen epiglottis) — H. influenzae type B, tripod position, "hot potato" voice. Never examine the throat in epiglottitis — risk of complete obstruction.

</details>

**Q22. A 3-year-old febrile child presents with sore throat and drooling. The child sits upright in a tripod position refusing to move the neck. Lateral neck X-ray shows a swollen, thumb-shaped epiglottis. What is the most dangerous immediate complication?**
- A) Peritonsillar abscess formation
- B) Complete airway obstruction
- C) Meningitis
- D) Epiglottic perforation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Thumb sign on lateral neck X-ray = epiglottitis — the swollen epiglottis resembles a thumb. The most feared complication is complete acute airway obstruction (supraglottitis can cause rapid total occlusion). Never use tongue depressor or attempt throat examination — triggers laryngospasm. Management: keep child calm, immediate airway control in OT (intubation/tracheostomy), IV cefotaxime. Largely prevented by Hib vaccine.

</details>

**Q23. A 6-month-old infant presents with paroxysmal colicky pain, currant-jelly stools, and a sausage-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant. USG abdomen is performed. What is the classic sonographic sign and initial treatment?**
- A) Double bubble sign on USG — surgical laparotomy
- B) Target sign (concentric rings) on USG — pneumatic or hydrostatic reduction
- C) Coffee bean sign on USG — sigmoidoscopic decompression
- D) Pseudokidney sign only — IV antibiotics

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Intussusception on USG: target sign (concentric rings = bowel within bowel) on transverse section; pseudokidney sign on longitudinal section. Classic triad: colicky pain + currant-jelly stools (blood/mucus) + abdominal mass. Peak age: 6 months–2 years. Initial treatment: pneumatic (air) or hydrostatic (saline/contrast) reduction under fluoroscopy/USG guidance. Surgical reduction if failed or peritonitis present.

**Q24. A 65-year-old smoker with dyspnea has HRCT chest showing bilateral perihilar "bat-wing" or "butterfly" pattern of haziness that spares the costophrenic angles and peripheral lung. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Bilateral pneumonia — consolidation in all zones
- B) Pulmonary edema — bat-wing/butterfly perihilar pattern
- C) Pulmonary fibrosis — honeycomb pattern
- D) Sarcoidosis — bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bat-wing/butterfly opacity on CXR or CT = pulmonary edema — bilateral perihilar haziness due to fluid accumulation in the central lung (around bronchi/vessels), sparing the peripheral lung and costophrenic angles. Ground-glass opacity is the early CT finding; bat-wing is the alveolar flooding pattern. HRCT patterns: honeycomb = IPF/UIP, crazy-paving = PCP/alveolar proteinosis, tree-in-bud = endobronchial TB.

</details>

**Q25. A 35-year-old HIV-positive patient presents with progressive dyspnea and dry cough. HRCT chest shows bilateral ground-glass opacities with a "crazy-paving" pattern (ground-glass + interlobular septal thickening). CD4 count is 80 cells/μL. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Pulmonary tuberculosis
- B) Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP)
- C) Usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP/IPF)
- D) Sarcoidosis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Crazy-paving pattern on HRCT (ground-glass opacity + interlobular septal thickening) in an HIV patient with CD4 <200 = PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia). ## MRI PRINCIPLES AND INTERVENTIONAL (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. On MRI brain, a lipoma of the corpus callosum appears bright on T1 and dark on T2 (with fat suppression). Which of the following also appears bright (hyperintense) on T1-weighted MRI?**
- A) Simple cyst (CSF signal)
- B) Acute ischemic infarct
- C) Subacute hematoma (methemoglobin)
- D) Intervertebral disc herniation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

T1-bright (hyperintense) signals: fat (lipoma, bone marrow), subacute blood (methemoglobin — 3 days to 3 weeks), proteinaceous fluid (colloid cyst, mucocele), gadolinium enhancement (breakdown of blood-brain barrier), melanin. T2-bright: CSF, edema, most tumors, inflammation, nucleus pulposus. CSF (simple cyst) is T1-dark/T2-bright. Acute ischemic infarct is T1-isodense; best seen on DWI.

</details>

**Q27. A 70-year-old presents with acute onset left hemiplegia within 45 minutes of onset. CT head is normal. Which MRI sequence detects acute ischemic stroke most sensitively and within what time frame?**
- A) T2 FLAIR — detects within 6 hours
- B) T1 post-contrast — detects enhancement after BBB breakdown
- C) Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) — detects within 30 minutes
- D) T2 gradient echo — detects hemorrhagic transformation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

DWI (Diffusion-Weighted Imaging) detects acute ischemic stroke within 30 minutes — cytotoxic edema restricts water diffusion, appearing bright on DWI with corresponding dark area on ADC map (restricted diffusion). CT becomes positive only after 6–24 hours. FLAIR shows ischemic changes after 6–8 hours. DWI also shows: abscess (restricted diffusion = bright), epidermoid cyst (bright), vs arachnoid cyst (dark = free diffusion).

</details>

**Q28. A 55-year-old with known liver cirrhosis and portal hypertension develops massive upper GI bleeding from esophageal varices not controlled by endoscopic therapy. Which interventional radiology procedure is indicated?**
- A) Uterine artery embolization
- B) ERCP with sphincterotomy
- C) Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS)
- D) Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt) creates an intrahepatic channel between portal and hepatic veins using a stent, reducing portal pressure — indicated for variceal bleeding refractory to endoscopic therapy, refractory ascites, and Budd-Chiari syndrome. ERCP is for CBD stones/pancreatic duct pathology. Uterine artery embolization is for fibroids. PTC is for biliary obstruction when ERCP fails.

</details>

**Q29. A 35-year-old woman presents with menorrhagia and a 10-week-sized uterus due to multiple fibroids. She wishes to preserve her uterus. Which interventional radiology procedure is appropriate?**
- A) TIPS — reduces portal venous pressure
- B) Uterine artery embolization (UAE)
- C) Renal artery stenting
- D) Inferior vena cava filter placement

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Uterine artery embolization (UAE) — polyvinyl alcohol particles injected via femoral artery catheterization into uterine arteries to infarct fibroids, reducing size by 40–60%. Advantages: uterine preservation, effective for menorrhagia, less invasive than surgery. Contraindicated if malignancy suspected or future fertility desired (can affect ovarian reserve). Alternative to myomectomy/hysterectomy.

</details>

**Q30. A 60-year-old presents with obstructive jaundice due to CBD stones. ERCP is performed. Which of the following is the correct indication for ERCP versus other biliary imaging modalities?**
- A) ERCP is the best imaging modality to diagnose CBD stones — used for diagnosis only
- B) ERCP is both diagnostic and therapeutic — used for CBD stone extraction + sphincterotomy
- C) MRCP is therapeutic and ERCP is only diagnostic
- D) PTC is the first-line procedure for CBD stone extraction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) is both diagnostic and therapeutic — it can extract CBD stones (with balloon/basket), perform sphincterotomy, place biliary stents, and obtain tissue biopsies. MRCP is non-invasive and purely diagnostic (no intervention possible). PTC (Percutaneous Transhepatic Cholangiography) is used when ERCP fails or for proximal biliary obstruction. ERCP is the treatment of choice for CBD stones.

</details>
