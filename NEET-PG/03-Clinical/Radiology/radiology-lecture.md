## How X-rays Work — The Physics of Seeing Through People

### Electromagnetic Radiation and Tissue Attenuation

To understand radiology from the ground up, begin with physics. X-rays are electromagnetic radiation — the same fundamental phenomenon as visible light, radio waves, and microwaves — but at a much higher energy (shorter wavelength, higher frequency). Specifically, X-rays have energies in the range of 20-150 keV. This high energy is what allows them to penetrate biological tissue: the photons carry enough energy to pass through soft tissue rather than being absorbed by it. But different tissues absorb (attenuate) X-rays to very different degrees, and this differential attenuation is the entire physical basis of radiographic imaging.

The attenuation of X-rays by tissue depends on two properties: the physical density of the tissue (how tightly packed the atoms are — denser structures absorb more) and the atomic number (Z) of the constituent atoms (higher Z elements absorb more X-rays, because the photoelectric effect scales with Z to the third power — Z³). Bone contains calcium (Z = 20) and phosphorus (Z = 15) — heavy atoms in a dense crystalline matrix. Bone absorbs X-rays very strongly. Soft tissue is predominantly water (Z of oxygen = 8) and carbon-based organic molecules — moderate absorption. Fat is less dense than water. Air is essentially empty space — almost zero absorption.

The X-ray film (or digital detector) records how many X-ray photons reach it. Where many photons arrive (because tissue absorbed few), the film blackens — this is radiolucent or black on film. Where few photons arrive (because tissue absorbed most), the film remains white/pale — this is radio-opaque or white on film. This is the inverse relationship that confuses beginners: dense structures appear white (they stop the X-rays from reaching the film), and empty structures appear black (X-rays pass straight through to blacken the film).

**Analogy:** Imagine shining a torch (X-ray source) through a curtain and looking at the shadow on a wall (the film). Dense objects in the curtain block the torch beam — they cast a white shadow (no light reaches the wall). Empty gaps in the curtain let the torch beam through freely — they appear dark (maximum light reaches the wall). X-ray imaging is exactly this: a shadow picture of differential tissue attenuation.

The five fundamental densities visible on plain radiograph, from most radio-opaque (white) to most radiolucent (black):

1. **Metal / contrast agent** — brightest white (lead, barium, iodine-based contrast)
2. **Bone** — white (calcium, high Z, dense matrix)
3. **Soft tissue / water / blood** — grey
4. **Fat** — dark grey (less dense than water, lower Z)
5. **Air / gas** — black (essentially zero attenuation)

**Clinical connection:** A pulmonary infiltrate — whether from pneumonia, pulmonary edema, or hemorrhage — replaces the normally air-filled alveoli with fluid or cellular material. Fluid has the density of soft tissue (grey), while air is black. So an infiltrated lung zone changes from black to grey-white on the chest X-ray. This is the "opacity" or "haziness" of consolidation: you are seeing the replacement of air with higher-density material.

### The Chest X-Ray — A Systematic Approach Grounded in Physics

A chest X-ray is the most common and information-rich plain radiograph. Interpreting it systematically means applying the attenuation principles to each structure. The normal chest X-ray shows:

- **Lung fields** — predominantly black (air-filled alveoli), with a fine vascular pattern (soft tissue/blood-density lines, because pulmonary arteries and veins contain blood, which is soft tissue density)
- **Heart and mediastinum** — white-grey (soft tissue/blood density solid structures)
- **Bones (ribs, clavicles, vertebrae)** — white
- **Diaphragm** — white-grey (muscle), separated from the liver below (also grey) by nothing visible — they are the same density, so there is no visible interface between them in normal circumstances. You can see the right hemidiaphragm because lung (black) is above it. When there is free air under the diaphragm (perforation), there is now a visible air-tissue interface — this is the radiological sign of perforation.

The principle of interface visibility is fundamental. Two adjacent structures are only visible as separate entities on a plain X-ray if they have different densities. A pneumonia consolidating the right middle lobe (against the right heart border) will obliterate the right heart border — because the consolidated lobe (soft tissue density, grey) is now the same density as the adjacent heart (also soft tissue density). This is the silhouette sign: when an expected interface disappears, it implies pathology in the structure adjacent to that interface.

![Chest X-ray showing obliteration of the right heart border by right middle lobe consolidation — the silhouette sign](../images/silhouette-sign-rml-consolidation.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Fix your eyes on the right heart border — if it is invisible (merged with adjacent lung density), consolidation is in the right middle lobe. Contrast with right lower lobe consolidation, which obliterates the right hemidiaphragm but leaves the right heart border intact.

> **Key exam insight (Silhouette Sign):** Right heart border silhouetted (obliterated) → right middle lobe consolidation (anatomically adjacent to right heart). Left heart border silhouetted → lingula consolidation. Right hemidiaphragm silhouetted → right lower lobe consolidation. Left hemidiaphragm silhouetted → left lower lobe consolidation.

## CT Scanning and Hounsfield Units

### The Physics of CT — Why We Need a Number System

Conventional X-rays produce a two-dimensional summation image — all structures along the path of the X-ray beam are superimposed. This limits our ability to detect subtle differences in tissue density and makes localisation in 3D impossible. CT (computed tomography) solves this by acquiring multiple X-ray projections at different angles around the patient and using mathematical reconstruction (filtered back projection or iterative algorithms) to compute cross-sectional density maps of the body. Each point in the cross-section is assigned a numerical density value — the Hounsfield Unit (HU), named after Godfrey Hounsfield, who invented CT in 1972 (Nobel Prize 1979).

Hounsfield Units are a linear scale calibrated with two reference points: distilled water = 0 HU, and air = -1000 HU. Everything else falls on this scale relative to these reference points. The mathematical formula is: HU = 1000 × (μ_tissue - μ_water) / (μ_water - μ_air), where μ is the linear attenuation coefficient of the tissue. The scale is absolute and reproducible — the same tissue should have the same HU value on any calibrated CT scanner in the world. This makes CT interpretation systematic and quantitative.

The full Hounsfield scale for tissues you must know:

| Tissue | HU Range |
|---|---|
| Air | -1000 |
| Fat | -80 to -120 |
| Water / CSF | 0 |
| Blood (acute) | +50 to +80 |
| Soft tissue (muscle) | +20 to +80 |
| Calcification | +100 to +400 |
| Compact bone | +400 to +1000 |
| Dense cortical bone | >1000 |

**Clinical connection — systematic HU application:** If you see a lesion on CT and want to characterise it, the HU value gives you the composition: Negative HU = fat (lipoma, angiomyolipoma, fatty liver). 0 HU = fluid (cyst, abscess, necrosis). +50-80 HU = acute blood (acute subdural hematoma, acute haemorrhage). >100 HU = calcification (calcified lymph node, gallstones, renal stones). This principle allows you to distinguish a simple renal cyst (0 HU, water-density, no enhancement with contrast) from a solid renal tumour (soft-tissue density, enhances with contrast) without a biopsy.

**Analogy:** Hounsfield Units are the CT's periodic table — a universal, quantitative language that converts subjective visual impression (light vs. dark grey) into an objective number, making diagnosis reproducible and systematic.

### CT Contrast — Iodinated Agents and the Enhancement Principle

Intravenous CT contrast agents contain iodine (Z = 53 — much higher than the Z of soft tissue elements). Iodine dramatically absorbs X-rays (photoelectric effect ∝ Z³, so iodine absorbs 53³/8³ ≈ 275 times more than oxygen). When iodinated contrast is injected, it distributes through the vascular system and then into the interstitial space. Tissues with rich blood supply (tumours, inflammation, vascular malformations) accumulate contrast and appear brighter (higher HU) on CT. This is contrast enhancement.

Multiphasic CT acquisition exploits the pharmacokinetics of contrast distribution. The arterial phase (20-30 seconds post-injection) shows arterially enhancing structures (hepatocellular carcinoma, hypervascular metastases, arteriovenous malformations). The portal venous phase (60-70 seconds) shows maximum hepatic parenchymal enhancement (because 75% of hepatic blood supply is portal venous). The delayed phase (3-5 minutes) shows cholangiocarcinoma, transitional cell carcinoma, and areas of ongoing contrast leak (hemorrhage). Understanding the physiology of contrast distribution makes multiphasic imaging logical rather than arbitrary.

## MRI Principles

### Protons, Magnetic Fields, and Relaxation — Why MRI Works

MRI exploits a fundamental quantum mechanical property of hydrogen nuclei (single proton) — nuclear spin. A proton behaves like a tiny bar magnet: it has a north and south pole, a magnetic moment. In the absence of an external magnetic field, these protons are randomly oriented. When placed in a strong external magnetic field (1.5 or 3 Tesla — tens of thousands of times stronger than the Earth's magnetic field), protons align with the field, like compass needles aligning with north. They precess (wobble) around the direction of the field at a specific frequency — the Larmor frequency — which is proportional to the field strength (Larmor equation: ω = γB₀, where γ is the gyromagnetic ratio of hydrogen, 42.58 MHz/Tesla).

A radiofrequency (RF) pulse at the Larmor frequency is then applied. This is resonance — the RF pulse is at exactly the right frequency to be absorbed by the precessing protons. The protons are "tipped" out of alignment with the main field — they absorb energy and their magnetic moments tip by 90° (or 180°, depending on the pulse). When the RF pulse is switched off, the protons return to their equilibrium alignment with the main field, releasing the absorbed energy as an RF signal that is detected by receiver coils. This emitted signal is what MRI measures. The spatial location of the signal is encoded by superimposing gradient magnetic fields — the Larmor frequency varies with position in these gradients, allowing mathematical reconstruction (Fourier transform) of a spatial image.

**Analogy:** Imagine spinning tops (protons) on a table. In a magnetic field, they all spin and wobble (precess) in the same direction. An RF pulse at the right frequency is like a precisely timed push that knocks all the tops sideways. When you stop pushing, the tops wobble back to upright, and as they do, they emit a ringing sound (the MRI signal). The quality and timing of that ringing sound tells you what kind of material you're dealing with.

### T1 and T2 Relaxation — The Source of MRI Contrast

The MRI signal is characterised by two independent relaxation time constants: T1 and T2. Understanding these from physics makes the "fat bright on T1, fluid bright on T2" rule not a rote fact but an inevitable consequence.

**T1 relaxation** (longitudinal relaxation, spin-lattice relaxation): After the RF pulse, how quickly do protons realign with the main magnetic field? This is T1. Protons give up their absorbed energy to the surrounding molecular lattice (neighbouring atoms and molecules). T1 is short when there is efficient energy transfer between the proton and the lattice — this occurs when molecular tumbling rates match the Larmor frequency. Fat molecules have a tumbling rate near the Larmor frequency → efficient energy transfer → short T1 → protons realign quickly → they are ready to emit a strong signal before the next RF pulse → FAT IS BRIGHT ON T1. Water molecules tumble very rapidly (much faster than the Larmor frequency) → inefficient energy transfer → long T1 → protons are slow to realign → they have not fully recovered before the next RF pulse → WATER/CSF IS DARK ON T1.

**T2 relaxation** (transverse relaxation, spin-spin relaxation): After the RF pulse, the protons are all precessing in phase (coherently). They gradually lose this phase coherence as they interact with each other (spin-spin interactions). T2 is how quickly this dephasing occurs. Free water has very slow dephasing (long T2 — water molecules move freely, interactions are averaged out) → WATER STAYS IN PHASE LONGER → BRIGHT ON T2. Fat has faster dephasing (shorter T2) → DARKER ON T2 (though still reasonably bright). Solid tissues have very short T2 (rigid lattice — strong spin-spin interactions, rapid dephasing) → DARK ON T2.

The clinical mnemonic: **"WW" — Water is White on T2. "FB" — Fat is Bright on T1.** Or the classic: "Bright blood on T1 is bold — Fat is bright; Fluid is bright on T2."

> **Key exam insight:** On T1: fat = bright white, fluid (CSF, urine, simple cysts) = dark. On T2: fluid = bright white, fat = intermediate, fibrous tissue / cortical bone = black. Post-gadolinium T1: enhancing structures (tumour, infection, inflammation) become brighter.

### Gadolinium Contrast — Why Tumours Light Up

Gadolinium (Gd) is a paramagnetic heavy metal (7 unpaired electrons — highest paramagnetism of any element). In its free form, Gd is toxic; it is chelated (bound to DTPA or similar ligands) for safe intravenous administration. Gadolinium shortens the T1 relaxation time of adjacent protons (the paramagnetic Gd creates local fluctuating magnetic fields that match the Larmor frequency, enhancing energy transfer → shorter T1 → brighter on T1-weighted images). Tissues that accumulate gadolinium enhance on T1.

Normal brain is protected by the blood-brain barrier (BBB) — tight junctions between endothelial cells prevent large molecules (including Gd-chelates) from entering the brain parenchyma. Therefore, normal brain does NOT enhance with gadolinium. Pathological processes that disrupt the BBB — tumours (primary CNS tumours, metastases), abscesses, active demyelination (acute MS plaques), encephalitis, and cerebral infarction (in the subacute phase, approximately days 3-14) — allow gadolinium to enter the brain parenchyma → T1 shortening → ring enhancement or solid enhancement of the pathological tissue.

**Clinical connection:** Ring-enhancing lesions on contrast-enhanced MRI brain have a differential diagnosis: brain abscess (rim enhancing — the enhancing ring represents the vascularised fibrous capsule of the abscess, surrounding the necrotic pus-filled centre), glioblastoma multiforme (irregular, thick enhancing ring — the ring represents the highly vascular proliferating tumour margin, the dark centre is necrosis), single metastasis (smooth, thin enhancing ring or solid enhancement), and cerebral toxoplasmosis in HIV patients (multiple ring-enhancing lesions — the ring is the zone of active inflammation around the central necrotic parasite-laden tissue).

## Radiological Signs and Their Pathological Basis

### Air Bronchogram — Seeing the Invisible

Normally, on a chest X-ray, you cannot see individual bronchi — they are air-filled tubes surrounded by air-filled alveoli, and both have the same density (black). There is no contrast between bronchus and alveolus, so the bronchus is invisible. Now consider what happens in consolidation — the alveoli fill with fluid (pneumonia exudate, pulmonary edema fluid, blood from hemorrhage). The alveoli change density from air (black) to fluid (grey-white). But the bronchi — which are connected to the patent airways — remain air-filled (black). Now there IS a density difference: black air-filled bronchi against grey-white fluid-filled alveoli. The bronchi become visible as dark tubular structures running through the white opacity. This is the air bronchogram sign.

![Chest X-ray showing dark air-filled bronchi as branching tubular lucencies within a white area of lobar consolidation — the air bronchogram sign](../images/air-bronchogram-consolidation.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look for branching dark (black) tubular streaks running through a white area of consolidation — the patent bronchi stand out against the fluid-filled alveoli. Contrast with collapse/atelectasis from an obstructed bronchus, where no air bronchogram is seen because the bronchus itself is also airless.

**The critical diagnostic implication of the air bronchogram:** Because the bronchi remain open and air-filled (connected to the patent central airway), the pathology is ALVEOLAR (the bronchi are not obstructed — the air space distal to the bronchi is filled with fluid/cells but the bronchi themselves are clear). Air bronchograms indicate: pneumonia (bacterial — most classic), pulmonary edema (if severe), pulmonary hemorrhage, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and lymphoma infiltrating the lung. Air bronchograms are NOT seen in: atelectasis from an obstructed bronchus (the bronchi collapse along with the alveoli — no air in the bronchus), pulmonary infarction (the alveoli are collapsed and airless), or pleural effusion.

> **Key exam insight:** Air bronchogram = patent airway with alveolar filling. It implies alveolar (airspace) disease, NOT obstructive collapse.

### Bat-Wing / Butterfly Pattern — The Physics of Pulmonary Edema Distribution

In pulmonary edema, fluid leaks from pulmonary capillaries into the alveolar interstitium and, when severe, into the alveolar lumen. One might expect this fluid to be evenly distributed throughout both lungs — but the bat-wing or butterfly distribution shows perihilar haziness with relative sparing of the peripheral lung. Why?

![Chest X-ray showing bilateral perihilar fluffy white haziness with peripheral lung sparing in acute pulmonary edema — the bat-wing or butterfly pattern](../images/bat-wing-opacity-pulmonary-edema.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look for bilateral, perihilar, fluffy white opacities that spare the costophrenic angles and peripheral lung — like outstretched bat wings. Contrast with lobar pneumonia, which is unilateral, peripheral, and touches the pleura, or with ARDS, which produces diffuse bilateral haziness without the central-sparing pattern.

The explanation is lymphatic drainage. The peripheral lung has a much richer lymphatic network than the central (perihilar) lung. Lymphatics actively pump fluid back from the interstitium into the venous circulation. The peripheral lung can therefore handle more interstitial fluid before it overflows into the alveoli. The perihilar regions, with fewer lymphatics, fill with fluid first. In addition, the distribution of pulmonary capillary pressure (related to gravity and pulmonary venous hypertension) and the geometry of the pulmonary venous circulation contribute to the preferential perihilar distribution.

**Analogy:** Think of the lung as a city, where the city centre (perihilar) has few drainage channels and floods easily, while the suburbs (periphery) have an extensive drainage network that copes with the overflow until it is overwhelmed.

**Clinical connection:** The bat-wing pattern is associated with cardiogenic pulmonary edema — particularly acute left heart failure, mitral stenosis, and fluid overload. Non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema (ARDS) typically produces diffuse, bilateral, uniform haziness without the bat-wing distribution, because the mechanism (inflammatory capillary leak) is diffuse and not governed by the same lymphatic gradient.

### Hampton's Hump and Westermark's Sign — Pulmonary Embolism

Pulmonary embolism (PE) is often called "the great masquerader" because the plain CXR is frequently normal or shows only subtle signs. Understanding the pathophysiology of each sign makes them memorable.

**Hampton's hump** is a wedge-shaped, pleural-based, dome-shaped opacity with its base against the pleural surface and its apex pointing toward the hilum. It represents pulmonary infarction — hemorrhagic infarction distal to an occluded pulmonary artery branch. When a pulmonary artery is embolised, the distal lung parenchyma loses its pulmonary arterial blood supply. In most patients, the bronchial arteries (systemic circulation) prevent true infarction. But in patients with pre-existing cardiac or pulmonary disease (where bronchial supply is compromised), infarction occurs. The infarcted segment is peripheral (because distal pulmonary arteries are peripheral) and pleural-based (the pleural surface is where peripheral emboli preferentially lodge). The dome shape is because the infarcted tissue collapses slightly but is held against the pleural surface, giving a convex (dome-shaped) superior border.

![Chest X-ray showing a pleural-based, dome-shaped white opacity in the lower zone with its convex border pointing inward toward the hilum — Hampton's hump sign of pulmonary infarction](../images/hamptons-hump-pe.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Fix your eyes on the peripheral lung adjacent to the pleura — a dome-shaped (convex superiorly) opacity with its flat base flush against the pleural surface is Hampton's hump. Contrast with a simple pleural effusion, which layers dependently and has a meniscus, and with pneumonia, which is not dome-shaped and is not strictly pleural-based.

**Westermark's sign** is the opposite: an area of focal oligaemia (decreased vascularity) distal to a large central pulmonary embolus — the lung field appears paradoxically hyperlucent (darker) compared to the contralateral lung, because fewer blood vessels are perfusing it (occluded blood flow = fewer blood-density vascular markings = darker area). This is a sign of a large, proximal PE.

![Chest X-ray showing focal hyperlucency (darker area) in one lung zone due to absent vascular markings distal to a large proximal pulmonary embolus — Westermark's sign](../images/westermark-sign-proximal-pe.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Compare both lung fields — a region that appears darker (more radiolucent) with visibly fewer vascular markings than the contralateral side indicates Westermark's sign. Contrast with a pneumothorax, which shows complete absence of lung markings with a visible lung edge, whereas Westermark's sign shows reduced (not absent) markings without a lung edge.

> **Key exam insight:** On CXR in PE — most common finding is a normal CXR or subtle changes. When present: Hampton's hump (hemorrhagic infarction — pleural-based dome opacity), Westermark's sign (oligaemia, hyperlucency), elevation of hemidiaphragm (ipsilateral to PE, from loss of lung volume due to atelectasis and surfactant depletion), and pleural effusion (exudative, from infarction). The definitive investigation is CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA).

### Kerley Lines — The Anatomy of Interstitial Edema

When pulmonary venous pressure rises (mitral stenosis, left heart failure), fluid accumulates in the pulmonary interstitium before entering the alveoli. The interstitium of the lung has a specific architecture: interlobular septa (connective tissue walls that separate secondary pulmonary lobules), bronchovascular bundles, and subpleural tissue. Interstitial edema distends these septa, making them visible as fine lines on the chest X-ray.

**Kerley B lines** are the most important: short (1-2 cm), horizontal, dense white lines at the lung bases, perpendicular to the pleural surface, extending to the pleural surface. They represent distended interlobular septa — which run horizontally in the lower lung bases. They are the radiological signature of pulmonary venous hypertension and interstitial edema. They appear when pulmonary capillary wedge pressure exceeds approximately 18-20 mmHg.

![Chest X-ray showing short horizontal dense white lines at the lung bases perpendicular to and reaching the pleural surface — Kerley B lines of interstitial pulmonary edema](../images/kerley-b-lines-interstitial-edema.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look at the costophrenic angles and lower lung zones — tiny (1-2 cm), horizontal, non-branching white lines touching the pleural surface are Kerley B lines. Contrast with vascular markings, which are longer, branching, and taper toward the periphery; Kerley B lines are shorter, non-branching, and always reach the pleura.

**Kerley A lines** are longer (2-6 cm), diagonal lines in the mid-upper zones — they represent edema in the deeper interstitial connective tissue strands that run diagonally from hilum to periphery. Kerley C lines are a reticular (net-like) pattern at the bases, representing a superimposition of Kerley B lines on a frontal projection.

![Chest X-ray showing longer diagonal white lines in the mid and upper lung zones extending from the hilum toward the periphery — Kerley A lines of advanced interstitial edema](../images/kerley-a-lines-interstitial-edema.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** In the mid-upper zones, look for longer (2-6 cm) oblique white lines that run diagonally from the hilum outward — these are Kerley A lines. They differ from Kerley B lines by their longer length, diagonal (not horizontal) orientation, and mid-upper (not basal) location.

## Bone Radiology — Periosteal Reactions and Their Meaning

### Understanding Periosteal Reactions from First Principles

The periosteum is a highly active, responsive tissue — a sleeve of connective tissue and osteoblasts that wraps around the cortex of every bone. Its osteoblastic layer continuously lays down new bone in response to mechanical stress, inflammation, or growth signals. When a disease process (tumour, infection, trauma) disrupts the bone or the periosteum, the periosteum responds by trying to lay down new bone to contain the threat. The pattern of this periosteal response tells you about the aggressiveness of the underlying process.

A slow-growing, benign process (e.g., a simple bone cyst or osteoid osteoma) allows the periosteum to lay down a thick, well-organised, smooth continuous shell of new bone over weeks to months — a thick, solid periosteal reaction. An aggressive process (malignant tumour, osteomyelitis) grows too fast for the periosteum to keep up — the periosteum is repeatedly lifted from the cortex, tries to lay down bone, is lifted again, producing interrupted, spiculated, or laminated periosteal patterns. The type of periosteal reaction is a radiological surrogate for the aggressiveness (tempo) of the underlying pathology.

### Codman's Triangle — Osteosarcoma

Osteosarcoma is the most common primary malignant bone tumour in children and young adults, typically arising in the metaphysis of long bones around the knee (distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus). It is an extremely aggressive tumour that grows rapidly, expanding outward through the cortex into the surrounding soft tissue.

As the tumour expands, it elevates the periosteum from the cortex. The periosteum is not idle — its osteoblastic inner layer continuously tries to lay down new bone to seal the expanding tumour. But the tumour grows faster than the periosteum can keep up. The periosteum is lifted progressively from the cortex outward, laying down a thin layer of new bone at its leading edge. At the margin — where the periosteum is still attached to the cortex before being lifted — a triangle of reactive new bone is formed at the angle between the newly elevated periosteum and the original cortex. This triangular bony spur at the margin of the periosteal elevation is Codman's triangle.

![X-ray of distal femur showing a triangular bony spur at the margin where the elevated periosteum meets the cortex — Codman's triangle in osteosarcoma](../images/codmans-triangle-osteosarcoma.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look at the metaphysis of a long bone — a triangular area of new bone at the edge of a periosteal elevation, forming an acute angle between the lifted periosteum and the original cortex, is Codman's triangle. Contrast with a benign periosteal reaction, which is a smooth, thick, uninterrupted shell without this angular elevation.

Codman's triangle is not specific to osteosarcoma — it indicates any aggressive process that elevates the periosteum rapidly (aggressive osteomyelitis, Ewing's sarcoma, subperiosteal haematoma). But in the context of a destructive metaphyseal lesion in a young patient, it strongly suggests osteosarcoma. Associated radiological features of osteosarcoma: mixed sclerotic-lytic lesion (tumour matrix may be osteoblastic — producing dense new bone — or osteolytic), aggressive periosteal reaction, "sunburst" pattern (fine radiating spicules of bone perpendicular to the cortex, produced by tumour bone following the pattern of blood vessels penetrating the periosteum).

![X-ray of long bone metaphysis showing fine radiating spicules of new bone extending perpendicularly from the cortex in a sunburst or sunray pattern — osteosarcoma](../images/sunburst-pattern-osteosarcoma.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look for thin, straight bony spicules radiating outward from the cortex like rays of a sun — the sunburst pattern is due to tumour bone mineralising along blood vessel channels penetrating the periosteum. Contrast with onion peel pattern (Ewing's), which shows concentric parallel layers along the shaft rather than perpendicular outward spicules.

> **Key exam insight:** Codman's triangle = periosteal elevation with new bone at the margin of elevation. Associated with osteosarcoma but NOT specific. "Sunburst" pattern = aggressive tumour matrix calcification in perpendicular spicules = more specific for osteosarcoma. Both can also be seen in Ewing's sarcoma.

### Onion Peel / Onion Skin Pattern — Ewing's Sarcoma

Ewing's sarcoma is the second most common primary malignant bone tumour in children, arising typically in the diaphysis (shaft) of long bones (and flat bones — ilium is a common site). It is caused by the EWS-FLI1 translocation t(11;22). Histologically, it consists of small round blue cells arranged in sheets (like many other paediatric small round blue cell tumours — the differential includes neuroblastoma, rhabdomyosarcoma, and lymphoma — requiring immunohistochemistry to distinguish).

The radiological hallmark is the onion peel (laminated) periosteal reaction. Ewing's sarcoma grows rapidly, repeatedly lifting the periosteum from the cortex in waves. Each time the periosteum is lifted, it attempts to lay down a new layer of bone. As the tumour continues expanding, the periosteum is lifted again, and another layer of bone is attempted. The result is multiple concentric layers of periosteal new bone, like the layers of an onion, visible on X-ray as parallel lines of density running along the shaft of the bone. Each layer represents one episode of periosteal elevation and attempted repair.

![X-ray of long bone diaphysis showing multiple concentric parallel layers of periosteal new bone along the shaft like layers of an onion — onion peel periosteal reaction in Ewing's sarcoma](../images/onion-peel-ewing-sarcoma.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look along the diaphysis (shaft) of a long bone — multiple thin, parallel, concentric lines of new bone layered along the cortex like onion rings is the onion peel pattern. Contrast with the sunburst pattern (osteosarcoma), which has perpendicular spicules radiating outward, not parallel layers along the shaft.

**Analogy:** Imagine repeatedly lifting a tablecloth slightly off a table (each lift = one wave of tumour expansion), and each time it is lifted, someone slides a thin cardboard sheet underneath before it is lowered again. After many episodes, you have a stack of thin cardboard sheets — the "onion layers."

The clinical presentation of Ewing's sarcoma includes pain (worse at night, like osteosarcoma), soft tissue swelling, and — importantly — systemic features: fever, elevated ESR, elevated CRP, leukocytosis. This systemic inflammation makes Ewing's sarcoma mimic osteomyelitis clinically, a classic diagnostic trap. The key differentiating point: bone biopsy and histology (small round blue cells + EWS-FLI1 translocation).

> **Key exam insight:** Onion peel / laminated periosteal reaction = Ewing's sarcoma (diaphysis of long bone or flat bone; diaphyseal location distinguishes it from osteosarcoma which is metaphyseal). Associated with t(11;22) translocation, small round blue cells on histology, and often accompanied by a large soft tissue mass.

## Special Radiological Signs — Systematic Catalogue

### Abdominal Radiology Signs

**Rigler's sign (double wall sign):** Normally, the inner wall of the bowel is visible (mucosal pattern outlined by intraluminal gas), but the outer wall is invisible (bowel wall is soft tissue density, adjacent to other soft tissue structures — no density difference). In pneumoperitoneum (free intraperitoneal gas), the outer wall of the bowel now has gas on both sides — intraluminally and in the peritoneal cavity. The outer wall of the bowel becomes visible as a thin white line between two black gas columns. This is Rigler's sign: you can see BOTH walls of the bowel, indicating free peritoneal gas. It indicates hollow viscus perforation until proven otherwise.

![Abdominal X-ray showing both the inner and outer walls of bowel loops visible as thin white lines outlined by gas on both sides — Rigler's sign of pneumoperitoneum](../images/riglers-sign-pneumoperitoneum.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look at bowel loops and focus on the outer wall — if you can see a thin white line on the outside of the bowel wall (not just the inside), you are seeing both walls simultaneously, which means free gas is present in the peritoneal cavity. Contrast with normal AXR where only the inner (mucosal) surface of gas-containing bowel is visible.

**Coffee bean sign (sigmoid volvulus):** In sigmoid volvulus, the massively dilated, twisted sigmoid colon appears on AXR as a large gas-filled oval with a midline crease (the torted mesenteric fold), pointing toward the right upper quadrant. The shape resembles a coffee bean. The apex of the "coffee bean" points away from the obstruction. The underlying mechanism is that the sigmoid colon twists on its mesenteric pedicle → obstruction → massive gas accumulation in the closed loop → the dilated sigmoid expands toward the right upper quadrant (where there is space).

![Abdominal X-ray showing a massively dilated gas-filled loop of sigmoid colon with a central midline crease pointing toward the right upper quadrant — the coffee bean sign of sigmoid volvulus](../images/coffee-bean-sign-sigmoid-volvulus.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look for a giant, bean-shaped gas shadow filling much of the abdomen with a visible central dense line (the torted mesenteric fold) — the apex points to the right upper quadrant. Contrast with caecal volvulus, where the dilated loop points to the left upper quadrant (away from its right-sided origin).

**String sign (Crohn's disease):** In Crohn's disease affecting the terminal ileum, severe transmural inflammation causes thickening of the bowel wall and luminal narrowing. On barium follow-through, the terminal ileum appears as a thin, thread-like column of barium — the "string sign" of Kantor. This represents high-grade luminal narrowing from inflammation and fibrosis. Associated features: cobblestone mucosa (from aphthous ulcers and intervening mucosal islands), skip lesions, rose-thorn ulcers, fistulae.

![Barium follow-through showing the terminal ileum as a thin, thread-like string of contrast with markedly narrowed lumen — string sign of Kantor in Crohn's disease](../images/string-sign-kantor-crohn.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** On barium follow-through, focus on the terminal ileum — a long, thin, string-like column of contrast (instead of a normal wide lumen) is the string sign of Kantor. Contrast with a normal terminal ileum which fills widely; also contrast with apple core lesion of colonic carcinoma, which is shorter, abrupt, and has shouldering at both ends.

**Thumb printing (ischaemic colitis):** Bowel wall oedema — from ischemia, inflammatory bowel disease, or infection — causes the haustra (normal outward projections of the colonic wall) to thicken and project inward, like thumbs indenting the lumen of the colon. On AXR or barium enema, these thick oedematous haustral folds appear as rounded, thumb-like projections into the lumen of the colon. The splenic flexure ("watershed zone" of the colon — watershed between superior mesenteric artery and inferior mesenteric artery territories) is the most common location for ischaemic colitis.

![Abdominal X-ray or barium enema showing rounded, thumb-like indentations projecting into the lumen of the colon from thickened oedematous haustral folds — thumbprinting in ischaemic colitis](../images/thumbprinting-ischaemic-colitis.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** Look along the colonic wall, particularly around the splenic flexure — rounded, regular indentations projecting inward into the lumen like thumbs pressed into clay are thumbprinting. Contrast with apple core carcinoma, which shows an abrupt, irregular, asymmetric narrowing at a single point rather than multiple rounded symmetric projections throughout a segment.

### Neurological Radiology Signs

**Crescent sign (avascular necrosis of femoral head):** In avascular necrosis (AVN) of the femoral head, the subchondral bone undergoes ischaemic collapse. A thin rim of necrotic bone separates from the viable subchondral cortex, with a thin layer of fluid or gas between them. On X-ray (or MRI), this appears as a thin, crescent-shaped lucency just beneath the subchondral cortex of the femoral head — the crescent sign. It is a sign of subchondral collapse and indicates advanced AVN. On MRI, the "double line sign" of AVN is seen on T2: a dark inner band (sclerotic margin of necrosis) and a bright outer band (reactive hyperemia/granulation tissue) at the interface between necrotic and viable bone.

![X-ray of hip showing a thin crescent-shaped lucent line just beneath the subchondral cortex of the femoral head — crescent sign of avascular necrosis](../images/crescent-sign-avn-femoral-head.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** On a pelvic or hip X-ray, look just beneath the subchondral surface of the femoral head — a thin, curved (crescent-shaped) lucency parallel to the joint surface indicates subchondral collapse in AVN. Contrast with osteoarthritis, which shows subchondral sclerosis and joint space narrowing without this subchondral lucent crescent.

![MRI of hip on T2 showing a dark inner band and bright outer band at the margin between necrotic and viable bone in the femoral head — double line sign of avascular necrosis](../images/double-line-sign-avn-mri.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** On T2 MRI of the femoral head, look for two parallel curved bands at the interface of the necrotic segment — the inner dark band (sclerosis) and the outer bright band (reactive hyperaemia/granulation tissue) constitute the double line sign. This is specific to AVN and is not seen in other causes of femoral head pathology such as Paget's disease or metastasis.

**Lemon sign and banana sign (neural tube defects on ultrasound):** In open spina bifida (myelomeningocele), the Chiari II malformation causes descent of the hindbrain through the foramen magnum. On antenatal ultrasound, two signs are characteristic: the Lemon sign — the frontal bones of the fetal skull are concave inward (scalloped), giving the skull a lemon shape (normally the skull is round or oval). The mechanism: descent of the hindbrain causes reduced intracranial CSF pressure → frontal bones are sucked inward. The Banana sign — the cerebellum is pulled through the foramen magnum and wraps around the brainstem in a banana shape (rather than the normal kidney/dumbbell shape). These two signs together have high sensitivity for open spina bifida on second-trimester anomaly scan.

![Antenatal ultrasound of fetal skull showing bilateral concave indentation of the frontal bones giving a lemon-shaped appearance — lemon sign of open spina bifida and Chiari II](../images/lemon-sign-spina-bifida-ultrasound.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** On second-trimester fetal head ultrasound, look at the shape of the skull in axial section — if the frontal bones bow inward (concave rather than convex), giving a lemon shape instead of the normal oval, this is the lemon sign. Contrast with a normal fetal skull, which has smooth, convex frontal contours.

![Antenatal ultrasound of fetal posterior fossa showing the cerebellum pulled through the foramen magnum and wrapping around the brainstem in a banana shape — banana sign of Chiari II malformation](../images/banana-sign-chiari-ii-ultrasound.jpg)
> **IBQ tip:** On axial ultrasound through the fetal posterior fossa, look at the shape of the cerebellum — a banana-shaped curved structure wrapping around the brainstem (instead of the normal bilobed kidney/dumbbell shape) is the banana sign. The cisterna magna is also obliterated. Contrast with a normal posterior fossa where the cerebellum has two visible lobes and the cisterna magna is visible as an anechoic space behind it.
