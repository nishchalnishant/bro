## PSYCHOTIC DISORDERS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 24-year-old male presents with auditory hallucinations, disorganized speech, and flat affect for the past 8 months. He had a 1-month prodrome of social withdrawal before the active symptoms began. There are no mood episodes. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Brief psychotic disorder
- B) Schizophreniform disorder
- C) Schizophrenia
- D) Schizoaffective disorder

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Schizophrenia requires at least 2 characteristic symptoms for ≥1 month AND total illness duration ≥6 months (including prodrome/residual phases). This patient's 8-month total course with 1-month active psychosis meets criteria. Brief psychotic disorder lasts 1 day to 1 month; schizophreniform disorder lasts 1–6 months; schizoaffective disorder requires a concurrent major mood episode with psychosis.

</details>

---

**Q2. A 22-year-old develops sudden-onset auditory hallucinations and paranoid delusions following his girlfriend's death 2 weeks ago. He had been fully functional before. There is no prior psychiatric history. The most appropriate diagnosis is:**
- A) Schizophrenia
- B) Brief psychotic disorder
- C) Schizophreniform disorder
- D) Acute stress reaction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Brief psychotic disorder is characterized by sudden onset of at least one positive psychotic symptom lasting ≥1 day but <1 month, often preceded by a psychosocial stressor. Full return to premorbid functioning is expected. Schizophrenia requires 6 months of illness; schizophreniform requires 1–6 months; acute stress reaction does not include frank psychosis.

</details>

---

**Q3. A 30-year-old woman with a history of depressive episodes presents with persecutory delusions, auditory hallucinations, and a concurrent major depressive episode for the past 3 months. The psychotic symptoms were also present for 2 weeks when she was not depressed. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) MDD with psychotic features
- B) Schizophrenia
- C) Schizoaffective disorder
- D) Bipolar I disorder with psychotic features

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Schizoaffective disorder requires: (1) a major mood episode concurrent with Criterion A schizophrenia symptoms, AND (2) delusions or hallucinations for ≥2 weeks in the absence of a major mood episode. The 2-week psychosis-only period distinguishes this from MDD or bipolar with psychotic features, where psychosis only occurs during mood episodes.

</details>

---

**Q4. Which of the following is a NEGATIVE symptom of schizophrenia?**
- A) Auditory hallucinations
- B) Grandiose delusions
- C) Alogia
- D) Thought insertion

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Negative symptoms reflect a diminution or absence of normal functions — the "5 As": Alogia (poverty of speech), Affective flattening, Avolition, Anhedonia, Attention deficits. Auditory hallucinations, grandiose delusions, and thought insertion are all positive symptoms (additions to normal experience). Negative symptoms respond poorly to typical antipsychotics.

</details>

**Q5. A 45-year-old married man firmly believes his wife is being unfaithful despite no evidence. He functions normally at work and has no other psychotic symptoms. This has persisted for 7 months. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Schizophrenia, paranoid type
- B) Delusional disorder, jealous type
- C) Paranoid personality disorder
- D) Schizoaffective disorder

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Delusional disorder features one or more non-bizarre delusions for ≥1 month with relatively preserved functioning and no other schizophrenia criteria met. The jealous subtype (Othello syndrome) involves delusion of infidelity. Schizophrenia requires additional Criterion A symptoms; paranoid personality disorder involves pervasive suspiciousness but not fixed delusions; schizoaffective requires a major mood episode.

</details>

## MOOD DISORDERS (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 35-year-old woman presents with 3 weeks of depressed mood, early morning awakening, inability to experience pleasure, weight loss of 4 kg, difficulty concentrating, and recurrent thoughts of death. She denies suicidal ideation. The minimum duration required to diagnose a major depressive episode is:**
- A) 1 week
- B) 2 weeks
- C) 1 month
- D) 6 months

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

A major depressive episode requires ≥5 of the SIGECAPS symptoms (Sleep disturbance, Interest loss, Guilt, Energy loss, Concentration difficulty, Appetite change, Psychomotor changes, Suicidal ideation) for at least 2 weeks, with depressed mood or anhedonia mandatory. This patient meets criteria. The 2-week threshold distinguishes MDD from shorter grief or adjustment reactions.

</details>

**Q7. Which of the following is an absolute indication for ECT in a psychiatric patient?**
- A) Mild depression not responding to one antidepressant trial
- B) Severe depression with active suicidal intent and refusal of oral medication
- C) Schizophrenia with predominantly negative symptoms
- D) Generalized anxiety disorder refractory to SSRIs

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

ECT is indicated when rapid response is needed (imminent suicide risk, severe self-neglect, stupor) or when medications are contraindicated (pregnancy) or have failed. Severe depression with suicidal intent and oral medication refusal is a classic absolute indication. ECT is not first-line for mild depression, predominantly negative schizophrenia, or anxiety disorders. ECT has no absolute medical contraindications.

</details>

**Q8. A 28-year-old has had recurrent hypomanic episodes (4 days each) alternating with major depressive episodes. He has never had a manic episode requiring hospitalization. The most appropriate diagnosis is:**
- A) Bipolar I disorder
- B) Bipolar II disorder
- C) Cyclothymia
- D) MDD with hypomanic features

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bipolar II disorder requires ≥1 major depressive episode AND ≥1 hypomanic episode (≥4 days, not requiring hospitalization, no psychosis), with NO lifetime manic episodes. Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode (≥7 days or requiring hospitalization). Cyclothymia involves subsyndromal hypomania and depressive symptoms for ≥2 years that never meet full episode criteria.

</details>

**Q9. A 32-year-old complains of persistent low-grade depressed mood for 3 years. He has two of the following: poor appetite, insomnia, low energy, low self-esteem. He has never had a major depressive episode. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Major depressive disorder, recurrent
- B) Dysthymia (Persistent depressive disorder)
- C) Cyclothymia
- D) Adjustment disorder with depressed mood

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Dysthymia (DSM-5: persistent depressive disorder) requires depressed mood on most days for ≥2 years in adults (≥1 year in children) with ≥2 additional symptoms (sleep, appetite, energy, self-esteem, concentration, hopelessness). No major depressive episode during the initial 2 years. Cyclothymia involves hypomanic AND depressive symptoms alternating. Adjustment disorder follows a clear psychosocial stressor within 3 months.

</details>

**Q10. A 40-year-old with treatment-resistant bipolar I disorder is being considered for lithium therapy. Which parameter requires monitoring at regular intervals after initiation?**
- A) Liver function tests and CBC
- B) Serum lithium levels, thyroid function, and renal function
- C) ECG and serum magnesium
- D) Chest X-ray and pulmonary function tests

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index (0.6–1.2 mEq/L for maintenance); regular monitoring of serum lithium levels is mandatory. Lithium causes nephrogenic diabetes insipidus and progressive renal impairment (monitor serum creatinine/eGFR). Hypothyroidism occurs in up to 40% (monitor TSH). Lithium toxicity presents with coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion, and seizures above 1.5 mEq/L. LFTs are monitored for valproate, not lithium.

</details>

## ANXIETY AND OCD (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 28-year-old IT professional presents with recurrent unexpected episodes of palpitations, sweating, trembling, and intense fear of dying that peak within 10 minutes and last about 20 minutes. He now avoids public transport fearing another attack. The first-line pharmacological treatment is:**
- A) Alprazolam
- B) Sertraline
- C) Propranolol
- D) Buspirone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Panic disorder is characterized by recurrent unexpected panic attacks with ≥1 month of anticipatory anxiety or avoidance behavior. First-line pharmacotherapy is SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram) or SNRIs; first-line psychotherapy is CBT (interoceptive exposure). Benzodiazepines (alprazolam) provide rapid relief but risk dependence and are not first-line for maintenance. Buspirone is used for GAD; propranolol manages performance anxiety only.

</details>

**Q12. A 25-year-old woman spends 3 hours/day checking that gas knobs are turned off, repeatedly washing hands until her skin is raw, and arranging objects symmetrically. She finds these behaviors distressing and recognizes them as excessive. This recognition is called:**
- A) Ego-syntonic nature of OCD
- B) Ego-dystonic nature of OCD
- C) Overvalued ideation
- D) Thought-action fusion

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

OCD obsessions and compulsions are ego-dystonic — the patient recognizes them as products of their own mind, finds them alien to their self-concept, and experiences distress. This distinguishes OCD from OCPD (obsessive-compulsive personality disorder), where rigidity and perfectionism are ego-syntonic (felt as reasonable and desirable). Overvalued ideation is a single, firmly held but not delusional belief; thought-action fusion is a cognitive distortion in OCD.

</details>

**Q13. A 22-year-old soldier returns from active combat. For the past 6 weeks, he has intrusive flashbacks, nightmares about the combat, avoids war movies, feels emotionally numb, and has exaggerated startle responses. He was exposed to a combat incident 2 months ago. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Acute stress disorder
- B) PTSD
- C) Adjustment disorder
- D) Complex PTSD

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

PTSD requires exposure to a traumatic event followed by: intrusion symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares), avoidance, negative alterations in cognition/mood, and hyperarousal, persisting for >1 month. Symptoms began >1 month after trauma, ruling out acute stress disorder (ASD: 3 days to 1 month post-trauma). Adjustment disorder lacks the specific intrusion/hyperarousal cluster. The diagnosis meets all PTSD criteria (DSM-5 requires symptoms in all 4 clusters for >1 month).

</details>

**Q14. A 19-year-old medical student is excessively worried about her grades, family finances, and personal health almost every day for 8 months. She cannot control the worry, feels restless, fatigued, has poor concentration, and muscle tension. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Panic disorder
- B) Social anxiety disorder
- C) Generalized anxiety disorder
- D) Illness anxiety disorder

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

GAD requires excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple domains (not just one specific trigger) for ≥6 months, with ≥3 somatic symptoms (restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulty, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance). This distinguishes GAD from social anxiety (worry limited to social evaluation) and panic disorder (discrete, unexpected panic attacks). First-line treatment: SSRIs/SNRIs + CBT; buspirone is an alternative.

</details>

**Q15. Which of the following combinations represents the first-line treatment for OCD?**
- A) TCA (clomipramine) + cognitive restructuring
- B) SSRI (fluoxetine/fluvoxamine) + ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention)
- C) MAOI + supportive psychotherapy
- D) Benzodiazepine + relaxation therapy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The first-line treatment of OCD is the combination of an SSRI and ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention — a specific form of CBT where the patient is exposed to the feared stimulus and prevented from performing the compulsion). SSRIs require higher doses and longer trials (10–12 weeks) in OCD than in depression. Clomipramine (TCA) is a second-line agent — equally effective but worse side-effect profile. MAOIs and benzodiazepines are not indicated.

</details>

## SUBSTANCE USE AND PERSONALITY (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 45-year-old man presents to casualty 48 hours after his last drink with coarse tremors, profuse sweating, disorientation, visual hallucinations, and a generalized tonic-clonic seizure. His pulse is 120/min, BP 160/90 mmHg. The immediate treatment of choice is:**
- A) Haloperidol
- B) Diazepam
- C) Naloxone
- D) Disulfiram

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Delirium tremens (DTs) is a life-threatening alcohol withdrawal syndrome peaking at 48–72 hours, characterized by autonomic instability, delirium, and seizures. Benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam, chlordiazepoxide) are the treatment of choice — they cross-react with alcohol at the GABA-A receptor, suppressing withdrawal. Haloperidol does not prevent seizures and lowers the seizure threshold. Naloxone reverses opioid overdose. Disulfiram is used for alcohol aversion therapy, not withdrawal.

</details>

**Q17. A 38-year-old presents with rhinorrhea, lacrimation, myalgia, piloerection, yawning, and diarrhea 16 hours after his last heroin use. He has dilated pupils and is extremely restless. The symptoms described are consistent with:**
- A) Opioid intoxication
- B) Opioid withdrawal
- C) Stimulant withdrawal
- D) Benzodiazepine withdrawal

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Opioid withdrawal presents with the "flu-like" syndrome: rhinorrhea, lacrimation, yawning, piloerection (goosebumps — hence "cold turkey"), mydriasis (dilated pupils), restlessness, myalgias, vomiting, and diarrhea. This contrasts with opioid intoxication (miosis, sedation, respiratory depression). Onset depends on opioid half-life (heroin: 6–12 hours). Clonidine (alpha-2 agonist) manages autonomic symptoms; methadone/buprenorphine for opioid substitution.

</details>

**Q18. A patient on disulfiram consumes alcohol and develops flushing, palpitations, nausea, vomiting, and hypotension within 30 minutes. The mechanism behind this reaction is:**
- A) Inhibition of alcohol dehydrogenase
- B) Inhibition of aldehyde dehydrogenase causing acetaldehyde accumulation
- C) Competitive antagonism at opioid receptors
- D) Enhancement of GABA-A receptor activity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Disulfiram inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), the enzyme that converts acetaldehyde to acetate. Acetaldehyde accumulation causes the disulfiram-ethanol reaction (DER): flushing, tachycardia, nausea, vomiting, hypotension. This aversive conditioning deters alcohol use. Alcohol dehydrogenase converts alcohol to acetaldehyde (disulfiram does NOT inhibit this step). Naltrexone (opioid receptor antagonist) and acamprosate are alternative alcohol deterrents.

</details>

**Q19. A 30-year-old woman with a history of self-harm, intense unstable relationships, identity disturbance, and impulsive behavior (reckless driving, binge eating) presents after a suicide attempt following a minor disagreement with her boyfriend. She has marked fear of abandonment. The personality cluster this belongs to is:**
- A) Cluster A (Odd/Eccentric)
- B) Cluster B (Dramatic/Emotional)
- C) Cluster C (Anxious/Fearful)
- D) This does not fit any personality disorder cluster

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Borderline personality disorder (BPD) belongs to Cluster B ("Wild, Bad, Dramatic") — along with antisocial, histrionic, and narcissistic PDs. Cluster B is characterized by dramatic, emotional, erratic behavior. The clinical features (identity disturbance, affective instability, self-harm, fear of abandonment, impulsivity) are hallmark BPD features. Cluster A (paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal); Cluster C (avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive PD).

</details>

**Q20. The CAGE questionnaire is used to screen for alcohol use disorder. Which of the following is NOT a component of the CAGE questionnaire?**
- A) Have you ever felt you should Cut down on your drinking?
- B) Have you ever felt Annoyed by people criticizing your drinking?
- C) Have you ever felt Guilty about drinking?
- D) Have you ever felt you needed a drink in the morning to Energize yourself?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

CAGE stands for: **C**ut down, **A**nnoyed, **G**uilty, **E**ye-opener (a morning drink to steady nerves, not "energize"). ## PSYCHOPHARMACOLOGY (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 28-year-old with treatment-resistant schizophrenia is started on clozapine. After 4 weeks, he develops a high-grade fever, sore throat, and oral ulcers. His WBC count is 1200/mm³. The most likely complication and the required action is:**
- A) Neuroleptic malignant syndrome — stop clozapine, give dantrolene
- B) Agranulocytosis — stop clozapine immediately, avoid rechallenge
- C) Tardive dyskinesia — reduce dose gradually
- D) Metabolic syndrome — add metformin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Clozapine-induced agranulocytosis occurs in 1–2% of patients and is potentially fatal. It is the most feared adverse effect of clozapine. Mandatory WBC monitoring: weekly for 6 months, biweekly for 6 months, then monthly. Stop clozapine immediately if ANC <500/mm³ (or WBC <3000/mm³) — rechallenge is absolutely contraindicated. Clozapine is reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia and suicidality in schizophrenia/schizoaffective disorder precisely because of this risk.

</details>

**Q22. A 35-year-old on haloperidol for 6 months develops involuntary repetitive movements of the tongue, lips, and jaw that persist even after the drug is stopped. This condition is:**
- A) Acute dystonia
- B) Drug-induced Parkinsonism
- C) Tardive dyskinesia
- D) Akathisia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Tardive dyskinesia (TD) is a late-onset movement disorder caused by chronic dopamine receptor blockade (D2), typically from typical antipsychotics, manifesting as repetitive orofacial movements (lip smacking, tongue protrusion), choreoathetoid limb movements. It can be irreversible even after stopping the drug. Treatment: switch to clozapine or quetiapine (low D2 blockade), or use valbenazine/deutetrabenazine. Acute dystonia (within hours-days) is reversible; drug-induced Parkinsonism (weeks) is reversible; akathisia is subjective motor restlessness.

</details>

**Q23. A patient on phenelzine (MAOI) for depression eats a tyramine-rich meal (aged cheese). Within 1 hour he develops a severe hypertensive crisis. Which of the following is the mechanism?**
- A) MAO inhibition prevents tyramine breakdown, causing norepinephrine surge
- B) Phenelzine competitively blocks norepinephrine reuptake
- C) Tyramine directly stimulates serotonin receptors
- D) MAO inhibition reduces dopamine metabolism causing excess dopamine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Normally, intestinal and hepatic MAO-A degrades dietary tyramine before it reaches systemic circulation. MAOIs irreversibly inhibit MAO, allowing tyramine to enter the bloodstream, where it acts as an indirect sympathomimetic — displacing norepinephrine from presynaptic vesicles, causing massive NE release and hypertensive crisis ("cheese effect"). This is managed with phentolamine (alpha-blocker) or nifedipine. MAOIs require a tyramine-free diet (no aged cheese, cured meats, fermented foods).

</details>

**Q24. A patient on citalopram is mistakenly given tramadol for pain. He presents with agitation, confusion, myoclonus, hyperreflexia, diaphoresis, and hyperthermia. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
- B) Serotonin syndrome
- C) Anticholinergic toxidrome
- D) Malignant hyperthermia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Serotonin syndrome is caused by excess serotonergic activity, typically from drug combinations (SSRI + tramadol, triptans, linezolid, MAOIs). The Hunter criteria triad: clonus/myoclonus + hyperreflexia + autonomic instability (hyperthermia, diaphoresis). Onset is rapid (within 24 hours). Treatment: stop offending drugs, cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist), supportive care. NMS (from dopamine blockers) has slower onset, lead-pipe rigidity, no myoclonus; malignant hyperthermia follows inhalational anesthetics.

</details>

**Q25. Lithium toxicity is most likely to be precipitated by which of the following?**
- A) Addition of an SSRI
- B) Addition of a thiazide diuretic
- C) Concurrent use of valproate
- D) High sodium diet

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Lithium is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule alongside sodium. Thiazide diuretics cause sodium depletion, leading to compensatory increased proximal tubular reabsorption of both sodium AND lithium — raising serum lithium to toxic levels. NSAIDs (reduce renal prostaglandins → reduce lithium excretion) and ACE inhibitors also raise lithium levels. High sodium diet INCREASES lithium excretion (protective). SSRIs combined with lithium risk serotonin syndrome but do not directly raise lithium levels. Valproate does not affect lithium pharmacokinetics.

</details>

## SPECIAL TOPICS (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. A 35-year-old man is charged with murder. His defense lawyer argues he did not know what he was doing was wrong at the time of the act due to a mental disorder. This defense is based on:**
- A) Diminished responsibility
- B) McNaughton's rule
- C) Durham rule
- D) Irresistible impulse test

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

McNaughton's rules (1843, House of Lords, UK — applicable in India): a person is not criminally responsible if, at the time of the act, due to a disease of the mind, they did not know the nature of the act OR did not know that what they were doing was wrong. In India, this is codified in Section 84 of the Indian Penal Code (IPC). Diminished responsibility (Section 2, Homicide Act 1957) reduces murder to manslaughter due to abnormal mental functioning — this is a UK-specific partial defense. The Durham rule ("but for" the mental disease) is an American doctrine.

</details>

**Q27. A 7-year-old boy is brought with a 12-month history of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity in both home and school settings. He cannot sit still, often interrupts, and forgets instructions. His IQ is normal. The diagnosis and first-line pharmacological treatment are:**
- A) Conduct disorder — risperidone
- B) ADHD — methylphenidate
- C) Autism spectrum disorder — aripiprazole
- D) Intellectual disability — haloperidol

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

ADHD requires inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms present in ≥2 settings (home + school) for ≥6 months, onset before age 12. First-line pharmacotherapy: methylphenidate (stimulant, dopamine/NE reuptake inhibitor). Non-stimulant alternative: atomoxetine (NE reuptake inhibitor). In India, methylphenidate is the most commonly used agent (controlled substance). Conduct disorder involves violation of others' rights/social norms; ASD has social communication deficits and restricted/repetitive behaviors.

</details>

**Q28. A 3-year-old child has no speech, does not make eye contact, does not respond to his name, insists on the same daily routine, and flaps his hands repeatedly when excited. He was a normal baby until 18 months. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Intellectual disability
- B) Selective mutism
- C) Autism spectrum disorder
- D) Childhood schizophrenia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is characterized by: (1) deficits in social communication and interaction (no eye contact, no response to name, absent speech), AND (2) restricted, repetitive behaviors (stereotypies like hand flapping, insistence on sameness). Onset is typically before 3 years. Regression after 18 months is recognized in ASD. Diagnosis is clinical; management is behavioral (ABA therapy). Intellectual disability lacks the social communication deficit pattern; childhood schizophrenia is extremely rare before age 12.

</details>

**Q29. Which of the following is the STRONGEST risk factor for completed suicide?**
- A) Female sex
- B) Previous suicide attempt
- C) Young age (18–25 years)
- D) Alcohol use alone without depression

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The single strongest predictor of completed suicide is a previous suicide attempt — it increases the risk 20–30-fold. Other high-risk factors include: male sex (completes more), older age, psychiatric illness (depression, schizophrenia, alcohol use disorder), hopelessness (Beck Hopelessness Scale), access to lethal means, social isolation, and chronic physical illness. Women attempt more (3:1), but men complete more (3:1). Young age alone is not the strongest factor; SAD PERSONS scale in clinical use.

</details>

**Q30. Which of the following is a key difference between ICD-11 and DSM-5 in psychiatric classification?**
- A) DSM-5 uses a purely categorical approach while ICD-11 is dimensional
- B) ICD-11 uses the term "persistent depressive disorder" while DSM-5 retains "dysthymia"
- C) ICD-11 includes a "gaming disorder" as a recognized diagnosis
- D) DSM-5 classifies Asperger syndrome as a separate entity from autism

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

ICD-11 (WHO, 2022) introduced "gaming disorder" (6C51) as a formal diagnosis under disorders due to addictive behaviors — a significant new addition relevant to NEET PG. DSM-5 does NOT recognize gaming disorder as a formal diagnosis (only lists "internet gaming disorder" in the appendix for further study). DSM-5 collapsed Asperger syndrome into ASD (not a separate entity). ICD-11 retained "dysthymia" terminology alongside persistent depressive disorder; DSM-5 uses "persistent depressive disorder." Both systems use dimensional specifiers now.

</details>
