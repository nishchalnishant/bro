## SHOCK & TRAUMA (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 28-year-old male is brought to the ER after a road traffic accident. BP is 80/50 mmHg, HR 130/min, RR 28/min, urine output 10 mL/hr, and the patient is confused. Which class of hemorrhagic shock does this represent?**
- A) Class I
- B) Class II
- C) Class III
- D) Class IV

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Class III hemorrhagic shock involves 30–40% blood loss (1500–2000 mL), producing BP drop, HR >120, RR 30–40, urine output 5–15 mL/hr, and altered mental status — all matching this patient. Class II causes tachycardia without BP drop; Class IV has no measurable BP and is immediately life-threatening. The one-liner: Class III = the first class where blood pressure falls.

</details>

**Q2. Which of the following is the most common cause of immediate (within minutes) death following major trauma?**
- A) Tension pneumothorax
- B) Airway obstruction
- C) Hemorrhage
- D) Cardiac tamponade

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Airway obstruction is the most common cause of immediate death in trauma — it kills in minutes, faster than hemorrhage or tension pneumothorax. ATLS prioritizes Airway (A) first for exactly this reason. Hemorrhage is the most common cause of preventable death but acts over a slightly longer timeframe. The one-liner: No airway = death in 4 minutes.

</details>

**Q3. A trauma patient has distended neck veins, absent breath sounds on the right, hypotension, and tracheal deviation to the left. Immediate management is?**
- A) Chest X-ray
- B) Needle decompression at 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line
- C) Pericardiocentesis
- D) Intubation and mechanical ventilation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

This is tension pneumothorax — a clinical diagnosis requiring immediate needle decompression before any imaging. The triad of absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation away from the affected side, and hemodynamic compromise distinguishes it from cardiac tamponade (which causes muffled heart sounds, not absent breath sounds). The one-liner: Tension pneumothorax = treat first, X-ray never.

</details>

**Q4. In cardiogenic shock, which hemodynamic profile is expected?**
- A) Low CVP, Low PCWP, High CO, Low SVR
- B) High CVP, High PCWP, Low CO, High SVR
- C) Low CVP, Low PCWP, Low CO, Low SVR
- D) High CVP, Low PCWP, High CO, Low SVR

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cardiogenic shock results from pump failure: the failing heart backs up blood (high CVP, high PCWP), reduces forward output (low CO), and triggers compensatory vasoconstriction (high SVR). Septic shock shows low SVR with high CO (distributive pattern). Hypovolemic shock shows low CVP/PCWP. The one-liner: Cardiogenic shock = everything backed up, nothing going forward.

</details>

**Q5. A 35-year-old with multiple stab wounds has BP 60/40, HR 140, muffled heart sounds, and distended neck veins. The most appropriate immediate intervention is?**
- A) Needle decompression
- B) Emergency thoracotomy
- C) Pericardiocentesis
- D) IV fluid bolus

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Beck's triad (hypotension + muffled heart sounds + distended neck veins) is pathognomonic for cardiac tamponade. Emergency pericardiocentesis is the immediate life-saving procedure. Needle decompression treats tension pneumothorax (which has absent breath sounds, not muffled heart sounds). The one-liner: Beck's triad = cardiac tamponade = pericardiocentesis.

</details>

## SURGICAL INFECTIONS & WOUND HEALING (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 60-year-old diabetic develops a spreading necrotizing infection of the perineum and scrotum with crepitus and foul-smelling discharge 5 days after perianal abscess drainage. The most important step in management is?**
- A) IV broad-spectrum antibiotics alone
- B) Hyperbaric oxygen therapy
- C) Urgent wide surgical debridement
- D) CT scan to define extent before surgery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Fournier's gangrene (necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum) requires immediate, aggressive surgical debridement — this is the single most important step. Antibiotics alone are insufficient; the necrotic tissue acts as a barrier. Hyperbaric oxygen is an adjunct, not primary treatment. CT scan should not delay surgery when the diagnosis is clinically obvious. The one-liner: Necrotizing fasciitis = cut first, scan never.

</details>

**Q7. Which of the following best describes the sequence of wound healing in primary intention?**
- A) Inflammation → Proliferation → Hemostasis → Remodeling
- B) Hemostasis → Inflammation → Proliferation → Remodeling
- C) Proliferation → Hemostasis → Inflammation → Remodeling
- D) Hemostasis → Proliferation → Inflammation → Remodeling

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Wound healing proceeds in an orderly sequence: Hemostasis (immediate clot formation) → Inflammation (days 1–4, neutrophils then macrophages) → Proliferation (days 4–21, fibroblasts, collagen, angiogenesis) → Remodeling (weeks to months, type III collagen replaced by type I). Macrophages are the most important cells in wound healing. The one-liner: Heal, Inflame, Proliferate, Remodel — HIPR.

</details>

**Q8. A patient develops a surgical site infection 8 days post-operatively. Pus is drained from the wound. The wound is left open and closes over 3 weeks. This is an example of which type of wound healing?**
- A) Primary intention
- B) Secondary intention
- C) Tertiary intention (delayed primary closure)
- D) Quaternary intention

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Secondary intention healing occurs when a wound is left open to granulate and close from the base up — typical after drainage of an infected wound. Primary intention = closure within 6–8 hours; Tertiary intention (delayed primary) = deliberately left open then closed at 4–5 days. The one-liner: Infected wound left open to granulate = secondary intention.

</details>

**Q9. The drug of choice for surgical prophylaxis in clean-contaminated abdominal surgery is?**
- A) Metronidazole alone
- B) Cefazolin alone
- C) Cefazolin + Metronidazole
- D) Piperacillin-tazobactam

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Clean-contaminated abdominal surgery (e.g., elective bowel surgery) requires coverage for both gram-negative aerobes (cefazolin) and anaerobes (metronidazole). Cefazolin alone is adequate for clean surgeries (hernia, mastectomy). Piperacillin-tazobactam is reserved for treatment, not prophylaxis. The one-liner: Bowel surgery prophylaxis = cefazolin + metronidazole.

</details>

**Q10. Which cell type is most responsible for collagen synthesis in wound healing and also produces the maximum tensile strength in a healing wound?**
- A) Neutrophil
- B) Macrophage
- C) Fibroblast
- D) Myofibroblast

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Fibroblasts are the primary producers of collagen (types I and III) in wound healing and are responsible for tensile strength. Neutrophils debride bacteria; macrophages orchestrate healing via cytokines (most important regulatory cell); myofibroblasts cause wound contraction. Maximum tensile strength (70–80% of original) is achieved at 3 months but never returns to 100%. The one-liner: Fibroblast = collagen factory = tensile strength.

</details>

## FLUIDS & ELECTROLYTES (Q11–Q12)

**Q11. A post-operative patient develops confusion, seizures, and serum sodium of 118 mEq/L. The rate of correction of sodium should not exceed?**
- A) 1 mEq/L/hr; max 8–10 mEq/L in first 24 hours
- B) 2 mEq/L/hr; max 20 mEq/L in 24 hours
- C) 0.5 mEq/L/hr; max 4 mEq/L in 24 hours
- D) 3 mEq/L/hr; max 25 mEq/L in 24 hours

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Rapid correction of chronic hyponatremia risks osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis). Safe correction: ≤1–2 mEq/L/hr acutely, and ≤8–10 mEq/L in the first 24 hours (≤18 mEq/L in 48 hours). Symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures) warrants 3% saline but with strict rate limits. The one-liner: Chronic hyponatremia — correct slowly or cause osmotic demyelination.

</details>

**Q12. Which intravenous fluid is the most appropriate initial resuscitation fluid for a patient in hypovolemic shock due to hemorrhage?**
- A) 5% Dextrose
- B) 0.45% Normal saline
- C) Ringer's lactate
- D) 3% Hypertonic saline

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Ringer's lactate (Hartmann's solution) is the preferred crystalloid for initial resuscitation in hemorrhagic shock — it is isotonic, lactate is metabolized to bicarbonate, and it is less likely to cause hyperchloremic acidosis than normal saline. Dextrose provides no volume expansion. 3% saline is for severe hyponatremia. The one-liner: Hemorrhagic shock initial fluid = Ringer's lactate.

</details>

## BREAST (Q13–Q14)

**Q13. A 45-year-old woman has a breast lump, bloody nipple discharge, and mammography shows a 1.5 cm spiculated mass with microcalcifications. The most appropriate next step is?**
- A) Fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC)
- B) Core needle biopsy (Tru-cut biopsy)
- C) Excision biopsy
- D) Repeat mammography after 6 months

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Core needle biopsy (CNB) is the investigation of choice for a suspicious breast mass — it provides histological diagnosis with receptor status (ER, PR, HER2), which is essential for treatment planning. FNAC gives cytology only (no architecture, no receptor status). The triple assessment (clinical + imaging + pathology) is standard; CNB is preferred over FNAC for definitive diagnosis. The one-liner: Breast lump IOC = core needle biopsy (histology + receptors).

</details>

For lactational breast abscess, current evidence supports ultrasound-guided aspiration with antibiotics as first-line management. Breastfeeding should be continued (or milk expressed) to prevent milk stasis and engorgement. Incision and drainage is reserved for large or recurrent abscesses, or those failing aspiration. The one-liner: Lactational breast abscess = US-guided aspiration + continue breastfeeding.

## THYROID SURGERY (Q15–Q16)

**Q15. A patient undergoes total thyroidectomy. On post-op day 2, she develops perioral tingling, positive Chvostek's sign, and carpopedal spasm. Which complication has occurred, and what is the immediate treatment?**
- A) Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy; voice rest
- B) Hypoparathyroidism; IV calcium gluconate
- C) Thyroid storm; propranolol and PTU
- D) Hematoma; surgical re-exploration

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia from inadvertent parathyroid damage presents with perioral tingling, positive Chvostek's/Trousseau's signs, and tetany. Immediate treatment is IV calcium gluconate (10 mL of 10% solution over 10 minutes). Chvostek's sign (facial twitch on tapping the facial nerve) and Trousseau's sign (carpal spasm with BP cuff inflation) are hallmarks. The one-liner: Post-thyroidectomy tetany = hypoparathyroidism = IV calcium gluconate.

</details>

**Q16. Which nerve, if injured during thyroidectomy, causes hoarseness of voice?**
- A) External branch of superior laryngeal nerve
- B) Recurrent laryngeal nerve
- C) Hypoglossal nerve
- D) Glossopharyngeal nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) supplies all intrinsic laryngeal muscles except the cricothyroid. Unilateral RLN injury causes hoarseness; bilateral injury causes respiratory distress (both vocal cords in adduction). The external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve (cricothyroid muscle) injury causes loss of high-pitched phonation and voice fatigue, not frank hoarseness. The one-liner: RLN injury = hoarseness; bilateral = stridor.

</details>

## GI SURGERY (Q17–Q19)

**Q17. A 55-year-old male presents with progressive dysphagia — first to solids then to liquids — with significant weight loss. Barium swallow shows a "rat-tail" (shouldering) appearance in the mid-esophagus. Most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Achalasia cardia
- B) Carcinoma of the esophagus
- C) Esophageal web
- D) Pharyngeal pouch

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Progressive dysphagia from solids to liquids with weight loss and a "rat-tail/shouldering" appearance on barium swallow is classic for carcinoma of the esophagus (squamous cell carcinoma in mid-esophagus). Achalasia shows "bird-beak" appearance with dysphagia to solids and liquids simultaneously (no progression). The one-liner: Progressive dysphagia solid→liquid + rat-tail = esophageal carcinoma.

- A) Stomach
- B) Duodenum
- C) Appendix
- D) Rectum

<details>

## LIVER, BILIARY & PANCREAS (Q20–Q22)

**Q20. A 40-year-old obese woman presents with recurrent right hypochondrial pain after fatty meals, fever, and jaundice. Ultrasound shows gallstones with a distended gallbladder and common bile duct dilation. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Acute cholecystitis
- B) Choledocholithiasis with cholangitis
- C) Carcinoma of the gallbladder
- D) Primary sclerosing cholangitis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Charcot's triad — right hypochondrial pain + fever + jaundice — is diagnostic of acute cholangitis due to choledocholithiasis. CBD dilatation on ultrasound confirms CBD obstruction. Acute cholecystitis has Murphy's sign but not jaundice. Reynolds' pentad (Charcot's triad + hypotension + confusion) indicates severe/suppurative cholangitis. The one-liner: Charcot's triad = pain + fever + jaundice = cholangitis.

</details>

**Q21. A 50-year-old alcoholic develops severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, and vomiting. Serum amylase is 1200 U/L. CT abdomen on day 3 shows >30% pancreatic necrosis. Which scoring system best predicts severity of acute pancreatitis on CT?**
- A) Ranson's criteria
- B) APACHE II score
- C) Balthazar (CT Severity Index)
- D) Glasgow score

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The Balthazar CT Severity Index (CTSI) combines the Balthazar grade (A–E based on pancreatic and peripancreatic inflammation) with the degree of pancreatic necrosis to predict morbidity and mortality of acute pancreatitis on CT. Ranson's criteria use clinical/lab parameters at admission and 48 hours. APACHE II is used for ICU severity. The one-liner: CT severity of pancreatitis = Balthazar (CT Severity Index).

</details>

**Q22. A 70-year-old male presents with painless progressive jaundice, weight loss, and a palpable non-tender gallbladder. The most likely diagnosis is?**
- A) Choledocholithiasis
- B) Carcinoma of the head of pancreas
- C) Hepatocellular carcinoma
- D) Cholangiocarcinoma at the hilum

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Courvoisier's law: a palpable, non-tender gallbladder with obstructive jaundice is unlikely due to gallstones (chronic inflammation makes the gallbladder fibrotic and non-distensible) and is more likely due to periampullary or pancreatic head carcinoma. Painless jaundice + Courvoisier's gallbladder is the classic presentation of carcinoma head of pancreas. The one-liner: Courvoisier's sign = palpable non-tender GB + jaundice = pancreatic head carcinoma.

</details>

## VASCULAR SURGERY (Q23–Q24)

**Q23. A 65-year-old male smoker with hypertension complains of calf pain on walking 200 meters that is relieved by rest. ABI (ankle-brachial index) is 0.6. What is the diagnosis and the ABI threshold for critical limb ischemia?**
- A) Intermittent claudication; ABI <0.4
- B) Buerger's disease; ABI <0.5
- C) Intermittent claudication; ABI <0.5 indicates critical ischemia
- D) Acute limb ischemia; ABI <0.3

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

ABI 0.5–0.9 = intermittent claudication; ABI <0.5 (or <0.4 by some criteria) = critical limb ischemia (rest pain, ulcers, gangrene). This patient with exertional calf pain relieved by rest has classic intermittent claudication. Buerger's disease affects young smokers but presents differently (migratory thrombophlebitis). The one-liner: ABI <0.5 = critical limb ischemia; 0.5–0.9 = claudication.

</details>

- A) >4 cm
- B) >4.5 cm
- C) >5.5 cm
- D) >6.5 cm

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

- A) Intravenous urography (IVU)
- B) Ultrasound KUB
- C) Non-contrast CT KUB (NCCT)
- D) MRI urogram

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Non-contrast CT KUB (NCCT) is the gold standard for suspected ureteric colic — it detects >95% of stones (including radiolucent uric acid stones missed on plain X-ray), assesses obstruction, and excludes other diagnoses. Ultrasound is first-line in pregnancy and children (radiation-free). IVU has largely been replaced by NCCT. The one-liner: Ureteric colic IOC = NCCT KUB (gold standard).

## HERNIAS (Q27–Q28)

**Q27. A 55-year-old male has a hernia that emerges medial to the inferior epigastric vessels, through Hesselbach's triangle. It is unlikely to strangulate. What type of hernia is this?**
- A) Indirect inguinal hernia
- B) Direct inguinal hernia
- C) Femoral hernia
- D) Spigelian hernia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Direct inguinal hernias pass through Hesselbach's triangle (medial to inferior epigastric vessels, lateral to rectus abdominis, above inguinal ligament) and have a wide neck — making strangulation rare. Indirect hernias pass lateral to inferior epigastric vessels through the deep inguinal ring and have a narrow neck (higher strangulation risk). The one-liner: Direct = medial to inferior epigastric vessels = wide neck = rarely strangulates.

</details>

- A) Direct inguinal hernia
- B) Indirect inguinal hernia
- C) Femoral hernia
- D) Obturator hernia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Femoral hernias have the highest rate of strangulation (up to 40%) due to the rigid femoral ring (bounded by inguinal ligament, lacunar ligament, femoral vein, and pectineal ligament). They are more common in women (though inguinal hernias are still most common overall in women). The one-liner: Femoral hernia = most likely to strangulate among groin hernias.

## BURNS (Q29–Q30)

- A) 27%

**Q30. A 40-year-old male with 40% TBSA burns is being resuscitated using the Parkland formula. His weight is 70 kg. What volume of Ringer's lactate should be given in the first 8 hours from the time of burn?**
- A) 2800 mL
- B) 5600 mL
- C) 11200 mL
- D) 4000 mL

</details>

## THYROID SURGERY (Q31–Q35)

**Q31. A 40-year-old woman presents with a solitary thyroid nodule 2 cm in diameter. Thyroid function tests are normal. Ultrasound shows a hypoechoic solid nodule with microcalcifications and irregular margins. FNAC shows nuclear grooves, intranuclear pseudoinclusions, and psammoma bodies. The most likely diagnosis and recommended surgery is:**
- A) Follicular adenoma; hemithyroidectomy
- B) Papillary thyroid carcinoma; total thyroidectomy
- C) Medullary thyroid carcinoma; total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection
- D) Anaplastic carcinoma; palliative chemoradiotherapy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) is the most common thyroid malignancy. FNAC findings are pathognomonic: nuclear grooves, Orphan Annie eye nuclei (intranuclear pseudoinclusions), and psammoma bodies. Microcalcifications and hypoechoic nodule with irregular margins on US are high-risk features (TIRADS 5). Total thyroidectomy is the standard for tumors >1 cm, allowing subsequent radioiodine ablation and thyroglobulin monitoring. The one-liner: papillary thyroid carcinoma = psammoma bodies + Orphan Annie nuclei + total thyroidectomy.

</details>

**Q32. A 50-year-old woman undergoes total thyroidectomy for papillary thyroid carcinoma. Two hours post-operatively she develops perioral numbness, tingling in the fingers, and a positive Chvostek's sign. Serum calcium is 6.8 mg/dL. The most likely cause and treatment is:**

**Correct: B**

**Q33. A 45-year-old man has a thyroid nodule with elevated serum calcitonin (850 pg/mL). FNAC shows amyloid deposits in the stroma with polygonal cells in a nested pattern. Genetic testing reveals a RET proto-oncogene mutation. The recommended surgery includes:**
- A) Hemithyroidectomy alone
- B) Total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection + bilateral lateral neck dissection
- C) Total thyroidectomy alone
- D) Radioiodine ablation followed by thyroidectomy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) arises from parafollicular C cells and secretes calcitonin (the diagnostic marker). Amyloid deposits on FNAC (from calcitonin precursors) are characteristic. MTC is NOT iodine-avid (no role for radioiodine). RET mutation indicates MEN2. Surgery is total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection; lateral dissection if lateral nodes involved. Prophylactic thyroidectomy is offered to RET mutation carriers. The one-liner: medullary thyroid carcinoma = calcitonin marker + RET mutation + total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection.

</details>

**Q34. A 60-year-old man presents with a rapidly enlarging thyroid mass, hoarseness, and dysphagia over 6 weeks. The mass is fixed to adjacent structures. CT shows local invasion of trachea. FNAC reveals undifferentiated spindle and giant cells. Which statement about this tumor is correct?**
- A) 5-year survival is 70% with total thyroidectomy
- B) It is iodine-avid and responds well to radioiodine
- C) Median survival is <6 months even with aggressive treatment
- D) Chemotherapy with cisplatin is curative in 50% of cases

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Anaplastic (undifferentiated) thyroid carcinoma is the most aggressive thyroid malignancy. It presents in older patients with rapidly enlarging neck mass, local invasion (trachea, esophagus), and early distant metastases. Median survival is 3–6 months; virtually all patients die within 1 year. It is not iodine-avid (FNAC shows undifferentiated cells — no follicular structure). Treatment is palliative (chemo-radiation). The one-liner: anaplastic thyroid carcinoma = worst prognosis, <6 months median survival, NOT radioiodine-avid.

</details>

**Q35. Which investigation is the gold standard to differentiate follicular adenoma from follicular carcinoma of the thyroid?**
- A) FNAC showing microfollicular pattern
- B) Serum thyroglobulin level
- C) Histopathological examination of the capsule for vascular/capsular invasion
- D) PET-CT scan

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

FNAC cannot distinguish follicular adenoma from follicular carcinoma — both show similar microfollicular patterns. The diagnosis of follicular carcinoma requires histopathological evidence of capsular invasion and/or vascular invasion on the excised specimen. This is why hemithyroidectomy (diagnostic excision) is performed first, and completion thyroidectomy done if carcinoma is confirmed. The one-liner: follicular adenoma vs. carcinoma = ONLY distinguished by capsular/vascular invasion on histology — FNAC cannot differentiate.

</details>
