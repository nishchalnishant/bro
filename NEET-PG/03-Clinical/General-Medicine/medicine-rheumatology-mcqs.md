## RHEUMATOID ARTHRITIS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 38-year-old woman has symmetric painful swelling of MCPs, PIPs, and wrists for 8 weeks with morning stiffness >1 hour. X-ray shows periarticular osteoporosis. RF is low-positive, anti-CCP is high-positive. ESR is elevated. Using ACR/EULAR 2010 criteria, what is her score?**
- A) Score 4 — not RA
- B) Score 6 — satisfies RA classification criteria
- C) Score 3 — possible RA, needs further investigation
- D) Score 8 — definite RA with erosions

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — ACR/EULAR 2010 criteria (score ≥6 = RA): Joints: >10 joints including at least 1 small (3 pts = MCP, PIP, wrists) → 5 pts; Serology: high-positive anti-CCP → 3 pts; Acute-phase reactants: elevated ESR → 1 pt; Duration: ≥6 weeks → 1 pt. Total = 10 pts (score ≥6 = RA). Anti-CCP (specificity ~95%) is more specific than RF (IgM anti-IgG Fc; positive in 80% of RA but also in SLE, Sjogren's, endocarditis). Anti-CCP precedes RA onset by years.

</details>

**Q2. A 42-year-old woman with RA is started on methotrexate (MTX). What is the mechanism of action, important supplementation required, and monitoring parameter?**
- A) Inhibits TNF-alpha; calcium supplementation; renal function tests
- B) Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase → blocks purine synthesis; folic acid supplementation; liver function tests
- C) Inhibits IL-6 receptor; no supplementation; blood counts
- D) Blocks T-cell co-stimulation; vitamin D supplementation; echocardiography

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Methotrexate (first-line DMARD in RA): inhibits dihydrofolate reductase → impairs folate metabolism → reduces purine/pyrimidine synthesis → antiproliferative. Folic acid (5 mg/week, not on MTX day) is given to prevent mucositis and hepatotoxicity — does NOT reduce MTX efficacy. Monitor: LFTs (hepatotoxicity, contraindicated in liver disease/alcohol), renal function (methotrexate renally cleared), CBC (myelosuppression), and pulmonary function (MTX pneumonitis).

</details>

**Q3. A 50-year-old man with longstanding seropositive RA develops splenomegaly and recurrent infections. CBC shows neutropenia (ANC 900/mm³). What is this triad called?**
- A) Reiter's syndrome
- B) Felty's syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia)
- C) Still's disease
- D) Caplan's syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Felty's syndrome: occurs in longstanding seropositive RA. The triad is: Rheumatoid Arthritis + Splenomegaly + Neutropenia. Neutropenia leads to recurrent bacterial infections. Associated with HLA-DR4. Treatment: treat underlying RA (MTX, biologics); G-CSF for severe neutropenia; splenectomy rarely. Caplan's syndrome (D): RA + pneumoconiosis (multiple pulmonary nodules in coal workers/silica-exposed patients). Still's disease = systemic JIA in children (or adult-onset Still's = AOSD).

</details>

**Q4. A 45-year-old woman with RA refractory to 2 DMARDs (MTX + hydroxychloroquine) is started on adalimumab (anti-TNF). Which screening is MANDATORY before starting any biologic?**
- A) Serum uric acid
- B) Latent TB screening (TST or IGRA) + CXR
- C) Anti-CCP levels
- D) Echocardiography

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Before starting any biologic (especially anti-TNF agents), latent TB must be screened and treated. Anti-TNF therapy can reactivate latent TB. Screening: tuberculin skin test (TST/Mantoux) or IGRA (QuantiFERON-TB Gold) + CXR. If latent TB confirmed, treat with INH for 9 months before starting biologic (or at least 1 month pre-biologic). Also screen for hepatitis B (reactivation risk). DAS28 score: <2.6 = remission; >5.1 = high activity (target for biologic initiation).

</details>

**Q5. A 52-year-old RA patient has atlantoaxial subluxation discovered on cervical spine MRI during pre-anesthetic assessment. What is the most important concern?**
- A) Risk of vertebral artery thrombosis
- B) Risk of spinal cord compression during neck manipulation/intubation
- C) Risk of cervical lymphadenopathy
- D) Risk of atlantoaxial fusion causing rigidity only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Atlantoaxial subluxation (C1–C2) is a serious extra-articular complication of RA due to erosion of the transverse ligament of the atlas. The odontoid process can compress the spinal cord during neck flexion — especially dangerous during intubation (neck hyperextension) or procedures involving neck movement. Patient may present with neck pain, upper limb weakness, myelopathic signs. Preoperative cervical spine X-rays (flexion/extension views) are required. Awake fiberoptic intubation may be needed.

</details>

## SYSTEMIC LUPUS ERYTHEMATOSUS (Q6–Q9)

**Q6. A 25-year-old woman has malar rash, oral ulcers (painless), non-erosive arthritis, pleuritis, hemolytic anemia, and proteinuria 1.2 g/day. ANA is positive (1:640). Anti-dsDNA is strongly positive. Which antibody is MOST SPECIFIC for SLE (though less sensitive) and which one BEST correlates with disease activity?**
- A) ANA — most specific; anti-Sm — correlates with activity
- B) Anti-Sm — most specific; anti-dsDNA — correlates with disease activity and nephritis
- C) Anti-dsDNA — most specific; anti-Ro — correlates with activity
- D) Anti-histone — most specific; ANA — correlates with activity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — ANA is the most SENSITIVE (95–99%) but not specific. Anti-Sm (anti-Smith) is the most SPECIFIC for SLE (specificity ~99%, sensitivity only 25–30%) — positive anti-Sm = SLE until proven otherwise. Anti-dsDNA (specificity ~70%): correlates with disease activity and lupus nephritis activity (levels rise during flares, fall in remission). Anti-histone (D): drug-induced lupus (procainamide, hydralazine, isoniazid). Anti-Ro/SSA: neonatal lupus, congenital heart block, Sjogren's.

</details>

**Q7. A 28-year-old SLE patient undergoes renal biopsy. It shows diffuse proliferative glomerulonephritis (>50% glomeruli involved) with "wire-loop" lesions on light microscopy and subendothelial immune deposits on EM. What ISN/RPS class is this and what is the treatment?**
- A) Class II — mesangial proliferative; hydroxychloroquine alone
- B) Class III — focal proliferative; low-dose steroids
- C) Class IV — diffuse proliferative; cyclophosphamide (Eurolupus) or MMF + steroids
- D) Class V — membranous; ACEi and steroids only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — ISN/RPS Class IV (diffuse proliferative LN): >50% of glomeruli involved, "wire-loop" lesions (massive subendothelial deposits), most severe and common form. Treatment: induction = mycophenolate mofetil (MMF, preferred — less toxic) OR cyclophosphamide (Eurolupus low-dose regimen: 500 mg IV q2 weeks × 6 doses) + high-dose steroids. Maintenance: MMF or azathioprine. Hydroxychloroquine should be given to all SLE patients (reduces flares and mortality). Belimumab (anti-BLyS) for non-renal active SLE.

</details>

**Q8. A 30-year-old woman has three first-trimester miscarriages. Her aPTT is prolonged (65 sec), but adding normal plasma DOES NOT correct it. Lupus anticoagulant is positive on two occasions 14 weeks apart. Anticardiolipin IgG is also positive. What is the paradox and treatment for obstetric APLS?**
- A) aPTT prolonged in lab → hemorrhagic in vivo; anticoagulation for 3 months
- B) aPTT prolonged in lab (LA interferes with phospholipid-dependent clotting tests) → THROMBOTIC in vivo; low-dose aspirin + LMWH throughout pregnancy
- C) aPTT normal → thrombotic; warfarin INR 3–4
- D) aPTT prolonged → thrombotic; no treatment needed during pregnancy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — APLS paradox: antiphospholipid antibodies prolong aPTT IN VITRO (lupus anticoagulant interferes with phospholipid-dependent coagulation tests) but cause THROMBOSIS in vivo (by activating endothelium, complement, and platelets). Obstetric APLS (pregnancy loss/morbidity): low-dose aspirin + LMWH throughout pregnancy. Thrombotic APLS: long-term warfarin (INR 2–3; INR 3–4 for recurrent arterial events). Diagnosis requires positive test on 2 occasions ≥12 weeks apart.

</details>

**Q9. A 32-year-old woman with SLE is taking long-term hydroxychloroquine. Which monitoring is required because of the drug's potential toxicity?**
- A) Annual liver function tests
- B) Annual fundoscopy/visual field testing for retinal toxicity (hydroxychloroquine maculopathy)
- C) Monthly CBC for neutropenia
- D) Annual echocardiogram

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Hydroxychloroquine can cause retinal toxicity (hydroxychloroquine maculopathy) — early changes affect perifoveal cone photoreceptors. Annual ophthalmological screening with fundoscopy, automated visual fields, and SD-OCT is recommended, especially after 5 years of use and in high-risk patients (high dose, renal/liver disease). Maximum safe dose: ≤5 mg/kg/day. Also used for skin disease in RA (monitor for maculopathy). No routine CBC or LFT monitoring needed for HCQ alone.

</details>

## GOUT (Q10–Q13)

**Q10. A 52-year-old man presents with an acutely swollen, red, hot first MTP joint (podagra). Serum uric acid is 9.4 mg/dL. Joint aspiration shows negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals. What is the FIRST-LINE treatment for an acute attack?**
- A) Start allopurinol immediately to lower uric acid
- B) Colchicine (within 36 hours) or NSAIDs (indomethacin); do NOT start/stop urate-lowering therapy during acute attack
- C) IV corticosteroids + allopurinol
- D) Probenecid + colchicine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Acute gout: colchicine is most effective if given within 36 hours (inhibits microtubule polymerization → blocks neutrophil migration). NSAIDs (indomethacin, naproxen) are equally effective. Corticosteroids if colchicine/NSAIDs contraindicated. Crucially: do NOT start or stop urate-lowering therapy (allopurinol) during an acute attack — changes in uric acid levels can prolong or precipitate further flares. Negatively birefringent crystals (yellow when parallel to slow vibration axis of polarizer) = MSU crystals.

</details>

**Q11. A 55-year-old man with recurrent gout (3 attacks/year) and tophi is started on allopurinol. He is also on azathioprine for inflammatory bowel disease. What is the critical drug interaction?**
- A) Allopurinol reduces azathioprine clearance → azathioprine toxicity (myelosuppression); reduce azathioprine dose by 75%
- B) Allopurinol increases azathioprine metabolism — needs dose increase
- C) No significant interaction
- D) Allopurinol and azathioprine can be safely combined at standard doses

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A** — Critical interaction: allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase, which is responsible for metabolizing azathioprine (via 6-mercaptopurine). This causes azathioprine accumulation → severe myelosuppression (pancytopenia), potentially fatal. If allopurinol is essential (febuxostat is an alternative), reduce azathioprine dose by 75% and monitor CBC closely. Febuxostat (selective XOI) also has this interaction but may be less severe. Consider switching to another uricosuric (probenecid) or using febuxostat with careful azathioprine monitoring.

</details>

**Q12. Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate deposition — CPPD) is differentiated from gout by joint aspiration findings. What are the crystal characteristics under compensated polarized light?**
- A) Negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals — yellow when parallel
- B) Positively birefringent rhomboid-shaped crystals — blue when parallel (yellow when perpendicular)
- C) Negatively birefringent rod-shaped crystals — blue when parallel
- D) No crystals visible on polarized light

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — CPPD (pseudogout) crystals: calcium pyrophosphate → weakly positively birefringent, rhomboid/rectangular shape. Under compensated polarized light: blue when parallel to slow vibration axis (and yellow when perpendicular) — opposite to gout. Gout (MSU): negatively birefringent, needle-shaped, yellow when parallel. Pseudogout most commonly affects the knee. Associated with hemochromatosis, hyperparathyroidism, hypomagnesemia, hypothyroidism, acromegaly (the "CHARM" causes of chondrocalcinosis).

</details>

**Q13. A patient with gout develops a serum uric acid of 5.2 mg/dL after 6 months of allopurinol therapy. He still has occasional mild flares. What is the appropriate target and prophylaxis strategy?**
- A) Target uric acid <6 mg/dL; no prophylaxis needed once target is reached
- B) Target uric acid <6 mg/dL (<5 in tophaceous gout); colchicine prophylaxis (0.5 mg twice daily) for 6 months when starting ULT
- C) Target uric acid <8 mg/dL; flares are expected and no prophylaxis needed
- D) Stop allopurinol since uric acid is now normal

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Target uric acid: <6 mg/dL for most patients; <5 mg/dL for tophaceous gout (dissolves existing urate deposits). Flares can paradoxically increase when ULT is started (due to mobilization of urate crystals) — therefore, colchicine prophylaxis (0.5 mg twice daily or 0.5 mg once daily in CKD) is given for the first 6 months of ULT to prevent flares. NSAIDs are an alternative if colchicine is not tolerated. The patient in this question has already reached target but needs prophylaxis continued.

</details>

## SPONDYLOARTHROPATHIES & VASCULITIS (Q14–Q17)

**Q14. A 28-year-old man has inflammatory low back pain (worse at rest, better with activity) for 3 years, bilateral sacroiliac joint tenderness, and limited lumbar flexion. X-ray shows bilateral grade 2 sacroiliitis. HLA-B27 is positive. What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment?**
- A) Psoriatic arthritis; methotrexate
- B) Ankylosing spondylitis; NSAIDs (first-line), anti-TNF if NSAID failure
- C) Reactive arthritis; antibiotics
- D) Enteropathic arthritis; sulfasalazine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Ankylosing spondylitis: HLA-B27 (>90%), young men, inflammatory back pain (better with activity/exercise, worse with rest), bilateral sacroiliitis, bamboo spine (syndesmophytes), enthesitis. Extra-articular: anterior uveitis (most common), aortitis, apical lung fibrosis. Modified NY Criteria: clinical + bilateral sacroiliitis ≥grade 2. Treatment: NSAIDs are first-line (regular use, anti-inflammatory doses). Anti-TNF biologics (etanercept, adalimumab, golimumab) for NSAID failure. DMARDs (MTX, sulfasalazine) have NO role for axial disease.

</details>

**Q15. A 72-year-old woman develops severe unilateral temporal headache, jaw pain when chewing, and sudden visual loss in the left eye. ESR is 110 mm/h. Which test provides a definitive diagnosis and what treatment should be started IMMEDIATELY?**
- A) Carotid Doppler; aspirin
- B) Temporal artery biopsy; high-dose prednisolone (40–60 mg/day) started IMMEDIATELY (even before biopsy if visual symptoms)
- C) Anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA) testing; cyclophosphamide
- D) MRI brain; IV methylprednisolone × 5 days only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA/temporal arteritis): elderly (>50 years), temporal headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, anterior ischemic optic neuropathy → blindness (most feared complication, often irreversible). ESR typically >50 mm/h. Definitive diagnosis: temporal artery biopsy (skip lesions; giant cell granulomatous inflammation). CRITICAL: Start prednisolone IMMEDIATELY if visual symptoms — do not wait for biopsy (biopsy remains positive for days–weeks after steroids). Associated with PMR (shoulder/hip girdle aching) in 50%.

</details>

**Q16. A 35-year-old woman has recurrent sinusitis, nasal septum perforation, saddle nose deformity, hemoptysis, and hematuria. c-ANCA (anti-PR3) is strongly positive. Renal biopsy shows crescentic (pauci-immune) glomerulonephritis. What is the diagnosis and induction treatment?**
- A) Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN); prednisolone alone
- B) Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA/Wegener's); cyclophosphamide + prednisolone (or rituximab)
- C) Microscopic polyangiitis; prednisolone only
- D) Eosinophilic GPA (Churg-Strauss); mepolizumab

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — GPA (Wegener's): ANCA-associated vasculitis with triad of upper respiratory tract (saddle nose, sinusitis, nasal perforation), lower respiratory (pulmonary nodules, cavitating lesions), and necrotizing crescentic GN. c-ANCA (anti-PR3) highly specific. Biopsy: necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis. Induction: cyclophosphamide + prednisolone (or rituximab as equivalent alternative). Maintenance: azathioprine or MTX + low-dose prednisolone. PAN (A) spares the lungs and glomeruli (medium vessel, p-ANCA negative, associated with HBV).

</details>

**Q17. A 50-year-old man with hepatitis B infection develops hypertension, mononeuritis multiplex (foot drop), testicular pain, and livedo reticularis. p-ANCA is NEGATIVE. Angiography shows microaneurysms and beading of medium-sized vessels. Lungs and glomeruli are spared. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) GPA (Wegener's); c-ANCA positive
- B) Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN); associated with HBV; prednisolone ± cyclophosphamide + treat HBV
- C) Takayasu's arteritis; large vessel involvement
- D) Microscopic polyangiitis; p-ANCA positive

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — PAN: medium-vessel vasculitis (spares glomerular capillaries and pulmonary vasculature). p-ANCA NEGATIVE (distinguishes from microscopic polyangiitis). Associated with HBV (30%). Features: mononeuritis multiplex (most characteristic), testicular pain, mesenteric ischemia, renal artery stenosis → hypertension, livedo reticularis. Diagnosis: angiography (microaneurysms, beading) or biopsy. Treatment: prednisolone ± cyclophosphamide + antiviral therapy for HBV (lamivudine/tenofovir). Plasmapheresis for HBV-associated PAN.

</details>

## REACTIVE ARTHRITIS & COMBINED CONCEPTS (Q18–Q20)

**Q18. A 24-year-old man develops asymmetric oligoarthritis, urethritis, and conjunctivitis 3 weeks after urethral discharge. Urine cultures are negative. HLA-B27 is positive. Skin shows keratoderma blenorrhagica on palms. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Gonococcal arthritis
- B) Reactive arthritis (Reiter's syndrome) — Chlamydia trachomatis (genitourinary form)
- C) Ankylosing spondylitis
- D) Psoriatic arthritis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Reactive arthritis (formerly Reiter's syndrome): "Can't see, can't pee, can't climb a tree" = conjunctivitis + urethritis + arthritis. Triggered post-infection (genitourinary form: Chlamydia — more common in West; enteric form: Salmonella/Shigella/Yersinia/Campylobacter). HLA-B27 positive in ~70%. Skin: keratoderma blenorrhagica (hyperkeratotic lesions on palms/soles), circinate balanitis. Oral ulcers, nail changes. Usually self-limiting; NSAIDs for arthritis. Antibiotics if active Chlamydia infection.

</details>

**Q19. A 25-year-old woman with Takayasu's arteritis has no palpable radial pulses bilaterally and BP differential of 20 mmHg between arms. Which vessel is most commonly involved and what is the investigation of choice?**
- A) Temporal artery; temporal artery biopsy
- B) Aorta and its branches; angiography (or MRA/CTA) — gold standard for diagnosis
- C) Coronary arteries; coronary angiography
- D) Pulmonary arteries; V/Q scan

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Takayasu's arteritis: large vessel vasculitis affecting the aorta and its major branches. Young women from Asia. Features: absent upper limb pulses ("pulseless disease"), BP differential >10 mmHg between arms, subclavian steal, AR (from aortitis), renovascular HTN. Angiography is the gold standard (shows arterial stenoses and occlusions); MRA/CTA are non-invasive alternatives. Treatment: corticosteroids; MTX or azathioprine as steroid-sparing agents. Distinguished from GCA by younger age (<40 years) and large vessel pattern.

</details>

**Q20. A 28-year-old woman with SLE develops a purplish-red ulcerated wound on her right shin that started as a pustule after minor trauma. It has undermined edges and is exquisitely painful. This is independent of her current lupus activity. Which is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Erythema nodosum — parallels disease activity
- B) Pyoderma gangrenosum — independent of IBD/SLE disease activity; steroid treatment
- C) Discoid lupus — photodistributed scarring
- D) Vasculitic ulcer — positive ANCA

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Pyoderma gangrenosum: non-infectious, ulcerative skin condition associated with IBD (especially UC), RA, SLE. Key features: starts as pustule/nodule, rapidly ulcerates with undermined edges, violaceous/blue-purple border, pathergy (worsened by trauma — do NOT debride). Independent of underlying disease activity. Treatment: topical/systemic steroids, ciclosporin, anti-TNF for refractory cases. Erythema nodosum (A) parallels disease activity, is tender subcutaneous nodules on shins (no ulceration).

</details>
