## PEPTIC ULCER & H. PYLORI (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A 42-year-old man has recurrent duodenal ulcers. H. pylori testing is positive. He is prescribed triple therapy. After 4 weeks, urea breath test remains positive. What is the most appropriate NEXT step?**
- A) Continue triple therapy for another 4 weeks
- B) Switch to quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole)
- C) Surgical gastrectomy
- D) Repeat endoscopy with CLO test

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Failed H. pylori eradication with standard triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin for 14 days) most commonly indicates clarithromycin resistance (rising globally). Second-line treatment is quadruple therapy (bismuth quadruple): PPI + bismuth subcitrate + tetracycline + metronidazole for 10–14 days. Urea breath test and stool antigen test are used to confirm eradication (not serology — serology cannot confirm eradication). Repeat at least 4 weeks after completion of therapy.

</details>

**Q2. A 35-year-old man has multiple duodenal ulcers and secretory diarrhea. Fasting serum gastrin is 1200 pg/mL. When secretin is injected, gastrin levels rise paradoxically. What is the diagnosis and its association?**
- A) Chronic atrophic gastritis — associated with pernicious anemia
- B) Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (gastrinoma) — associated with MEN-1 in 20–25%
- C) H. pylori gastritis — associated with MALT lymphoma
- D) Gastric carcinoid — associated with G-cell hyperplasia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome (ZES): gastrinoma (usually in pancreas or duodenum) → excess gastrin → massive HCl production → multiple ulcers (especially distal duodenum/jejunum) + secretory diarrhea. Fasting serum gastrin >1000 pg/mL = diagnostic; secretin stimulation test shows paradoxical rise in gastrin (normally gastrin should fall). Associated with MEN-1 (20–25%). Treatment: high-dose PPI + surgical resection.

</details>

**Q3. A 45-year-old woman reports epigastric pain that is WORSE 30–60 minutes AFTER meals. Endoscopy shows a gastric ulcer on the lesser curvature. H. pylori CLO test is positive. What is the correct treatment and monitoring?**
- A) H. pylori eradication only; no follow-up needed
- B) H. pylori eradication + PPI; repeat endoscopy at 6–8 weeks to confirm healing (rule out malignancy)
- C) Immediate surgical resection — all gastric ulcers are malignant
- D) Sucralfate only for 4 weeks

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Gastric ulcer: pain precipitated/worsened by food (vs. duodenal ulcer — pain relieved by food). Gastric ulcers carry a risk of malignancy (H. pylori-associated gastric cancer). All gastric ulcers must be biopsied at endoscopy (4–6 quadrant biopsies from margin and base), and a repeat endoscopy at 6–8 weeks after treatment is mandatory to confirm healing and exclude malignancy. H. pylori eradication + PPI is standard treatment.

</details>

**Q4. A 2-year-old child presents with painless rectal bleeding. A Tc-99m pertechnetate scan (Meckel's scan) shows uptake in the right iliac fossa. What is the anatomical rule and most common ectopic tissue responsible for symptoms?**
- A) Rule of 3s; ectopic pancreatic tissue
- B) Rule of 2s; ectopic gastric mucosa
- C) Rule of 4s; ectopic colonic mucosa
- D) Rule of 2s; ectopic hepatic tissue

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Meckel's diverticulum: Rule of 2s — 2% of population, within 2 feet of ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric > pancreatic), presents in first 2 years of life. Ectopic gastric mucosa secretes HCl → ileal ulceration → painless rectal bleeding (most common presentation). Meckel's scan (Tc-99m pertechnetate) detects gastric mucosa. Treatment: surgical excision if symptomatic.

</details>

## INFLAMMATORY BOWEL DISEASE (Q5–Q7)

**Q5. A 28-year-old man presents with bloody diarrhea, tenesmus, and rectal urgency for 3 months. Colonoscopy shows continuous mucosal inflammation starting from the rectum and extending to the splenic flexure. Biopsy shows crypt abscesses and goblet cell depletion. What is the diagnosis and the associated cancer risk?**
- A) Crohn's disease — moderate cancer risk
- B) Ulcerative colitis — high colorectal cancer risk especially with pancolitis >8–10 years
- C) Ischemic colitis — low cancer risk
- D) Pseudomembranous colitis — low cancer risk

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — UC: rectum always involved, continuous mucosal inflammation, crypt abscesses, goblet cell depletion. UC carries a high risk of colorectal cancer (especially pancolitis >8–10 years duration — surveillance colonoscopy required). Key differences: Crohn's = skip lesions, transmural, non-caseating granulomas, fistulas; smoking increases Crohn's risk but is paradoxically protective in UC (nicotine). UC serology: p-ANCA; Crohn's serology: ASCA.

</details>

**Q6. A 32-year-old woman with Crohn's disease has a perianal fistula. She has failed methotrexate and azathioprine. Which biologic is MOST appropriate for fistulizing Crohn's disease?**
- A) Natalizumab
- B) Infliximab (anti-TNF)
- C) Ustekinumab (anti-IL-12/23)
- D) Vedolizumab (anti-integrin)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Anti-TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab) are the preferred biologics for fistulizing Crohn's disease — they have the strongest evidence base for fistula closure and remission. Infliximab is used both for induction and maintenance. Vedolizumab (D) is gut-selective and good for luminal disease but less evidence for fistulas. Ustekinumab (C) is for moderate-severe Crohn's refractory to anti-TNF. Test for latent TB before starting any biologic.

</details>

**Q7. A 30-year-old man with UC develops right eye pain, redness, and photophobia. His bowel disease is in remission. Which extraintestinal manifestation does NOT parallel UC disease activity?**
- A) Peripheral arthritis
- B) Oral aphthous ulcers
- C) Uveitis
- D) Erythema nodosum

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — IBD extraintestinal manifestations: those that parallel disease activity (flare with the bowel disease) = peripheral arthritis, episcleritis, oral aphthous ulcers, erythema nodosum. Those INDEPENDENT of disease activity (can occur in remission) = ankylosing spondylitis (HLA-B27), uveitis, pyoderma gangrenosum, primary sclerosing cholangitis. Uveitis in IBD requires ophthalmologic treatment regardless of bowel disease status.

</details>

## LIVER DISEASE & CIRRHOSIS (Q8–Q12)

**Q8. A cirrhotic patient is scored: bilirubin 2.5 mg/dL, albumin 2.6 g/dL, INR 2.0, moderate ascites, grade 2 encephalopathy. What is the Child-Pugh class and its clinical implication?**
- A) Class A (5–6 points) — good surgical candidate
- B) Class B (7–9 points) — moderate surgical risk
- C) Class C (10–15 points) — poor surgical candidate, consider transplant
- D) Class B (7–9 points) — safe for elective surgery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Child-Pugh scoring: Bilirubin 2–3 = 2 pts; Albumin <2.8 = 3 pts; INR 1.7–2.3 = 2 pts; Moderate ascites = 2 pts; Grade 1–2 encephalopathy = 2 pts → Total = 11 points → Class C (10–15). Class C = poor surgical risk, high mortality, should be considered for transplant. MELD score >15 = listing for liver transplant. Class A (5–6) = good risk; Class B (7–9) = moderate risk.

</details>

**Q9. A cirrhotic patient with ascites develops fever, abdominal pain, and altered mentation. Paracentesis shows PMN count of 320 cells/mm³ with no secondary cause. What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment?**
- A) Secondary bacterial peritonitis; surgical exploration
- B) Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP); cefotaxime IV + albumin
- C) Hepatorenal syndrome; terlipressin only
- D) TB peritonitis; ATT

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — SBP: PMN count ≥250 cells/mm³ in ascitic fluid is diagnostic (regardless of culture result). Most common organisms: E. coli, Klebsiella, S. pneumoniae. Treatment: cefotaxime 2g IV q8h × 5 days + albumin infusion (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3) — albumin prevents HRS development (reduces mortality). SBP prophylaxis: norfloxacin or ciprofloxacin for patients with prior SBP or low ascitic protein (<1 g/dL).

</details>

**Q10. A 40-year-old man with Wilson's disease presents with hepatic failure and hemolytic anemia. Slit-lamp examination shows Kayser-Fleischer rings. Labs: serum ceruloplasmin 12 mg/dL (low), 24h urinary copper 200 μg/day (elevated). What gene is mutated and what is the inheritance?**
- A) HFE gene; autosomal recessive
- B) ATP7B gene (chromosome 13); autosomal recessive
- C) CFTR gene; autosomal recessive
- D) ATP7A gene; X-linked recessive

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Wilson's disease: ATP7B gene mutation (chromosome 13), autosomal recessive → copper accumulates in liver, basal ganglia, cornea (KF rings), kidneys. Low ceruloplasmin (<20 mg/dL), high 24h urinary copper (>100 μg/day), high serum copper. KF rings (Descemet's membrane) — seen by slit-lamp. Coombs-negative hemolytic anemia. Treatment: D-penicillamine (first-line); trientine (if intolerant); zinc (maintenance). HFE gene (A) = hemochromatosis.

</details>

**Q11. A 50-year-old man with known cirrhosis has rising serum creatinine (1.4 → 2.8 mg/dL over 10 days). Urinalysis is normal. Urine sodium <10 mEq/L. No improvement after albumin infusion for 48 hours. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Acute tubular necrosis
- B) Pre-renal azotemia due to dehydration
- C) Type 1 Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS-AKI)
- D) Contrast nephropathy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — HRS type 1 (HRS-AKI): rapid renal failure in cirrhosis — Cr doubles to >2.5 mg/dL in <2 weeks. Urine Na <10 mEq/L (avid sodium retention), normal urinalysis, no response to 2-day albumin challenge. Pathophysiology: splanchnic vasodilation → reduced effective arterial volume → renal vasoconstriction (functional, not structural). Treatment: terlipressin + albumin (first-line); liver transplant (definitive). Must rule out: hypovolemia, nephrotoxins, obstruction, parenchymal renal disease.

</details>

**Q12. A young woman presents with fatigue, jaundice, and elevated transaminases. Labs show high IgG, positive ANA and anti-smooth muscle antibody (ASMA). Biopsy shows interface hepatitis (piecemeal necrosis). Which is the treatment?**
- A) Antiviral therapy with pegylated interferon
- B) Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA)
- C) Prednisolone + azathioprine
- D) Cholestyramine + vitamin K

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) Type 1: ANA + ASMA positive, high IgG, interface hepatitis on biopsy, young women, associated with other autoimmune diseases. Treatment: prednisolone + azathioprine (reduces steroid dose). AIH Type 2: anti-LKM1 positive (young girls). UDCA (B) is for Primary Biliary Cholangitis (AMA positive, middle-aged women, pruritus, high IgM). PSC: p-ANCA, associated with UC, beaded biliary ducts on MRCP.

</details>

## PANCREATITIS (Q13–Q15)

**Q13. A 45-year-old alcoholic man presents with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, vomiting, and abdominal rigidity. On admission: age 48, WBC 18,000/mm³, glucose 230 mg/dL, LDH 380 IU/L, AST 280 IU/L. At 48 hours: HCT fall 12%, BUN rise 8 mg/dL, PaO2 55 mmHg, Ca²⁺ 7.2 mg/dL. What is the Ranson's score and severity?**
- A) Score 3 — mild pancreatitis
- B) Score 6 — severe pancreatitis
- C) Score 4 — moderate pancreatitis
- D) Score 8 — very severe (not a valid category)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Ranson's criteria: Admission (5): Age >55 (1), WBC >16,000 (1), Glucose >200 (1), LDH >350 (1), AST >250 (1) = 5. 48h (5): HCT fall >10% (1), BUN rise >5 (1), PaO2 <60 (1), Ca <8 (1), Fluid sequestration >6L (0) = 4. Total = 5+4 = wait — at admission: 5 criteria met (5); at 48h: 4 criteria met (1+1+1+1=4). Total Ranson's = 9? Recount: admission criteria: 5 pts; 48h criteria: 4 pts → total = well, actually each criterion is 1 point → admission = 5 criteria met (5 pts), 48h = 4 criteria met. However Ranson's total ≥3 = severe. Score here is ≥6 = very severe → ICU care needed.

</details>

**Q14. Most common cause of acute pancreatitis in India and the corresponding test to confirm it:**
- A) Alcohol; serum amylase only
- B) Gallstones; ultrasound abdomen
- C) ERCP; CT scan
- D) Drugs; history alone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — In India, gallstones are the most common cause of acute pancreatitis. In Western countries, alcohol is the most common cause. Gallstones are confirmed by ultrasound abdomen (shows gallstones, CBD dilation). Serum amylase/lipase are raised >3× upper limit of normal for diagnosis. CT abdomen (contrast-enhanced) is used for assessing severity and complications. ERCP is both diagnostic and therapeutic for impacted common bile duct stones.

</details>

**Q15. A 50-year-old alcoholic man has chronic epigastric pain with steatorrhea and diabetes. Plain X-ray of abdomen shows calcifications in the epigastric region. What confirms chronic pancreatitis and what treats steatorrhea?**
- A) Serum amylase elevation; oral PPI
- B) Ductal calcifications on X-ray/CT; pancreatic enzyme replacement (lipase)
- C) Elevated fecal elastase; cholestyramine
- D) MRCP showing dilated CBD; biliary drainage

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Chronic pancreatitis: calcifications on plain X-ray are pathognomonic (ductal stones). CT confirms calcifications and ductal dilation. The triad: chronic abdominal pain + steatorrhea (fat malabsorption from exocrine insufficiency) + diabetes (endocrine insufficiency). Treatment: pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT — lipase-containing preparations with meals) for steatorrhea, pain management (analgesics, celiac block), endoscopic drainage for ductal obstruction.

</details>

## HEPATITIS SEROLOGY & MALABSORPTION (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A patient has these hepatitis B serology results: HBsAg negative, anti-HBs positive, anti-HBc IgG positive. What is the correct interpretation?**
- A) Acute hepatitis B infection
- B) Chronic hepatitis B carrier
- C) Resolved past HBV infection with natural immunity
- D) Successful hepatitis B vaccination

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Hepatitis B serology interpretation: Resolved past HBV infection = HBsAg negative + anti-HBs positive + anti-HBc IgG positive (both surface antibody AND core antibody present). Vaccination (D) = anti-HBs positive ONLY (no anti-HBc, as the vaccine contains only surface antigen). Acute infection = HBsAg positive + anti-HBc IgM positive. Chronic carrier = HBsAg positive >6 months + anti-HBc IgG positive. Window period = HBsAg neg, anti-HBs neg, anti-HBc IgM positive.

</details>

**Q17. A 35-year-old woman has chronic diarrhea, weight loss, and iron-deficiency anemia refractory to oral iron. Endoscopy shows scalloping of duodenal folds and villous atrophy. Anti-tissue transglutaminase IgA (anti-tTG IgA) is strongly positive. What is the diagnosis and treatment?**
- A) Tropical sprue; tetracycline + folate
- B) Celiac disease (gluten-sensitive enteropathy); strict lifelong gluten-free diet
- C) Crohn's disease; azathioprine + steroids
- D) Whipple's disease; ceftriaxone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Celiac disease (gluten-sensitive enteropathy): autoimmune response to gliadin (wheat, barley, rye) → villous atrophy → malabsorption. Classic: iron and folate deficiency anemia (proximal small bowel affected), diarrhea, weight loss, dermatitis herpetiformis. Diagnosis: anti-tTG IgA (most sensitive/specific), anti-endomysial IgA; confirmed by duodenal biopsy (Marsh classification). Treatment: strict lifelong gluten-free diet. Tropical sprue (A) = responds to tetracycline + folate, no specific antibodies.

</details>

**Q18. A patient returning from Southeast Asia has chronic diarrhea, megaloblastic anemia, and malabsorption affecting both fat AND carbohydrates. Jejunal biopsy shows partial villous atrophy. Anti-tTG antibody is negative. Response to tetracycline + folic acid is excellent. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Celiac disease
- B) Tropical sprue
- C) Giardiasis
- D) Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Tropical sprue: seen in tropical regions (Southeast Asia, Caribbean, India), causes malabsorption of fat AND carbohydrates (unlike celiac which primarily affects proximal bowel). Megaloblastic anemia from folate (and vitamin B12) deficiency. Jejunal biopsy: partial villous atrophy (not as severe as celiac). No specific antibodies. Responds dramatically to tetracycline (3 months) + folic acid. Celiac (A) = anti-tTG positive, gluten-related. Giardia (C) = watery diarrhea, no villous atrophy.

</details>

**Q19. A 60-year-old woman presents with fatigue, yellow eyes, and pruritus. Labs: ALP 450 IU/L, GGT elevated, mildly elevated ALT/AST, high IgM. Anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA) M2 is positive. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)
- B) Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)
- C) Autoimmune Hepatitis Type 1
- D) Drug-induced cholestasis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — PBC: middle-aged women, fatigue, pruritus, jaundice (late), high ALP, high IgM, AMA (anti-M2) positive (>95% sensitive and specific). Liver biopsy: florid duct lesion (granulomatous destruction of interlobular bile ducts). Treatment: UDCA (ursodeoxycholic acid) — first-line; obeticholic acid (second-line); transplant for end-stage. PSC (A): associated with UC, beaded bile ducts on MRCP, p-ANCA positive, risk of cholangiocarcinoma, predominantly in young men.

</details>

**Q20. A 30-year-old man has Crohn's disease with terminal ileal resection. He now has chronic diarrhea and macrocytic anemia. Which vitamin deficiency is the MOST likely cause, and what is the site of absorption?**
- A) Folate; jejunum
- B) Vitamin B12; terminal ileum (intrinsic factor–B12 complex absorbed via cubilin receptors)
- C) Vitamin D; duodenum
- D) Vitamin K; colon

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is absorbed exclusively in the terminal ileum via binding of the intrinsic factor (IF)–B12 complex to cubilin receptors. Terminal ileal resection or disease (Crohn's) → B12 malabsorption → megaloblastic anemia + subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord. Folate (A) is absorbed in the proximal jejunum — deficiency in celiac disease. Vitamin D (C) = duodenum/jejunum. Schilling test (now rarely done) differentiates cause of B12 deficiency.

</details>
