## CARDIOLOGY (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 55-year-old man presents with crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm for 2 hours. ECG shows ST elevation in leads II, III, and aVF. Which coronary artery is most likely occluded?**
- A) Left anterior descending artery
- B) Left circumflex artery
- C) Right coronary artery
- D) Obtuse marginal artery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The right coronary artery (RCA) supplies the inferior wall of the left ventricle, and its occlusion causes inferior MI with ST elevation in II, III, and aVF. LAD occlusion causes anterior MI (V1–V4 changes). Left circumflex occlusion causes lateral MI (I, aVL, V5–V6). The key one-liner: inferior MI = RCA territory.

</details>

**Q2. A 60-year-old hypertensive woman is started on a drug that causes dry cough as a side effect. She is switched to another antihypertensive that acts on the same pathway without causing cough. Which drug was she most likely switched to?**
- A) Metoprolol
- B) Losartan
- C) Amlodipine
- D) Hydrochlorothiazide

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

ACE inhibitors (e.g., enalapril, ramipril) cause dry cough due to bradykinin accumulation. Angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) like losartan block the same renin-angiotensin pathway at the AT1 receptor but do not cause bradykinin accumulation, so they do not cause cough. The one-liner: ARBs = same RAAS pathway, no cough.

</details>

**Q3. A 45-year-old man has an echocardiogram showing a mid-systolic click followed by a late systolic murmur, best heard at the apex. Which valve is most likely affected?**
- A) Aortic valve
- B) Pulmonary valve
- C) Tricuspid valve
- D) Mitral valve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) classically produces a mid-systolic click followed by a late systolic murmur at the apex due to prolapse of the mitral leaflets into the left atrium during systole. It is the most common valvular abnormality in young adults. The one-liner: mid-systolic click + late systolic murmur at apex = MVP.

</details>

**Q4. A patient presents with pulse rate of 40/min, hypotension, and cannon A waves in the JVP. ECG shows complete dissociation of P waves and QRS complexes. What is the drug of choice for immediate management?**
- A) Adenosine
- B) Digoxin
- C) Atropine
- D) Amiodarone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Complete (third-degree) heart block presents with bradycardia, hypotension, and cannon A waves (atria contracting against closed tricuspid valve). Atropine is the immediate pharmacological treatment for symptomatic bradycardia; it blocks vagal tone and increases heart rate. Definitive management is pacemaker insertion. The one-liner: symptomatic complete heart block → atropine first, then pacemaker.

</details>

**Q5. A 70-year-old man with known heart failure presents with worsening dyspnea, orthopnea, and bilateral basal crackles. His ejection fraction is 35%. Which diuretic reduces mortality in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF)?**
- A) Furosemide
- B) Hydrochlorothiazide
- C) Spironolactone
- D) Mannitol

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist) is the only diuretic proven to reduce mortality in HFrEF, as shown in the RALES trial. Furosemide relieves symptoms but does not reduce mortality. Hydrochlorothiazide and mannitol have no role in HFrEF mortality reduction. The one-liner: spironolactone = only diuretic that reduces mortality in heart failure.

</details>

## PULMONOLOGY (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 30-year-old non-smoker presents with progressive dyspnea, basal crepitations, and a restrictive pattern on spirometry (FEV1/FVC > 0.8, reduced TLC). CT chest shows bilateral ground-glass opacities with a honeycombing pattern. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
- B) Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis
- C) Sarcoidosis
- D) Bronchiectasis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) presents with progressive dyspnea, bilateral basal fine crepitations ("Velcro crackles"), restrictive spirometry, and CT showing bilateral basal honeycombing with traction bronchiectasis. Sarcoidosis typically shows upper lobe involvement and bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy. COPD is obstructive. The one-liner: bilateral basal honeycombing + restrictive pattern = IPF until proven otherwise.

</details>

**Q7. A 25-year-old man presents with sudden onset right-sided pleuritic chest pain and dyspnea. He is tall, thin, and has no prior illness. CXR shows a thin white line at the right apex with absent lung markings beyond it. What is the immediate management if he is hemodynamically stable?**
- A) Intubation and mechanical ventilation
- B) Needle aspiration / observation if small
- C) Emergency thoracotomy
- D) IV antibiotics

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

This is a primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP), classically in tall, thin young men. A small PSP (< 2 cm rim) in a stable patient can be managed with observation and supplemental oxygen, or needle aspiration if larger. Emergency thoracotomy is reserved for tension pneumothorax. The one-liner: stable PSP < 2 cm = observation; > 2 cm = needle aspiration or intercostal drain.

</details>

**Q8. A 50-year-old smoker presents with hemoptysis, weight loss, and a right hilar mass on CXR. Biopsy shows small cells with scant cytoplasm and "salt-and-pepper" chromatin. Which paraneoplastic syndrome is most commonly associated with this tumor?**
- A) Hypercalcemia
- B) Cushing syndrome due to ectopic ACTH
- C) Hyponatremia due to SIADH
- D) Polycythemia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Small cell lung carcinoma (SCLC) is the most common lung cancer associated with SIADH (hyponatremia) and ectopic ACTH production. However, SIADH (ADH secretion) is the most frequently tested and most common paraneoplastic syndrome of SCLC. Hypercalcemia is associated with squamous cell carcinoma. The one-liner: SCLC = SIADH (most common paraneoplastic) + ectopic ACTH.

</details>

**Q9. A 40-year-old woman with rheumatoid arthritis presents with fever, pleuritic chest pain, and a pleural effusion. Pleural fluid analysis shows glucose < 30 mg/dL, LDH > 1000 IU/L, and pH < 7.2. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Transudative effusion due to heart failure
- B) Chylothorax
- C) Rheumatoid pleuritis
- D) Parapneumonic empyema

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Rheumatoid pleuritis characteristically shows very low pleural fluid glucose (< 30 mg/dL, even lower than the serum), high LDH, and low pH — the lowest glucose of any non-empyema effusion. This triad in the context of RA is pathognomonic. Empyema would also show low glucose/pH but in the setting of infection. The one-liner: RA pleural effusion = glucose < 30 mg/dL (pathognomonic low glucose).

</details>

**Q10. A 35-year-old man presents with worsening exertional dyspnea over 3 years. Spirometry shows FEV1 60%, FEV1/FVC 55%, with 15% improvement after bronchodilator. He is a non-smoker. Serum shows markedly low alpha-1 antitrypsin levels. Which lobe of the lung is most prominently involved?**
- A) Upper lobe
- B) Middle lobe
- C) Lower lobe
- D) Perihilar region

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency causes panacinar emphysema predominantly in the lower lobes, in contrast to smoking-related centrilobular emphysema which is upper lobe predominant. The deficiency leads to unchecked neutrophil elastase activity, destroying alveolar walls. The one-liner: alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency = panacinar emphysema, lower lobe predominant.

</details>

## GASTROENTEROLOGY (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 45-year-old man with chronic alcohol use presents with sudden onset severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, and vomiting. Serum amylase is 900 U/L. CT abdomen shows peripancreatic fat stranding. Which scoring system is used to assess the severity of acute pancreatitis at 48 hours?**
- A) Child-Pugh score
- B) Ranson's criteria
- C) APACHE II
- D) MELD score

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ranson's criteria is the classic scoring system for acute pancreatitis severity, assessed at admission and at 48 hours (5 criteria each). A score ≥ 3 indicates severe pancreatitis. APACHE II is also used but is more complex. Child-Pugh and MELD are for liver cirrhosis severity. The one-liner: Ranson's criteria = severity scoring in acute pancreatitis at 48 hours.

</details>

**Q12. A 55-year-old woman presents with progressive dysphagia — initially to solids, now also to liquids — weight loss, and regurgitation of undigested food. Barium swallow shows a "bird-beak" appearance at the lower esophageal sphincter. What is the investigation of choice to confirm the diagnosis?**
- A) Upper GI endoscopy
- B) Esophageal manometry
- C) CT chest
- D) 24-hour pH monitoring

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Achalasia cardia presents with dysphagia to both solids and liquids from the outset, regurgitation, and the classic "bird-beak" appearance on barium swallow. Esophageal manometry is the gold standard for diagnosis, showing absent peristalsis and incomplete LES relaxation (high LES pressure). Endoscopy is done to rule out secondary achalasia (pseudoachalasia). The one-liner: achalasia diagnosis = esophageal manometry (gold standard).

</details>

**Q13. A 30-year-old man presents with recurrent abdominal cramps, bloody diarrhea, and tenesmus for 6 months. Colonoscopy shows continuous mucosal involvement from the rectum extending proximally, with no skip lesions. Biopsy shows crypt abscesses and goblet cell depletion. What is the most appropriate maintenance therapy?**
- A) Metronidazole
- B) Sulfasalazine / Mesalazine
- C) Methotrexate
- D) Rifaximin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

This is ulcerative colitis (UC): continuous involvement starting from the rectum, crypt abscesses, and goblet cell depletion are characteristic. The drug of choice for maintenance of mild-to-moderate UC is 5-aminosalicylates (mesalazine/sulfasalazine). Metronidazole is used for Crohn's perianal disease. Methotrexate is used in Crohn's, not UC. The one-liner: UC maintenance = 5-ASA (mesalazine/sulfasalazine).

</details>

**Q14. A 50-year-old man with known cirrhosis presents with confusion, asterixis (flapping tremor), and fetor hepaticus. His serum ammonia is elevated. Which drug reduces blood ammonia by converting it to a non-absorbable form in the gut?**
- A) Neomycin
- B) Rifaximin
- C) Lactulose
- D) Both B and C

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Lactulose is the drug of choice for hepatic encephalopathy. It is a non-absorbable disaccharide that acidifies the colon, converting NH3 (absorbable) to NH4+ (non-absorbable), thereby reducing serum ammonia. Rifaximin is used as an adjunct or for prevention of recurrence. Neomycin has largely been abandoned. The one-liner: lactulose = first-line for hepatic encephalopathy (traps ammonia in gut).

</details>

**Q15. A 60-year-old woman presents with painless obstructive jaundice (bilirubin 12 mg/dL), pale stools, dark urine, and a palpable, non-tender gallbladder. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Cholelithiasis
- B) Acute cholecystitis
- C) Carcinoma head of pancreas
- D) Primary sclerosing cholangitis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Courvoisier's law states: painless obstructive jaundice with a palpable, non-tender gallbladder is unlikely to be due to gallstones (which cause a fibrosed, contracted gallbladder) and is more likely due to carcinoma of the head of the pancreas. This is one of the most tested signs in surgery/medicine. The one-liner: painless jaundice + palpable non-tender gallbladder = Courvoisier's sign → carcinoma head of pancreas.

</details>

## NEUROLOGY (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 65-year-old right-handed man presents with sudden onset right-sided hemiplegia and aphasia. He is brought within 2 hours of symptom onset. CT head shows no hemorrhage. Which artery territory is most likely affected?**
- A) Right middle cerebral artery
- B) Left middle cerebral artery
- C) Posterior cerebral artery
- D) Basilar artery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The left hemisphere is dominant for language in right-handed individuals (and most left-handers). Left MCA territory infarction causes contralateral (right) hemiplegia + aphasia. Right MCA occlusion would cause left hemiplegia and neglect. Posterior cerebral artery strokes cause homonymous hemianopia. The one-liner: right hemiplegia + aphasia = left MCA territory stroke.

</details>

**Q17. A 70-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of resting tremor in the right hand ("pill-rolling"), bradykinesia, and cogwheel rigidity. He has a stooped posture and shuffling gait. What is the pathological hallmark of his condition?**
- A) Neurofibrillary tangles in the cortex
- B) Lewy bodies (alpha-synuclein) in substantia nigra neurons
- C) Pick bodies in the frontal lobe
- D) Prion protein aggregates

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Parkinson's disease is characterized by loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, with intraneuronal inclusions called Lewy bodies (composed of alpha-synuclein). Neurofibrillary tangles are seen in Alzheimer's disease. Pick bodies are in frontotemporal dementia. The one-liner: Parkinson's disease pathological hallmark = Lewy bodies (alpha-synuclein) in substantia nigra.

</details>

**Q19. A 40-year-old woman presents with diplopia worse in the evening, ptosis that worsens with repeated activity, and proximal muscle weakness. A Tensilon (edrophonium) test dramatically improves her symptoms. What antibody is most likely positive?**
- A) Anti-Hu antibody

- C) Anti-MuSK antibody

<summary>Answer</summary>

Myasthenia gravis presents with fatigable weakness (worse with activity, better with rest), ptosis, diplopia, and responds dramatically to edrophonium (short-acting acetylcholinesterase inhibitor). **Q20. A 55-year-old man with uncontrolled hypertension presents with sudden onset severe headache described as "the worst headache of my life," neck stiffness, and photophobia. CT head is normal. What is the next investigation?**
- A) MRI brain with contrast
- B) Lumbar puncture
- C) EEG
- D) Carotid Doppler

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

"Thunderclap headache" (worst headache of life, sudden onset) with neck stiffness and photophobia strongly suggests subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH). CT is 98% sensitive within 6 hours but can miss small bleeds. If CT is normal, lumbar puncture must be done to look for xanthochromia (CSF bilirubin from lysed RBCs) — the gold standard when CT is negative. The one-liner: normal CT + thunderclap headache → lumbar puncture to exclude SAH.

## ENDOCRINOLOGY (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 35-year-old woman presents with heat intolerance, weight loss, palpitations, and a diffuse goiter. On examination, she has exophthalmos and pretibial myxedema. TSH is suppressed, free T4 is elevated. What is the pathognomonic antibody?**
- A) Anti-TPO antibody
- B) Anti-thyroglobulin antibody
- C) TSH receptor stimulating antibody (TSI/TRAb)
- D) Anti-microsomal antibody

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Graves' disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism in young women and is an autoimmune condition. The pathognomonic antibody is TSH receptor stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI/TRAb), which stimulates the TSH receptor, causing autonomous thyroid hormone production. Anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies are seen in Hashimoto's thyroiditis. The one-liner: Graves' disease = TSI/TRAb is pathognomonic.

</details>

**Q22. A 50-year-old obese man with type 2 diabetes on metformin has HbA1c of 9.2%. He also has a BMI of 36 kg/m² and NASH-related liver disease. Which drug class, if added, would provide weight loss, cardiovascular benefit, AND glucose lowering?**
- A) Sulfonylureas
- B) DPP-4 inhibitors
- C) GLP-1 receptor agonists
- D) Insulin glargine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g., semaglutide, liraglutide) provide significant weight loss, HbA1c reduction, and proven cardiovascular mortality benefit (LEADER, SUSTAIN-6 trials). They are preferred in obese T2DM patients with or at risk of cardiovascular disease. Sulfonylureas and insulin cause weight gain. DPP-4 inhibitors are weight-neutral. The one-liner: GLP-1 RA = weight loss + CV benefit + glucose lowering in T2DM.

**Q23. A 30-year-old woman presents with secondary amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and visual field defect (bitemporal hemianopia). MRI pituitary shows a 1.5 cm mass. Prolactin level is 3500 ng/mL. What is the first-line treatment?**
- A) Transsphenoidal surgery
- B) External beam radiotherapy
- C) Bromocriptine or cabergoline (dopamine agonist)
- D) Octreotide

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

This is a macroprolactinoma (> 1 cm). The first-line treatment for prolactinomas (both micro and macro) is a dopamine agonist — cabergoline (preferred due to fewer side effects) or bromocriptine. Surgery is reserved for dopamine agonist-resistant or intolerant cases, or acute visual loss. Octreotide is for acromegaly (GH-secreting adenoma). The one-liner: prolactinoma → dopamine agonist first-line (surgery is second-line).

</details>

**Q26. A 35-year-old woman presents with bilateral hand stiffness lasting > 1 hour in the morning, symmetric small joint arthritis (MCP and PIP joints), and subcutaneous nodules on the extensor surface of the forearms. RF is positive. Which investigation is more specific for her diagnosis than RF?**

**Q30. A 45-year-old man presents with acute monoarthritis of the first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra), with erythema, warmth, and exquisite tenderness. Joint aspiration shows negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals under polarized light. Which enzyme deficiency leads to uric acid accumulation in a rare X-linked form of this disease?**
- A) Adenosine deaminase
- B) Xanthine oxidase
- C) HGPRT (hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase)
- D) Glucose-6-phosphatase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

## NEPHROLOGY (Q31–Q35)

**Q31. A 10-year-old boy presents with periorbital puffiness, frothy urine, and hypoalbuminemia (2.0 g/dL). Urinary protein is 4.5 g/day. Blood pressure and serum complement levels are normal. Renal biopsy is most likely to show:**
- A) Diffuse mesangial hypercellularity with IgA deposits
- B) Normal light microscopy with foot process effacement on electron microscopy
- C) Subepithelial "spike and dome" deposits on silver stain
- D) Diffuse endocapillary proliferation with neutrophil infiltration

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The clinical picture — heavy proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminemia, normal BP, normal complement — is classic nephrotic syndrome in a child, most likely minimal change disease (MCD). MCD shows normal LM and IF with only foot process effacement on EM. It is steroid-responsive. "Spike and dome" on silver stain (C) indicates membranous nephropathy. Endocapillary proliferation (D) is nephritic (post-streptococcal GN). The one-liner: child + nephrotic syndrome + normal complement = MCD → foot process effacement on EM.

</details>

**Q32. A 30-year-old woman with type 1 diabetes for 15 years has persistent microalbuminuria (urine albumin 200 mg/day) and BP 138/88 mmHg. The single most important intervention to slow progression of diabetic nephropathy is:**
- A) Strict glycemic control alone (HbA1c <7%)
- B) ACE inhibitor (e.g., ramipril) regardless of blood pressure
- C) Sodium restriction + loop diuretic
- D) Protein restriction to <0.4 g/kg/day

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

ACE inhibitors (or ARBs) are the first-line intervention in diabetic nephropathy with proteinuria — they reduce intraglomerular pressure by dilating the efferent arteriole, independently of their antihypertensive effect. They are indicated even in normotensive diabetics with microalbuminuria. Strict glycemic control prevents onset but has less impact on established nephropathy. The one-liner: diabetic nephropathy + proteinuria → ACE inhibitor/ARB is the cornerstone regardless of BP.

</details>

**Q33. A 25-year-old man presents with recurrent painless macroscopic hematuria that occurs simultaneously with upper respiratory tract infections (synpharyngitic hematuria). Renal biopsy shows mesangial IgA deposits on immunofluorescence. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN)
- B) IgA nephropathy (Berger disease)
- C) Henoch-Schönlein purpura nephritis
- D) Thin basement membrane disease

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

IgA nephropathy (Berger disease) is the most common glomerulonephritis worldwide.
