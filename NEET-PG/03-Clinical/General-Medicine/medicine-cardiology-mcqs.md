## HEART FAILURE (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A 58-year-old man with ischemic cardiomyopathy has an ejection fraction of 32%. He is on furosemide and ramipril. Which additional drug has been shown to reduce mortality in this condition in the PARADIGM-HF trial?**
- A) Digoxin
- B) Sacubitril/valsartan
- C) Ivabradine
- D) Hydralazine + nitrates

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Sacubitril/valsartan (ARNI) was shown to be superior to enalapril in reducing cardiovascular death and HF hospitalizations in HFrEF (EF <40%) in the PARADIGM-HF trial. It cannot be combined with ACEi — a 36-hour washout is required. Ivabradine (C) reduces HR but not mortality as first-line add-on. Hydralazine+nitrates (D) is for ACEi/ARB-intolerant patients (especially Black patients).

</details>

---

**Q2. A 65-year-old woman with hypertension and diabetes presents with exertional dyspnea. Echo shows EF of 58% with impaired diastolic relaxation (Grade II diastolic dysfunction). BNP is elevated. What is the most appropriate diagnosis?**
- A) HFrEF
- B) HFmrEF
- C) HFpEF
- D) Restrictive cardiomyopathy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — HFpEF is defined by EF ≥50% with symptoms of heart failure and evidence of structural/diastolic abnormality. It is classically seen in hypertensive, diabetic, and obese patients. HFrEF (A) = EF <40%; HFmrEF (B) = EF 40–49%. Restrictive cardiomyopathy (D) would show marked diastolic filling abnormality on echo and is a specific diagnosis.

</details>

**Q3. A patient with NYHA Class III HFrEF (EF 28%) is in sinus rhythm with HR 76/min despite maximally tolerated beta-blocker. Which drug is indicated to reduce heart rate further?**
- A) Digoxin
- B) Amiodarone
- C) Ivabradine
- D) Diltiazem

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Ivabradine is indicated in HFrEF patients in sinus rhythm with HR >70/min despite maximum tolerated beta-blocker dose. It reduces heart rate by blocking the If (funny) current in the SA node. Digoxin (A) has a role in AF with HF but its mortality benefit is unproven. Diltiazem (D) is a negative inotrope — contraindicated in HFrEF.

</details>

**Q4. In acute pulmonary edema, a patient is brought in sitting upright with severe respiratory distress and frothy sputum. Using the LMNOP mnemonic, which drug reduces preload by venodilation and is given sublingually or intravenously?**
- A) Furosemide
- B) Morphine
- C) Nitrates
- D) Dopamine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Nitrates (e.g., sublingual GTN, IV isosorbide dinitrate) are venodilators that reduce preload and afterload, providing rapid relief in acute pulmonary edema. Furosemide (A = L in LMNOP) acts via diuresis and initial venodilation. Morphine (B = M) reduces anxiety/preload but is controversial. Dopamine/dobutamine are used for cardiogenic shock, not standard pulmonary edema.

</details>

## ISCHEMIC HEART DISEASE (Q5–Q8)

**Q5. A 52-year-old man presents with 3 hours of severe chest pain. ECG shows ST elevation in V1–V4. Troponin is elevated. Which is the MOST appropriate immediate management in a PCI-capable center?**
- A) IV streptokinase within 30 minutes
- B) IV alteplase within 30 minutes
- C) Primary PCI within 90 minutes (door-to-balloon)
- D) IV heparin and observation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — In a PCI-capable center, the goal is primary PCI with a door-to-balloon time of <90 minutes. This is superior to thrombolysis and is the standard of care for anterior STEMI. Streptokinase (A) is non-fibrin specific, antigenic, and cannot be repeated within 6 months — reserved for when PCI is unavailable. Alteplase (B) is used only when PCI is unavailable within 120 minutes.

</details>

---

**Q6. A patient has inferior STEMI complicated by hypotension and elevated JVP. ECG shows ST elevation in lead V4R. What is the CONTRAINDICATED drug in this setting?**
- A) IV normal saline bolus
- B) Dobutamine
- C) Sublingual nitrates
- D) Aspirin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Right ventricular infarction (diagnosed by ST elevation in V4R in the context of inferior STEMI) depends on adequate preload. Nitrates reduce preload and can precipitate severe hypotension. The correct management is fluid challenge (IV NS). Dobutamine (B) may be needed for cardiogenic shock. Aspirin (D) is indicated in all STEMI patients.

</details>

**Q7. A 48-year-old man is brought in 6 days after a STEMI with new onset of holosystolic murmur at the lower left sternal border, thrill, and step-up in oxygen saturation from right atrium to right ventricle. What is the most likely mechanical complication?**
- A) Papillary muscle rupture
- B) Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
- C) Free wall rupture
- D) LV aneurysm

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Post-MI VSD presents 3–5 days after MI with a new holosystolic murmur, thrill at the lower sternal border, and oxygen step-up from RA to RV on right heart catheterization. Papillary muscle rupture (A) causes acute severe MR with pulmonary edema and a murmur at the apex radiating to the axilla. Free wall rupture (C) leads to rapid hemopericardium and sudden death.

</details>

**Q8. A 44-year-old woman experiences chest pain at rest, typically early morning. ECG during pain shows transient ST elevation that resolves spontaneously. Coronary angiography is normal. What is the mechanism and treatment?**
- A) Plaque rupture; aspirin + statin
- B) Coronary vasospasm; calcium channel blockers
- C) Demand ischemia; beta-blockers
- D) Coronary embolism; anticoagulation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — This is Prinzmetal (variant) angina caused by coronary vasospasm. It occurs at rest, often at night/early morning, shows transient ST elevation during pain, and the coronary angiogram is normal. Treatment is CCB (nifedipine/diltiazem) and nitrates. Beta-blockers (C) are contraindicated as they can worsen vasospasm by allowing unopposed alpha-adrenergic tone.

</details>

## ARRHYTHMIAS (Q9–Q12)

**Q9. A 30-year-old man presents with sudden palpitations. ECG shows a delta wave, short PR interval, and wide QRS. He develops rapid irregular wide complex tachycardia. Which drug is CONTRAINDICATED?**
- A) Procainamide
- B) IV flecainide
- C) DC cardioversion
- D) IV verapamil

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D** — This is WPW syndrome complicated by atrial fibrillation (rapid, irregular, wide complex due to conduction via the accessory pathway). AV nodal blocking drugs — digoxin, verapamil, adenosine, beta-blockers — are contraindicated as they can accelerate conduction via the accessory pathway, causing ventricular fibrillation. Procainamide (A) or DC cardioversion (C) is the correct management.

</details>

---

**Q10. A patient with paroxysmal palpitations has ECG showing regular narrow complex tachycardia at 180/min with P waves buried in the QRS. Vagal maneuvers fail. What is the drug of choice?**
- A) Amiodarone 300 mg IV
- B) Adenosine 6 mg rapid IV bolus
- C) Verapamil 5 mg IV
- D) Metoprolol 5 mg IV

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — This is AVNRT (AV nodal re-entrant tachycardia), the most common SVT. First-line after failed vagal maneuvers is adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push (can repeat with 12 mg). Adenosine has a half-life of ~10 seconds and transiently blocks the AV node. It is contraindicated in WPW (antidromic SVT) and asthma. Amiodarone (A) is for VT/VF or refractory SVT.

</details>

---

**Q11. A 70-year-old man has symptomatic bradycardia. His ECG shows constant PR interval with sudden non-conducted P waves (no progressive PR lengthening). What is the most appropriate management?**
- A) Observation; no treatment needed
- B) Atropine and observation
- C) Permanent pacemaker implantation
- D) Digoxin reduction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — This is Mobitz type II (second-degree AV block), characterized by a constant PR interval with sudden dropped beats. It originates below the AV node (His–Purkinje system) and is unpredictable — can progress to complete heart block without warning. A permanent pacemaker is always indicated. Mobitz I (Wenckebach) is benign (progressive PR lengthening before dropped beat) and usually does not require pacing.

</details>

**Q12. A 65-year-old man with AF has a CHADS-VASc score of 3. He has no contraindications to anticoagulation. Which statement about anticoagulation is CORRECT?**
- A) Aspirin alone is sufficient for stroke prevention
- B) Anticoagulation is indicated; DOACs preferred over warfarin
- C) Rate control with digoxin prevents thrombus formation
- D) Anticoagulation only if cardioversion planned

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — A CHADS-VASc score ≥2 in men (≥3 in women) warrants anticoagulation. DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) are preferred over warfarin in non-valvular AF due to comparable/superior efficacy and lower intracranial bleed risk. Aspirin (A) is not adequate for AF stroke prevention. Anticoagulation is independent of rate/rhythm control strategy (D).

</details>

## HYPERTENSION & VALVULAR DISEASE (Q13–Q16)

**Q13. A 32-year-old pregnant woman at 28 weeks develops BP of 156/98 mmHg. Which antihypertensive is SAFEST and preferred in pregnancy?**
- A) Enalapril
- B) Losartan
- C) Methyldopa
- D) Atenolol

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Methyldopa is the safest antihypertensive in pregnancy with the longest safety record. Labetalol and nifedipine are also used. ACEi (A) and ARBs (B) are absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy — they cause renal tubular dysplasia, oligohydramnios, and fetal renal failure. Atenolol (D) is associated with intrauterine growth restriction and avoided in most guidelines.

</details>

**Q14. A 45-year-old woman with known mitral stenosis presents with worsening dyspnea. Auscultation reveals a loud S1, opening snap, and mid-diastolic rumble at the apex. An earlier opening snap indicates what about MS severity?**
- A) Less severe MS (smaller pressure gradient)
- B) More severe MS (higher LA pressure, smaller mitral valve area)
- C) Mitral regurgitation coexisting
- D) Pulmonary hypertension only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — The opening snap occurs when the mitral valve opens in diastole. A higher left atrial pressure (as in severe MS) forces the valve open earlier in diastole → shorter A2–OS interval → earlier opening snap. Critical MS is defined as MVA <1 cm². S1 becomes soft when the valve is heavily calcified (pliable = loud S1).

</details>

**Q15. A 38-year-old man has an early diastolic murmur at the left sternal edge, wide pulse pressure (180/50 mmHg), and a bobbing head. De Musset's sign and Duroziez's sign are positive. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Mitral stenosis
- B) Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
- C) Aortic regurgitation
- D) Pulmonary regurgitation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Aortic regurgitation classically presents with an early diastolic murmur (left sternal edge, best heard sitting forward in expiration), wide pulse pressure, and peripheral signs: de Musset's (head bobbing), Corrigan's/water-hammer pulse, Duroziez's sign (femoral bruit), Quincke's sign (nail pulsation), Hill's sign (popliteal BP > brachial by >20 mmHg), Traube's sign (pistol shot over femoral).

</details>

**Q16. A 28-year-old woman has a mid-systolic click and late systolic murmur at the apex. Standing up from squatting makes the click occur earlier. Which is the most likely diagnosis and mechanism?**
- A) Mitral stenosis; increased LA pressure
- B) Aortic stenosis; increased turbulence
- C) Mitral valve prolapse; decreased LV preload shifts click earlier
- D) Tricuspid regurgitation; increased RV pressure

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — MVP produces a mid-systolic click + late systolic murmur. Standing or Valsalva decreases LV preload → LV becomes smaller → leaflets prolapse earlier → click moves toward S1 (earlier). Squatting or leg raise increases preload → click moves later toward S2. This dynamic behavior is pathognomonic for MVP.

</details>

## CARDIOMYOPATHY & PERICARDIAL DISEASE (Q17–Q20)

**Q17. A 32-year-old athlete collapses during exercise. Echo shows asymmetric septal hypertrophy (septum 22 mm), dynamic LVOT obstruction, and systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve. What is the drug of CHOICE for symptoms?**
- A) Digoxin
- B) Nifedipine
- C) Beta-blocker (metoprolol/atenolol)
- D) Dobutamine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — This is Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM). Beta-blockers are first-line for symptomatic relief — they reduce heart rate, improve diastolic filling, and reduce the LVOT gradient. Drugs to avoid: vasodilators (reduce afterload → worsen obstruction), positive inotropes like digoxin (D) and dobutamine (D) — worsen obstruction. Nifedipine (B) is a vasodilator and contraindicated.

</details>

**Q18. A 40-year-old man develops fever, sharp pleuritic chest pain relieved by sitting forward, and a friction rub. ECG shows saddle-shaped ST elevation in all leads except aVR. What is the earliest and most specific ECG finding in this condition?**
- A) ST elevation in all leads
- B) Electrical alternans
- C) PR segment depression
- D) Q waves in inferior leads

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — PR depression is the earliest and most specific ECG finding in acute pericarditis. It reflects atrial inflammation. Saddle-shaped (concave up) ST elevation in all leads except aVR (which shows ST depression) is characteristic. Electrical alternans (B) = cardiac tamponade. Q waves (D) = myocardial necrosis (MI). The most common cause of acute pericarditis is viral (Coxsackievirus B).

</details>

**Q19. A patient with known malignancy presents with hypotension, elevated JVP, and muffled heart sounds. ECG shows low voltage with alternating QRS amplitude. Which is the MOST appropriate immediate intervention?**
- A) IV furosemide
- B) IV fluids challenge only
- C) Pericardiocentesis
- D) Pericardiectomy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Beck's triad (hypotension + JVD + muffled heart sounds) + electrical alternans = cardiac tamponade. Pericardiocentesis is the definitive emergent treatment. IV furosemide (A) would worsen hypotension by reducing preload. IV fluids (B) may temporarily maintain CO but is not definitive. Pericardiectomy (D) is for constrictive pericarditis. Kussmaul's sign is absent in tamponade (present in constrictive pericarditis).

</details>

**Q20. A patient with constrictive pericarditis is examined. Which clinical sign differentiates constrictive pericarditis from cardiac tamponade?**
- A) Pulsus paradoxus
- B) Elevated JVP
- C) Kussmaul's sign (JVP rises with inspiration)
- D) Muffled heart sounds

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Kussmaul's sign (paradoxical rise in JVP with inspiration) is present in constrictive pericarditis but absent in cardiac tamponade. In tamponade, the pericardial fluid compresses the heart uniformly, and the JVP does not rise on inspiration (pulsus paradoxus is present instead). Constrictive pericarditis: prominent y-descent (Friedreich's sign), pericardial knock, calcification on CXR/CT (eggshell).

</details>
