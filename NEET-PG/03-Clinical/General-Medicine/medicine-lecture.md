## Heart Failure

### The Fundamental Problem

What is heart failure fundamentally trying to do to the patient? It is depriving the tissues of oxygen. That is the core disaster. The heart's sole job — its reason for existence — is to act as a pump that delivers oxygenated blood to every organ at a rate sufficient to meet metabolic demand. Heart failure is simply the state where the heart can no longer do that job adequately. Everything else — the swollen ankles, the breathlessness, the raised JVP — is either a downstream consequence of that failure or a consequence of the body's frantic, increasingly counterproductive attempts to compensate.

Let us start with the physics. Cardiac output is stroke volume multiplied by heart rate. Stroke volume is determined by three things: preload (how much the ventricle is filled before contraction), afterload (the resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood), and contractility (the intrinsic force of the myocardium). When the heart begins to fail, cardiac output drops. The body senses this as a threat — not unlike sensing haemorrhage — and activates a cascade of compensatory mechanisms that are, initially, life-saving, and eventually, lethal.

### The Compensatory Mechanisms — Brilliant in Principle, Disastrous in Practice

The first response is the **Frank-Starling mechanism**. The failing heart stretches. When myocardial fibres are stretched to a greater length before contraction, they contract with greater force — up to a point. Think of a rubber band: stretch it a little, and it snaps back harder. So the heart dilates, stretching the fibres, increasing the force of contraction, temporarily restoring cardiac output. This is why we see cardiac enlargement on the chest X-ray of a heart failure patient — it's not random, it's the heart desperately trying to use physics to compensate.

The second response is **sympathetic nervous system activation**. The failing heart means falling blood pressure. Baroreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid sinus fire to the vasomotor centre. Sympathetic output surges. Norepinephrine is released, causing tachycardia (increasing cardiac output via heart rate), peripheral vasoconstriction (maintaining blood pressure), and increased contractility. Short term, this saves your life. Long term, this chronic norepinephrine bath is directly toxic to cardiomyocytes — it causes apoptosis, hypertrophy, and electrical instability (arrhythmias). The very drug that saves you in acute failure (adrenaline) kills you slowly in chronic failure.

The third response is **RAAS activation**. Reduced renal perfusion activates the juxtaglomerular apparatus to release renin. Renin converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, ACE converts it to angiotensin II. Angiotensin II does two critical things: it causes intense vasoconstriction (raising blood pressure) and it stimulates aldosterone secretion from the adrenal cortex. Aldosterone causes sodium and water retention at the distal nephron, expanding blood volume, increasing venous return, increasing preload, which via Frank-Starling increases cardiac output. Clever. But in heart failure, this retention of fluid becomes the problem — it causes the pulmonary oedema and peripheral oedema that fill our wards.

The fourth response is **ventricular hypertrophy**. Chronically increased workload on the myocardium (from high afterload, high sympathetic drive, angiotensin II's direct trophic effects) causes the muscle to hypertrophy. In pressure overload states like hypertension, you get concentric hypertrophy — the walls thicken inward, like a room with thickening walls getting smaller. In volume overload, you get eccentric hypertrophy — the chamber enlarges. Hypertrophied myocardium is stiffer, requires more oxygen, is more prone to arrhythmias, and has impaired relaxation. The compensation becomes the disease.

**Analogy:** Think of the compensatory mechanisms like a person running on credit cards to pay their bills. Initially it works — the spending continues, the bills get paid. But the interest accumulates. Eventually the debt is greater than the original problem. That is maladaptive remodelling — the neurohormonal responses designed to maintain cardiac output end up destroying the myocardium they were meant to protect.

### Left Heart Failure — Why It Causes Pulmonary Symptoms

The left ventricle receives oxygenated blood from the pulmonary veins via the left atrium, and pumps it into the aorta. When the left ventricle fails — whether from MI, hypertension, or cardiomyopathy — it cannot empty properly. Pressure builds up behind it. Left atrial pressure rises. Pulmonary venous pressure rises. Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure rises above 18 mmHg and fluid begins to leak from the pulmonary capillaries into the interstitium, and eventually into the alveoli themselves.

This is the mechanism of pulmonary oedema. The patient cannot breathe because their alveoli are full of fluid — the same alveoli that are supposed to be full of air. Dyspnoea on exertion occurs because exercise increases the demand for cardiac output, which the failing heart cannot meet, and also because exercise redistributes blood, increasing venous return and thus pulmonary vascular pressure. Orthopnoea — breathlessness on lying flat — occurs because lying down redistributes fluid from the peripheral vasculature into the central circulation, increasing preload and therefore pulmonary venous pressure. The patient sleeps on many pillows. Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea is the same mechanism but more dramatic: the patient wakes from sleep gasping because lying flat for hours has gradually increased pulmonary oedema until they are drowning.

**Clinical connection:** The classic chest X-ray of left heart failure tells the entire pathophysiology story. Upper lobe venous diversion (because high pulmonary venous pressure redirects blood to the upper lobes, which have lower resistance when distended). Kerley B lines (thickened interlobular septa from interstitial fluid). Bat-wing perihilar haziness (alveolar oedema). Cardiomegaly (from ventricular dilatation). Bilateral pleural effusions (fluid in the pleural space from high hydrostatic pressure). Read the X-ray, recite the mechanism.

### Right Heart Failure — Why It Causes Peripheral Symptoms

The right ventricle receives deoxygenated blood from the systemic veins and pumps it into the pulmonary circulation. When the right ventricle fails — whether from left heart failure causing pulmonary hypertension (the most common cause), primary pulmonary hypertension, or pulmonary embolism — blood backs up behind it. Systemic venous pressure rises.

Elevated jugular venous pressure is simply the manometer of the right atrium — it reflects right-sided filling pressures. When right atrial pressure is high, the venous column in the jugular veins is elevated. You can see the pressure with your eyes at the bedside. Hepatomegaly occurs because the hepatic veins drain into the inferior vena cava, which is under elevated pressure — the liver engorges. Chronic hepatic congestion causes "cardiac cirrhosis." Ankle oedema occurs because high venous pressure, combined with low oncotic pressure from the resultant poor liver function, drives fluid into the interstitial space — most prominently at the ankles, where hydrostatic pressure is also maximal.

### Drug Therapy — First Principles

Understanding the drugs becomes trivial once you know the pathophysiology.

**ACE inhibitors** (enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril): The RAAS is activated in heart failure and is directly harmful to the myocardium (angiotensin II causes fibrosis and hypertrophy). ACE inhibitors block the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, cutting off this harmful neurohumoral signal. The result: vasodilation (reduced afterload → heart pumps against less resistance), reduced aldosterone secretion (less fluid retention → reduced preload), and reversal of maladaptive remodelling. They have been proven to reduce mortality in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF). The side effect that every student knows — dry cough — occurs because ACE also breaks down bradykinin. Blocked ACE → bradykinin accumulates → irritates bronchial receptors → cough. ARBs (losartan, valsartan) block the angiotensin II receptor directly, so they don't cause bradykinin accumulation and are used when patients can't tolerate ACEi.

**Beta-blockers** (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol): The chronic sympathetic overstimulation in heart failure causes direct cardiomyocyte toxicity, arrhythmias, and tachycardia. Beta-blockers block the beta-1 adrenergic receptor on the cardiomyocyte, protecting it from this norepinephrine bath. They slow the heart rate (allowing better diastolic filling), reduce myocardial oxygen demand, and have anti-arrhythmic effects. Crucially — and this is an important point — beta-blockers must be started at low doses when the patient is clinically stable and euvolaemic. Starting them in acute decompensated heart failure can precipitate acute cardiogenic shock by acutely dropping contractility. The body was relying on sympathetic drive to maintain cardiac output; take that away suddenly and the patient crashes.

**Diuretics** (furosemide): These don't improve mortality, but they improve symptoms dramatically. Loop diuretics block the Na-K-2Cl transporter in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle, causing massive sodium and water excretion. This reduces the excess fluid load — the preload — that was causing pulmonary oedema and peripheral oedema. The patient breathes again. Watch for hypokalaemia, which worsens in the setting of secondary hyperaldosteronism.

**Aldosterone antagonists** (spironolactone, eplerenone): These block aldosterone at the collecting duct, reducing sodium retention. More importantly, aldosterone has direct fibrotic effects on the myocardium and vasculature — blocking it reverses some of the remodelling. The RALES trial showed spironolactone reduced mortality by 30% in severe heart failure. Watch for hyperkalaemia, especially when combined with ACEi.

**SGLT2 inhibitors** (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin): These are the newest addition and arguably the most intellectually fascinating. Originally diabetes drugs, they block the sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 in the proximal tubule of the kidney, preventing glucose reabsorption and causing glucosuria. The osmotic diuresis they produce reduces intravascular volume and thus preload — similar to a diuretic but with a different mechanism. But the benefits in heart failure go far beyond simple diuresis. SGLT2 inhibitors appear to shift myocardial metabolism from glucose to fatty acid oxidation (a more oxygen-efficient fuel in the failing heart), reduce inflammation, reduce myocardial fibrosis, and protect the kidney. The DAPA-HF and EMPEROR-Reduced trials showed significant mortality benefits even in non-diabetic heart failure patients. They are now standard of care in HFrEF regardless of diabetes status.

> **NEET PG Insight:** The four pillars of HFrEF treatment that reduce mortality are ACEi/ARBs (or sacubitril-valsartan), beta-blockers, aldosterone antagonists, and SGLT2 inhibitors. Know the trial names: CONSENSUS/SOLVD (ACEi), MERIT-HF/CIBIS (beta-blockers), RALES (spironolactone), DAPA-HF (dapagliflozin). Diuretics improve symptoms but not mortality.

## Coronary Artery Disease and Acute MI

### Atherosclerosis — The Root of All Cardiovascular Evil

What is coronary artery disease fundamentally trying to do to the patient? It is starving the myocardium of oxygen. To understand how, you must start with atherosclerosis — not as a plumbing problem of fat clogging a pipe, but as an inflammatory disease of the arterial wall.

The story begins with endothelial dysfunction. Risk factors — hypertension, dyslipidaemia, smoking, diabetes, age — damage the arterial endothelium. Damaged endothelium loses its antithrombotic, anti-inflammatory properties and becomes sticky. LDL cholesterol, particularly small dense LDL, enters the subintimal space and undergoes oxidative modification. Oxidised LDL is recognised by macrophages (derived from circulating monocytes that were recruited by endothelial-derived chemokines) as foreign material. Macrophages engulf the oxidised LDL via scavenger receptors — but unlike normal phagocytosis, this process is unregulated. The macrophages become engorged with lipid droplets, transforming into foam cells. Foam cells die, releasing their lipid contents, forming the lipid core of the atherosclerotic plaque. Smooth muscle cells migrate from the media, laying down a fibrous cap over the lipid core. This is the stable plaque.

The **vulnerable plaque** is the dangerous one. It has a large, soft, lipid-rich necrotic core and a thin fibrous cap — like a pimple about to burst. Inflammatory cells (macrophages, T-lymphocytes) secrete metalloproteinases that digest the fibrous cap. When the cap ruptures — triggered by haemodynamic shear stress, inflammation, even a cold morning causing sympathetic surge — the thrombogenic lipid core is exposed to the flowing blood. Platelets adhere and aggregate (primary haemostasis), the coagulation cascade is activated (secondary haemostasis), and a thrombus forms within seconds. If the thrombus is occlusive, you have a STEMI.

### STEMI Pathophysiology — The Electrical Story

A complete occlusion of a major coronary artery causes transmural ischaemia. From the inside of the myocardium out, three zones form. The **zone of necrosis** at the core: cells are irreversibly dead, K+ leaks out, Na+ and Ca2+ flood in, cell membranes rupture. These cells are electrically inert. The **zone of injury** surrounding it: cells are severely ischaemic but not yet dead. They cannot maintain their resting membrane potential — they are in a state of partial depolarisation. This manifests on the ECG as ST elevation, because the injured zone has a different baseline potential than the healthy myocardium, creating a current of injury. The **zone of ischaemia** outermost: mildly ischaemic cells that repolarise abnormally, manifesting as T-wave changes.

This three-zone model explains every ECG change in MI. ST elevation = zone of injury (actively dying myocardium — this is your emergency, call the cath lab). T-wave inversion = zone of ischaemia. Q waves develop as the zone of necrosis expands — the necrotic cells are electrically silent, so the ECG electrode overlying them sees only the opposing wall's electrical activity, manifesting as a pathological Q wave (representing an electrical "window" through dead tissue).

> **The Time-Muscle Equation:** In a STEMI, the occlusion is complete. Myocardium begins dying within 20 minutes of total ischaemia. By 6 hours, the entire territory at risk is usually infarcted. Every minute of delay costs approximately 1.7 million cardiomyocytes. "Time is muscle" is not a slogan — it is biology.

### Thrombolysis vs Primary PCI — The Logical Comparison

**Primary PCI** (percutaneous coronary intervention — balloon angioplasty and stenting): This is the preferred treatment when available within 120 minutes of first medical contact. A catheter is passed into the coronary artery; the clot is crossed with a guidewire; a balloon is inflated to crack open the occluded segment; a stent is deployed to hold it open. This works mechanically — it doesn't depend on the clot being fresh or fibrin-rich. PCI achieves TIMI-3 flow (complete reperfusion) in over 90% of cases.

**Thrombolysis** (streptokinase, alteplase, tenecteplase): These drugs activate plasminogen to plasmin, which dissolves fibrin clots. They work only on fresh, fibrin-rich clots — older organised thrombus is resistant. Hence the time window (best results within 3 hours of symptom onset, acceptable up to 12 hours). They achieve TIMI-3 flow in only 50-60% of cases. The major risk is haemorrhage — including intracranial haemorrhage (the most feared complication, occurring in ~1%). Used when PCI is not available within the time window.

The decision tree is simple: if a cath lab is available and the patient can be on the table within 120 minutes of first medical contact, do PCI. If not — and if there are no contraindications — thrombolyse now, transfer for "pharmaco-invasive" PCI.

### Complications of MI — Time-Based Logic

The complications of MI follow a beautiful temporal logic that you can derive from the pathophysiology.

**0-24 hours — Arrhythmias:** The ischaemic myocardium has abnormal automaticity and re-entry circuits because of inhomogeneous conduction — some cells are dead, some injured, some ischaemic, each with different electrical properties. This is the substrate for ventricular fibrillation and ventricular tachycardia. This is why MI patients die suddenly in the first few hours — VF before they reach hospital. This is why defibrillators in ambulances save lives. Reperfusion itself can cause arrhythmias (reperfusion arrhythmias — accelerated idioventricular rhythm is actually a good sign, indicating reperfusion).

**3-5 days — Papillary Muscle Rupture:** The papillary muscles — which tether the mitral valve leaflets via chordae tendineae — derive their blood supply from the terminal branches of the coronary arteries. In an inferior MI (RCA territory), the posteromedial papillary muscle (supplied by a single artery, the RCA) is at risk. After 3-5 days of ischaemia, the muscle undergoes necrosis and can rupture. The mitral valve becomes acutely incompetent — massive mitral regurgitation → acute pulmonary oedema → cardiogenic shock. Clinically: sudden haemodynamic deterioration 3-5 days post-MI with a new loud holosystolic murmur at the apex. Echo confirms it. Emergency surgical repair is required.

**5-10 days — Free Wall Rupture:** The necrotic myocardial wall (now replaced by soft, weak, inflammatory tissue in the early stages of healing — before it has been replaced by strong fibrous scar) is susceptible to rupture under the pressure of systolic contraction. Blood pours into the pericardial sac. A small amount of fluid in the rigid pericardium causes dramatic pressure rise — acute cardiac tamponade. Beck's triad: hypotension, elevated JVP, muffled heart sounds. Immediate pericardiocentesis as a temporising measure, emergency surgery. This is catastrophic and usually fatal. Ventricular septal rupture occurs by the same mechanism — the necrotic septum ruptures, creating a VSD with an acute left-to-right shunt, presenting with a new harsh pansystolic murmur at the left sternal edge.

**Weeks later — Dressler's Syndrome:** Several weeks after MI, some patients develop fever, pleuritic chest pain, and pericarditis. This is an autoimmune response — necrotic myocardial antigens, released during the MI, are presented to the immune system, triggering an antibody response against pericardial and pleural tissue. The treatment is anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs, colchicine, steroids if severe). Aspirin is preferred if the patient is already on it for the MI.

> **NEET PG Insight:** Know the timing of complications: 0-24h (arrhythmias), 1-3 days (extension of infarct, cardiac failure), 3-5 days (papillary muscle rupture, pericarditis), 5-10 days (free wall rupture, VSR), weeks (Dressler's, LV aneurysm). The anterior MI most commonly causes LV aneurysm (LAD territory = anterior wall → thin, akinetic, paradoxically bulging scar = aneurysm → mural thrombus → stroke).

## Hypertension

### First Principles of Blood Pressure

Blood pressure equals cardiac output multiplied by systemic vascular resistance. This is not a formula to memorise — it is an equation of physiology that tells you everything you need to know about hypertension. If blood pressure is chronically elevated, either cardiac output is elevated, or systemic vascular resistance is elevated, or both.

In essential (primary) hypertension — which accounts for 95% of all hypertension — the fundamental defect is increased systemic vascular resistance. This is driven by increased arteriolar tone. The arterioles — the small-resistance vessels — are chronically vasoconstricted, and over time their walls undergo structural changes: smooth muscle hypertrophy, wall thickening, narrowing of the lumen. The arterioles become fixed and non-compliant. Secondary causes (the other 5%) include renal artery stenosis (↑RAAS → ↑SVR), primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome: ↑aldosterone → Na+ retention → ↑blood volume → ↑CO), phaeochromocytoma (↑catecholamines → ↑CO and ↑SVR), and Cushing's syndrome. Always think of secondary causes in young patients with resistant hypertension.

### RAAS as the Driver of Hypertension

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system is not just important in heart failure — it is central to the pathophysiology of hypertension. Angiotensin II is one of the most potent vasoconstrictors known to biology. It causes direct arteriolar vasoconstriction (raising SVR), stimulates aldosterone (raising blood volume), promotes sympathetic activity (raising CO), and has direct trophic effects on the arterial wall (causing smooth muscle hypertrophy — the structural changes that make hypertension self-perpetuating). Understanding this explains why ACEi and ARBs are so effective in hypertension — they attack the central driver.

### End-Organ Damage — The Price of Chronic Hypertension

The consequences of untreated hypertension are written in the organs it damages. The **heart** must pump against chronically elevated afterload. This causes left ventricular hypertrophy — concentric hypertrophy, with the walls thickening inward, reducing the cavity. The LVH myocardium is stiff, leading to diastolic dysfunction (the ventricle can't relax properly to fill). The thickened myocardium also outgrows its blood supply, becoming ischaemic. Hypertension accelerates coronary atherosclerosis. The result: ischaemic heart disease, heart failure.

The **brain** receives high-pressure blood flow. Chronic hypertension causes lipohyalinosis of the small penetrating arteries (particularly the lenticulostriate arteries, which supply the basal ganglia and internal capsule) — their walls weaken and they develop microaneurysms (Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms). These can rupture — hypertensive intracerebral haemorrhage, most commonly in the basal ganglia. Alternatively, the small arteries can occlude — lacunar infarcts. Large vessel disease from accelerated atherosclerosis causes thromboembolic strokes.

The **kidneys** are both victims and perpetuators. High pressure damages the glomerular capillaries (hypertensive nephrosclerosis — afferent arteriolar thickening, glomerular ischaemia, tubular atrophy). Damaged kidneys produce more renin, driving up blood pressure further — a vicious cycle. CKD and hypertension feed each other until the kidneys fail.

The **eyes** show arteriolar changes on fundoscopy — the one place you can directly visualise arterioles in vivo. Silver-wiring (increased light reflex from thickened arteriolar walls), arteriovenous nipping (where arterioles cross veins, compressing them), flame haemorrhages, cotton-wool spots (microinfarcts of the nerve fibre layer), and disc oedema in malignant hypertension.

**Clinical connection:** Hypertensive emergency (malignant hypertension) is defined by end-organ damage — encephalopathy (hypertensive PRES), acute kidney injury, aortic dissection, acute LVF — regardless of the exact BP number. The goal is controlled, gradual BP reduction (not rapid normalisation, which can cause cerebral ischaemia due to the shifted autoregulation curve).

### Drug Selection by Mechanism

Every antihypertensive drug addresses either CO or SVR. Once you know which, drug selection becomes rational.

**Calcium channel blockers** (amlodipine, nifedipine, diltiazem): Block voltage-gated L-type calcium channels on vascular smooth muscle cells. Calcium is necessary for smooth muscle contraction — block calcium entry → smooth muscle relaxes → vasodilation → ↓SVR. Dihydropyridines (amlodipine, nifedipine) are vascular-selective. Non-dihydropyridines (diltiazem, verapamil) also affect the heart, slowing heart rate. CCBs are particularly effective in elderly patients and black patients (who tend to have low-renin hypertension) and in patients with angina (vasodilation reduces afterload and myocardial oxygen demand).

**Beta-blockers** (atenolol, metoprolol): Block beta-1 adrenergic receptors. In the heart: reduce heart rate and contractility → ↓CO. They also reduce renin secretion from the juxtaglomerular apparatus (juxtaglomerular cells have beta-1 receptors) → ↓RAAS activity. Less preferred as first-line antihypertensives now (poorer stroke protection compared to other agents, and metabolic side effects — weight gain, dyslipidaemia, masking hypoglycaemia). But essential when the patient also has angina, post-MI, or heart failure with reduced EF.

**ACE inhibitors / ARBs:** Block RAAS — ↓angiotensin II → vasodilation (↓SVR) + ↓aldosterone → ↓sodium retention (↓volume → ↓CO). First choice in patients with diabetes (renoprotective — reduce intraglomerular pressure by dilating the efferent arteriole), CKD with proteinuria, heart failure, post-MI.

**Thiazide diuretics** (hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide, chlorthalidone): Block Na-Cl co-transporter in the distal convoluted tubule → ↑urinary sodium and water excretion → ↓blood volume → ↓CO. Acutely the effect is haemodynamic (volume depletion); chronically, the BP-lowering effect persists even after volume is restored, suggesting additional vasodilatory mechanisms. Effective, cheap, and evidence-based. Watch for hypokalaemia, hyperuricaemia (gout), and glucose intolerance.

> **NEET PG Insight:** Compelling indications — ACEi/ARBs: diabetic nephropathy, post-MI LV dysfunction, proteinuric CKD, heart failure. Beta-blockers: angina, post-MI, tachyarrhythmias, heart failure. CCBs: elderly, isolated systolic hypertension, angina, Raynaud's. Thiazides: elderly, isolated systolic hypertension, osteoporosis (they reduce urinary calcium excretion). Contraindications: beta-blockers in asthma; ACEi in bilateral renal artery stenosis (removing efferent arteriole tone → acute kidney injury); spironolactone + ACEi → hyperkalaemia risk.

## Diabetes Mellitus

### Type 1 vs Type 2 — From First Principles

What is diabetes fundamentally trying to do to the patient? It is denying cells access to glucose while paradoxically flooding the blood with it. The mechanisms by which this catastrophe occurs differ between Type 1 and Type 2, and understanding this difference explains everything — symptoms, complications, treatment.

**Type 1 DM** is autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells. Genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR3, HLA-DR4) combined with an environmental trigger (viral infection — perhaps Coxsackievirus B4 — molecular mimicry) causes T-lymphocytes to attack and destroy the insulin-producing beta cells. By the time of clinical presentation, over 80-90% of beta cells have been destroyed. Insulin production is essentially zero. Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells (GLUT-4 on muscle and fat cells is insulin-dependent). Glucose accumulates in the blood. Cells, starving of glucose despite being surrounded by it, switch to fat metabolism. This is the setting for DKA.

**Type 2 DM** is more complex and more common (90% of all diabetes). It begins with insulin resistance — the peripheral tissues (muscle, fat, liver) stop responding normally to insulin. To maintain normal blood glucose, the pancreatic beta cells must secrete more and more insulin (compensatory hyperinsulinaemia). For years, the beta cells succeed — blood glucose remains normal but insulin levels are high. Eventually, the chronically over-worked, glucolipotoxic beta cells begin to fail. Insulin secretion falls. Blood glucose rises. The disease declares itself.

**Analogy:** Insulin resistance is like shouting at someone who is wearing earplugs. The pancreas shouts louder (more insulin) to be heard. Eventually it loses its voice (beta cell failure). Type 2 diabetes is the progressive loss of that voice.

### DKA — The Biochemical Catastrophe

DKA is the most intellectually beautiful example of biochemistry causing clinical disease. Let us trace the logic.

Absent (or severely deficient) insulin → the insulin:glucagon ratio crashes → glucagon dominates → the liver receives the signal "starvation emergency." The liver activates glycogenolysis (breaking down glycogen stores), gluconeogenesis (making glucose from amino acids, lactate, glycerol), and most critically, **ketogenesis**. Fat cells, sensing insulin absence (insulin normally suppresses hormone-sensitive lipase), undergo massive lipolysis, releasing free fatty acids into the bloodstream. The liver converts these free fatty acids into ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone) via beta-oxidation. Ketone bodies are mildly acidic. As they accumulate in the bloodstream, they overwhelm the bicarbonate buffer system. Blood pH falls. **Anion gap metabolic acidosis** develops.

Now derive the clinical features. **Polyuria and polydipsia**: hyperglycaemia exceeds the renal threshold (~10 mmol/L) → glucosuria → osmotic diuresis → dehydration → compensatory thirst. **Weight loss and weakness**: no insulin means cells can't use glucose → they break down protein and fat for energy → catabolism → weight loss. **Kussmaul breathing**: the body detects metabolic acidosis and responds by hyperventilating to blow off CO₂ (a volatile acid), compensating for the fixed acid load. Deep, sighing breaths — Kussmaul respiration. **Fruity breath**: acetone is volatile and exhaled through the lungs — the classic fruity or pear-drop smell. **Nausea and vomiting**: acidosis directly irritates the gastrointestinal tract, and the abdominal pain in DKA can mimic an acute abdomen (always reconsider the abdominal signs after the DKA is treated).

**DKA vs HHS — The Contrast:** Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS) occurs in Type 2 DM where there is just enough insulin to prevent ketogenesis, but not enough to control glucose. Glucose rises to astronomically high levels (>30-40 mmol/L), causing extreme hyperosmolarity and dehydration. Because insulin is present (even if inadequate), fat breakdown is suppressed and ketone bodies don't form — no acidosis, no Kussmaul breathing. The mortality in HHS is higher than DKA (partly because patients are older) but the biochemistry is less dramatic.

### DKA Treatment — Every Step Has a Reason

**IV fluids first**: The patient is profoundly dehydrated (4-6 litres of fluid deficit typical). Normal saline is used initially to restore circulatory volume. Do not start insulin before correcting hypovolaemia — insulin drives glucose into cells (lowering plasma glucose) and shifts water intracellularly, worsening hypovolaemia.

**Insulin**: IV insulin infusion (0.1 units/kg/hour). Insulin does three critical things: it suppresses glucagon, stopping ketogenesis; it facilitates glucose uptake into cells; and it drives potassium into cells along with glucose. This last point is critical for the next discussion.

**Potassium monitoring and replacement**: The DKA patient is typically total-body potassium depleted (osmotic diuresis causes massive K+ loss in urine). Paradoxically, serum K+ may appear normal or even elevated on presentation because acidosis drives K+ out of cells (H+ enters cells, K+ exits to maintain electrical neutrality — K+/H+ exchange). When you give insulin and correct the acidosis, K+ floods back into cells. If the patient is not receiving potassium supplementation, serum K+ can drop precipitously, causing life-threatening hypokalaemia and arrhythmias. This is why potassium is checked and replaced continuously during DKA management.

> **NEET PG Insight:** DKA — anion gap metabolic acidosis + hyperglycaemia + ketonaemia. Calculate anion gap: Na - (Cl + HCO₃) — normal is 8-12 mEq/L. In DKA it is elevated (ketones are unmeasured anions). Key: check potassium before starting insulin, replace K+ as the insulin brings it into cells. Cerebral oedema is a dreaded complication of DKA treatment, particularly in children — caused by overly rapid correction of hyperosmolarity.

### Long-Term Complications — The Microvascular and Macrovascular Divide

Chronic hyperglycaemia causes damage through several mechanisms: non-enzymatic glycation of proteins (HbA1c reflects this — glycated haemoglobin), formation of advanced glycation end-products (AGEs), polyol pathway activation, and oxidative stress. These mechanisms preferentially damage the endothelium of small blood vessels (microangiopathy) — retina, kidneys, peripheral nerves — and accelerate atherosclerosis in large vessels (macroangiopathy — MI, stroke, peripheral arterial disease).

**Diabetic nephropathy** follows a logical progression: hyperfiltration (early, due to efferent arteriolar dilation under the influence of hyperglycaemia, increasing GFR) → glomerular hypertrophy → mesangial expansion (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules on biopsy) → progressive proteinuria (microalbuminuria → macroalbuminuria) → declining GFR → end-stage renal disease. ACEi/ARBs protect the kidney by reducing intraglomerular pressure (by dilating the efferent arteriole less — actually by reducing the intense RAAS-driven efferent constriction, normalising filtration pressure).

## Stroke and Neurology

### The Brain's Impossible Vulnerability

What is stroke fundamentally trying to do to the patient? It is killing neurons in real time. The brain is one of the most metabolically active organs in the body, consuming 20% of the body's oxygen and glucose despite comprising only 2% of body weight. Unlike muscle, which can function anaerobically for a period, neurons are almost entirely dependent on oxidative metabolism. They have no meaningful glycogen stores. They cannot survive more than a few minutes of total ischaemia.

Cerebral blood flow is normally maintained by autoregulation — the intrinsic ability of cerebral arterioles to maintain constant flow over a wide range of systemic blood pressures (60-150 mmHg mean arterial pressure). In chronic hypertension, the autoregulatory curve shifts rightward — the brain adapts to running at higher pressures, but becomes vulnerable to hypoperfusion at pressures that would be perfectly adequate in a normotensive person. This has direct clinical consequences: in a hypertensive patient with acute stroke, you do not aggressively lower the blood pressure (unless it is extreme) because you may worsen the ischaemia by dropping below their shifted autoregulatory threshold.

### The Penumbra Concept — The Reason Thrombolysis Works

When a cerebral artery is acutely occluded, blood flow drops in the territory it supplies. At the core of the territory — where flow is most severely reduced (below 10 ml/100g/min) — neurons die within minutes. Irreversible infarction occurs here essentially immediately. But surrounding the core is a zone of tissue where blood flow is severely reduced but not zero — collateral circulation from adjacent vascular territories provides a trickle of flow. In this **ischaemic penumbra**, neurons are electrically silent (they have shut down their activity to reduce energy consumption) but metabolically viable. They are not dead. They can be rescued.

The penumbra is the therapeutic target of thrombolysis. If you open the blocked artery quickly enough, you restore flow to the penumbra before those neurons die. The therapeutic window for intravenous tPA (alteplase) is 4.5 hours from symptom onset. Beyond 4.5 hours, the penumbra has largely infarcted — the tissue that you would be "rescuing" is already dead. Worse, reperfusing already-infarcted tissue causes haemorrhagic transformation (blood flooding into dead tissue whose blood-brain barrier has been destroyed) and reperfusion injury (free radical burst). You cause more harm than good.

**Clinical connection:** This is why the phrase "the patient woke up with the symptoms" is a problem. If the patient went to sleep at 10pm feeling fine and woke at 6am with a hemiplegia, the time of onset is unknown — it could have been any time during the night. The last known well time is 10pm, making the onset potentially 8 hours prior. You cannot safely thrombolyse. Advanced imaging with perfusion CT or MRI can identify the penumbra (diffusion-perfusion mismatch) and guide treatment decisions even in wake-up stroke or late-presenting stroke.

### Ischaemic vs Haemorrhagic Stroke — The Critical Distinction

This distinction must be made before any treatment, because the treatments are diametrically opposed. Thrombolysis is life-saving in ischaemic stroke and lethal in haemorrhagic stroke (you are dissolving the clot that is plugging the ruptured vessel). This is why CT of the head is mandatory before thrombolysis.

**Ischaemic stroke** (85% of all strokes): a clot (thrombus or embolus) occludes a cerebral artery. The brain tissue supplied becomes pale, soft, and eventually liquefactive necrotic. Cardioembolic stroke (from AF — clot in left atrial appendage, embolises to brain) tends to cause large territory infarcts. Small vessel disease (lacunar infarcts from lipohyalinosis of penetrating arteries in hypertensives/diabetics) causes small, deep infarcts with characteristic pure motor, pure sensory, or ataxic-hemiparesis syndromes.

**Haemorrhagic stroke** (15%): a blood vessel ruptures. Blood in the brain parenchyma acts as an expanding mass lesion, causing pressure damage beyond the volume of blood itself. In intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH), the most common cause is hypertension (Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysm rupture, typically in basal ganglia, thalamus, cerebellum, pons). In subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH), the most common cause is rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm at the circle of Willis — presenting with the classic "thunderclap headache" (worst headache of life, instantaneous onset). CT detects SAH with very high sensitivity in the first 6-12 hours; if negative, lumbar puncture for xanthochromia (yellow discolouration from bilirubin breakdown of blood in CSF) is required.

> **NEET PG Insight:** AF causes cardioembolic stroke (emboli lodge in MCA most commonly → contralateral hemiplegia + hemisensory loss + homonymous hemianopia + aphasia if dominant hemisphere). Anticoagulation with warfarin (target INR 2-3) or DOACs reduces embolic stroke risk in AF by ~65%. Know CHA₂DS₂-VASc score for deciding when to anticoagulate.

## Rheumatology

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus — A Disease of Immune Complexes

What is SLE fundamentally trying to do to the patient? It is directing the immune system against the patient's own tissues — particularly against nuclear material. To understand SLE, you must understand why nuclear antigens become immunogenic.

Normally, cells undergoing apoptosis package their nuclear contents neatly into apoptotic bodies, which are rapidly cleared by phagocytes. In SLE, this clearance mechanism is defective. Apoptotic nuclear material — including double-stranded DNA, histones, RNA — accumulates and persists. This prolonged exposure of nuclear antigens to the immune system, combined with genetic defects in immune regulation (complement deficiencies, defective regulatory T cells, interferon pathway hyperactivation), leads to the production of autoantibodies: anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, anti-Ro, anti-La, antiphospholipid antibodies.

Anti-dsDNA antibodies are the ones that cause nephritis — and the mechanism is beautiful in its logic. Anti-dsDNA antibodies form immune complexes with their antigens. These immune complexes circulate in the blood and deposit preferentially in the glomeruli (because of the high filtration pressure and the mesangial affinity for immune complexes). Deposited immune complexes activate complement (the C1q-C4-C3 cascade) → membrane attack complex formation → glomerular injury → lupus nephritis. The pattern of deposition determines the class of nephritis (WHO classification I-VI). Class III and IV (diffuse proliferative GN) are the most severe and require aggressive immunosuppression.

**The butterfly rash** (malar rash, sparing the nasolabial folds) is triggered by UV light. UV radiation damages keratinocytes → apoptosis is induced → nuclear material (the same antigens that drive the systemic autoimmunity) is exposed on the cell surface → anti-nuclear antibodies bind → complement activation → inflammatory rash. The distribution (malar prominence, sparing nasolabial folds) reflects the pattern of UV exposure on the face.

**Antiphospholipid syndrome** — seen in ~30% of SLE patients — is caused by antibodies against phospholipid-binding proteins (beta-2 glycoprotein I, prothrombin). These antibodies paradoxically cause thrombosis (not bleeding) — arterial and venous. The name "lupus anticoagulant" is confusing — it prolongs the APTT in vitro (hence "anticoagulant") but causes thrombosis in vivo. Recurrent miscarriages (thrombosis of placental vessels), DVT, PE, stroke, and livedo reticularis are the hallmarks. Treatment: lifelong anticoagulation with warfarin.

> **NEET PG Insight:** ANA is sensitive for SLE (present in >95%) but not specific. Anti-dsDNA is specific (>95%) and tracks disease activity (titres rise with flares, fall with remission). Anti-Sm is highly specific for SLE but less sensitive. Anti-Ro/La → Sjögren's features, neonatal lupus. Drug-induced lupus: hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid — anti-histone antibodies, no nephritis/CNS disease.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis — The Synovial Inflammation Cascade

RA is fundamentally a disease of synovial inflammation. The target is the synovium — the lining of joints. But unlike the immune complex deposition of SLE, RA is driven by cellular immunity: CD4+ T helper cells (particularly Th17 cells) and macrophages are the primary drivers.

The story begins in a genetically susceptible person (HLA-DR4 — the "shared epitope" on the HLA-DR molecule that preferentially presents citrullinated peptides to T cells). An environmental trigger (smoking, periodontitis with Porphyromonas gingivalis, viral infection) initiates an immune response. Citrullinated proteins (proteins in which arginine has been converted to citrulline by peptidyl-arginine deiminase — PAD enzymes, activated by smoking) are presented to T cells. These T cells recognise citrullinated self-antigens as foreign. Autoimmunity begins.

In the joint, activated macrophages and T cells produce a torrent of pro-inflammatory cytokines: **TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-17**. These cytokines do several things: they cause the synovial lining cells (synoviocytes) to proliferate, creating the **pannus** — a mass of invasive inflammatory tissue that grows over the articular cartilage like ivy over a wall. The pannus is not just inflammatory; it is invasive. It secretes metalloproteinases and cathepsins that directly digest cartilage matrix. Osteoclasts, activated by RANKL (produced by the synovium under cytokine stimulation), erode the bone at the joint margins — the characteristic juxta-articular erosions seen on X-ray.

The systemic effects of RA — anaemia of chronic disease, elevated ESR/CRP, fatigue, weight loss, fever — are all consequences of the same pro-inflammatory cytokines (particularly IL-6) spilling into the circulation. IL-6 drives hepatic acute-phase protein production (CRP, fibrinogen, hepcidin — hepcidin blocks iron release from macrophages, causing the anaemia of chronic disease).

**Clinical connection:** The pattern of joint involvement distinguishes RA from other arthropathies. RA affects small joints of the hands (MCP and PIP joints, but characteristically spares the DIP joints — this distinguishes it from osteoarthritis). Symmetrical (both hands). Morning stiffness lasting over 1 hour (the synovitis is worse after inactivity — movement "warms up" the joint; this is opposite to OA where pain worsens with use and improves with rest).

**Treatment logic from mechanism:** If the disease is driven by TNF-α, IL-6, and activated T cells — target these. Methotrexate (first-line DMARD) inhibits dihydrofolate reductase → reduces folate for rapidly dividing cells (like the proliferating synoviocytes) → also has anti-inflammatory effects via adenosine pathway. Anti-TNF biologics (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept) neutralise TNF-α. Anti-IL-6 (tocilizumab). Abatacept (blocks T-cell co-stimulation). JAK inhibitors (baricitinib, tofacitinib) block the intracellular signalling pathways of multiple cytokines simultaneously.

> **NEET PG Insight:** Anti-CCP (anti-citrullinated protein antibody) antibodies are the most specific test for RA (>95% specific). Present years before clinical disease. RF (rheumatoid factor — IgM against IgG) is sensitive (~70-80%) but not specific (positive in many conditions: SLE, Sjögren's, chronic infections, normal elderly). Seronegative RA exists (RF and anti-CCP negative) — diagnosed on clinical grounds.

## Pneumonia and Respiratory Medicine

### The Lung's Fundamental Vulnerability

The lungs are the only internal organ that is directly exposed to the external environment with every breath. Approximately 10,000 litres of air — carrying bacteria, viruses, fungi, particles, and toxins — pass through the lungs every day. That the lungs remain sterile below the carina in a healthy person is a remarkable feat of immunological architecture. Pneumonia occurs when this defence system is overwhelmed.

The defences are layered. The upper airway filters large particles — nasal turbinates, mucociliary escalator. The lower airway relies on the cough reflex (mechanically ejects particles), mucociliary clearance (the cilia of the bronchial epithelium beat in coordinated waves, moving the mucus blanket and its trapped particles upward toward the glottis at approximately 1mm per minute), secretory IgA (in the mucus — opsonises inhaled organisms), and alveolar macrophages (the final line — they patrol the alveolar surface, engulf and kill inhaled organisms). When any of these defences fail — aspiration (alcohol, anaesthesia, neurological disease bypasses the cough reflex), viral upper respiratory tract infection (destroys the mucociliary epithelium), immunosuppression (depletes alveolar macrophages), or an overwhelming inoculum — pneumonia can establish itself.

### Community-Acquired Pneumonia — The Bug, the Host, the Damage

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is defined as pneumonia presenting in a patient who has not been hospitalised in the preceding 48 hours. The most common causative organism is **Streptococcus pneumoniae** (pneumococcus) — and understanding how this organism causes disease illuminates the entire clinical picture. Pneumococcus has a polysaccharide capsule that makes it resistant to phagocytosis. It colonises the nasopharynx. In susceptible individuals, it aspirates micro-droplets into the lower airways, where the alveolar macrophages are overwhelmed. The organism activates complement, triggers an intense inflammatory response — neutrophil recruitment, cytokine storm, capillary leak. The alveoli fill with proteinaceous exudate and neutrophils. This is **lobar consolidation** — the solidification of an entire lobe or segment of lung with inflammatory material.

The four classical pathological stages of lobar pneumonia tell the story chronologically. **Congestion** (day 1-2): vascular congestion, initial oedema — the lung is heavy and wet. **Red hepatisation** (day 3-4): the alveoli are filled with red blood cells, neutrophils, and fibrin — the lung looks like liver (hence "hepatisation"), red because of the erythrocytes. **Grey hepatisation** (day 5-7): red cells lyse, the exudate becomes grey and fibrinous — the lung is still consolidated but the red colour is gone. **Resolution** (day 8 onwards): macrophages clear the debris, the exudate is reabsorbed via the lymphatics, and normal aeration is restored. This resolution — complete in a healthy host — is what distinguishes pneumonia from abscess (where the central part liquefies into pus).

**Clinical connection:** The presentation of pneumococcal pneumonia follows the pathology. Fever (cytokines — IL-1, TNF-α — reset the hypothalamic thermostat). Pleuritic chest pain (the parietal pleura, which is pain-sensitive, is inflamed — unlike the visceral pleura, which has no somatic innervation). Productive cough with rust-coloured sputum (the characteristic "rusty" colour is from haemosiderin, the breakdown product of the red cells in the alveolar exudate). Decreased breath sounds, bronchial breathing, and dullness to percussion over the consolidated area (because consolidated lung transmits high-frequency sounds from the bronchi directly to the chest wall — normal lung filters these out, so bronchial breathing means solid tissue conducting sound).

**Analogy:** A healthy lung is like a sponge — full of air spaces. A consolidated lung is like a solid piece of rubber — it no longer filters or absorbs, it conducts everything directly. This is why voice sounds are louder and bronchial breathing is audible over consolidation.

### Atypical Pneumonia — The Different Presentation

Atypical pneumonia — caused by Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Legionella pneumophila, Chlamydophila pneumoniae, and viruses — presents differently from typical pneumococcal pneumonia. The organisms are atypical in that they lack a cell wall (Mycoplasma) or live intracellularly (Legionella, Chlamydophila), making them intrinsically resistant to beta-lactam antibiotics (which target cell wall synthesis).

Mycoplasma pneumoniae is the classic atypical — particularly in young adults, causing epidemics in closed communities (schools, military barracks, universities). The clinical picture is one of a "walking pneumonia" — the patient is not prostrated, the fever is lower grade, the cough is dry and hacking (not productive), and the systemic features dominate (headache, myalgia, malaise). The chest X-ray looks worse than the patient — bilateral, patchy, interstitial infiltrates (not the lobar consolidation of pneumococcal pneumonia), yet the patient is relatively well. Cold agglutinins (IgM antibodies against the I antigen on red cells, produced in response to Mycoplasma) are a classical (though insensitive and non-specific) serological marker.

Legionella pneumophila causes the most severe atypical pneumonia — Legionnaire's disease. Legionella lives in warm water (air conditioning cooling towers, hospital water systems, jacuzzis) and is inhaled as aerosols. It infects and replicates inside alveolar macrophages (it has evolved to avoid phagolysosome fusion). The clinical picture includes not just pneumonia but prominent extrapulmonary features: severe hyponatraemia (SIADH), deranged liver function tests, haematuria, and confusion (encephalopathy). The diagnosis is made by urinary Legionella antigen (detects only Legionella pneumophila serogroup 1, the most common cause of Legionnaire's disease). Treatment: fluoroquinolones or macrolides (both penetrate intracellularly, where the organism lives — beta-lactams cannot reach it).

> **NEET PG Insight:** CURB-65 scoring for CAP severity: Confusion (new onset), Urea >7 mmol/L, Respiratory rate ≥30/min, Blood pressure (systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60 mmHg), age ≥65. Score 0-1: low risk, outpatient treatment. Score 2: moderate risk, consider hospitalisation. Score 3-5: high risk, hospital admission, consider ICU for score 4-5. Treatment of CAP: amoxicillin for typical CAP; add macrolide (clarithromycin) or use a respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin) if atypical organisms suspected or severe disease.

## Kidney Disease and Nephrology

### Acute Kidney Injury — The Kidney Under Threat

What is AKI fundamentally trying to do to the patient? It is failing to regulate the composition of blood — specifically failing to excrete waste products (urea, creatinine, potassium) and regulate water, electrolyte, and acid-base balance. The consequences of this failure are what kills: hyperkalaemia (cardiac arrhythmias and arrest), metabolic acidosis (cardiovascular depression), fluid overload (pulmonary oedema), and uraemia (encephalopathy, pericarditis, platelet dysfunction).

AKI is classified by the site of the problem into **pre-renal, intrinsic renal, and post-renal** — and this classification is not merely academic, because the cause determines the treatment entirely. Getting the classification wrong leads to treating the wrong thing.

**Pre-renal AKI** is the most common type (accounting for ~55% of AKI). The kidney itself is structurally normal — the problem is inadequate blood supply. Whether from hypovolaemia (haemorrhage, dehydration), pump failure (cardiogenic shock), or vasodilation (septic shock), the renal perfusion pressure drops. The kidney responds in the only way it can: by conserving water and sodium as aggressively as possible. RAAS is activated, ADH is released. Urine becomes concentrated (osmolality >500 mOsm/kg), oliguria (urine output <400ml/day), and the urine sodium is very low (<20 mmol/L) because the kidney is maximally reabsorbing sodium. The fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) is <1% — the kidney is holding onto virtually all the sodium that filters through it. Creatinine rises because reduced GFR means less creatinine is excreted. If perfusion is restored quickly, the kidney recovers completely. If not, prolonged ischaemia causes tubular necrosis — the transition to intrinsic AKI.

**Intrinsic renal AKI** — damage to the kidney parenchyma itself. The most common cause is **acute tubular necrosis (ATN)**, which is the structural consequence of prolonged ischaemia (ischaemic ATN) or nephrotoxic injury (nephrotoxic ATN — aminoglycosides, contrast agents, NSAIDs, myoglobin in rhabdomyolysis). In ATN, the renal tubular cells — which are exquisitely oxygen-sensitive because of their high metabolic activity — undergo ischaemic necrosis. Dead tubular cells slough off and form casts that obstruct the tubular lumen. The tubules also lose their ability to reabsorb sodium — so the urine sodium is now HIGH (>40 mmol/L) and the FENa is >2%. The tubular cells regenerate over days to weeks if the patient survives. Other causes of intrinsic AKI: acute glomerulonephritis (immune complex deposition or anti-GBM antibody causing rapid glomerular destruction — "rapidly progressive GN"), acute interstitial nephritis (drug hypersensitivity — NSAIDs, penicillins, PPIs — causing inflammatory infiltration of the renal interstitium, characterised by eosinophilia and eosinophiluria).

**Post-renal AKI** — obstruction to urine flow. Any cause of bilateral ureteric obstruction or obstruction below the bladder. Bilateral ureteric obstruction: retroperitoneal fibrosis, malignancy (cervical cancer, prostate cancer, colorectal cancer compressing or invading the ureters), bilateral ureteric stones. Bladder outflow obstruction: BPH (the most common cause in elderly men), prostate cancer, blood clots, urethral stricture. Obstruction raises intratubular pressure back-pressure through the collecting system to Bowman's space, opposing filtration and reducing GFR. The key diagnostic test is renal ultrasonography — bilateral hydronephrosis (dilatation of the collecting system) confirms post-renal AKI. Urgent catheterisation or nephrostomy relieves the obstruction.

> **NEET PG Insight:** The most life-threatening consequence of AKI is hyperkalaemia — a serum K+ above 6.5 mEq/L (or any level with ECG changes) is a medical emergency. ECG changes in hyperkalaemia: peaked T waves (the earliest change — enhanced repolarisation), PR prolongation, QRS widening, sine wave pattern, ventricular fibrillation. Treatment sequence: calcium gluconate (membrane stabilisation — does not lower K+, just protects the heart while other measures work), insulin + dextrose (drives K+ into cells), salbutamol nebulisation (β2 agonist → Na-K-ATPase activation → K+ into cells), sodium bicarbonate (in acidotic patients — correcting acidosis drives K+/H+ exchange inward), kayexalate/patiromer (GI cation exchangers — removes K+ over hours), dialysis (definitive).

### Nephrotic Syndrome — The Protein Leak

Nephrotic syndrome is defined by the triad of massive proteinuria (>3.5g/24h in adults), hypoalbuminaemia (<30 g/L), and peripheral oedema. The logic of the syndrome flows from a single defect: the glomerular filtration barrier has become permeable to protein.

The glomerular filtration barrier consists of three layers: fenestrated glomerular endothelium (with its negatively charged glycocalyx), the glomerular basement membrane (GBM — rich in type IV collagen and negatively charged proteoglycans — heparan sulphate), and the podocyte foot processes linked by the slit diaphragm (containing nephrin, podocin — proteins that are crucial for barrier function). In nephrotic syndrome, this barrier is disrupted — specifically, the charge-selective and size-selective properties are lost. Albumin, which is normally repelled by the negative GBM charge, passes through in massive quantities. The kidney cannot reclaim it — the tubular reabsorptive capacity is overwhelmed.

Albumin is the dominant plasma protein maintaining oncotic pressure. When albumin falls, intravascular oncotic pressure drops. Starling's forces shift — fluid moves from the intravascular space into the interstitium (where oncotic pressure is now relatively higher). The result: oedema. The falling intravascular volume activates RAAS and ADH → sodium and water retention → the "underfill" hypothesis of nephrotic oedema. Paradoxically, many patients with nephrotic syndrome have normal or even expanded intravascular volume (the "overfill" hypothesis — primary renal sodium retention) — explaining why salt and water restriction plus diuretics are the cornerstone of oedema management.

**Clinical connection:** The most dangerous complication of nephrotic syndrome is thrombosis. Several mechanisms converge: loss of clotting inhibitors (protein C, protein S, antithrombin III — all lost in the urine because of their molecular weight); increased coagulation factor production by the liver (compensating for protein loss); platelet hyperreactivity. Renal vein thrombosis is the classic thrombotic complication — presents with acute flank pain, haematuria, and deterioration of renal function in a nephrotic patient. Deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism are also common. Prophylactic anticoagulation is considered when albumin falls below 20 g/L.

> **NEET PG Insight:** Common causes of nephrotic syndrome by age: children — **minimal change disease** (MCD, most common, idiopathic, associated with atopy and NSAIDs, responds dramatically to steroids, normal biopsy on light microscopy but effacement of podocyte foot processes on electron microscopy). Adults — **focal segmental glomerulosclerosis** (FSGS, most common in adults, particularly in Black patients, HIV, heroin use, obesity), **membranous nephropathy** (commonest in white adults, associated with HBV, malignancy, anti-PLA2R antibody — a specific biomarker), **diabetic nephropathy**, **amyloidosis**. In secondary nephrotic syndrome, always exclude malignancy (membranous nephropathy in elderly = paraneoplastic until proven otherwise) and infections (HBV, HCV, malaria).

## Infectious Diseases

### Tuberculosis — The Master of Disguise

What is tuberculosis fundamentally doing to the patient? It is exploiting the very cells designed to kill it — the macrophage — as its home and fortress, while slowly destroying whatever tissue it inhabits. Mycobacterium tuberculosis is among the most successful human pathogens because it has co-evolved with human immunity for millennia, developing sophisticated mechanisms to survive within the host.

Mycobacterium tuberculosis enters via respiratory droplets — as few as 1-10 bacilli are sufficient to establish infection in a susceptible host. The bacilli reach the alveoli and are engulfed by alveolar macrophages. A normal bacterium in a phagosome would be killed when the phagosome fuses with the lysosome (the "phagolysosome"). Mycobacterium tuberculosis prevents this fusion — it secretes proteins that inhibit phagolysosome maturation, so the bacterium sits in the phagosome, protected from the lysosomal enzymes, happily replicating. The infected macrophage activates T cells, which secrete interferon-gamma (IFN-γ). IFN-γ activates the macrophage, enhancing its bactericidal capacity. But even activated macrophages cannot fully clear the infection.

The cellular immune response cannot kill the organism, so it does the next best thing — it walls it off. Macrophages cluster around the focus of infection, fuse together into **multinucleated giant cells** (Langhans giant cells — the characteristic large cells with peripheral nuclei arranged in a horseshoe pattern), surrounded by a rim of lymphocytes and fibroblasts. This is the **granuloma**. The granuloma is the pathological signature of TB — and of all granulomatous diseases. At the centre of the granuloma, the macrophages — starved of oxygen in the avascular centre — die, releasing their proteolytic enzymes. The central tissue undergoes **caseous necrosis** — necrosis that looks like cottage cheese because of the high lipid content (from the mycobacterial cell wall lipids). This is **primary TB**.

In 90% of immunocompetent individuals, primary TB is contained. The granuloma calcifies, the infection is walled off. The patient has been infected (tuberculin skin test positive, IGRA positive) but not diseased. This is latent TB. Years later, if immunity wanes (malnutrition, HIV, diabetes, steroid therapy, ageing), the granuloma can break down — bacilli escape and multiply. This is **reactivation TB** (post-primary TB), which is responsible for most clinical TB seen in adults. Reactivation preferentially affects the **upper lobes** — because these have higher oxygen tension (the bacillus is a strict aerobe, thriving where pO₂ is highest) and relatively less lymphatic drainage.

The classical post-primary TB picture: cavitation in the upper lobe (the liquefied caseous material is expelled via the bronchus, leaving a cavity — a perfect aerobic culture medium), productive cough (initially dry, then with mucopurulent or blood-stained sputum), haemoptysis (from bronchial artery erosion — Rasmussen's aneurysm), night sweats (the classic 3am drenching diaphoresis from autonomic dysfunction), low-grade fever, and progressive weight loss (cachexia from the combined effects of anorexia, high metabolic rate from chronic inflammation, and muscle wasting from TNF-α).

> **NEET PG Insight:** Drug therapy — DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment Short-course). Standard regimen: 2 months of **RIPE** (Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol) — the intensive phase, then 4 months of **RI** (Rifampicin + Isoniazid) — the continuation phase. Total 6 months for pulmonary TB, longer for CNS or bone TB. Drug side effects: Rifampicin — orange urine/secretions, hepatotoxicity, enzyme inducer (reduces efficacy of OCP, warfarin); Isoniazid — peripheral neuropathy (prevented by pyridoxine/Vit B6), hepatotoxicity, psychosis; Pyrazinamide — hyperuricaemia (gout), hepatotoxicity; Ethambutol — optic neuritis (check visual acuity and colour vision before and during therapy). MDR-TB: resistant to at least Rifampicin and Isoniazid — requires 18-24 months of second-line drugs (fluoroquinolones, injectables).
