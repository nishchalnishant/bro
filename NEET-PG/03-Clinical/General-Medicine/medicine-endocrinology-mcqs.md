## DIABETES MELLITUS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 45-year-old obese man has FPG 118 mg/dL on two occasions, 2h OGTT 175 mg/dL, and HbA1c 6.1%. Which is the CORRECT interpretation?**
- A) Type 2 diabetes mellitus — start metformin immediately
- B) Pre-diabetes (impaired fasting glucose + impaired glucose tolerance) — lifestyle intervention
- C) Normal — no intervention needed
- D) Type 1 diabetes — start insulin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Pre-diabetes criteria: FPG 100–125 mg/dL (IFG), 2h OGTT 140–199 mg/dL (IGT), HbA1c 5.7–6.4%. Diabetes diagnosis requires ANY ONE: FPG ≥126, 2h PG ≥200, HbA1c ≥6.5%, or random PG ≥200 + symptoms. This patient has IFG (118 mg/dL) + IGT (175 mg/dL) + HbA1c 6.1% = pre-diabetes. Management: intensive lifestyle modification (diet + exercise), consider metformin in high-risk pre-diabetes.

</details>

**Q2. A 22-year-old woman with Type 1 DM presents with vomiting, abdominal pain, and fruity breath. Blood glucose is 420 mg/dL, pH 7.15, bicarbonate 12 mEq/L, anion gap 22. Serum potassium is 3.2 mEq/L on arrival. What is the FIRST priority before starting insulin?**
- A) IV bicarbonate immediately
- B) Potassium replacement to bring K⁺ ≥3.5 mEq/L before insulin
- C) Start insulin infusion regardless of potassium level
- D) Give glucagon to reverse the metabolic changes

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — DKA management: IV normal saline first for resuscitation, then insulin infusion (0.1 unit/kg/h). CRITICAL: Insulin must NOT be started until K⁺ ≥3.5 mEq/L — insulin drives K⁺ into cells, and starting insulin with low K⁺ can cause life-threatening hypokalemia → cardiac arrhythmias. Potassium replacement is added to IV fluids when K⁺ <5.5 mEq/L; aggressive replacement if K⁺ <3.5. Bicarbonate (A) only if pH <6.9.

</details>

**Q3. A 70-year-old Type 2 diabetic with known COPD has HbA1c 9.2%. His eGFR is 48 mL/min. Which drug requires DOSE REDUCTION or CAUTION in this patient due to lactic acidosis risk?**
- A) Glipizide (sulfonylurea)
- B) Metformin (biguanide)
- C) Sitagliptin (DPP-4 inhibitor)
- D) Pioglitazone (thiazolidinedione)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Metformin is contraindicated when eGFR <30 mL/min and requires caution/dose reduction when eGFR 30–45 mL/min (KDIGO guidelines) due to risk of lactic acidosis from reduced renal clearance. Also contraindicated in acute/chronic liver disease, alcohol excess, and iodinated contrast use. Sitagliptin (C) requires dose reduction in CKD but is generally safe. Pioglitazone (D) is contraindicated in heart failure and bladder cancer risk. Glipizide (A) — shorter-acting SU, safer in mild CKD than glibenclamide.

</details>

**Q4. A patient with Type 1 DM has consistently high blood glucose readings at 7 AM (fasting) but normal glucose at 3 AM. What is the most likely explanation?**
- A) Somogyi phenomenon (rebound hyperglycemia after nocturnal hypoglycemia)
- B) Dawn phenomenon (physiological GH surge causing early morning hyperglycemia)
- C) Waning of evening insulin
- D) Insufficient evening snack

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Dawn phenomenon: physiological rise in GH (and cortisol) between 3–8 AM → insulin resistance → early morning hyperglycemia without prior hypoglycemia. Blood glucose rises from ~3 AM. Somogyi phenomenon (A): nocturnal hypoglycemia (glucose drops at 3 AM) → counter-regulatory response (glucagon, epinephrine) → rebound morning hyperglycemia. Distinguishing them: measure 3 AM blood glucose — Dawn: normal/high at 3 AM; Somogyi: low at 3 AM.

</details>

**Q5. A 55-year-old diabetic has blurred central vision. Fundus exam shows neovascularization at the disc and vitreous hemorrhage. HbA1c is 10.2%. Which complication is this and what is the most common cause of blindness in diabetic retinopathy?**
- A) Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR); hard exudates
- B) Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR); vitreous hemorrhage/tractional detachment
- C) Macular edema; cotton wool spots — most common cause of blindness
- D) NPDR; dot hemorrhages

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B/C combined — C is technically the answer for most common cause of blindness.** Neovascularization = PDR (Proliferative DR). However, **macular edema is the most common cause of blindness in diabetic retinopathy** (can occur in both NPDR and PDR). PDR features: neovascularization, vitreous hemorrhage, tractional retinal detachment. NPDR: microaneurysms (earliest finding), dot/blot hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, hard exudates. Treatment: laser photocoagulation (PRP for PDR), anti-VEGF for macular edema.

</details>

## THYROID DISORDERS (Q6–Q9)

**Q6. A 30-year-old woman has palpitations, heat intolerance, weight loss, and exophthalmos. TSH is undetectable, T4 is markedly elevated. TRAb (TSH receptor antibody) is positive. Which finding is SPECIFIC to Graves' disease and not seen in other causes of hyperthyroidism?**
- A) Elevated T4
- B) Suppressed TSH
- C) Pretibial myxedema and thyroid acropachy
- D) Tachycardia and tremor

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Pretibial myxedema (infiltrative dermopathy — non-pitting edema over the anterior shin) and thyroid acropachy (clubbing + periosteal new bone formation) are specific to Graves' disease. Exophthalmos (proptosis) is also Graves'-specific. Elevated T4 (A), suppressed TSH (B), tachycardia/tremor (D) occur in any cause of hyperthyroidism. TRAb/TSI positivity confirms Graves'. Treatment: carbimazole/PTU, beta-blockers, radioiodine, thyroidectomy.

</details>

**Q7. A woman in her first trimester of pregnancy develops hyperthyroidism. Which antithyroid drug is PREFERRED in the first trimester and why?**
- A) Carbimazole — preferred throughout pregnancy
- B) Propylthiouracil (PTU) — preferred in first trimester due to carbimazole's risk of aplasia cutis and choanal atresia (embryopathy)
- C) Radioiodine — safe in pregnancy
- D) Methimazole — equivalent to PTU in first trimester

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — PTU is preferred in the FIRST trimester because carbimazole is associated with embryopathy (aplasia cutis, choanal atresia). However, in the second and third trimesters, carbimazole is preferred because PTU has significant hepatotoxicity risk (fulminant liver failure). Radioiodine (C) is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (concentrates in fetal thyroid). Beta-blockers (propranolol) are used for symptom control. Monitor TFTs monthly.

</details>

**Q8. A patient with known hyperthyroidism develops very high fever (40°C), extreme tachycardia (HR 160), confusion, and diarrhea after emergency surgery. Burch-Wartofsky score is >45. What is the IMMEDIATE treatment?**
- A) IV radioiodine
- B) PTU 600 mg loading → propranolol → hydrocortisone → Lugol's iodine (1h after PTU) + treat precipitant
- C) Thyroidectomy immediately
- D) IV carbimazole + cooling

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Thyroid storm treatment (mnemonic BSTHI + treat precipitant): PTU 600 mg loading dose (blocks synthesis AND peripheral conversion of T4→T3) → propranolol (blocks adrenergic effects and T4→T3 conversion) → hydrocortisone (stress dosing, also blocks T4→T3) → Lugol's iodine (given 1 hour AFTER PTU — Wolff-Chaikoff effect; if given before PTU, iodine provides substrate for more T4 synthesis). Treat precipitant (infection, surgery). Cooling measures. Mortality: 10–30%.

</details>

**Q9. A 50-year-old woman has fatigue, constipation, weight gain, bradycardia (50/min), and delayed relaxation of deep tendon reflexes. TSH is 62 mIU/L. She is found unconscious with hypothermia (34°C) after a cold night. What is the immediate treatment?**
- A) Oral levothyroxine (T4) 100 mcg daily
- B) IV T3 (liothyronine) + IV hydrocortisone + warming + supportive care
- C) IV thyroxine (T4) 400 mcg + radioiodine
- D) Wait for TSH to normalize before treatment

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Myxedema coma: severe hypothyroidism precipitated by cold, infection, drugs (sedatives), or surgery. Features: hypothermia, bradycardia, hypoventilation, coma. Treatment: IV T3 (liothyronine) — faster onset than T4; or IV T4 if T3 unavailable. IV hydrocortisone is given as adrenal insufficiency may coexist (and cortisol is needed for thyroid hormone to work). Passive warming (not aggressive — vasodilation risk). Mechanical ventilation if needed. High mortality (20–50%).

</details>

## ADRENAL DISORDERS (Q10–Q13)

**Q10. A 35-year-old woman has central obesity, purple striae, buffalo hump, and hypertension. 24h urinary cortisol is elevated. Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test (1 mg overnight) does NOT suppress cortisol. High-dose DST (8 mg) suppresses cortisol by >50%. ACTH is mildly elevated. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Adrenal adenoma (Cushing's syndrome)
- B) Ectopic ACTH syndrome (small cell lung cancer)
- C) Cushing's disease (pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma)
- D) Pseudo-Cushing's syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — High-dose DST suppression (>50% fall) + mildly elevated ACTH = pituitary source = Cushing's DISEASE (ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma). Adrenal adenoma (A): ACTH is LOW (autonomous cortisol production suppresses pituitary ACTH) and does NOT suppress on any DST. Ectopic ACTH (B): ACTH very HIGH, does NOT suppress even on high-dose DST. Iatrogenic (exogenous steroid use) = most common overall cause of Cushing's.

</details>

**Q11. A 40-year-old man develops severe hypotension, nausea, vomiting, and confusion following a minor infection. Investigations show hyponatremia (Na 128), hyperkalemia (K 6.2), and hypoglycemia. Serum cortisol is very low. ACTH is markedly elevated. Skin shows hyperpigmentation. What is the FIRST drug to administer?**
- A) IV fludrocortisone
- B) IV hydrocortisone 100 mg bolus + IV normal saline
- C) IV dexamethasone + ACTH stimulation test
- D) Oral prednisolone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Addisonian crisis (acute adrenal insufficiency): present with shock, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, hyperpigmentation (high ACTH/MSH in primary adrenal failure). Immediate treatment: IV hydrocortisone 100 mg bolus, then 100 mg q6–8h + IV normal saline (not D5W — hypoglycemia). Fludrocortisone (A) is for long-term mineralocorticoid replacement, not acute crisis. High ACTH + low cortisol = primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's). Autoimmune (developed countries) or TB (worldwide) are the most common causes.

</details>

**Q12. A 45-year-old hypertensive man has paroxysmal episodes of palpitations, profuse sweating, and severe headache. BP during attack: 220/130 mmHg. 24h urine VMA and metanephrines are markedly elevated. CT abdomen shows a right adrenal mass. Before surgical resection, what is the CORRECT sequence of preoperative drug preparation?**
- A) Beta-blocker first, then alpha-blocker
- B) Alpha-blocker first (phenoxybenzamine), then beta-blocker after adequate alpha-blockade
- C) Calcium channel blocker alone
- D) ACE inhibitor + diuretic

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Pheochromocytoma: preoperative management requires alpha-blockade FIRST (phenoxybenzamine — irreversible; or phentolamine/prazosin) for 10–14 days to control hypertension and allow volume expansion. Beta-blockers are added AFTER adequate alpha-blockade to prevent reflex tachycardia. Starting beta-blockers first is dangerous — it causes unopposed alpha-adrenergic stimulation → severe hypertensive crisis. Rule of 10: 10% bilateral, 10% malignant, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% familial (MEN2, VHL, NF1).

</details>

**Q13. A patient has MEN-1 syndrome. Which is the most common manifestation and which pancreatic tumor is most commonly associated?**
- A) Pituitary prolactinoma; insulinoma
- B) Parathyroid hyperplasia (most common manifestation); gastrinoma (most common pancreatic tumor)
- C) Medullary thyroid cancer; pheochromocytoma
- D) Adrenal carcinoma; VIPoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — MEN-1 (Werner's syndrome) = "3 P's": Pituitary + Parathyroid + Pancreas. Most common manifestation overall: Parathyroid hyperplasia (hyperparathyroidism). Most common pituitary tumor: prolactinoma. Most common pancreatic tumor: gastrinoma (causing ZES — recurrent peptic ulcers, diarrhea). MEN-1 gene = Menin (tumor suppressor, chromosome 11). MEN-2A (Sipple): MTC + Pheo + Parathyroid (RET proto-oncogene). MEN-2B: MTC + Pheo + marfanoid habitus + mucosal neuromas (NO parathyroid).

</details>

## PITUITARY DISORDERS (Q14–Q17)

**Q14. A 40-year-old man has coarsening of facial features, enlargement of hands and feet, prognathism, and macroglossia. He has hypertension and diabetes. Serum IGF-1 is markedly elevated. OGTT fails to suppress GH below 1 ng/mL. What is the diagnosis and most common cause?**
- A) Gigantism; GH-secreting pituitary adenoma before epiphyseal fusion
- B) Acromegaly; GH-secreting pituitary macroadenoma (most common cause in adults)
- C) Cushing's disease; ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma
- D) Hyperprolactinemia; prolactinoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Acromegaly: excess GH in adults (after epiphyseal fusion) → IGF-1-mediated soft tissue and bone overgrowth. Classic features: coarse facial features, acral enlargement (hands, feet, jaw), macroglossia, HTN, DM, carpal tunnel syndrome, sleep apnea. Most common cause: GH-secreting pituitary macroadenoma. Diagnosis: IGF-1 elevated (screening); GH non-suppression on 75g OGTT (confirmatory); MRI pituitary. Treatment: transsphenoidal surgery (first-line); somatostatin analogues (octreotide) if surgery fails.

</details>

**Q15. A 25-year-old woman has polyuria (8 L/day), polydipsia, and pale dilute urine with specific gravity 1.001. Serum sodium is 148 mEq/L. After water deprivation, urine osmolality remains <300 mOsm/kg. After desmopressin (ADH) administration, urine osmolality rises to 650 mOsm/kg. What is the diagnosis?**
- A) Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus
- B) Primary polydipsia (psychogenic)
- C) Central (cranial) diabetes insipidus
- D) SIADH

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Central DI: deficiency of ADH (vasopressin) from the posterior pituitary/hypothalamus. Water deprivation test: urine fails to concentrate (osmolality <300 mOsm/kg despite dehydration → confirms DI). Desmopressin response: urine osmolality rises markedly (>50% increase) = central DI (responsive to ADH). Nephrogenic DI (A): no response to desmopressin. Primary polydipsia (B): urine concentrates with dehydration before desmopressin needed. Treatment of central DI: intranasal/oral desmopressin.

</details>

**Q16. A 60-year-old woman with small cell lung cancer develops confusion, nausea, and headache. Lab: Na 118 mEq/L, plasma osmolality 252 mOsm/kg, urine osmolality 580 mOsm/kg (inappropriately concentrated), urine Na 45 mEq/L (high). TSH and cortisol are normal. What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment for mild-moderate cases?**
- A) Cerebral salt wasting; hypertonic saline
- B) SIADH; fluid restriction (800–1000 mL/day)
- C) Addison's disease; hydrocortisone
- D) Hypothyroidism; levothyroxine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH secretion): euvolemic hyponatremia, low plasma osmolality, inappropriately concentrated urine (>100 mOsm/kg), high urine Na (>20–40 mEq/L), normal thyroid/adrenal function. Common causes: SCLC (ectopic ADH), CNS disorders, pulmonary disease, drugs (carbamazepine, SSRIs, cyclophosphamide). Treatment: mild/moderate → fluid restriction (800–1000 mL/day); severe/symptomatic → hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) — correct Na slowly (<10–12 mEq/L per 24h to avoid osmotic demyelination). Tolvaptan (V2 receptor antagonist) for refractory cases.

</details>

**Q17. A 28-year-old woman has amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and infertility. Prolactin is 180 ng/mL. MRI shows a 1.2 cm pituitary adenoma. TSH is normal. What is the first-line treatment?**
- A) Transsphenoidal surgery
- B) Bromocriptine or cabergoline (dopamine agonists)
- C) Radiation therapy
- D) Estrogen replacement therapy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Prolactinoma is the most common pituitary adenoma. Dopamine agonists (cabergoline preferred over bromocriptine — fewer side effects, once-weekly dosing) are first-line treatment even for macroadenomas — they reduce prolactin levels and shrink the tumor in >90% of cases. Surgery (A) is reserved for dopamine agonist resistance/intolerance or vision-threatening tumors. Cabergoline is also used in MEN-1 (most common pituitary tumor in MEN-1 = prolactinoma).

</details>

## DIABETES COMPLICATIONS & INSULIN (Q18–Q20)

**Q18. A patient with Type 1 DM has progressive proteinuria (2.4 g/day), creatinine 1.6 mg/dL, and HTN. What is the MOST important nephroprotective drug class?**
- A) Thiazide diuretics
- B) Calcium channel blockers
- C) ACE inhibitors or ARBs
- D) Beta-blockers

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — ACE inhibitors (or ARBs if ACEi-intolerant) are the first-line nephroprotective drugs in diabetic nephropathy — they reduce intraglomerular pressure by dilating the efferent arteriole, reducing proteinuria and slowing progression of CKD. They are indicated from stage 3 (microalbuminuria) onwards. Blood pressure target in DM with nephropathy: <130/80 mmHg. SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) also have proven renoprotective effects.

</details>

**Q19. A Type 1 diabetic is started on a new insulin regimen. She is prescribed insulin glargine (U-100) once daily at bedtime and insulin lispro with each meal. Which describes the pharmacological properties of insulin glargine?**
- A) Rapid-acting insulin; peaks at 1–2 hours
- B) Intermediate-acting (NPH); twice daily dosing needed
- C) Long-acting basal insulin; peakless, 24-hour duration
- D) Short-acting regular insulin; given IV in DKA

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Insulin glargine (Lantus) is a long-acting insulin analogue — modified to have peakless, flat action profile lasting ~24 hours, administered once daily. It provides basal insulin coverage. Insulin lispro/aspart/glulisine are rapid-acting (onset 15 min, peak 1–2h, duration 4h) — for prandial coverage. NPH (B) = intermediate-acting, peaks 4–10h, twice daily. Regular insulin (D) = short-acting, used IV in DKA. Basal-bolus = glargine/detemir (basal) + rapid-acting (bolus) = physiological regimen.

</details>

**Q20. A 60-year-old Type 2 diabetic has HbA1c 7.8% despite metformin, and has established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD). Which drug addition has the strongest evidence for cardiovascular mortality reduction?**
- A) Glipizide (sulfonylurea)
- B) Pioglitazone (TZD)
- C) Empagliflozin or liraglutide (SGLT2i or GLP-1 RA with proven CV benefit)
- D) Sitagliptin (DPP-4 inhibitor)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — In T2DM with established ASCVD, guidelines recommend adding drugs with proven CV mortality reduction: SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin — EMPA-REG OUTCOME; dapagliflozin — DECLARE-TIMI) reduce CV death and HF hospitalizations. GLP-1 RAs (liraglutide — LEADER trial; semaglutide — SUSTAIN-6) also reduce MACE. DPP-4 inhibitors (D) are CV-neutral (SAVOR-TIMI, TECOS). Sulfonylureas (A) and TZDs (B) have no proven CV mortality reduction.

</details>
