## COPD & ASTHMA (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 58-year-old smoker has chronic productive cough for 4 months per year for the past 3 years, cyanosis, and bilateral pitting edema. SpO2 is 82%. Spirometry shows FEV1/FVC of 0.58 post-bronchodilator. FEV1 is 42% predicted. What is the GOLD stage and clinical type?**
- A) GOLD 2, Pink puffer (emphysema)
- B) GOLD 3, Blue bloater (chronic bronchitis)
- C) GOLD 2, Blue bloater
- D) GOLD 4, Pink puffer

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — COPD is defined as FEV1/FVC <0.70 post-bronchodilator. GOLD staging by FEV1: ≥80% = GOLD 1, 50–79% = GOLD 2, 30–49% = GOLD 3, <30% = GOLD 4. FEV1 42% = GOLD 3. Blue bloater (chronic bronchitis) = productive cough ≥3 months/year ≥2 years, cyanosis, edema, CO2 retention, polycythemia. Clinical diagnosis. Pink puffer (emphysema) = thin, barrel chest, pursed-lip breathing, near-normal PaO2, PaCO2 low/normal.

</details>

**Q2. A COPD patient with known CO2 retention is brought in with acute exacerbation. SpO2 on arrival is 84%. What is the TARGET oxygen saturation and the RISK of giving high-flow O2?**
- A) Target SpO2 98–100%; no risk
- B) Target SpO2 88–92%; risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure (removing hypoxic drive)
- C) Target SpO2 94–98%; risk of oxygen toxicity only
- D) No supplemental oxygen — CO2 retainers should never get O2

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — In COPD with chronic CO2 retention, the respiratory drive depends partly on hypoxia (hypoxic drive). High-flow O2 can abolish this drive, causing hypoventilation and worsening hypercapnia. Target SpO2 is 88–92% (controlled oxygen therapy). NIV (BiPAP) is indicated if pH <7.35 and PaCO2 >6 kPa despite bronchodilators and steroids. Short-acting bronchodilators (salbutamol + ipratropium) and prednisolone 40 mg for 5 days are also standard.

</details>

**Q3. A 25-year-old woman has episodic wheeze and dyspnea, worse at night and with exercise. Spirometry shows FEV1/FVC of 0.65; after salbutamol, FEV1 improves by 15% AND 280 mL. What does this spirometry pattern indicate?**
- A) Fixed obstruction — consistent with COPD
- B) Significant reversibility — consistent with asthma
- C) Restrictive pattern — consistent with ILD
- D) Normal spirometry — no pathology

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Significant reversibility in spirometry is defined as FEV1 improvement ≥12% AND ≥200 mL after bronchodilator — indicating asthma. COPD shows <12% or <200 mL improvement (fixed obstruction). The patient has both criteria met (15% = ≥12%; 280 mL = ≥200 mL). Restrictive pattern (C) would show FVC reduction with normal FEV1/FVC ratio.

</details>

**Q4. A 30-year-old man with severe asthma has SpO2 90%, silent chest on auscultation, PaCO2 of 44 mmHg (normal), and is confused. What does the NORMAL PaCO2 indicate in this context?**
- A) The attack is mild and resolving
- B) Normal CO2 is reassuring in asthma
- C) Impending respiratory failure — normocapnia in severe asthma is alarming
- D) The patient has hyperventilated and CO2 is now normal

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — In asthma, the expected response is hyperventilation with LOW PaCO2. A normal or rising PaCO2 in a severe asthmatic indicates respiratory muscle fatigue and impending respiratory failure. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate escalation: IV/nebulized salbutamol, IV magnesium sulfate (4g over 20 min for severe/life-threatening asthma), IV hydrocortisone, and preparation for intubation if GCS falling or PaCO2 continues to rise.

</details>

**Q5. A 28-year-old non-smoker has a family history of early-onset emphysema. Spirometry confirms obstructive pattern. HRCT shows panacinar emphysema affecting the LOWER lobes. Which is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Usual COPD from smoking
- B) Sarcoidosis
- C) Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (PiZZ genotype)
- D) Bronchiectasis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency presents with panacinar emphysema predominantly in the lower lobes (unlike smoking-related upper/apical emphysema). The PiZZ genotype is most severe. Key: young non-smoker or atypical emphysema distribution. Also causes liver disease (cirrhosis). Treatment: IV AAT augmentation therapy for lung disease. Standard COPD management applies. Diagnosis by serum AAT level and genotyping.

</details>

## TUBERCULOSIS (Q6–Q8)

**Q6. A 35-year-old man with a 2-month history of productive cough, hemoptysis, fever, and night sweats. CXR shows a cavitary lesion in the right upper lobe with consolidation. Sputum AFB smear is positive (3+). Which ATT regimen is prescribed under NTEP (India) for new DS-TB?**
- A) 4HRZE / 2HR
- B) 2HRZE / 4HR
- C) 6HRZ / 3HR
- D) 2HRZE / 4HRE

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Under India's NTEP (National TB Elimination Programme) daily regimen, DS-TB is treated with 2HRZE (intensive phase: Isoniazid + Rifampicin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol for 2 months) followed by 4HR (continuation phase: Isoniazid + Rifampicin for 4 months). Upper lobe cavitary lesion with AFB positivity = post-primary (reactivation) TB — classic. Miliary TB requires 9–12 months of treatment.

</details>

**Q7. A patient on ATT develops visual blurring and inability to distinguish red from green. Which drug is responsible and what is the monitoring recommendation?**
- A) Isoniazid — monitor liver function tests
- B) Rifampicin — monitor bilirubin
- C) Ethambutol — monitor visual acuity and color vision monthly
- D) Pyrazinamide — monitor uric acid

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Ethambutol causes retrobulbar (optic) neuritis, presenting with color vision loss (first) followed by reduced visual acuity. Monthly monitoring of visual acuity and color vision is recommended. The condition is usually reversible if caught early. Isoniazid (A) causes peripheral neuropathy (Vit B6/pyridoxine supplement given). Rifampicin (B) causes orange discoloration of fluids and is a CYP inducer. Pyrazinamide (D) causes hyperuricemia and hepatitis.

</details>

**Q8. A 30-year-old HIV-positive patient starts ATT for pulmonary TB. Two weeks later, after starting ART, he develops fever and worsening of pulmonary infiltrates despite ATT compliance. What is the most likely explanation?**
- A) Drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB)
- B) Hepatotoxicity from ATT
- C) Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS)
- D) Co-infection with Pneumocystis jirovecii

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — IRIS (Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome) occurs when ART is started in an HIV-TB patient, restoring immune function and causing a paradoxical inflammatory response against the TB antigens — clinical worsening despite effective treatment. Treatment: NSAIDs for mild cases; prednisolone for severe IRIS. ART should be continued (not stopped). MDR-TB (A) would not appear within 2 weeks of ATT — requires resistance testing.

</details>

## PNEUMONIA (Q9–Q11)

**Q9. A 72-year-old man presents with fever, productive cough, and confusion. BP 100/60, RR 32/min, BUN 22 mmol/L, age 72. Using CURB-65, what is his score and appropriate disposition?**
- A) Score 2 — outpatient management
- B) Score 3 — hospital admission, consider ICU
- C) Score 4 — ICU admission
- D) Score 1 — outpatient with antibiotics

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — CURB-65: Confusion (1) + Urea >7 mmol/L (1) + RR ≥30/min (1) + BP: SBP <90 or DBP ≤60 (1) + Age ≥65 (1) = Score 4 (Urea >7mmol/L, RR ≥30, confusion, age 72 — BP not given but using stated values: 100/60 qualifies). Score 0–1 = outpatient; 2 = hospital; 3–5 = consider ICU. Most common CAP organism: Streptococcus pneumoniae. Empiric: beta-lactam + macrolide or respiratory fluoroquinolone.

</details>

**Q10. A 45-year-old man with pneumonia has hyponatremia (Na 126 mEq/L), confusion, and GI symptoms. He works in a hotel with a central air conditioning system. Urinary antigen test is ordered. Which organism is most likely?**
- A) Streptococcus pneumoniae
- B) Mycoplasma pneumoniae
- C) Legionella pneumophila
- D) Chlamydia psittaci

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Legionella pneumophila pneumonia: hyponatremia (most characteristic lab finding), GI symptoms (diarrhea, vomiting), confusion, and association with contaminated water sources (cooling towers, air conditioning, hotel water systems). Diagnosis: urinary antigen test (most practical). Mycoplasma (B) causes cold agglutinins and bullous myringitis in young adults. Psittacosis (D) is linked to bird exposure. Treatment for Legionella: fluoroquinolone or azithromycin.

</details>

**Q11. A 25-year-old college student develops gradually worsening cough, mild fever, and patchy infiltrates on CXR. Blood shows cold agglutinins positive (IgM anti-I). His ear examination shows bilateral bullous myringitis. Which organism is responsible?**
- A) Streptococcus pneumoniae
- B) Mycoplasma pneumoniae
- C) Legionella pneumophila
- D) Klebsiella pneumoniae

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Mycoplasma pneumoniae is the classic atypical pneumonia in young adults. Hallmarks: cold agglutinins (IgM antibodies against I antigen on RBCs — can cause hemolysis), bullous myringitis (hemorrhagic bullae on tympanic membrane). CXR may show more infiltrates than clinical examination suggests ("walks and talks" appearance). Treatment: macrolides (azithromycin) or doxycycline (beta-lactams ineffective — no cell wall).

</details>

## PLEURAL EFFUSION & PE (Q12–Q14)

**Q12. Pleural fluid analysis shows: protein 4.2 g/dL (serum 6.0 g/dL); LDH 350 IU/L (serum 500 IU/L). Upper limit of normal serum LDH is 200 IU/L. What does Light's criteria indicate?**
- A) Transudate — likely CHF
- B) Exudate — all three Light's criteria are met
- C) Indeterminate — borderline findings
- D) Transudate — LDH ratio is within limits

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Light's criteria: exudate if ANY ONE met: (1) pleural/serum protein >0.5 [4.2/6.0 = 0.70 ✓]; (2) pleural/serum LDH >0.6 [350/500 = 0.70 ✓]; (3) pleural LDH >2/3 ULN serum LDH [350 > 133 ✓]. All three criteria are met → exudate. Transudates: CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, hypothyroidism (all three criteria NOT met). Glucose <60 in effusion: empyema, TB, RA, esophageal rupture.

</details>

**Q13. A 55-year-old woman on oral contraceptives presents with sudden pleuritic chest pain and dyspnea after a long-haul flight. HR 110, RR 22, O2 sat 93%. D-dimer is elevated. Wells score is 5 (high probability). What is the MOST appropriate next step?**
- A) D-dimer is already elevated — start LMWH empirically without further imaging
- B) CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)
- C) Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan first
- D) Echocardiography

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — In high clinical probability (Wells ≥5) PE, proceed directly to CTPA (gold standard for PE diagnosis). D-dimer is useful to RULE OUT PE in low/intermediate probability patients (high sensitivity, low specificity). CTPA has >95% sensitivity and specificity. LMWH/anticoagulation should be started while awaiting CTPA if there is high clinical suspicion and low bleeding risk. V/Q scan (C) is used when CTPA is contraindicated (e.g., contrast allergy, renal failure, pregnancy).

</details>

**Q14. A patient is diagnosed with submassive PE (normal BP, RV strain on echo). Which anticoagulation regimen is preferred for outpatient management?**
- A) IV unfractionated heparin only (admit to ICU)
- B) DOAC (rivaroxaban or apixaban) — preferred for non-massive PE
- C) Vitamin K antagonist (warfarin) alone from day 1
- D) Systemic thrombolysis with alteplase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — For hemodynamically stable (non-massive/submassive) PE, DOACs (rivaroxaban or apixaban) are preferred — easier to use, no monitoring required, equivalent efficacy to LMWH/warfarin. Warfarin (C) requires bridging with heparin/LMWH for at least 5 days (slow onset). Systemic thrombolysis (D) is reserved for massive PE (hemodynamic instability, cardiac arrest). Minimum anticoagulation duration: 3 months for provoked PE, 6–12 months or indefinitely for unprovoked.

</details>

## ILD & SARCOIDOSIS (Q15–Q17)

**Q15. A 62-year-old ex-smoker has progressive exertional dyspnea. HRCT shows bilateral basal predominant subpleural honeycombing with traction bronchiectasis. BAL shows no lymphocytosis. Pulmonary function shows restrictive pattern. Which diagnosis and treatment applies?**
- A) NSIP — respond well to steroids
- B) Sarcoidosis — steroids curative
- C) IPF (UIP pattern) — pirfenidone or nintedanib
- D) Hypersensitivity pneumonitis — remove antigen

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — UIP (Usual Interstitial Pneumonia) pattern on HRCT: honeycombing + traction bronchiectasis, bilateral, basal, subpleural distribution = Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF). Anti-fibrotic agents: pirfenidone (TGF-β inhibitor) and nintedanib (tyrosine kinase inhibitor) — slow progression but do not improve survival significantly. Corticosteroids are NOT effective in IPF and may be harmful. Lung transplant is definitive. NSIP (A) = ground glass without honeycombing, responds to steroids.

</details>

**Q16. A 35-year-old Black woman presents with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, erythema nodosum, and arthralgia. ACE level is elevated. Serum calcium is high. Biopsy of a lymph node shows non-caseating granulomas. What is the diagnosis and drug of choice?**
- A) Tuberculosis; HRZE regimen
- B) Sarcoidosis; systemic corticosteroids
- C) Lymphoma; chemotherapy
- D) Histoplasmosis; itraconazole

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Sarcoidosis: young adults, Black women more severely affected; bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL) ± erythema nodosum + arthralgia = Löfgren's syndrome (good prognosis, often self-limiting). Non-caseating granulomas on biopsy, elevated ACE, hypercalcemia (vitamin D activated by macrophages in granulomas). Steroids are the treatment for symptomatic/organ-threatening disease. Löfgren's syndrome often resolves spontaneously. TB granulomas are caseating.

</details>

**Q17. In Eisenmenger's syndrome, which pathophysiological sequence is correct?**
- A) Right-to-left shunt → pulmonary hypertension → cyanosis
- B) Left-to-right shunt → pulmonary hypertension → reversal to right-to-left → cyanosis
- C) Left-to-right shunt → pulmonary hypertension → cyanosis without shunt reversal
- D) Right-to-left shunt → systemic hypertension → polycythemia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Eisenmenger's syndrome: initially a left-to-right shunt (VSD/ASD/PDA) → increased pulmonary blood flow → pulmonary vascular remodeling → pulmonary hypertension → when PAP exceeds systemic pressure, shunt reversal occurs (right-to-left) → central cyanosis, clubbing, and polycythemia. Most common cause: VSD. Treatment: PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil), endothelin receptor antagonists (bosentan), prostacyclins. The defect cannot be surgically closed once Eisenmenger's develops.

</details>

## PULMONARY HYPERTENSION (Q18–Q20)

**Q18. Which group of pulmonary hypertension is potentially CURABLE with surgical intervention?**
- A) Group 1 (Idiopathic PAH)
- B) Group 2 (Left heart disease)
- C) Group 3 (Lung disease/hypoxia)
- D) Group 4 (Chronic thromboembolic PH — CTEPH)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D** — Group 4 CTEPH (chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension) is the only potentially curable form through pulmonary thromboendarterectomy (PTE). Group 1 (PAH) is treated with vasodilators but not curable medically; lung transplant is definitive. Group 2 and 3 PH are managed by treating the underlying cause. Riociguat (soluble guanylate cyclase stimulator) is also used for inoperable CTEPH.

</details>

**Q19. A 28-year-old woman with progressive dyspnea and no identifiable cause has right heart catheterization showing mean PAP 32 mmHg, PCWP 8 mmHg (normal), PVR elevated. Acute vasodilator testing with inhaled nitric oxide shows significant reduction in PAP. Which treatment is preferred for this vasoreactive patient?**
- A) Sildenafil (PDE-5 inhibitor)
- B) Calcium channel blockers (nifedipine/diltiazem)
- C) Bosentan (endothelin receptor antagonist)
- D) Epoprostenol IV (prostacyclin)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — In idiopathic PAH, acute vasoreactivity testing (using inhaled NO, adenosine, or epoprostenol) identifies ~10–15% of patients who respond (>10 mmHg fall in mean PAP, fall below 40 mmHg). These vasoreactive patients can be treated with calcium channel blockers (high-dose nifedipine or diltiazem) with excellent long-term outcomes. Non-responders require PDE-5 inhibitors (A), ERA (C), or prostacyclins (D).

</details>

**Q20. Long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT) criteria for COPD include oxygen use for >15 hours/day. What PaO2 threshold qualifies a patient for LTOT?**
- A) PaO2 ≤60 mmHg on two measurements
- B) PaO2 ≤55 mmHg (or SpO2 ≤88%) on two measurements 3 weeks apart
- C) PaO2 ≤70 mmHg with any symptoms
- D) PaO2 ≤65 mmHg with nighttime desaturation only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — LTOT criteria: PaO2 ≤55 mmHg (or SpO2 ≤88%) on room air, on 2 measurements 3 weeks apart; OR PaO2 56–59 mmHg with cor pulmonale, polycythemia, or nocturnal hypoxemia. LTOT (>15 hours/day) improves survival in COPD. It reduces pulmonary hypertension, polycythemia, and exercise-related desaturation. The only intervention proven to improve survival in COPD (along with smoking cessation).

</details>
