## STROKE & TIA (Q1–Q4)

**Q1. A 68-year-old hypertensive man develops sudden right-sided hemiplegia and aphasia. CT head is normal. He arrives 2.5 hours after symptom onset and BP is 170/95 mmHg after treatment. Platelets 180,000, INR 1.1. Which is the MOST appropriate treatment?**
- A) Aspirin 300 mg and admit for observation
- B) IV alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg)
- C) Emergency carotid endarterectomy
- D) Warfarin anticoagulation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — IV alteplase (tPA) is indicated for ischemic stroke within 3–4.5 hours of onset if contraindications are absent. Absolute contraindications include ICH on CT, BP >185/110 (after treatment attempt), INR >1.7, platelets <100,000, active bleeding, and recent intracranial surgery. Here, the CT is normal (no hemorrhage), BP is controlled, and INR/platelets are acceptable. Dose: 0.9 mg/kg, 10% as bolus, rest over 60 minutes.

</details>

**Q2. A 60-year-old woman has a sudden "thunderclap" headache — the worst of her life — with vomiting. CT head is normal. What is the next MOST important investigation?**
- A) MRI brain with contrast
- B) Lumbar puncture for CSF analysis
- C) Carotid Doppler ultrasound
- D) EEG

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Thunderclap headache (maximal at onset, "worst headache of life") is subarachnoid hemorrhage until proven otherwise. CT is >95% sensitive in the first 6 hours but may be negative. If CT is negative, LP is mandatory — xanthochromia (yellow CSF discoloration from oxyhemoglobin/bilirubin) appears after 12 hours. Spectrophotometry is the gold standard for detecting xanthochromia. CT angiography follows to identify the aneurysm.

</details>

**Q3. A patient has a TIA with sudden-onset unilateral weakness lasting 30 minutes, BP 160/95, age 65, with diabetes. Using the ABCD2 score, what is the risk category?**
- A) Low risk (score 0–3)
- B) Intermediate risk (score 4–5)
- C) High risk (score ≥6)
- D) Cannot determine without MRI

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — ABCD2 score: Age ≥60 (1) + BP ≥140/90 (1) + Clinical: unilateral weakness (2) + Duration 10–59 min (1) + Diabetes (1) = 6. Score ≥6 = high risk (2-day stroke risk ~8%). Such patients should be investigated urgently (within 24 hours). Score 4–5 = intermediate; 0–3 = low risk.

</details>

**Q4. A 55-year-old man presents with pure motor hemiparesis affecting face, arm, and leg equally, without any cortical signs (no aphasia, no neglect). MRI shows a small hypointense lesion in the posterior limb of the internal capsule. What type of stroke is this?**
- A) Embolic cortical infarct
- B) Lacunar infarct
- C) Hemorrhagic stroke
- D) Watershed infarct

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Lacunar infarcts involve small perforating arteries supplying deep structures. Pure motor hemiparesis (face, arm, leg) is the most common lacunar syndrome, involving the posterior limb of the internal capsule or basis pontis. The absence of cortical signs (no aphasia, no hemineglect, no visual field defects) distinguishes lacunar from cortical infarcts. Other lacunar syndromes: pure sensory (thalamus), ataxic hemiparesis (basis pontis).

</details>

## MENINGITIS & CNS INFECTIONS (Q5–Q7)

**Q5. CSF analysis of a 22-year-old college student with fever, neck stiffness, and non-blanching petechiae shows: WBC 2000 cells (90% PMNs), glucose 28 mg/dL (serum 90 mg/dL), protein 180 mg/dL, turbid appearance. What is the most likely organism and empirical treatment?**
- A) HSV; acyclovir
- B) Cryptococcus; amphotericin B + flucytosine
- C) Neisseria meningitidis; ceftriaxone + dexamethasone
- D) Mycobacterium tuberculosis; HRZE + steroids

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Bacterial meningitis CSF pattern: >1000 WBC (PMN predominant), very low glucose (<45 mg/dL or <50% serum glucose), high protein (>100 mg/dL), turbid. Petechiae/purpura + meningism in a young adult = Neisseria meningitidis (can cause DIC — Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome). Empiric treatment: ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h + dexamethasone (0.15 mg/kg q6h × 4 days, given before/with first antibiotic).

</details>

---

**Q6. Which CSF finding is pathognomonic for TB meningitis that is NOT seen in bacterial or viral meningitis?**
- A) Elevated opening pressure
- B) Lymphocyte predominance
- C) Cobweb coagulum on standing + elevated ADA
- D) Mildly elevated protein

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — TB/fungal CSF: opalescent/xanthochromic, lymphocyte predominant, high protein (100–500 mg/dL), low glucose. Elevated adenosine deaminase (ADA) and cobweb coagulum (fibrin web visible on standing) are highly characteristic of TB meningitis. India ink preparation is used for Cryptococcus. AFB smear/culture confirms TB (low sensitivity). PCR for TB and HSV are important adjuncts.

</details>

**Q7. A neonate presents with fever and seizures. CSF culture grows a Gram-positive rod forming hemolysis on blood agar. Which organism is responsible and which antibiotic must be added to the empiric regimen?**
- A) S. pneumoniae; vancomycin
- B) N. meningitidis; ceftriaxone alone
- C) Listeria monocytogenes; ampicillin
- D) Group B Streptococcus; penicillin alone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Listeria monocytogenes is a Gram-positive rod that causes meningitis in neonates, elderly, and immunocompromised patients. It is resistant to cephalosporins; ampicillin must be added to empirical ceftriaxone in these age groups. Group B Strep (D) is the most common cause in neonates (<1 month) but is a Gram-positive coccus, not a rod. Listeria: tumbling motility, cold enrichment, meconium in utero.

</details>

## EPILEPSY (Q8–Q10)

**Q8. A 12-year-old girl has episodes of brief staring lasting 5–10 seconds, with no postictal confusion, occurring multiple times daily. EEG shows 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharges. Which is the drug of FIRST choice?**
- A) Carbamazepine
- B) Valproate or Ethosuximide
- C) Phenytoin
- D) Levetiracetam

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Typical absence seizures show 3 Hz spike-and-wave on EEG. First-line drugs are ethosuximide (most effective for pure absence, safest) and valproate (preferred if GTCS also present). Carbamazepine (A) and phenytoin (C) are absolutely contraindicated in absence and myoclonic seizures — they worsen them by blocking sodium channels without affecting T-type calcium channels.

</details>

---

**Q9. A 25-year-old man presents with status epilepticus. He has been seizing continuously for 8 minutes. IV access is established. Which is the CORRECT sequence of first-line management?**
- A) IV phenytoin → IV lorazepam → IV valproate
- B) IV lorazepam → IV levetiracetam/phenytoin if no response → intubation if refractory
- C) IM midazolam → oral carbamazepine → observation
- D) IV dexamethasone → IV phenobarbital → intubation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Status epilepticus management: 0–5 min: IV access, O2, glucose check. 5–20 min (1st line): IV lorazepam 4 mg (or diazepam 10 mg IV/rectal; IM midazolam if no IV access). 20–40 min (2nd line if seizure continues): IV levetiracetam, phenytoin, or valproate. >40 min (refractory): Intubation + IV anesthesia (thiopentone/propofol/midazolam infusion). Benzodiazepines are always the first pharmacological step.

</details>

**Q10. A woman with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME) is planning pregnancy. She is currently on valproate. What is the recommended management?**
- A) Continue valproate — it is the safest AED in pregnancy
- B) Switch to levetiracetam or lamotrigine before conception
- C) Discontinue all AEDs immediately
- D) Switch to carbamazepine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Valproate is the most teratogenic AED (neural tube defects, cognitive impairment — fetal valproate syndrome). For women with JME planning pregnancy, levetiracetam or lamotrigine are safer alternatives. Note: Lamotrigine is less effective for myoclonic seizures than valproate. Carbamazepine (D) worsens myoclonic seizures and also has teratogenic risk. Pre-conception counseling and folate supplementation are essential.

</details>

## PARKINSON'S DISEASE & MS (Q11–Q14)

**Q11. A 62-year-old man has a resting pill-rolling tremor (4–6 Hz), cogwheel rigidity, bradykinesia, and stooped posture. Which pathological finding confirms this diagnosis?**
- A) Negri bodies in hippocampus
- B) Lewy bodies (alpha-synuclein aggregates) in substantia nigra
- C) Pick bodies in frontal cortex
- D) Neurofibrillary tangles in entorhinal cortex

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Parkinson's disease is characterized by loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, with Lewy body (alpha-synuclein aggregates) formation. TRAP = Tremor (resting), Rigidity (cogwheel), Akinesia/bradykinesia, Postural instability. Non-motor features: anosmia (earliest), constipation, REM sleep behavior disorder. Negri bodies (A) = rabies; Pick bodies (C) = frontotemporal dementia; Neurofibrillary tangles (D) = Alzheimer's.

</details>

---

**Q12. A 62-year-old man with Parkinson's disease is started on levodopa/carbidopa. After 3 years, he develops involuntary writhing movements (dyskinesias) and motor fluctuations ("wearing off"). What is the role of carbidopa in this combination?**
- A) Inhibits MAO-B in the brain
- B) Inhibits peripheral DOPA decarboxylase, increasing CNS levodopa delivery
- C) Blocks dopamine reuptake
- D) Acts as a COMT inhibitor to reduce levodopa breakdown

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Carbidopa inhibits peripheral aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (DOPA decarboxylase), preventing conversion of levodopa to dopamine outside the CNS. This increases the amount of levodopa reaching the brain and reduces peripheral side effects (nausea, vomiting, cardiac arrhythmias). It does NOT cross the blood-brain barrier. COMT inhibitors (D) = entacapone/tolcapone. MAO-B inhibitors (A) = selegiline/rasagiline.

</details>

**Q13. A 28-year-old woman develops sudden painful loss of vision in her right eye. She has had a prior episode of unilateral limb weakness that resolved spontaneously. MRI shows periventricular ovoid lesions perpendicular to the corpus callosum ("Dawson's fingers"). What finding on CSF supports the diagnosis?**
- A) India ink positive
- B) Xanthochromia
- C) Oligoclonal IgG bands (>2)
- D) AFB positive

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — This is Multiple Sclerosis (McDonald criteria: DIS + DIT). Optic neuritis (painful monocular vision loss + afferent pupillary defect = Marcus Gunn pupil) + prior episode = DIT. Dawson's fingers on MRI = MS plaques along medullary veins. CSF shows oligoclonal IgG bands (>2 bands, present in >90% of MS cases), mildly elevated protein, lymphocytic pleocytosis. Lhermitte's sign and Uhthoff's phenomenon are clinical hallmarks.

</details>

**Q14. A patient with RRMS has an acute relapse with worsening vision and arm weakness. What is the treatment for an acute MS relapse?**
- A) Interferon-beta-1a (long-term DMT)
- B) IV methylprednisolone (1g/day × 3–5 days)
- C) Natalizumab (anti-VLA-4)
- D) Baclofen (muscle relaxant)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Acute MS relapses are treated with IV methylprednisolone, which speeds up recovery but does NOT alter the long-term course or disability. Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) like interferon-beta (A) or natalizumab (C) are for relapse prevention. Baclofen (D) manages spasticity. High-efficacy DMTs: Natalizumab (risk of PML with JC virus), Alemtuzumab (anti-CD52), Ocrelizumab (anti-CD20).

</details>

## MYASTHENIA GRAVIS & GBS (Q15–Q17)

**Q15. A 35-year-old woman complains of ptosis and diplopia worse in the evening and after sustained activity, with partial improvement after rest. She has proximal limb weakness. Tensilon (edrophonium) test is positive. Which antibody is most commonly positive in this condition?**
- A) Anti-VGCC (voltage-gated calcium channel)
- B) Anti-AChR (acetylcholine receptor)
- C) Anti-NMDA receptor
- D) Anti-GM1 ganglioside

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Myasthenia gravis (MG) is caused by anti-AChR antibodies (85% of generalized MG) blocking/destroying postsynaptic acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Key features: fatigable weakness worse with sustained activity, ocular involvement (ptosis, diplopia), positive edrophonium/ice pack test, decremental response on repetitive nerve stimulation (EMG). Associated with thymoma — CT chest mandatory. Anti-VGCC (A) = Lambert-Eaton syndrome.

</details>

**Q16. Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) differs from Myasthenia Gravis in which way?**
- A) Both cause decremental response on EMG at 3 Hz stimulation
- B) LEMS causes proximal limb weakness that IMPROVES with repeated activity; EMG shows incremental response
- C) MG causes incremental response on high-frequency stimulation
- D) Both are autoimmune and associated with thymoma

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — LEMS is caused by anti-VGCC antibodies (commonly paraneoplastic — SCLC). Pathophysiology: impaired presynaptic ACh release. Key: weakness that paradoxically IMPROVES briefly with repeated activity (potentiation). EMG: incremental response at high-frequency stimulation (50 Hz). MG: decremental response at low frequency (3 Hz). LEMS associated with SCLC (not thymoma). Autonomic features (dry mouth, constipation) are common in LEMS but not MG.

</details>

**Q17. A 28-year-old man develops ascending flaccid paralysis 2 weeks after a bout of gastroenteritis (Campylobacter jejuni). CSF shows albuminocytologic dissociation (elevated protein, normal WBC). Nerve conduction studies show slowing. Which is the treatment of choice?**
- A) IV corticosteroids
- B) IV immunoglobulin (IVIG) or plasmapheresis
- C) Pyridostigmine
- D) Azathioprine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) is an acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy triggered by infection (most common: Campylobacter jejuni, also CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma). Classic CSF: albuminocytologic dissociation (elevated protein with normal cell count). Treatment: IVIG (0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days) or plasmapheresis — equally effective. Corticosteroids (A) are NOT beneficial in GBS. Respiratory monitoring (FVC) is critical — intubate if FVC <15 mL/kg.

</details>

## PERIPHERAL NEUROPATHY & HEADACHE (Q18–Q20)

**Q18. A 60-year-old diabetic man has bilateral burning pain and numbness in a "stocking and glove" distribution. Nerve conduction shows reduced amplitude sensory action potentials and slowed conduction velocities. Which is the most common type of diabetic neuropathy?**
- A) Autonomic neuropathy
- B) Mononeuropathy multiplex
- C) Distal symmetric polyneuropathy
- D) Diabetic amyotrophy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C** — Distal symmetric polyneuropathy (DSPN) is the most common form of diabetic neuropathy, presenting with "glove and stocking" distribution of sensory loss, burning pain, and numbness — worse distally and at night. Autonomic neuropathy (A) includes gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension, and bladder dysfunction. Mononeuropathy multiplex (B) involves sudden foot drop or wrist drop. Diabetic amyotrophy (D) = asymmetric proximal motor weakness.

</details>

**Q19. A 35-year-old man has severe unilateral periorbital pain lasting 45 minutes, occurring nightly in clusters over 6 weeks, with ipsilateral ptosis, lacrimation, and nasal congestion. He is restless during attacks. What is the first-line ACUTE treatment?**
- A) Oral sumatriptan 100 mg
- B) 100% oxygen at 15 L/min for 15 minutes via non-rebreather mask
- C) IV morphine
- D) Paracetamol 1 g

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Cluster headache: strictly unilateral periorbital pain (15–180 min), autonomic features (Horner's syndrome — ptosis, miosis, lacrimation, rhinorrhea), patient is restless (unlike migraine). First-line acute treatment: 100% O2 (15 L/min, 15 min) or subcutaneous/intranasal sumatriptan. Prophylaxis: verapamil (drug of choice). Oral sumatriptan (A) is too slow for cluster headache; SC/intranasal is preferred.

</details>

**Q20. A 25-year-old woman has throbbing unilateral headache with nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia lasting 18 hours. She takes NSAIDs frequently, resulting in medication overuse. Which drug class is FIRST-LINE for aborting a migraine attack?**
- A) Opioids
- B) Triptans (sumatriptan — 5-HT1B/1D agonist)
- C) Ergotamine
- D) Corticosteroids

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B** — Triptans (e.g., sumatriptan) are first-line for moderate-severe migraine, acting as selective 5-HT1B/1D agonists causing cranial vasoconstriction and inhibiting trigeminal nerve activation. Contraindicated in IHD, uncontrolled HTN, hemiplegic migraine, and stroke. For prophylaxis: propranolol (first-line), topiramate, amitriptyline, valproate. Opioids (A) worsen medication overuse headache. Ergotamine (C) is a second-line option with more side effects.

</details>
