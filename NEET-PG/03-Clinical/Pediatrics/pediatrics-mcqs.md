## GROWTH, DEVELOPMENT AND IMMUNIZATION (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A mother brings her 15-month-old child for a routine visit. The child says 3–5 meaningful words, walks without support, builds a tower of 2 cubes, and waves bye-bye. Which milestone is NOT expected at this age?**
- A) Walks without support
- B) Says 3–5 meaningful words
- C) Speaks in 2-word phrases
- D) Waves bye-bye (social)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Two-word phrases (e.g., "mama go," "more milk") are a milestone expected at 24 months, not 15 months. At 15 months: walks alone, says 3–5 words, builds 2-cube tower, uses spoon, points to body parts on request, waves bye-bye. At 12 months: first words (1–3 meaningful), stands alone, walks with support. At 18 months: 8–10 words, tower of 3 cubes, runs. Language is the most sensitive indicator of cognitive development and social environment.

</details>

**Q2. An infant born at 32 weeks gestation with birth weight 1.4 kg is assessed at birth. The APGAR score is 6 at 1 minute and 8 at 5 minutes. The best immediate management is:**
- A) Emergency intubation and surfactant
- B) Supplemental oxygen by mask, stimulation, and reassessment
- C) Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (chest compressions)
- D) IV adrenaline 0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

APGAR 1-min score 6 = mild-moderate depression; APGAR 5-min score 8 = near-normal (reassuring). Management for APGAR 4–6: supplemental O₂, stimulation (dry, warm, reposition), reassess. APGAR 0–3: positive pressure ventilation. Intubation is for failed bag-mask ventilation or meconium-stained amniotic fluid with depressed infant. Chest compressions when HR <60 despite adequate ventilation for 30 seconds. Surfactant for RDS/HMD in preterm infants.

</details>

**Q4. A full-term newborn weighs 4.2 kg. By what age is birth weight expected to be DOUBLED in a healthy Indian infant?**
- A) 3 months
- B) 5 months
- C) 6 months
- D) 9 months

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Birth weight doubles by 5 months, triples by 1 year, and quadruples by 2 years. Average birth weight in India: 2.5–3.0 kg (WHO: 3.0–3.5 kg in developed countries). **Q5. A 14-month-old child has not yet started walking. Which finding would be most reassuring that this is a normal variant?**
- A) Absent deep tendon reflexes bilaterally
- B) Asymmetric leg movements with scissoring gait
- C) Normal tone, normal milestones in other domains, and family history of late walking
- D) Persistent Babinski sign with clonus at ankle

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

## NEONATOLOGY (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 3-day-old term neonate develops jaundice. Serum bilirubin is 14 mg/dL (predominantly unconjugated). The baby is feeding well and has no risk factors. The next best step is:**
- A) Immediate exchange transfusion
- B) Phototherapy
- C) IVIG infusion
- D) Observe — physiological jaundice, no treatment needed

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Phototherapy is indicated based on hour-specific bilirubin nomogram (Bhutani curve) and risk factors. A 3-day-old term infant with bilirubin 14 mg/dL falls in the zone where phototherapy is indicated in most guidelines (AAP 2004: phototherapy threshold ~17 mg/dL for low-risk term infant at 72 h, but 14 mg/dL at high-intermediate risk warrants treatment). Physiological jaundice (appears Day 2–3, peaks Day 3–5, resolves by Day 10) can still require phototherapy if levels are rising. Exchange transfusion is for bilirubin ≥25 mg/dL in term infants or with hydrops.

</details>

**Q7. A preterm neonate at 28 weeks gestation develops progressive respiratory distress, grunting, intercostal retractions, and cyanosis within 4 hours of birth. Chest X-ray shows "ground glass opacity" with air bronchograms. The diagnosis and pathophysiology are:**
- A) Meconium aspiration syndrome — air trapping
- B) Hyaline Membrane Disease (RDS) — surfactant deficiency
- C) Transient tachypnea of newborn — delayed lung fluid clearance
- D) Congenital pneumonia — Group B Streptococcus

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Hyaline Membrane Disease (Respiratory Distress Syndrome) is caused by surfactant deficiency in premature lungs (<35 weeks) → ↑surface tension → alveolar collapse → classic "ground glass" appearance with air bronchograms on CXR. Type II pneumocytes produce surfactant from ~24 weeks, mature by ~35 weeks. Treatment: antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone to mother), postnatal surfactant (intratracheal), CPAP, mechanical ventilation. TTN occurs in near-term/term, CXR shows streaky perihilar markings.

</details>

**Q10. In physiological jaundice of newborn, which enzyme is deficient causing accumulation of unconjugated bilirubin?**
- A) Glucuronyl transferase (UDP-glucuronosyltransferase)
- B) Heme oxygenase
- C) Biliverdin reductase
- D) Alkaline phosphatase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Physiological neonatal jaundice results from relative deficiency of hepatic UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT1A1) — the enzyme that conjugates bilirubin with glucuronic acid — combined with increased RBC breakdown (shorter neonatal RBC lifespan: 70–90 days vs. 120 days in adults) and increased enterohepatic circulation. Heme oxygenase converts heme → biliverdin. Biliverdin reductase converts biliverdin → unconjugated bilirubin. Phototherapy converts bilirubin to lumirubin (water-soluble, directly excreted).

</details>

- C) Parainfluenza virus — nebulized adrenaline

<details>

**Correct: B**

Ribavirin is used only in severe immunocompromised patients.

**Q12. WHO classification of pneumonia in a 3-year-old child with cough, fever, and respiratory rate of 45/min but no chest indrawing or danger signs is:**
- A) Severe pneumonia — hospitalise and IV antibiotics
- B) Very severe pneumonia — emergency treatment
- C) Pneumonia — oral amoxicillin as outpatient
- D) No pneumonia — cough or cold only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

WHO IMCI classification (children 2 months–5 years): Fast breathing alone (RR ≥40/min for 1–5 years) without chest indrawing = Pneumonia → treat with oral amoxicillin as outpatient. **Q13. A 6-week-old male infant presents with projectile non-bilious vomiting after feeds for 3 weeks. Examination reveals a hard, olive-shaped mass in the right hypochondrium. Ultrasound shows pyloric muscle thickness >4 mm. The metabolic abnormality expected is:**
- A) Metabolic acidosis with hyperkalemia
- B) Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis
- C) Hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis
- D) Respiratory alkalosis with low PaO₂

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Hypertrophic pyloric stenosis causes repeated loss of HCl-rich gastric contents → hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis with paradoxical aciduria (kidneys retain H⁺ to conserve Na⁺ when volume-depleted). Classic presentation: first-born male, 3–6 weeks, projectile non-bilious vomiting, visible peristalsis, palpable "olive." Diagnosis: USS (pyloric muscle thickness >3–4 mm, pyloric channel length >14–17 mm). Treatment: Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy after metabolic correction.

</details>

**Q15. A neonate fails to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth. Examination shows abdominal distension. Barium enema reveals a "transition zone" in the rectosigmoid colon. Rectal biopsy confirms absence of ganglion cells. The diagnosis is:**
- A) Meconium ileus (CF)
- B) Hirschsprung's disease (congenital aganglionic megacolon)
- C) Anorectal malformation (imperforate anus)
- D) Functional constipation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Hirschsprung's disease (congenital aganglionic megacolon) results from failure of neural crest cell migration → absence of Auerbach's and Meissner's plexuses in the distal colon → functional obstruction. </details>

## INFECTIOUS DISEASES AND HEMATOLOGY (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 4-year-old child develops a seizure during fever of 39.5°C. The convulsion is generalized tonic-clonic, lasts 3 minutes, and resolves spontaneously. The child is neurologically normal after the episode. The diagnosis and the single most important risk factor for recurrence are:**
- A) Epilepsy — prior febrile seizure
- B) Simple febrile seizure — young age at first seizure (<18 months)
- C) Complex febrile seizure — focal onset
- D) Febrile status epilepticus — fever >40°C

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Simple febrile seizure: age 6 months–5 years, generalized, duration <15 min, single episode within 24 h, no neurological deficit. ## CONGENITAL HEART DISEASE (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. The most common congenital heart defect overall is:**
- A) Atrial septal defect (ASD)
- B) Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
- C) Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
- D) Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

VSD is the most common congenital heart disease (CHD), accounting for ~30–35% of all CHDs. It is an acyanotic left-to-right shunt; most common location: perimembranous (80%). Small VSDs often close spontaneously. Signs: pansystolic murmur at left lower sternal border, loud P2 if pulmonary hypertension develops. ASD: fixed split S2, ejection systolic murmur at pulmonary area. TOF is the most common cyanotic CHD (10–15% of all CHD). PDA: continuous "machinery" murmur, bounding pulses.

</details>

**Q22. A 4-year-old child presents with episodic cyanosis and squatting. Echo shows pulmonary stenosis, RVH, VSD, dextroposed aorta, and RVH. The 5th component of Tetralogy of Fallot and the mechanism of "hypercyanotic spell" (Tet spell) are:**
- A) ASD — increased left-to-right shunting
- B) Right-sided aortic arch (in 25% cases) — infundibular spasm → ↓pulmonary flow → ↑right-to-left shunt
- C) PDA — compensatory pulmonary flow

<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Tetralogy of Fallot has 4 classic components: pulmonary stenosis (infundibular most common), VSD, dextroposed (overriding) aorta, and RVH. The 5th component (Pentalogy) is ASD. A right-sided aortic arch is present in 25% and is associated with TOF. Tet spells: infundibular spasm → acute ↓pulmonary blood flow → ↑right-to-left shunt → severe hypoxia → crying → worsening hypoxia. Management: knee-chest position, morphine, propranolol, O₂. CXR: boot-shaped heart (coeur en sabot).

</details>

**Q23. A cyanotic newborn on Day 2 deteriorates rapidly with no murmur, severe cyanosis, and single S2. CXR shows "egg on side" (egg on string) cardiac silhouette. The diagnosis and emergency treatment are:**
- A) TOF — propranolol
- B) Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA) — prostaglandin E1 (PGE1) to maintain PDA

**Q24. Eisenmenger syndrome is a complication of which type of congenital heart defect, and what determines its onset?**
- A) Cyanotic CHD — right-to-left shunt from birth
- B) Acyanotic left-to-right shunt — reversal due to severe pulmonary arterial hypertension
- C) Aortic stenosis — aortic valve calcification
- D) Coarctation of aorta — systemic hypertension

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Eisenmenger syndrome: originally left-to-right shunt (VSD, ASD, PDA) → chronic pulmonary overcirculation → irreversible pulmonary arterial hypertension → reversal to right-to-left shunt → cyanosis, clubbing, polycythemia. Occurs when PVR exceeds SVR. The shunt begins as acyanotic. Once Eisenmenger develops, surgical closure is contraindicated. Medical treatment: endothelin receptor antagonists (bosentan), PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil), prostacyclin analogues. VSD is most likely to cause Eisenmenger due to large shunt.

</details>

Spastic diplegia is the hallmark of periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) in premature infants; the descending corticospinal fibers controlling the legs run closest to the ventricles and are most vulnerable. Spastic CP is the most common type overall (~75–80%). Birth asphyxia in term infants → spastic quadriplegia (HIE). Kernicterus → dyskinetic/athetoid CP (damage to basal ganglia). Neonatal meningitis → mixed/quadriplegic CP. Key principle: CP is non-progressive, but clinical features may evolve. Botulinum toxin and physiotherapy are mainstays of management.
