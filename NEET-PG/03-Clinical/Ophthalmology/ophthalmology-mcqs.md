## GLAUCOMA (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 55-year-old man presents for a routine eye checkup. Examination reveals cup:disc ratio of 0.7 bilaterally, IOP of 26 mmHg, open angles on gonioscopy, and an arcuate scotoma on automated perimetry. He has no pain or redness. The first-line pharmacological treatment for this condition is:**
- A) Topical pilocarpine 2%
- B) Topical timolol 0.5% or latanoprost 0.005%
- C) Oral acetazolamide 250 mg
- D) Laser iridotomy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG) is the most common form of glaucoma worldwide — painless, progressive optic neuropathy with open angle, elevated IOP (>21 mmHg), cup:disc ratio >0.6 (suspect), and characteristic visual field loss (arcuate/Bjerrum scotoma → nasal step → tubular vision). First-line: topical prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost — once daily, most effective IOP reduction 25–35%) or topical beta-blockers (timolol). Pilocarpine is used in angle-closure glaucoma. Oral acetazolamide is adjunctive. Laser iridotomy is for closed-angle glaucoma.

</details>

**Q2. A 60-year-old woman presents to the emergency with sudden onset of severe right-sided headache, nausea, vomiting, decreased vision, and halos around lights. On examination, the right eye is red with a hazy cornea, mid-dilated fixed pupil, and IOP of 58 mmHg. The definitive treatment after initial medical management is:**
- A) Topical timolol alone
- B) Laser peripheral iridotomy
- C) Trabeculectomy
- D) Topical steroid drops

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Acute primary angle-closure glaucoma (PACG) presents with the classic pentad: severe pain, nausea/vomiting, red eye, fixed mid-dilated pupil (sphincter ischemia), and haloes (corneal edema). IOP >40 mmHg. Immediate management: IV acetazolamide 500 mg + topical timolol + topical pilocarpine (post-IOP reduction to constrict pupil and open angle) + IV mannitol if needed. Definitive treatment: laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) creates a hole in the peripheral iris to bypass pupillary block and allow aqueous flow. Fellow eye also treated prophylactically with LPI.

</details>

**Q3. A 45-year-old patient on long-term topical steroids for allergic conjunctivitis develops elevated IOP of 30 mmHg with open angle on gonioscopy. The mechanism of steroid-induced glaucoma is:**
- A) Increased aqueous production by the ciliary body
- B) Reduced outflow due to accumulation of glycosaminoglycans in the trabecular meshwork
- C) Pupillary block from steroid-induced posterior synechiae
- D) Angle closure from steroid-induced lens swelling

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Steroid-induced glaucoma occurs due to accumulation of glycosaminoglycans (and other extracellular matrix material) in the trabecular meshwork, reducing aqueous outflow. It is an open-angle type. Occurs with topical, periocular, or systemic steroids; topical ocular steroids carry the highest risk. "Steroid responders" (genetically predisposed — often relatives of POAG patients) show IOP rise within 4–6 weeks. Management: stop steroids, topical IOP-lowering agents. If irreversible trabecular damage: trabeculectomy. Differentials: pigmentary glaucoma (pigment in trabecular meshwork from iris friction).

</details>

**Q4. Goldmann applanation tonometry is the gold standard for measuring IOP. It measures IOP based on which principle?**
- A) Schiotz indentation principle (plunger weight displaces cornea)
- B) Imbert-Fick principle (force required to flatten a fixed area of cornea = IOP × area)
- C) Non-contact air-puff principle
- D) Rebound principle using a magnetic probe

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Goldmann applanation tonometry (GAT) is based on the Imbert-Fick law: the pressure inside a sphere equals the force applied to flatten its surface divided by the area of flattening (P = F/A). GAT flattens a standard 3.06 mm diameter area of cornea; the force required to achieve this applanation equals the IOP. It is the international gold standard. Limitations: affected by central corneal thickness (thin cornea → underestimates IOP; thick cornea → overestimates). Schiotz is the indentation tonometer. Non-contact (air-puff) tonometry is a screening tool, not gold standard.

</details>

**Q5. A 40-year-old Indian man is screened for glaucoma. He has a cup:disc ratio of 0.8, IOP of 16 mmHg, open angles, and normal visual fields. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG)
- B) Ocular hypertension
- C) Normal tension glaucoma
- D) Physiological cupping

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Normal tension glaucoma (NTG) is characterized by glaucomatous optic nerve damage and visual field loss with IOP consistently within the normal range (≤21 mmHg) and open angles. It is particularly common in Japanese populations and India. Pathogenesis: vascular insufficiency to the optic nerve (vasospasm, nocturnal hypotension). Management: IOP reduction by 30% from baseline (latanoprost preferred) still slows progression. Ocular hypertension = elevated IOP without optic nerve damage/VF loss. Physiological cupping = large cup:disc without glaucomatous VF defect or nerve damage — typically C:D ≤0.7 and symmetrical.

</details>

## RETINAL DISORDERS (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 65-year-old hypertensive woman presents with sudden, complete, painless loss of vision in the right eye. Fundus examination shows generalized retinal whitening (milky edema) with a cherry-red spot at the fovea. The afferent pupillary defect is present. The pathophysiology and time window for treatment are:**
- A) Venous occlusion; treat within 24 hours
- B) Central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO); treat within 90 minutes for best outcomes
- C) Retinal detachment; surgery within 24 hours
- D) Optic neuritis; IV steroids within 2 weeks

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

CRAO causes sudden, complete, painless monocular vision loss. Cherry-red spot at the macula: the macula receives blood from the choroid (choriocapillaris) and appears red against the surrounding ischemic white retina (infarcted inner retina becomes opaque). Afferent pupillary defect (APD) present. Treatment within 90 minutes (analogous to cerebral stroke): lower IOP (ocular massage, IV acetazolamide, anterior chamber paracentesis), vasodilators, and now intra-arterial thrombolysis in select centers. RAPD is a key examination finding. Associated with carotid disease, cardiac emboli, and giant cell arteritis in elderly.

</details>

**Q7. A 55-year-old diabetic man presents with sudden visual loss in his left eye. Fundus shows flame-shaped and dot-blot hemorrhages in all four quadrants, disc edema, dilated tortuous retinal veins, and "blood-and-thunder" fundus appearance. The complication most feared in the following months and its treatment are:**
- A) Retinal detachment; scleral buckle
- B) Macular edema and neovascular glaucoma from iris neovascularization (rubeosis iridis); anti-VEGF injections
- C) Cataract; phacoemulsification
- D) Optic atrophy; no treatment

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) — "blood-and-thunder" fundus: flame hemorrhages in all 4 quadrants, disc edema, dilated tortuous veins. Two outcomes: non-ischemic (good prognosis) vs. ischemic (>10 disc areas of capillary non-perfusion on FFA — poor prognosis). Ischemic CRVO leads to retinal ischemia → VEGF secretion → iris neovascularization (rubeosis iridis, appearing at 90 days = "100-day glaucoma") → neovascular glaucoma. Treatment: intravitreal anti-VEGF (bevacizumab/ranibizumab) for macular edema; pan-retinal photocoagulation to prevent/treat neovascularization.

</details>

**Q8. A 50-year-old diabetic patient has fundus findings of retinal neovascularization (new vessels on disc and elsewhere), preretinal hemorrhage, and fibrovascular proliferative membranes. This stage of diabetic retinopathy requires:**
- A) Observation and 3-monthly follow-up
- B) Focal laser photocoagulation only
- C) Pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP) ± intravitreal anti-VEGF
- D) Cataract surgery first

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR) is defined by neovascularization (new vessels on disc [NVD] or elsewhere [NVE]) — a high-risk characteristic. Neovessels arise from VEGF released by ischemic retina. PDR can cause vitreous hemorrhage, tractional retinal detachment, and rubeosis iridis. Treatment: pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP — 1500–2000 burns in peripheral retina to ablate ischemic tissue and reduce VEGF) ± intravitreal anti-VEGF (bevacizumab/ranibizumab) for rapid regression. Diabetic macular edema (DME) at any stage: anti-VEGF first-line. Vitrectomy for vitreous hemorrhage or TRD.

</details>

**Q9. A 45-year-old myopic man presents with flashes of light (photopsia), floaters, and a "curtain coming down from above" obscuring vision. Fundus examination shows an elevated, corrugated retina with a retinal break. The most common type of retinal detachment and primary treatment option are:**
- A) Exudative (secondary) detachment; treat the underlying cause
- B) Tractional detachment; vitrectomy
- C) Rhegmatogenous detachment (from a retinal break); scleral buckling or pars plana vitrectomy
- D) Retinoschisis; observation only

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (RRD) is the most common type, caused by a full-thickness retinal break (hole/tear) through which liquefied vitreous seeps behind the retina. Risk factors: myopia, trauma, lattice degeneration, prior vitreous surgery. Symptoms: photopsia (traction on retina), floaters (vitreous hemorrhage or pigment cells — "tobacco dust" = Shafer's sign), and field defect (curtain). Treatment: scleral buckling (for peripheral breaks), pars plana vitrectomy + gas tamponade (for posterior or complex breaks), or pneumatic retinopexy (for superior breaks). Macula-off detachment = emergency.

</details>

**Q10. A 70-year-old woman presents with gradual central visual distortion and decreased reading vision in her right eye. Fundus shows drusen at the macula and a grayish subretinal membrane. OCT reveals choroidal neovascular membrane (CNV). The treatment of choice is:**
- A) Laser photocoagulation to the fovea
- B) Intravitreal anti-VEGF (ranibizumab or bevacizumab)
- C) Photodynamic therapy with verteporfin alone
- D) Vitrectomy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Age-related macular degeneration (ARMD): dry (atrophic) type — drusen + geographic atrophy, slowly progressive, no proven treatment (AREDS vitamins slow progression); wet (exudative) type — choroidal neovascular membrane (CNVM) under the retina, causing rapid central vision loss and metamorphopsia (distortion). Treatment of wet ARMD: intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab/bevacizumab/aflibercept) as first-line — monthly injections initially, then treat-and-extend. Ranibizumab is FDA-approved specifically for wet ARMD. The Amsler grid detects metamorphopsia in early disease.

</details>

## LENS AND CATARACT (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 70-year-old man with poorly controlled diabetes presents with glare and difficulty reading in bright light. Slit-lamp examination shows opacification of the posterior lens capsule. This type of cataract is associated with which conditions, and why does it cause particularly severe glare?**
- A) Nuclear cataract — associated with aging; glare from nuclear sclerosis
- B) Posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC) — associated with DM, steroids, radiation; lies at the nodal point of the optical axis, causing maximum glare
- C) Cortical cataract — associated with UV exposure; spoke-wheel opacities scatter light
- D) Anterior subcapsular — associated with uveitis; blocks direct axial vision

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC) is the most visually disabling type because it lies at the posterior pole at the nodal point — even a small opacity causes maximum visual disturbance (glare in bright light, difficulty reading). Associated with: diabetes mellitus, long-term corticosteroid use, ionizing radiation, and posterior uveitis. Nuclear cataract (nuclear sclerosis) causes myopic shift ("second sight" in presbyopes). Cortical cataract appears as radial spoke-wheel opacities. PSC causes worst subjective symptoms for the size of the opacity.

</details>

**Q12. A patient undergoes uncomplicated phacoemulsification with posterior chamber IOL implantation. Two years later, they present with progressive visual blurring and glare. Slit-lamp shows pearl-like opacities on the posterior lens capsule. The diagnosis and treatment are:**
- A) Primary cataract recurrence; redo phacoemulsification
- B) Posterior capsule opacification (PCO / Elschnig pearls); Nd:YAG laser posterior capsulotomy
- C) IOL dislocation; IOL repositioning
- D) Uveitis-induced membrane; topical steroids

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Posterior capsule opacification (PCO) is the most common complication after extracapsular cataract extraction and phacoemulsification — caused by residual lens epithelial cells (A-cells and E-cells from the equatorial region) migrating across the posterior capsule and forming Elschnig pearls (bladder cells) or fibrous plaques. It occurs in 20–40% of patients within 2–5 years. Treatment: Nd:YAG laser posterior capsulotomy — creates a central opening in the opacified capsule; immediate visual improvement, outpatient procedure, permanent. Complications of YAG capsulotomy: IOL pitting, IOP spike, CME.

</details>

**Q13. A newborn is brought at 6 weeks of age with a white reflex (leukocoria) in both eyes. The mother had a febrile rash with lymphadenopathy in the first trimester of pregnancy. The most likely diagnosis and the most critical aspect of management timing are:**
- A) Retinoblastoma; enucleation within 2 weeks
- B) Congenital cataract from rubella; surgery before 6–8 weeks of age to prevent amblyopia
- C) Persistent fetal vasculature; observation
- D) Coat's disease; laser photocoagulation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Congenital cataract from rubella (most common infectious cause — first trimester maternal rubella causes pearly white nuclear cataract + PFV). The visual system requires clear retinal image for development during the critical period (0–8 years, most critical 0–6 months). Dense amblyogenic cataract must be surgically removed before 6–8 weeks of age (ideally by 4–6 weeks for dense bilateral cataracts), followed immediately by optical rehabilitation (aphakic glasses or contact lens) and patching of the better eye to prevent irreversible amblyopia. Delay leads to nystagmus and permanent visual impairment.

</details>

**Q14. In a NEET PG survey on blindness in India, cataract accounts for the highest proportion of blindness. Which statement about cataract-related blindness in the Indian context is CORRECT?**
- A) Cataract is the most common cause of irreversible blindness in India
- B) Cataract is the most common cause of treatable/avoidable blindness in India, accounting for ~66% of blindness
- C) Glaucoma is more common than cataract as a cause of blindness in India
- D) The National Blindness Control Programme has eliminated cataract blindness in India

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cataract is the most common cause of avoidable/treatable blindness in India, accounting for approximately 62–66% of all blindness (National Blindness Survey). **Q15. A 55-year-old woman with early nuclear cataract complains of improved reading vision without glasses for the first time in years. This phenomenon is known as:**
- A) Pseudo-exfoliation
- B) Second sight (myopic shift from nuclear sclerosis)
- C) Anisometropia
- D) Cortical sparing

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

"Second sight" is the paradoxical improvement in near vision (reading without glasses) that occurs in early nuclear cataract due to myopic shift — nuclear sclerosis increases the refractive index of the lens nucleus, making the eye more myopic overall. A presbyopic patient (who needed reading glasses) temporarily regains near vision as the increased lens power compensates for the lost accommodative amplitude. This is transient; as the cataract matures, vision worsens even at near. Nuclear cataract also causes worse vision in bright light (pupil constriction reduces depth of field) and monocular diplopia.

</details>

## CORNEA AND EXTERNAL EYE (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 25-year-old contact lens wearer presents with severe eye pain, photophobia, and a ring-shaped corneal infiltrate. Confocal microscopy reveals round, double-walled cysts. The diagnosis and treatment are:**
- A) Bacterial keratitis; topical fluoroquinolone
- B) Fungal keratitis; topical natamycin
- C) Acanthamoeba keratitis; polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) + propamidine isethionate (Brolene)
- D) HSV keratitis; topical acyclovir

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Acanthamoeba keratitis classically occurs in contact lens wearers (especially those who swim or use tap water for lens rinsing). The pathognomonic sign is a ring-shaped (annular) stromal infiltrate. Confocal microscopy reveals double-walled cysts (hallmark) and trophozoites. Severe pain out of proportion to clinical findings. Treatment: topical PHMB (biguanide) + propamidine isethionate (Brolene) ± hexamidine for 6–12 months. Fluoroquinolones for bacterial keratitis (hypopyon, rapidly progressive). Natamycin (antifungal) for fungal keratitis (feathery edges, satellite lesions). Acyclovir for HSV dendritic ulcer.

</details>

**Q17. A 30-year-old presents with a corneal ulcer showing a classic dendritic (branching) pattern on fluorescein staining with terminal bulbs. The causative virus and treatment are:**
- A) Adenovirus; supportive treatment only
- B) Herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1); topical acyclovir 3% ointment 5 times/day
- C) Varicella-zoster virus; topical natamycin
- D) Cytomegalovirus; ganciclovir gel

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

HSV keratitis is the most common cause of corneal blindness from infection in developed countries. The dendritic ulcer (branching with terminal bulbs — true dendrites) on fluorescein staining is pathognomonic of HSV epithelial keratitis. Treatment: topical acyclovir 3% ointment 5× daily for 14 days, or oral acyclovir. NEVER use topical steroids in active HSV epithelial keratitis (worsens viral replication → geographic ulcer → corneal melt). Steroids are used in HSV stromal keratitis (disciform) under antiviral cover. VZV causes pseudodendrites (no terminal bulbs). Adenovirus causes epidemic keratoconjunctivitis (follicular conjunctivitis, no corneal ulcer).

</details>

**Q20. A pterygium is being removed from a 50-year-old farmer who has worked outdoors for 30 years. Regarding the pathology and surgical principles, which statement is CORRECT?**
- A) Pterygium arises from the temporal (lateral) conjunctiva and grows toward the nasal limbus
- B) Pterygium arises from the nasal conjunctiva, grows from the nasal side onto the cornea, and is associated with UV radiation; bare sclera excision alone has high recurrence
- C) Pterygium is a malignant neoplasm and requires chemotherapy
- D) Pterygium never recurs after surgical excision

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

## NEURO-OPHTHALMOLOGY AND SQUINT (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 35-year-old man is examined with a swinging flashlight test. When the torch is swung from the left eye to the right eye, the right pupil dilates instead of constricting. This indicates:**
- A) A defect in the efferent (motor) pathway of the right eye
- B) A relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD / Marcus Gunn pupil) in the right eye, indicating optic nerve or retinal disease on the right
- C) Horner's syndrome on the left side
- D) Adie's tonic pupil on the right

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect / Marcus Gunn pupil): in the swinging flashlight test, the affected pupil paradoxically dilates when light is shone directly into it (after being illuminated from the other eye), indicating reduced afferent signal from that eye. Causes: optic neuritis, CRAO, optic atrophy, large retinal detachment, or glaucomatous optic neuropathy. It reflects asymmetric afferent input to the pretectal nucleus. Pure efferent defects (3rd nerve palsy, Adie's) cause anisocoria but NOT RAPD. RAPD is the most objective clinical sign of optic nerve disease.

</details>

**Q23. A patient with a pituitary macroadenoma undergoes visual field testing. The expected visual field defect is:**
- A) Unilateral central scotoma
- B) Bitemporal hemianopia
- C) Left homonymous hemianopia
- D) Inferior altitudinal defect

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The optic chiasm lies directly above the pituitary fossa. A pituitary macroadenoma compresses the chiasm from below, preferentially affecting the crossing nasal fibers (from the nasal half of each retina, serving the temporal visual field of each eye) → bitemporal hemianopia (loss of both temporal fields). **Q24. A 4-year-old child is noted by parents to have an inward deviation of the right eye that is constant and does not change with direction of gaze. The deviation is equal in all positions of gaze. Cover test reveals deviation. The most likely type of squint and key differentiating feature from paralytic squint are:**
- A) Paralytic squint; deviation varies with gaze direction (incomitant)
- B) Concomitant (comitant) esotropia; deviation is equal in all directions of gaze
- C) Exotropia; the eye deviates outward
- D) Hypertropia; vertical deviation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

**Q25. In a patient with raised intracranial pressure from a posterior fossa tumor, the earliest and most commonly affected cranial nerve causing diplopia is the 6th nerve. This is because:**
- A) The 6th nerve exits from the pons at the most superficial location
- B) The 6th nerve has the longest intracranial course, running along the base of the skull from the pons to the superior orbital fissure, making it most susceptible to stretch from raised ICP
- C) The posterior fossa tumor directly compresses the 6th nerve nucleus
- D) The 6th nerve has no bony protection throughout its course

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The 6th (abducens) nerve has the longest intracranial course of any cranial nerve — it exits the brainstem at the pontomedullary junction, travels upward along the clivus, makes a sharp bend over the petrous apex (Dorello's canal), and enters the cavernous sinus. This long course makes it most susceptible to stretching and compression from raised ICP, hydrocephalus, or any space-occupying lesion — hence it is a "false localizing sign" (6th nerve palsy does not indicate a localizing lesion). Clinically causes medial deviation of the eye and diplopia on lateral gaze.

</details>

## BLINDNESS AND TROPICAL EYE DISEASE (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. A 6-year-old child in a tribal district of Odisha, India, presents with leukocoria (white pupillary reflex) in the left eye. The eye is slightly enlarged (buphthalmos-like). Fundus reveals a white mass in the vitreous cavity. Family history reveals the child's father had the same disease treated in childhood. The genetic basis and first-line treatment are:**
- A) BRCA1 mutation; chemotherapy
- B) Rb gene mutation (chromosome 13q14, autosomal dominant with 90% penetrance); enucleation for unilateral advanced disease or systemic chemotherapy for bilateral/early disease
- C) TP53 mutation; radiation
- D) NF2 mutation; surgery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Retinoblastoma is the most common intraocular malignant tumor in children (most present before age 5). Leukocoria (cat's eye reflex) is the most common presenting sign (60%). The Rb tumor suppressor gene is on chromosome 13q14 — the "two-hit" hypothesis (Knudson): both alleles must be inactivated. Hereditary (40%): germline mutation (autosomal dominant, bilateral, multifocal) + second somatic hit. Sporadic (60%): two somatic hits (unilateral, unifocal). Treatment: enucleation for advanced unilateral disease; intra-arterial chemotherapy (melphalan) or systemic chemotherapy (vincristine-carboplatin-etoposide) for early/bilateral disease; external beam radiation (avoided in hereditary due to secondary malignancy risk).

</details>

- A) Anopheles mosquito; Wuchereria bancrofti
- B) Simulium damnosum (black fly); Onchocerca volvulus (river blindness)
- C) Culex mosquito; Loa loa
- D) Sandfly; Leishmania donovani

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Onchocerciasis (river blindness) is caused by Onchocerca volvulus, transmitted by the black fly Simulium damnosum, which breeds in fast-flowing rivers (hence "river blindness"). It is the second most common infectious cause of blindness worldwide. Microfilariae migrate to the eye and cause: sclerosing keratitis (fluffy anterior stromal opacities from dead mf), anterior uveitis, and eventually glaucoma and optic atrophy. Systemic: subcutaneous nodules (onchocercomata), pruritic skin (lizard skin). Treatment: ivermectin (annual single dose for mass drug administration — does not kill adults, only microfilariae). Suramin kills adult worms.

</details>

**Q28. A child in a community is diagnosed with vitamin A deficiency at the stage of keratomalacia (X3B). In addition to treating this child, which public health intervention is used for mass prevention in India?**
- A) Universal supplementation with Vitamin D
- B) National Vitamin A Supplementation Programme — high-dose oral Vitamin A every 6 months to children 9 months to 5 years
- C) Iron and folic acid supplementation only
- D) Zinc supplementation program

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

India's National Vitamin A Supplementation Programme provides high-dose Vitamin A supplementation every 6 months to all children aged 9 months to 5 years: 100,000 IU at 9 months (with measles vaccine), then 200,000 IU every 6 months up to 5 years. It is delivered through the Universal Immunization Programme (UIP) and ASHA/Anganwadi workers. Keratomalacia (corneal liquefaction) at stage X3B is irreversible — prevention is the goal. Vitamin A also reduces measles mortality by 50% and all-cause child mortality. VAD is the most common preventable cause of childhood blindness in India.

</details>

**Q29. A patient with long-standing type 2 diabetes (20 years) is being assessed for diabetic eye disease. The sequence of diabetic retinopathy from earliest to most advanced and the treatment at each key stage are:**
- A) Microaneurysms → hard exudates → neovascularization → vitreous hemorrhage; treat all stages with laser
- B) Microaneurysms (earliest) → NPDR (microaneurysms, hemorrhages, CWS, IRMA) → PDR (neovascularization) → tractional RD; NPDR: observe/anti-VEGF for DME; PDR: pan-retinal photocoagulation ± anti-VEGF; TRD: vitrectomy
- C) Cotton-wool spots → neovascularization → NPDR → cataract; treat with anti-VEGF at all stages
- D) Hard exudates → soft exudates → neovascularization → CRVO; treat with photocoagulation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Diabetic retinopathy progression: Microaneurysms (first ophthalmoscopic sign) → mild NPDR → moderate NPDR → severe NPDR (4-2-1 rule: hemorrhages in 4 quadrants, venous beading in 2 quadrants, IRMA in 1 quadrant) → PDR (neovascularization = new vessels on disc [NVD] or elsewhere [NVE]) → advanced PDR (vitreous hemorrhage, TRD). DME (diabetic macular edema) can occur at any stage. Management: NPDR — observation, optimize glycemic/BP control; DME — anti-VEGF (first-line) or focal laser; PDR — pan-retinal photocoagulation ± anti-VEGF; TRD with vision loss — vitrectomy.

</details>

**Q30. The National Programme for Control of Blindness (NPCB) in India has set a target to eliminate avoidable blindness. Which of the following correctly states a key target of the NPCB and the prevalence of blindness in India?**
- A) Eliminate all blindness by 2020; current prevalence 5%
- B) Reduce prevalence of blindness to 0.3% by 2020 (from baseline ~1.1%); key strategies include increasing cataract surgical rate and strengthening primary eye care
- C) Focus exclusively on glaucoma screening; current prevalence 0.1%
- D) Eliminate trachoma by 2025 without addressing cataract

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The National Programme for Control of Blindness (NPCB), launched in 1976 (revised as NPCB&VI — National Programme for Control of Blindness and Visual Impairment), aims to reduce the prevalence of blindness from ~1.1% (2006–07 National Survey) to 0.3% by 2020. Key strategies: increasing cataract surgical rate (CSR target: 6 million/year), school eye screening, low vision services, corneal transplantation, strengthening district-level eye care infrastructure, and control of diabetic retinopathy and glaucoma. Cataract (66%), refractive errors, and glaucoma are the top three targets. Vision 2020: The Right to Sight is the global initiative India is aligned with.

</details>
