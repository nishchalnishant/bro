## Anatomy of the Eye

### Layers of the Eyeball
1. **Outer fibrous coat**: Cornea (anterior, transparent) + Sclera (posterior, opaque, white)
2. **Middle vascular coat (Uvea)**: Iris + Ciliary body + Choroid
3. **Inner neural coat**: Retina (neurosensory + RPE)

### Cornea — 5 Layers (Anterior to Posterior)
1. **Epithelium** (5-6 layers stratified squamous, non-keratinized; regenerates rapidly)
2. **Bowman's layer** (acellular condensation of stroma; does NOT regenerate — opacities permanent)
3. **Stroma** (90% of corneal thickness; type I collagen, regular arrangement for transparency)
4. **Descemet's membrane** (basement membrane of endothelium; regenerates)
5. **Endothelium** (single layer; maintains corneal dehydration/deturgescence via Na-K ATPase pump; does NOT regenerate — cell density decreases with age; normal ~2500 cells/mm²)

### Segments and Chambers
- **Anterior segment**: Cornea, iris, lens, anterior chamber (AC), posterior chamber (PC)
- **Posterior segment**: Vitreous, retina, choroid, optic nerve
- **AC**: Between cornea and iris; filled with aqueous humor; normal depth 2.5-3 mm
- **PC**: Between iris and lens anterior surface; small space
- **Aqueous humor**: Produced by ciliary body epithelium (posterior chamber → through pupil → anterior chamber → trabecular meshwork → Schlemm's canal); also uveoscleral drainage (10-15%)

### Extraocular Muscles (EOM)

| Muscle | Primary Action | Secondary | Tertiary | Nerve |
|--------|---------------|-----------|---------|-------|
| Medial rectus (MR) | Adduction | — | — | **III** |
| Lateral rectus (LR) | Abduction | — | — | **VI (Abducens)** |
| Superior rectus (SR) | Elevation | Intorsion | Adduction | **III** |
| Inferior rectus (IR) | Depression | Extorsion | Adduction | **III** |
| **Superior oblique (SO)** | Intorsion | Depression | Abduction | **IV (Trochlear)** |
| **Inferior oblique (IO)** | Extorsion | Elevation | Abduction | **III** |

**Mnemonic**: **LR6(SO4)3** — LR by VI, SO by IV, rest by III

- **SO origin**: Lesser wing of sphenoid (not annulus of Zinn) → passes through **trochlea** (pulley) on medial orbital wall → inserts on posterior-superior globe
- Primary action of SO in adducted eye: **Depression** (clinically important — SO palsy = hypertropia in adduction)

### Optic Nerve
- Length: ~50 mm (intraocular 1mm + intraorbital 25mm + intracanalicular 5mm + intracranial 16mm)
- **Optic disc (blind spot)**: 1.5 mm diameter; nasal to fovea; NO photoreceptors
- **Fovea centralis**: Center of macula; highest visual acuity (highest cone density, no rods, no ganglion cells); avascular zone (FAZ)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: LR6SO4 rest III; cornea has 5 layers; endothelium does not regenerate; SO = trochlear nerve (IV)

## Refraction

### Definitions
- **Emmetropia**: Parallel rays focus exactly on retina at rest (no refractive error)
- **Ametropia**: Refractive error — myopia, hypermetropia, astigmatism

### Myopia (Shortsightedness)
- **Mechanism**: Eyeball too long OR high corneal curvature → light focuses **in front** of retina
- **Correction**: **Concave (diverging) lens** (minus power)
- **High myopia** (>-6D): Risk of
  - **P**osterior staphyloma (ectatic bulge, temporal to disc)
  - **R**hegmatogenous retinal detachment (lattice degeneration, atrophic holes)
  - **I**ncreased risk of glaucoma (open-angle)
  - **P**eripapillary atrophy (C and B zones)
  - **CNVM** (choroidal neovascular membrane)
  - Cataract (nuclear)

### Hypermetropia (Longsightedness/Hyperopia)
- **Mechanism**: Eyeball too short OR flat cornea → light focuses **behind** retina
- **Correction**: **Convex (converging) lens** (plus power)
- **Young patients compensate** with accommodation (ciliary muscle contraction → lens thickens)
- **Risk**: Acute primary angle-closure glaucoma (shallow anterior chamber due to short eye)

### Astigmatism
- Different meridians have different focal lengths
- **Regular**: Two principal meridians perpendicular; corrected by **cylindrical lens**
- **With the rule**: Vertical meridian more curved (common in young); **Against the rule**: Horizontal meridian more curved (common in elderly)

### Presbyopia
- **Age-related loss of accommodation** due to lens hardening and ciliary muscle weakening
- Onset: ~40 years; reading glasses needed (**+1D at 40 years, adds +0.5D every 5 years**)
- Standard reading glasses: **+2.5D for 40-year-old** (reading at 40 cm); increase as age advances
- **Does NOT mean hypermetropia** (separate conditions)

### Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)
- **Definition**: Reduced visual acuity NOT explained by structural abnormality; due to abnormal visual experience during critical period (<8 years, peak <3-5 years)
- **Causes**: Strabismus, anisometropia (>2D difference), deprivation (congenital cataract, ptosis)
- **Treatment**: Correct refractive error first → **patching of better eye** (force use of amblyopic eye); effective **before age 7-8 years**
- Occlusion amblyopia risk if patching for too long in young children — alternate patching

### Anisometropia
- Unequal refractive errors in the two eyes (>2D)
- **Contact lenses preferred** over spectacles (spectacle lenses cause aniseikonia)
- Significant cause of amblyopia

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Presbyopia reading glass +2.5D for 40yrs; amblyopia treatment before age 7-8; concave lens = myopia; convex lens = hypermetropia

## Eyelid Disorders

### Chalazion vs Hordeolum (Stye)

| Feature | Chalazion | Stye (Hordeolum externum) |
|---------|----------|--------------------------|
| Gland | **Meibomian gland** (in tarsal plate, lipid secretion) | **Glands of Zeis** (sebaceous, at lash follicle) or Moll (sweat gland) |
| Location | Mid-tarsal, upper lid more common | At lid margin (base of eyelash) |
| Inflammation | **Lipogranuloma** (sterile, chronic) | Acute bacterial infection (Staph aureus) |
| Pain | Painless (chronic) | Painful, tender |
| Treatment | **Intralesional triamcinolone** OR **incision and curettage (I&C)** from conjunctival side (Chalazion clamp) | **Hot fomentation + topical antibiotics**; drainage if pointing |

### Ptosis
| Cause | Features | Mechanism |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| **Horner's syndrome** | **2 mm ptosis** + miosis + anhidrosis (ipsilateral) | Sympathetic disruption → Müller's muscle paralysis |
| **3rd nerve palsy (complete)** | **Complete ptosis** + dilated pupil (mydriasis) + exotropia (eye down and out) | Levator palpebrae superioris paralysis |
| Myasthenia gravis | Variable (fatiguable, worse evening); bilateral; ptosis + diplopia | Neuromuscular junction — acetylcholine receptor antibodies |
| Congenital | Present at birth; one-third with associated amblyopia | Levator maldevelopment |
| Aponeurotic | Elderly; normal levator function; "senile ptosis" | Levator aponeurosis disinsertion |

- **Measurement**: Marginal reflex distance (MRD1 = upper lid to corneal light reflex; normal >2 mm)
- **Levator function**: Distance lid moves from downgaze to upgaze (normal >8 mm)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Horner's = 2mm ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis; III nerve palsy = complete ptosis + dilated pupil + exotropia

## Conjunctivitis

### Bacterial vs Viral vs Allergic

| Feature | Bacterial | Viral | Allergic |
|---------|----------|-------|---------|
| Discharge | **Mucopurulent** | **Watery** | **Ropy/mucoid** |
| Conjunctival reaction | Papillae | **Follicles** | Giant papillae (GPC) |
| Preauricular node | Absent | **Present** | Absent |
| Symptoms | Sticking lids in AM | Red, burning, watery eye | **Intense itching** |
| Treatment | Topical antibiotics | Supportive; antiviral if HSV | Antihistamines, mast cell stabilizers |

### Viral Conjunctivitis — Key Points
- **Adenovirus**: Most common viral cause; epidemic keratoconjunctivitis (EKC) — types 8, 19 (severe + corneal involvement with subepithelial infiltrates)
- **HSV**: Dendritic ulcer on cornea; acyclovir
- **Membranous conjunctivitis**: True membrane (bleeds when removed) — diphtheria (Corynebacterium), beta-hemolytic streptococcus
- **Pseudomembranous**: Peels without bleeding — adenoviral EKC, chlamydia, chemical

### Trachoma
- **Cause**: **Chlamydia trachomatis** serotypes **A, B, Ba, C** (ocular trachoma); D-K (genital STI); L1-3 (LGV)
- **World's leading cause of preventable blindness** (infectious)
- **Pathology**: Follicular conjunctivitis → scarring → **entropion** → trichiasis → corneal abrasion → scarring → **blindness**
- **Features**: Herbert's pits (healed follicles at limbus — pathognomonic), **pannus** (fibrovascular tissue over superior cornea), Arlt's line (subconjunctival scar, parallel to upper lid margin)
- **WHO Simplified Grading (TF/TI/TS/TT/CO)**:
  - TF = Trachoma Follicles (active)
  - TI = Trachoma Intense
  - TS = Trachoma Scarring (Arlt's line)
  - TT = Trichiasis
  - CO = Corneal Opacity
- **Treatment**: **SAFE strategy** — Surgery (entropion correction), Antibiotics (azithromycin 1g oral or tetracycline ointment), Face washing, Environmental improvement

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Herbert's pits = pathognomonic trachoma; SAFE strategy; C. trachomatis A-C = trachoma

## Cornea

### Keratitis
| Type | Feature | Treatment |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Bacterial | Corneal ulcer + stromal infiltrate + hypopyon; Pseudomonas in contact lens users | Topical fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin/ofloxacin) |
| **HSV (Herpes simplex)** | **Dendritic ulcer** (branching, terminal end-bulbs); **fluorescein staining**; reduced corneal sensation; geographic ulcer (advanced) | **Acyclovir** 3% ointment × 14 days; steroids CONTRAINDICATED (worsen viral keratitis) |
| **Acanthamoeba** | **Contact lens wearers**; swimming in contaminated water; **ring-shaped stromal infiltrate** (Wessley immune ring); pain disproportionate to signs | PHMB + propamidine (Brolene) drops |
| Fungal | Post-trauma with vegetation; Aspergillus/Fusarium; feathery edges, dry, satellite lesions | Natamycin topical |

### Corneal Dystrophies
| Type | Layer Affected | Heredity | Features |
|------|---------------|---------|---------|
| **Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy** | Endothelium | AD | Guttata (wartlike excrescences on Descemet's), corneal edema worst in morning, treatment: DMEK/DSAEK |
| **Granular dystrophy** | Bowman's/stroma | AD | Discrete white "breadcrumb" deposits; clear between; eosinophilic (Masson's trichrome stain red) |
| **Lattice dystrophy** | Stroma/Bowman's | AD | Refractile lattice lines; amyloid deposition (Congo red positive) |
| **Macular dystrophy** | Full stroma | **AR** | Diffuse ground-glass haze; mucopolysaccharide deposits (Alcian blue positive) |

- **Mnemonic for inheritance**: **G**ranular and **L**attice = AD; **M**acular = AR (GML → the M is recessive)

### Keratoconus
- **Bilateral**, asymmetric, progressive ectasia (thinning and cone-shaped protrusion)
- **Signs**:
  - **Fleischer ring**: Iron deposition in epithelium (hemosiderin) at base of cone (Perl's Prussian blue stain)
  - **Vogt's striae**: Fine vertical stress lines in posterior stroma
  - **Munson's sign**: V-shaped indentation of lower lid in downgaze
  - Oil droplet reflex on retinoscopy
  - Irregular astigmatism; **scissors reflex on retinoscopy**
- **Treatment**:
  - Mild: **Spectacles → rigid gas permeable (RGP) contact lenses** (best VA)
  - Moderate-advanced: **Corneal collagen cross-linking (C3R)** — arrest progression (riboflavin + UV-A)
  - Severe: **Penetrating keratoplasty (PK)** or DALK

### Corneal Transplant Types
| Type | Procedure | Indication |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **PK (Penetrating keratoplasty)** | Full-thickness corneal graft | Any corneal opacity, keratoconus (advanced) |
| **DALK (Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty)** | Anterior stroma; Descemet's + endothelium preserved | Keratoconus, anterior stromal opacity; no rejection of endothelium |
| **DSAEK/DSEK** | Descemet's stripping automated endothelial keratoplasty | Fuchs' dystrophy, pseudophakic bullous keratopathy |
| **DMEK** | Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty | Same as DSAEK; thinner graft; better outcomes |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Dendritic ulcer = HSV; steroids contraindicated in viral keratitis; Fleischer ring + Munson's sign = keratoconus; Macular dystrophy = AR

## Glaucoma

### POAG (Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma)

**Tonometry:**
- Normal IOP: **10-21 mmHg** (mean 16 mmHg)
- **Goldmann Applanation Tonometer (GAT)**: Gold standard
- Non-contact tonometer (air puff): Screening
- Schiotz tonometer: Portable, indentation type

**Optic Disc Changes:**
- **C:D ratio >0.6** (normal ≤0.5); or C:D asymmetry >0.2
- **Notching of neuroretinal rim** (ISNT rule: Inferior > Superior > Nasal > Temporal — violation suggests glaucoma)
- **RNFL (Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer) loss**: On OCT (earliest detectable change)
- **Nasalization of vessels** (vessels displaced nasally)
- **Baring of circumlinear vessel**: Vessel arching without underlying rim tissue

**Visual Field Defects (progressive order):**
1. Enlargement of blind spot
2. **Bjerrum's/arcuate scotoma** (arching from blind spot to nasal horizontal raphe)
3. **Nasal step** (defect crossing horizontal meridian nasally)
4. Temporal wedge
5. Tubular vision (only central tunnel remaining)
6. Total blindness

**Treatment (Step-wise):**
| Step | Drug | Mechanism |
|------|------|-----------|
| 1st line | **Prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost, bimatoprost, travoprost)** | Increase uveoscleral outflow; once daily; SE: iris color change, lash growth, periorbital fat atrophy |
| 2nd | **Beta-blockers (timolol)** | Reduce aqueous production; contraindicated in asthma/COPD/bradycardia |
| 3rd | **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide topical; acetazolamide oral)** | Reduce aqueous production |
| 4th | **Alpha-2 agonists (brimonidine)** | Reduce production + increase outflow |
| 5th | **Pilocarpine** | Miotic; increases trabecular outflow |
| Laser | **Laser trabeculoplasty (SLT/ALT)** | Improves trabecular outflow |
| Surgery | **Trabeculectomy** | Filtration surgery; creates bleb |

### PACG (Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma)

**Risk Factors**: Hypermetropia (short axial length), elderly women, Asian ethnicity, shallow anterior chamber, family history

**Acute PACG Attack:**
- Severe eye pain, **halos around lights** (corneal edema), headache, nausea/vomiting, decreased vision
- **Signs**: **Mid-dilated oval/fixed pupil** (most characteristic), **ciliary flush**, **hazy/steamy cornea** (edema), very **hard eyeball** on palpation, **shallow AC**

**Emergency Management:**
1. IV **mannitol** (20%, 1-2 g/kg over 30 min) — reduce IOP urgently
2. IV/oral **acetazolamide** 500 mg
3. Topical **pilocarpine 2%** (after IOP reduced — constricts pupil, opens angle)
4. Topical **beta-blockers** (timolol)
5. Definitive: **Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI)** — creates hole in peripheral iris; bilateral

**Van Herick grading**: Estimates AC angle depth on slit lamp — Grade 1-2 = narrow/closed angle

### Secondary Glaucoma

| Type | Cause | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Neovascular glaucoma (NVG)** | **Rubeosis iridis** (new vessels on iris) | Caused by ischemia: **Proliferative DR**, **CRVO**, ocular ischemia; fibrovascular membrane closes angle |
| Pigmentary glaucoma | Pigment dispersion from iris — Krukenberg spindle on cornea endothelium | Young myopic males; Iris transillumination defects; concave iris |
| Pseudoexfoliation glaucoma | Exfoliation material on lens capsule (target/bull's eye pattern), angle structures | Asymmetric; highest IOP levels; responds to prostaglandins |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Prostaglandin = 1st line POAG; mid-dilated oval pupil = PACG; rubeosis iridis = neovascular glaucoma; trabeculectomy = surgery

## Cataract

### Types by Morphology
| Type | Features |
|------|---------|
| **Nuclear sclerosis** | Gradual; **myopic shift** (refractive index increases → lens power increases → myopia — Index myopia); brown/brunescent |
| **Cortical** | Spoke-like peripheral opacities; water clefts |
| **Posterior subcapsular (PSC)** | **Worst visual impact** (at nodal point); glare in bright light; near vision more affected than distance; caused by steroids, diabetes, myotonic dystrophy, radiation |

### Congenital Cataract
- **Red reflex absent** (Bruckner test — white reflex/leukocoria) — most important early sign
- **Causes**: Rubella (most common infectious), Down syndrome, galactosemia, persistent fetal vasculature, lowe syndrome, steroid-induced, unknown (50%)
- **Treatment**: Surgical removal within **6-8 weeks** (before amblyopia irreversible); optical rehabilitation (contact lens/aphakic glasses in <2 years; IOL after 2 years)

### Surgery Types
| Method | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **ICCE** (Intracapsular cataract extraction) | Remove whole lens including capsule; requires large wound, aphakia — thick aphakic glasses; historical |
| **ECCE** (Extracapsular cataract extraction) | Anterior capsule removed, nucleus expressed, posterior capsule retained; IOL in PC; large wound (10-12mm) |
| **Phacoemulsification** | **Standard gold standard today**; ultrasonic fragmentation; small incision (2.2-2.8mm); **foldable IOL**; PCIOL; self-sealing incision |

### IOL Power Calculation
- **SRK/T formula**: A constant + 2.5 × (AL × 0.9) + 0.9 × K (simplified: P = A - 2.5L - 0.9K)
  - A = IOL constant; L = axial length (biometry); K = corneal power (keratometry)
- **Optical biometry (IOLMaster)**: Gold standard; more accurate than ultrasound

### Post-operative Complications
| Complication | Treatment |
|-------------|---------|
| **PCO (Posterior capsule opacification)** — most common late complication | **Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy** |
| **Endophthalmitis** — early post-op | **Intravitreal vancomycin + ceftazidime** (or amikacin); vitrectomy if severe |
| Corneal edema (Descemet's decompensation) | Hyperosmotics/corneal transplant |
| Cystoid macular edema (Irvine-Gass) | Topical NSAIDs/steroids |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: PSC = worst vision; PCO treated with Nd:YAG; endophthalmitis = intravitreal antibiotics; phacoemulsification = gold standard

## Uveitis

### Anterior Uveitis (Iritis/Iridocyclitis)
- **Symptoms**: Painful red eye, photophobia, lacrimation, blurred vision
- **Signs**:
  - **Ciliary flush (pericorneal injection)**: Redness maximal around corneal limbus
  - **KPs (Keratic precipitates)**: Cells deposited on corneal endothelium
  - **Cells and flare** in anterior chamber (Tyndall effect)
  - **Posterior synechiae**: Adhesion of iris to lens (irregular pupil)
  - **Hypopyon**: Pus (WBCs) settling inferiorly in AC (sterile: HLA-B27; infectious: bacteria/fungi)
  - Reduced IOP (acute); raised IOP (with pupillary block)

### Granulomatous vs Non-Granulomatous Uveitis
| Feature | Non-Granulomatous | Granulomatous |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| KPs | Fine (stellate) — "mutton fat" absent | **Large "mutton fat" KPs** |
| Nodules | Absent | **Busacca nodules** (iris stroma), Koeppe nodules (pupillary margin) |
| Onset | Acute | Chronic, insidious |
| Causes | HLA-B27 diseases | **Sarcoidosis, TB, syphilis, leprosy, toxoplasma, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada** |

### Specific Associations
| Disease | Uveitis Type | Associations |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| **HLA-B27** (AS, Reiter's, psoriatic, IBD) | Acute, non-granulomatous, anterior | Recurrent unilateral |
| **Sarcoidosis** | Granulomatous, anterior + posterior | Mutton fat KPs, Busacca nodes, candle wax exudates, lung involvement |
| **JIA (Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis)** | **Chronic anterior uveitis** (often asymptomatic!) | **ANA positive**, asymmetric oligoarthritis; pauciarticular type; females; require slit-lamp screening |
| **Behçet's disease** | Recurrent hypopyon uveitis | Oral/genital ulcers, pathergy test positive; HLA-B51 |
| **TB** | Granulomatous, chronic | Serpigenous choroiditis, mutton fat KPs |
| **Sympathetic ophthalmia** | Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis | Penetrating injury to one eye → fellow eye inflammation; Dalen-Fuchs nodules |

### Treatment
- **Topical steroids** (prednisolone acetate 1%) — first-line anterior uveitis
- **Cycloplegic** (cyclopentolate/atropine): Prevent synechiae + reduce ciliary spasm (pain relief)
- **Systemic steroids**: Posterior uveitis, bilateral, severe
- **Immunosuppressants** (methotrexate/mycophenolate/adalimumab): Chronic/steroid-sparing

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: JIA uveitis = asymptomatic + ANA positive + slit lamp screening; mutton fat KPs = granulomatous; hypopyon + recurrent = Behçet's

## Retina

### Diabetic Retinopathy (DR)

**Classification:**
| Stage | Features |
|-------|---------|
| **NPDR (Non-proliferative)** | |
| Mild | ≥1 microaneurysm |
| Moderate | Microaneurysms + dot/blot hemorrhages + hard exudates + cotton wool spots + venous beading |
| Severe (**4-2-1 rule**) | >20 hemorrhages in 4 quadrants OR venous beading in 2+ quadrants OR IRMA in 1+ quadrant |
| **PDR (Proliferative)** | **Neovascularization** of disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE), vitreous hemorrhage, tractional RD |

- **DME (Diabetic Macular Edema)**: **Most common cause of visual loss** in diabetes; can occur at any DR stage; center-involving vs non-center-involving
- **IRMA**: Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (dilated capillaries within retina — not true NV)
- **Hard exudates**: Lipid/protein deposits at outer plexiform layer (yellow, waxy, well-defined)
- **Cotton wool spots**: Nerve fiber layer infarcts (soft, fluffy, white, obscure vessels)

**Treatment:**
| Indication | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Severe NPDR/PDR | **Panretinal photocoagulation (PRP)** — laser to peripheral retina |
| DME (center-involving) | **Anti-VEGF** (ranibizumab/aflibercept/bevacizumab) — first-line; intravitreal injection |
| DME (non-responding) | **Macular laser** or intravitreal steroids |
| Vitreous hemorrhage/Tractional RD | **Vitrectomy** |

### Hypertensive Retinopathy — Keith-Wagener Classification
| Grade | Features |
|-------|---------|
| **Grade I** | Mild arteriolar narrowing, increased light reflex (silver wiring) |
| **Grade II** | Moderate arteriolar narrowing, **AV nipping/nicking** (crossing changes) |
| **Grade III** | Hemorrhages (flame/dot-blot), **hard exudates**, **cotton wool spots** |
| **Grade IV** | Grade III + **papilledema** (malignant hypertension) |

### Retinal Vascular Occlusions

| Feature | CRVO | BRVO | CRAO | BRAO |
|---------|------|------|------|------|
| Full name | Central retinal vein occlusion | Branch retinal vein occlusion | Central retinal **artery** occlusion | Branch retinal **artery** occlusion |
| Fundus | "**Blood and thunder**" — diffuse flame hemorrhages all 4 quadrants, disc swelling, dilated tortuous veins | Hemorrhages in sector (limited by vein drainage territory) | **Milky white retinal opacification** + **cherry red spot** at fovea | Whitish sectoral retinal infarct |
| Associations | Hypertension, DM, hyperviscosity | Hypertension, DM | **Carotid artery disease, cardiac embolism**, GCA | |
| Visual loss | Painless, sudden, variable | Sectoral visual field loss | **Sudden, painless, profound, monocular** | Sectoral |
| Emergency? | No | No | **YES — treat within 4 hours** | Yes |
| Treatment | Anti-VEGF (if DME/NV); monitor for NVG | Grid laser/anti-VEGF for macular edema | **Immediate** — ocular massage, lower IOP (AC paracentesis), O2, thrombolytics; rarely reverse | Anti-VEGF |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Cherry red spot = CRAO; blood and thunder = CRVO; anti-VEGF = DME first-line; 4-2-1 rule = severe NPDR

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

| Feature | Dry AMD | Wet AMD |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Pathology | **Drusen** (lipid deposits in Bruch's membrane) → geographic atrophy | **Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)** → subretinal fluid/hemorrhage/fibrosis |
| Frequency | 85-90% of AMD | 10-15%; responsible for 80% of severe visual loss |
| Visual loss | Slow, gradual | **Rapid, severe** |
| Amsler grid | Metamorphopsia (distortion) — earlier in wet | Metamorphopsia/scotoma |
| Treatment | **AREDS2 supplements**: Vitamin C + E + zinc + lutein + zeaxanthin (beta-carotene removed — lung cancer risk in smokers) | **Anti-VEGF intravitreal injections** (ranibizumab/aflibercept/bevacizumab) |

### Retinal Detachment

| Type | Mechanism | Causes | Features | Treatment |
|------|----------|--------|---------|---------|
| **Rhegmatogenous** | Retinal break → fluid enters subretinal space | Myopia, trauma, lattice degeneration, post-cataract surgery | Photopsia (flashes) → floaters → shadow/curtain | **Scleral buckle, vitrectomy, cryopexy**, C3F8/SF6 gas tamponade |
| **Tractional** | Vitreous membrane pulls retina | Proliferative DR, sickle cell, ROP, penetrating trauma | Tent-shaped detachment; no break | Vitrectomy |
| **Exudative (secondary)** | Subretinal fluid accumulation without break | Coats' disease, hypertension (malignant), tumors (choroidal melanoma), posterior uveitis | Shifting fluid; changes with position | Treat cause |

### ROP (Retinopathy of Prematurity)
- **Risk factors**: Prematurity (<32 weeks), birth weight <1500g, supplemental oxygen
- **Zones**: I (posterior, around disc), II (middle), III (anterior, peripheral)
- **Stages**: 1 (demarcation line) → 2 (ridge) → 3 (ridge + extraretinal fibrovascular proliferation) → 4 (partial RD) → 5 (total RD)
- **Plus disease**: Dilation + tortuosity of posterior vessels (indicates activity)
- **Treatment threshold**: Zone I or II, Stage 3+ with plus disease → **laser photocoagulation** (gold standard) OR **intravitreal anti-VEGF (bevacizumab)** — Zone I preferred
- **Screening**: Babies <32 weeks or <1500g; first exam at 4-6 weeks postnatal age

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Rhegmatogenous = most common type RD; Coats' disease = exudative; AREDS2 supplements = dry AMD; ROP Zone I = anti-VEGF preferred

## Optic Nerve Disorders

### Optic Neuritis
- **Most common cause**: **Multiple sclerosis** (>50% MS patients have at least one episode); also NMO, sarcoidosis, infections
- **Clinical**:
  - **Painful loss of vision** (pain on eye movement — earliest symptom; stretching of optic nerve sheath)
  - **RAPD (Relative afferent pupillary defect)** positive — Marcus Gunn pupil
  - **Central scotoma** (most common field defect)
  - Reduced color vision (red saturation)
  - Papillitis (disc swelling — 1/3) or retrobulbar neuritis (normal disc — 2/3: "patient sees nothing, doctor sees nothing")
- **Treatment**: IV **methylprednisolone** 1g/day × 3 days → speeds recovery but does not affect final visual acuity
- **Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial (ONTT)**: IV steroids → faster recovery; MRI lesions at presentation = 72% risk of MS within 15 years

### Papilledema
- **Definition**: Bilateral optic disc swelling due to raised intracranial pressure (ICP)
- **Mechanism**: CSF pressure transmitted along optic nerve sheath → axoplasmic flow stasis → disc swelling
- **Fundus findings**: Blurring of disc margins, loss of spontaneous venous pulsations (SVP — earliest sign), peripapillary flame hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, dilated veins, absent cup
- **Visual function**: Initially normal (enlarged blind spot); visual acuity loss is LATE
- **Investigation**: Urgent CT/MRI brain (rule out tumor/hydrocephalus); LP (measure opening pressure)
- **Causes**: Intracranial tumors, hydrocephalus, venous sinus thrombosis, IIH (idiopathic intracranial hypertension/pseudotumor cerebri — obese young women, on OCPs, visual acuity intact initially)

### Optic Atrophy
| Type | Appearance | Cause |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Primary** | Pale/white disc, sharp margins, normal vessels | Optic neuritis (MS), toxic (methanol/quinine), hereditary (Leber's), pituitary compression |
| **Secondary** | Pale disc, blurred margins (from papilledema/papillitis) | Post papilledema |
| **Consecutive** | Follows choroido-retinal disease spreading to optic nerve | Retinitis pigmentosa, extensive chorioretinitis |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Papilledema bilateral; optic neuritis painful + RAPD + IV methylprednisolone; loss of SVP = earliest papilledema sign

## Neuro-Ophthalmology

### Visual Field Defects and Anatomical Correlates

| Defect | Location of Lesion |
|--------|-------------------|
| Monocular blindness | Optic nerve (ipsilateral) |
| **Bitemporal hemianopia** | **Optic chiasm** (central lesion — pituitary tumor; lateral — craniopharyngioma) |
| Homonymous hemianopia | Optic tract / LGN / optic radiation / occipital cortex (contralateral) |
| Superior quadrantanopia ("**Pie in the sky**") | **Temporal lobe** (inferior optic radiation — Meyer's loop) |
| Inferior quadrantanopia ("**Pie on the floor**") | **Parietal lobe** (superior optic radiation) |
| Macular sparing homonymous hemianopia | Occipital lobe (dual blood supply of macular area) |

**Mnemonic**: PITS = Parietal (Inferior temporal superior) — parietal lobe lesion → inferior quadrantanopia; temporal lobe → superior quadrantanopia

### Pupillary Reflexes

**RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect) — Marcus Gunn Pupil:**
- **Test**: Swinging flashlight test — light swung from normal to abnormal eye → abnormal eye's pupil **dilates** (paradoxical dilation)
- **Indicates**: Optic nerve disease OR severe retinal disease affecting afferent arc
- **NOT present in cataracts, amblyopia, refractive error (these affect VA, not afferent)

**Argyll Robertson Pupil:**
- **Classic for neurosyphilis** (tertiary syphilis affecting Edinger-Westphal nucleus and pretectal area)
- **Light-near dissociation**: Absent direct/consensual light reflex + preserved accommodation reflex (near response)
- **Features**: Bilateral, miotic, irregular, unequal pupils, fail to dilate with mydriatics
- Mnemonic: **ARP = Accommodation Reflex Present, Light reflex absent**

### Horner's Syndrome
- **Triad**: **Ptosis (2mm)** + **Miosis** + **Anhidrosis** (ipsilateral face)
- **Fourth sign**: Apparent enophthalmos (pseudo-enophthalmos)
- **Pathway**: Hypothalamus → cervical sympathetic chain → superior cervical ganglion → pupil/Müller's muscle
- **Localizing lesions**:
  - First order (central): Hypothalamus/brainstem/cervical cord — stroke, syringomyelia
  - Second order (preganglionic): Pancoast tumor, cervical rib, thyroid tumor, lymphoma
  - Third order (postganglionic): Carotid dissection, cluster headache (painful Horner's), cavernous sinus
- **Cocaine test**: No dilation = Horner's (cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake; needs NE present to dilate)
- **Hydroxyamphetamine test**: No dilation = postganglionic lesion (releases NE from terminal)

### Cranial Nerve Palsies Affecting Eye

| Nerve | Palsy | Eye Position | Key Feature |
|-------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **III (Oculomotor)** | Complete | Down and out (exotropia + hypotropia) | **Dilated pupil** + complete ptosis; pupil-involving = posterior communicating artery aneurysm! |
| **IV (Trochlear)** | SO palsy | Hypertropia (eye elevated) | Head tilt away from lesion; worse in adduction and depression; longest intracranial course → trauma |
| **VI (Abducens)** | LR palsy | Esotropia (convergent) | Diplopia on horizontal gaze to affected side; raised ICP can cause false localizing VI palsy |

- **Pupil-sparing III nerve palsy**: Microvascular (DM, hypertension) — pupil fibers are peripherally located and may be spared in ischemic palsy but compressed in aneurysm
- **Duane's retraction syndrome**: Congenital, absence of VI nucleus; retraction of globe on adduction; limited abduction

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Argyll Robertson = syphilis; RAPD = optic nerve/severe retinal; III nerve palsy + dilated pupil = PCOMA aneurysm; pie in sky = temporal lobe; bitemporal hemianopia = chiasm (pituitary)

## Squint (Strabismus)

### Terminology
- **Esotropia**: Convergent squint (eye turns inward)
- **Exotropia**: Divergent squint (eye turns outward)
- **Hypertropia**: Eye turns upward
- **Hypotropia**: Eye turns downward
- **Tropia**: Manifest deviation (present with both eyes open)
- **Phoria**: Latent deviation (only manifest when binocular fusion broken by cover test)

### Tests for Squint
| Test | Method | Result |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Hirschberg test** | Shine pen torch at nose bridge; observe corneal light reflex | Reflex centered = orthotropic; each mm of decentration ≈ 7° of deviation |
| **Cover-uncover test** | Cover one eye, observe uncovered eye for movement | Tropia: Uncovered eye moves to take up fixation |
| **Alternate cover test** | Alternate cover between eyes | Phoria: Eye behind cover drifts; returns when uncovered |
| **Prism cover test** | Quantify deviation with prisms | Measures angle |

### Pseudostrabismus
- **Appearance** of squint without true deviation (Hirschberg test normal)
- Common cause: **Broad nasal bridge + epicanthal folds** in infants (appears as esotropia)
- Cover test: **No movement** (normal)

### Accommodative Esotropia
- **Mechanism**: High **hypermetropia** → excessive accommodation → excess convergence → esotropia
- Age: 2-5 years
- **Treatment**: **Full hypermetropic correction (convex lenses)** — can eliminate/reduce esotropia; bifocals if near deviation more than distance

### Surgical Treatment
- **Recession**: Weakens a muscle (move insertion posteriorly)
- **Resection**: Strengthens a muscle (remove segment, reattach anteriorly)
- Esotropia: Weaken MR (recession) ± strengthen LR (resection)

### Amblyopia Treatment
- **Patching (occlusion therapy)**: Cover better eye to stimulate use of amblyopic eye
- Optimal treatment before **age 7-8 years** (closure of critical period)
- **Atropine penalization**: Blur better eye with atropine drops (alternative to patching)
- Duration: Based on age; monitor for occlusion amblyopia in young children

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Hirschberg = corneal light reflex; pseudostrabismus = epicanthal folds; accommodative esotropia = convex lenses; recession = weakens, resection = strengthens
