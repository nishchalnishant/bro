## EAR — OTITIS MEDIA AND INFECTIONS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 6-year-old child presents with fever, ear pain, and a bulging, hyperemic tympanic membrane. The most common causative organism in this age group is:**
- A) Streptococcus pneumoniae
- B) Haemophilus influenzae
- C) Moraxella catarrhalis
- D) Pseudomonas aeruginosa

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

In children under 5–6 years, Haemophilus influenzae (non-typeable) is the most common cause of acute suppurative otitis media (ASOM); in adults and older children, Streptococcus pneumoniae predominates. First-line treatment is amoxicillin (80–90 mg/kg/day); amoxicillin-clavulanate for treatment failures. Pseudomonas causes otitis externa (not ASOM). Moraxella catarrhalis is a beta-lactamase producer but third in frequency. The eardrum is typically bulging, dull, and injected, with loss of light reflex.

</details>

**Q2. A 35-year-old man with chronic ear discharge for 10 years presents with a central tympanic membrane perforation, profuse mucopurulent (not foul-smelling) discharge, and intact ossicular chain on examination. This presentation is consistent with:**
- A) Atticoantral CSOM (unsafe type)
- B) Tubotympanic CSOM (safe type)
- C) Cholesteatoma
- D) Glomus tympanicum

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Tubotympanic (safe) CSOM is characterized by central (pars tensa) perforation, mucoid/mucopurulent non-offensive discharge, intact ossicular chain, and absence of cholesteatoma — classified as "safe" because it does not erode bone and complications are rare. Atticoantral (unsafe) CSOM has a marginal or pars flaccida (attic) perforation, foul-smelling discharge, cholesteatoma, bone erosion, and higher risk of intracranial complications. The "safe vs unsafe" distinction is the most tested CSOM concept in NEET PG.

</details>

**Q3. A 28-year-old woman with CSOM is found to have a pearly white mass in the attic region on otoscopy, with bone erosion on HRCT temporal bone. The fundamental pathology of this condition and its definitive treatment are:**
- A) Benign epithelial tumor; Rx medical management
- B) Squamous epithelium in the middle ear eroding bone via collagenase; Rx mastoidectomy
- C) Granulation tissue; Rx topical antibiotics
- D) Glomus tumor; Rx radiotherapy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cholesteatoma is keratinizing stratified squamous epithelium that has migrated into the middle ear cleft (acquired type: most common, via pars flaccida retraction pocket). It expands and erodes bone via collagenase (matrix metalloproteinases). Classic presentation: pars flaccida/marginal perforation, foul-smelling discharge, pearly white mass (attic cholesteatoma), conductive hearing loss. HRCT temporal bone confirms bone erosion. Definitive treatment is surgical: modified radical or canal wall-down mastoidectomy. It is never "safe" — always requires surgery.

</details>

**Q4. A 65-year-old diabetic man presents with severe unrelenting otalgia, otorrhoea, granulation tissue at the bony-cartilaginous junction of the external auditory canal, and facial nerve palsy. The most likely diagnosis and causative organism are:**
- A) ASOM; Streptococcus pneumoniae
- B) Malignant (necrotizing) otitis externa; Pseudomonas aeruginosa
- C) Herpes zoster oticus; Varicella-zoster virus
- D) Furuncle of EAC; Staphylococcus aureus

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Malignant (necrotizing) otitis externa is a life-threatening osteomyelitis of the skull base seen almost exclusively in elderly diabetics and immunocompromised patients. Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the causative organism in >95% of cases. Hallmarks: severe disproportionate otalgia, granulation tissue at the bony-cartilaginous junction (pathognomonic), progressive cranial nerve palsies (VII most common, then IX, X, XI). Treatment: prolonged anti-pseudomonal antibiotics (ciprofloxacin or piperacillin-tazobactam) for 6–8 weeks; surgical debridement if needed.

</details>

**Q5. A child develops post-auricular swelling, tenderness, and protrusion of the pinna anteriorly 2 weeks after an episode of ASOM. The sagging of the posterosuperior wall of the EAC is noted on otoscopy. The diagnosis and surgical treatment are:**
- A) Preauricular sinus infection; incision and drainage
- B) Acute mastoiditis; cortical mastoidectomy
- C) Parotitis; antibiotics alone
- D) Bezold's abscess; radical mastoidectomy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Acute coalescent mastoiditis is a complication of ASOM where the bony septa between mastoid air cells are destroyed. Signs: post-auricular erythema, fluctuance, tenderness, pinna displaced forward and downward, sagging of posterosuperior EAC wall (pathognomonic on otoscopy). Treatment: IV antibiotics + cortical (Schwartze) mastoidectomy to drain the mastoid. A Bezold's abscess is when the infection tracks down along the sternomastoid muscle — requires extended surgical drainage. HRCT temporal bone confirms diagnosis.

</details>

## HEARING LOSS AND AUDIOLOGY (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. In Rinne's test on a patient's right ear, the tuning fork (512 Hz) placed on the mastoid process is heard louder and longer than when held near the ear canal. The Weber test lateralizes to the right ear. The most likely diagnosis in the right ear is:**
- A) Normal hearing
- B) Sensorineural hearing loss in the right ear
- C) Conductive hearing loss in the right ear
- D) Mixed hearing loss in the left ear

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Rinne negative (BC > AC) = conductive hearing loss. Weber lateralizes to the worse (more affected) ear in conductive hearing loss (the affected ear does not hear ambient noise, so the bone-conducted sound appears louder). Together: Rinne negative right + Weber to right = right-sided conductive hearing loss. Rinne positive (AC > BC) occurs in normal hearing and sensorineural hearing loss; in SNHL, Weber lateralizes to the better (less affected) ear. False-negative Rinne: severe SNHL on tested side with good contralateral cochlea.

</details>

**Q7. A 45-year-old woman presents with progressive bilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, and a pinkish blush seen through the tympanic membrane (flamingo-pink sign). Audiometry shows a characteristic dip at 2000 Hz in bone conduction. The definitive treatment is:**
- A) Hearing aids alone
- B) Sodium fluoride tablets
- C) Stapedectomy
- D) Cochlear implantation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Otosclerosis is abnormal bone remodeling at the fissula ante fenestram, fixing the stapes footplate. Features: bilateral progressive conductive hearing loss (paracusis Willisii — hears better in noise), Schwartze sign (flamingo-pink blush through TM = active otospongiosis), and Carhart's notch (mechanical depression at 2 kHz on bone conduction audiogram — pathognomonic, disappears after surgery). Definitive treatment: stapedectomy (replacement with Teflon piston). Sodium fluoride slows progression. Hearing aids are an alternative if surgery is declined.

</details>

**Q8. A 50-year-old man presents with episodic vertigo (20 min–12 hours), fluctuating low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness. He had a sudden fall without loss of consciousness during one episode. The pathology and first-line management are:**
- A) Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo; Epley maneuver
- B) Meniere's disease (endolymphatic hydrops); low-salt diet + betahistine
- C) Acoustic neuroma; MRI and surgery
- D) Labyrinthitis; antivirals

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Meniere's disease is caused by endolymphatic hydrops (distension of the endolymphatic system). The classic tetrad: episodic vertigo (20 min–12 hours), fluctuating low-frequency SNHL, tinnitus, and aural fullness. Tumarkin crisis (otolithic catastrophe) = sudden fall without warning or loss of consciousness — pathognomonic of Meniere's. First-line: low-salt diet (<2 g/day) + betahistine (histamine H1 agonist/H3 antagonist). Diuretics (acetazolamide) for refractory cases. Endolymphatic sac decompression or gentamicin ablation for intractable disease.

</details>

**Q9. Pure tone audiometry of a 60-year-old man shows bilateral symmetrical sensorineural hearing loss predominantly affecting high frequencies (4–8 kHz), with gradual downward slope of the audiogram. No middle ear pathology. This pattern is diagnostic of:**
- A) Noise-induced hearing loss
- B) Presbycusis
- C) Otosclerosis
- D) Ototoxicity from aminoglycosides

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Presbycusis (age-related hearing loss) produces bilateral, symmetrical, gradually progressive high-frequency SNHL with a downsloping audiogram — caused by degeneration of outer hair cells in the basal turn of the cochlea (high-frequency region). Speech discrimination is disproportionately poor (presbycusis of discrimination). Noise-induced hearing loss also shows a 4 kHz notch but occurs in younger patients with noise exposure history. Aminoglycoside ototoxicity first affects 8 kHz (highest frequency). Management: hearing aids; cochlear implants if severe.

</details>

**Q10. A 4-year-old child fails a school hearing screening. BERA (Brainstem Evoked Response Audiometry) is ordered. The advantage of BERA over pure tone audiometry in this age group is:**
- A) It is cheaper and does not require any equipment
- B) It is objective, does not require voluntary response, and can be performed in sleeping or sedated children
- C) It tests both ears simultaneously
- D) It gives better frequency-specific information than pure tone audiometry

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

BERA (Brainstem Evoked Response Audiometry / ABR) is objective — it records electrical potentials from cochlea, cochlear nerve, and brainstem in response to click stimuli and does not require a voluntary behavioral response. It is the investigation of choice for infants, young children, and malingerers. Wave V threshold correlates with hearing threshold (within ±10 dB). BERA is also the earliest diagnostic test for acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma). Pure tone audiometry (PTA) requires voluntary cooperation (audiometric responses), typically feasible from age 4–5 years.

</details>

## NOSE AND PARANASAL SINUSES (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 55-year-old hypertensive man presents with profuse epistaxis from the posterior nasal cavity. Anterior rhinoscopy fails to identify a bleeding point, and anterior nasal packing does not control the bleeding. The artery most likely responsible for this posterior epistaxis and the next step in management are:**
- A) Anterior ethmoidal artery; surgical ligation
- B) Sphenopalatine artery; posterior nasal pack or endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation
- C) Greater palatine artery; palatal pack
- D) Superior labial artery; pressure and local vasoconstriction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

90% of epistaxis is anterior from Kiesselbach's (Little's) plexus on the anteroinferior nasal septum (anastomosis of anterior ethmoidal, superior labial, greater palatine, and sphenopalatine arteries) — controlled by pressure and cautery. 10% is posterior from the sphenopalatine artery (terminal branch of maxillary artery) — seen in elderly, hypertensives; more severe, requires posterior nasal packing (Foley catheter or Brighton balloon) or endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation/embolization. Internal maxillary artery ligation is the surgical option for failed embolization.

</details>

**Q12. A 40-year-old woman presents with unilateral nasal polyp filling the right nasal cavity, extending into the nasopharynx and visible behind the soft palate. She is 12 years old at first presentation (history revised: 12-year-old girl). The most likely diagnosis and treatment are:**
- A) Bilateral ethmoidal polyps; Samter's triad workup
- B) Antrochoanal polyp (Killian's polyp); Caldwell-Luc operation
- C) Inverted papilloma; endoscopic medial maxillectomy
- D) Angiofibroma; embolization + surgery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Antrochoanal polyp (Killian's polyp) arises from the maxillary sinus mucosa, exits through the accessory ostium, fills the nasal cavity, and extends into the nasopharynx through the choana — hence the name. It is unilateral, predominantly seen in children and young adults, and is gelatinous/semi-translucent. Treatment: Caldwell-Luc operation (sublabial antrostomy) to address the sinus origin + endoscopic removal of the nasal and nasopharyngeal component. Simple avulsion leads to recurrence because the sinus root is not removed.

</details>

**Q13. A 35-year-old aspirin-sensitive asthmatic woman is found to have bilateral nasal polyps on examination. This association is known as:**
- A) Kartagener syndrome
- B) Samter's triad (aspirin hypersensitivity triad)
- C) Young's syndrome
- D) Woakes' syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Samter's triad (aspirin exacerbated respiratory disease / Widal's triad) = bilateral ethmoidal nasal polyps + bronchial asthma + aspirin/NSAID hypersensitivity. Caused by aberrant arachidonic acid metabolism (COX-1 inhibition shunts AA to leukotriene pathway → bronchoconstriction). Management: avoidance of NSAIDs, intranasal/systemic corticosteroids, functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS). Kartagener syndrome = situs inversus + bronchiectasis + nasal polyps (from ciliary dyskinesia). Woakes' syndrome = nasal polyposis with broadened nose.

</details>

**Q14. A patient with chronic sinusitis has DNS to the right with a compensatory hypertrophy of the left inferior turbinate. On which side is the patient symptomatic (nasal obstruction), and what is the definitive surgical treatment for DNS?**
- A) Left side symptomatic; inferior turbinate reduction
- B) Right side symptomatic (convex side); septoplasty
- C) Both sides equally; bilateral FESS
- D) Right side; submucosal resection of the septum (SMR)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

In deviated nasal septum (DNS), the convex (deviated) side is narrow and symptomatic (nasal obstruction, recurrent sinusitis, headache). The concave side develops compensatory turbinate hypertrophy. Septoplasty (submucous resection preserving the L-strut) is the definitive treatment, addressing the deviation under direct vision while preserving structural cartilage. SMR (Killian's operation) is the older technique that removes too much cartilage, causing saddle nose deformity. FESS addresses sinus disease but not the septal deviation itself.

</details>

**Q15. A 35-year-old man presents with recurrent bilateral nasal obstruction, anosmia, and pale glistening polyps filling both nasal cavities. The most common sinus of origin for bilateral ethmoidal polyps and the first-line medical treatment are:**
- A) Maxillary sinus; antihistamines
- B) Ethmoid sinus; intranasal corticosteroids (budesonide/mometasone)
- C) Frontal sinus; systemic antibiotics
- D) Sphenoid sinus; decongestants

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bilateral nasal polyps arise predominantly from the ethmoidal sinuses (ethmoid bullae), are associated with chronic eosinophilic inflammation, asthma, and aspirin sensitivity. They are pale, insensate (no pain on probing — distinguishes from turbinates), gelatinous, and bilateral. First-line treatment: intranasal corticosteroids (budesonide, mometasone) ± short course systemic steroids for large polyps. FESS (functional endoscopic sinus surgery) is indicated for medical treatment failure. Maxillary sinus disease is the most common sinusitis, but polyps originate from the ethmoids.

</details>

## THROAT AND LARYNX (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 28-year-old man presents with fever, severe odynophagia, muffled voice, drooling, and trismus. On examination, there is swelling of the anterior pillar bulging toward the midline, with uvular deviation away from the affected side. The diagnosis and immediate management are:**
- A) Retropharyngeal abscess; neck CT + parenteral antibiotics
- B) Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy); incision and drainage
- C) Ludwig's angina; airway management + antibiotics
- D) Epiglottitis; emergency intubation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) is a collection between the tonsillar capsule and superior constrictor muscle, most commonly at the upper pole. The anterior pillar (palatoglossal arch) bulges medially, uvula is deviated to the opposite side, and there is trismus (pterygoid muscle spasm). Causative organism: mixed (Group A Streptococcus most common). Treatment: incision and drainage (at the point of maximum fluctuation = above the upper pole of the tonsil, or needle aspiration), + IV penicillin/amoxicillin. Interval tonsillectomy after 4–6 weeks.

</details>

**Q17. A 3-year-old unvaccinated child presents with sudden high fever, severe odynophagia, drooling, and sits in a "tripod" position (leaning forward on hands). Neck X-ray lateral view shows a thumb-shaped epiglottis. The causative organism and airway management priority are:**
- A) Parainfluenza virus; nebulized epinephrine
- B) Haemophilus influenzae type b; secure airway first (intubation/tracheostomy), then IV ceftriaxone
- C) Streptococcus pyogenes; IV penicillin
- D) Staphylococcus aureus; IV flucloxacillin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Acute epiglottitis (supraglottitis) is caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib), dramatically reduced by Hib vaccination. The "thumb sign" (swollen epiglottis on lateral soft-tissue neck X-ray) and "tripod/sniffing position" are hallmarks. This is a life-threatening airway emergency — secure the airway first (intubation in the OR under controlled conditions; tracheostomy as backup) before any examination that may precipitate complete obstruction. IV ceftriaxone or ampicillin-sulbactam thereafter. Do NOT examine the throat with a spatula in the ward.

</details>

**Q18. A 2-year-old child presents with barking (seal-like) cough, inspiratory stridor, and low-grade fever for 2 days. Anteroposterior X-ray of the neck shows a "steeple sign" (subglottic narrowing). The causative organism and treatment are:**
- A) Haemophilus influenzae; IV ceftriaxone
- B) Parainfluenza virus type 1; single dose dexamethasone + nebulized epinephrine for moderate-severe cases
- C) Respiratory syncytial virus; ribavirin
- D) Streptococcus pneumoniae; amoxicillin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis/LTB) is the most common cause of stridor in children 6 months–3 years, caused by parainfluenza virus type 1 (most common). The steeple sign (pencil-point/inverted V subglottic narrowing) on AP neck X-ray is pathognomonic. Treatment: single dose oral/IM/IV dexamethasone (0.15–0.6 mg/kg) for all cases; nebulized racemic epinephrine for moderate-severe cases (reduces mucosal edema, effect lasts 2 hours — observe for rebound). Humidified air and calm environment. Intubation only for severe respiratory failure.

</details>

**Q19. During a left-sided thyroid lobectomy, the surgeon inadvertently injures the recurrent laryngeal nerve. The left vocal cord is expected to be paralyzed. Why is the left RLN more commonly injured than the right during surgical procedures?**
- A) The left RLN is thinner and more fragile
- B) The left RLN has a longer course, looping under the aortic arch, making it more vulnerable
- C) The left RLN supplies more muscles than the right
- D) The left thyroid lobe is surgically more accessible, leading to more frequent operations

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The left recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) is the longest intrathoracic nerve, looping under the aortic arch (ligamentum arteriosum) before ascending in the tracheoesophageal groove to the larynx — making it vulnerable to injury in thyroid, parathyroid, esophageal, and mediastinal surgeries, as well as aortic aneurysms and lung cancer. The right RLN loops under the right subclavian artery. Left RLN paralysis: hoarseness (paramedian cord position). Treatment of unilateral cord palsy: medialization laryngoplasty or injection laryngoplasty.

</details>

**Q20. A 45-year-old male smoker presents with progressive hoarseness for 6 months. Laryngoscopy shows a lesion on the true vocal cord. This location of laryngeal carcinoma has the best prognosis because:**
- A) It responds better to chemotherapy than other subsites
- B) The true vocal cords have sparse lymphatics, delaying nodal spread, allowing early symptom detection
- C) Glottic tumors do not invade cartilage
- D) Radiotherapy is not available for this location

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Glottic carcinoma (true vocal cord) has the best prognosis among laryngeal subsites because: (1) the true cords have sparse lymphatics → late and infrequent nodal metastasis, (2) hoarseness appears early (even small tumors cause voice change) → early diagnosis. Supraglottic carcinoma presents late (silent until large), has rich lymphatics, and frequent bilateral nodal mets — worst prognosis for a given stage. Subglottic carcinoma is rare. Treatment of early glottic Ca: radiotherapy or laser cordectomy; advanced: total laryngectomy ± neck dissection.

</details>

## HEAD AND NECK TUMORS (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 50-year-old Chinese man presents with unilateral conductive hearing loss, right-sided palatal palsy, and pain/paraesthesia over the right mandible (V3). He has bilateral cervical lymphadenopathy. The most likely diagnosis, causative virus, and treatment are:**
- A) Parotid malignancy; HPV; surgery
- B) Nasopharyngeal carcinoma; EBV; radiotherapy
- C) Oro-pharyngeal carcinoma; EBV; chemoradiotherapy
- D) Maxillary sinus malignancy; HPV; surgery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC) — Trotter's triad: (1) unilateral conductive hearing loss (Eustachian tube obstruction), (2) unilateral palatal palsy, (3) trigeminal neuralgia/anaesthesia (V3). Associated with EBV (anti-VCA IgA is a screening marker), most common in Southeast Asian/Chinese men, and undifferentiated WHO type III is most common histological type. NPC is highly radiosensitive — primary treatment is chemoradiotherapy (cisplatin-based). Surgery is reserved for residual neck nodes. Bilateral neck nodes in a Chinese man = NPC until proven otherwise.

</details>

**Q22. A 60-year-old man undergoes parotidectomy for a benign tumor. Postoperatively, he notices sweating and flushing over the cheek during meals. This complication is due to:**
- A) Facial nerve injury during surgery
- B) Aberrant regeneration of auriculotemporal nerve fibers into sweat gland secretomotor fibers (Frey's syndrome)
- C) Parotid fistula formation
- D) Damage to the greater auricular nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Frey's syndrome (auriculotemporal syndrome / gustatory sweating) occurs after parotid surgery when parasympathetic secretomotor fibers from the auriculotemporal nerve (normally innervating the parotid) aberrantly regenerate into the sympathetic fibers supplying sweat glands of the skin — gustatory stimulation causes sweating and flushing instead of salivation. Diagnosed by Minor's starch-iodine test. Treatment: botulinum toxin injection into the affected skin (most effective). Interposition of temporoparietal fascia flap during parotidectomy prevents it.

</details>

**Q23. The most common site for a salivary gland calculus (sialolithiasis) causing submandibular gland swelling that increases at mealtimes is:**
- A) Stensen's duct of the parotid gland
- B) Wharton's duct of the submandibular gland
- C) Bartholin's duct of the sublingual gland
- D) Minor salivary glands of the lip

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

The submandibular gland accounts for 80–90% of salivary calculi because: (1) Wharton's duct is long (5 cm), wide, and has an upward course against gravity, (2) submandibular saliva is more alkaline, viscous, and mucous-rich. Stones cause mealtime swelling and pain (obstructive sialadenitis). Treatment: distal duct stones — intraoral removal via duct dilation; proximal stones — submandibular gland excision. The parotid (Stensen's duct) accounts for only 10–20% of calculi. Sialendoscopy is increasingly used.

</details>

**Q24. A 25-year-old woman presents with a midline neck swelling that moves upward both on swallowing and on tongue protrusion. The swelling has been present since childhood. The definitive surgical treatment involves:**
- A) Simple excision of the cyst
- B) Sistrunk's operation (excision of cyst + central hyoid body + core of tongue base tissue)
- C) Total thyroidectomy
- D) Radioiodine ablation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Thyroglossal duct cyst is the most common congenital neck swelling, arising from remnants of the thyroglossal duct (thyroid descent from foramen cecum). It is classically midline, moves with both swallowing (attached to hyoid via tract) and tongue protrusion (tract attached to foramen cecum). Sistrunk's operation is the definitive treatment — excision of the cyst + central portion of hyoid bone + core of tongue tissue up to the foramen cecum. Simple excision has a 30–50% recurrence rate; Sistrunk reduces it to <5%. Always perform thyroid scan first to confirm normal thyroid gland.

</details>

**Q25. A pleomorphic adenoma of the parotid gland is being discussed. Which statement about this tumor is CORRECT?**
- A) It is the most common malignant parotid tumor
- B) It is bilateral in 10% of cases, unlike Warthin's tumor which is always unilateral
- C) It is the most common benign salivary gland tumor, with risk of malignant transformation (carcinoma ex-pleomorphic adenoma)
- D) It arises from the deep lobe of the parotid in most cases

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Pleomorphic adenoma (mixed tumor) is the most common benign salivary gland tumor (70% of all parotid tumors), arising from myoepithelial cells — hence "pleomorphic" (epithelial + mesenchymal elements on histology). It occurs in the superficial lobe of the parotid (90%), is firm, lobulated, slow-growing, and painless. Risk of malignant transformation to carcinoma ex-pleomorphic adenoma (~5–10% if untreated >10 years). Treatment: superficial parotidectomy with facial nerve preservation. Warthin's tumor (papillary cystadenoma lymphomatosum) is bilateral in 10% and occurs in elderly male smokers.

</details>

## VERTIGO AND SPECIAL TOPICS (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. A 65-year-old woman complains of brief (30–60 second) episodes of severe rotatory vertigo triggered by rolling over in bed or looking upward. Nausea is present but no tinnitus or hearing loss. The Dix-Hallpike test is positive for the right ear. The diagnosis and treatment are:**
- A) Meniere's disease; betahistine
- B) BPPV of the right posterior semicircular canal; Epley canalith repositioning maneuver
- C) Acoustic neuroma; MRI and surgery
- D) Vestibular neuritis; antivirals

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo) is the most common cause of vertigo, caused by displaced otoconia (calcium carbonate crystals) in the semicircular canal — most commonly the posterior canal (85%). Dix-Hallpike test: patient brought rapidly from sitting to head-hanging position; positive = geotropic rotatory nystagmus (toward lower ear) with latency 2–5 sec, lasting <1 min, fatigable. Epley canalith repositioning maneuver (4-step head repositioning) clears the debris and is curative in 80–90% of cases. No tinnitus or hearing loss distinguishes BPPV from Meniere's.

</details>

**Q27. A 45-year-old man presents with unilateral progressive sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and unsteadiness. MRI shows a 2.5 cm cerebellopontine angle (CPA) tumor with widening of the internal auditory meatus. BERA shows prolonged I–V wave latency. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Meningioma
- B) Epidermoid cyst
- C) Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)
- D) Glomus jugulare tumor

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) arises from Schwann cells of the vestibular division of CN VIII at the CPA angle — the most common CPA tumor (75–80%). Features: unilateral SNHL (speech discrimination loss is disproportionate), tinnitus, vertigo/unsteadiness. BERA shows increased I–V interpeak latency (earliest change). MRI with gadolinium is gold standard for diagnosis. Treatment: observation (small), microsurgery (translabyrinthine, middle fossa, retrosigmoid approaches), or stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife). Bilateral acoustic neuromas = Neurofibromatosis type 2 (NF2).

</details>

**Q28. A 35-year-old woman wakes up with right-sided facial weakness affecting both the forehead and lower face, inability to close the right eye, and loss of taste on the anterior two-thirds of the tongue. The most likely diagnosis and first-line treatment are:**
- A) UMN facial palsy from stroke; aspirin and physiotherapy
- B) Bell's palsy (LMN facial palsy); oral prednisolone within 72 hours
- C) Ramsay Hunt syndrome; acyclovir alone
- D) Acoustic neuroma with facial nerve compression; microsurgery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bell's palsy is idiopathic LMN facial nerve palsy (CN VII) — characterized by complete ipsilateral facial weakness including forehead (frontalis involvement distinguishes LMN from UMN palsy, where forehead is spared due to bilateral cortical representation). Loss of taste (anterior 2/3 tongue via chorda tympani), hyperacusis, and reduced lacrimation may be present. Treatment: oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day for 10 days (started within 72 hours — improves recovery rate). Add acyclovir if Ramsay Hunt syndrome (herpes zoster oticus with vesicles) is suspected. Eye care (lubricants, patching) is mandatory.

</details>

**Q29. A 10-year-old boy with CSOM develops fever, headache, and papilledema. He has an ipsilateral 6th nerve palsy. On examination, there is tenderness over the mastoid and sigmoid sinus region. The most likely intracranial complication is:**
- A) Meningitis
- B) Cerebellar abscess
- C) Lateral (sigmoid) sinus thrombosis
- D) Subdural empyema

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Lateral (sigmoid) sinus thrombosis is a serious otogenic intracranial complication, occurring from direct extension of mastoid infection to the sigmoid sinus. Features: Picket-fence (spiking) fever (from septic emboli), mastoid tenderness, Griesinger's sign (edema over the mastoid emissary vein area), raised ICP with papilledema, and 6th nerve palsy (false localizing sign from raised ICP). Tobey-Ayer test (compression of ipsilateral jugular vein fails to raise CSF pressure). Treatment: mastoidectomy + sinus decompression + anticoagulation + IV antibiotics.

</details>

**Q30. A 25-year-old man is brought to the emergency department with rapidly progressing neck swelling, trismus, brawny induration of the floor of mouth and submandibular region bilaterally, and severe airway distress. The most likely diagnosis and the immediate priority are:**
- A) Peritonsillar abscess; incision and drainage
- B) Ludwig's angina; immediate airway management (awake tracheostomy), then IV antibiotics and surgical drainage
- C) Parapharyngeal abscess; CT neck and drainage
- D) Retropharyngeal abscess; lateral neck X-ray and drainage

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Ludwig's angina is a rapidly spreading, life-threatening bilateral cellulitis (not abscess) of the submandibular, submental, and sublingual spaces — most commonly from a lower molar (mandibular second/third molar) dental infection. Hallmarks: bilateral brawny (woody) induration, floor of mouth elevation ("hot potato" voice, drooling, trismus), no pus formation initially. Airway obstruction is the primary cause of death. Management: AIRWAY FIRST (awake fiberoptic intubation or surgical tracheostomy under local anesthesia — do NOT use rapid sequence induction), followed by high-dose IV antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate + metronidazole) and surgical drainage.

</details>
