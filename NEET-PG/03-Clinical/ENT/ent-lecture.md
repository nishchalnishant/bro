## The Ear — Hearing and Balance

### Sound Transduction: From Air to Neural Signal

Let us begin at the very beginning, because if you understand the physics of hearing, everything else — the diseases, the tests, the surgeries — falls into place naturally. Sound is nothing more than a pressure wave in air: alternating compressions and rarefactions traveling outward from a vibrating source. The question the auditory system must answer is this: how do you take that mechanical event in air and convert it into an electrical signal that the brain can interpret? The answer requires three distinct stages, and each stage corresponds to one anatomical division of the ear.

The outer ear — the auricle and the external auditory canal — is a sound collector. Look at the shape of the auricle: it is curved, folded, and funnel-shaped. This is not aesthetic whimsy. It functions as a parabolic reflector, gathering sound waves from the environment and directing them toward the tympanic membrane. The folds and ridges of the auricle create subtle delays in the arrival of sounds from different directions, which the brain uses for vertical sound localization — how we know whether a sound comes from above or below us. The external auditory canal is roughly 2.5 cm long and resonates at approximately 3500 Hz — exactly the frequency range of speech. This resonance amplifies incoming sound by about 10 dB. The ear is, in other words, already tuned to the frequencies that matter most for communication.

**Analogy:** Think of the outer ear as the parabolic dish of a radio telescope. The dish doesn't process the signal — it collects it and focuses it at a central point. The tympanic membrane is that focal point.

Now we arrive at the tympanic membrane and the middle ear, and here is where the real engineering problem begins. The inner ear — the cochlea — is fluid-filled. This matters enormously because sound travels through air very differently from how it travels through fluid. When a sound wave in air encounters a fluid surface, almost all of the energy (approximately 99.9%, about 30 dB) is reflected back — this is the impedance mismatch between the two media. Without the middle ear, sound arriving at a fluid-filled cochlea would barely enter it. You would hear almost nothing. The middle ear exists entirely to solve this one problem: to efficiently transfer sound energy from a low-impedance medium (air) to a high-impedance medium (cochlear fluid). It is an impedance-matching transformer.

It achieves this transformation through two mechanisms. First, the area ratio: the tympanic membrane has a surface area of approximately 55 mm², while the footplate of the stapes (which presses on the oval window of the cochlea) has a surface area of only about 3.2 mm². The same force applied over a smaller area produces greater pressure — the ratio amplifies pressure approximately 17-fold. Second, the lever ratio of the ossicular chain (malleus, incus, stapes) contributes an additional factor of about 1.3. The combined effect is a pressure amplification of approximately 22-fold — almost exactly the amplification needed to overcome the impedance mismatch. Evolution has tuned this system with remarkable precision.

**Clinical connection:** This is why middle ear problems (fluid, perforation, ossicular disruption) cause conductive hearing loss. The transformer is damaged, and sound energy is poorly transferred to the cochlea. You can also now understand why a large tympanic membrane perforation causes less hearing loss than you might expect — the impedance mismatch is partly overcome by the remaining membrane area, and by sound reaching the round window directly.

Once energy enters the cochlea via the oval window, the inner ear takes over: mechanical energy must be converted to electrical energy. The cochlea is a coiled tube, 2.75 turns long, divided into three fluid-filled compartments by two membranes — the basilar membrane (below) and Reissner's membrane (above). The organ of Corti sits on the basilar membrane and contains the hair cells — the actual transducers. Each hair cell has a bundle of stereocilia on its apical surface. When the basilar membrane vibrates, the tectorial membrane (which overlies the stereocilia of the outer hair cells) shears against them, deflecting the stereocilia. This deflection opens mechanically-gated potassium channels at the tips of the stereocilia (connected by "tip links"). Potassium rushes in from the endolymph (which has an unusually high K+ concentration, maintained by the stria vascularis) → the hair cell depolarizes → calcium influx at the basolateral surface → neurotransmitter (glutamate) release → excitation of the spiral ganglion neuron → signal travels along the cochlear division of the vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) to the brainstem.

**Analogy:** The hair cell is like a very sophisticated microphone capsule. The stereocilia are the microphone diaphragm. Deflection in one direction opens channels (depolarization); deflection in the opposite direction closes them (hyperpolarization). This is how the hair cell encodes both the amplitude (how far the stereocilia deflect) and the timing of sound vibrations.

One more crucial concept: the cochlea performs frequency analysis through what is called the tonotopic map. The basilar membrane is not uniform — it is narrow and stiff at the base (near the oval window) and wide and floppy at the apex. High-frequency sounds cause maximum vibration near the base; low-frequency sounds cause maximum vibration near the apex. This is the "place principle" of frequency coding. High-frequency sounds are encoded at the base, low-frequency at the apex. This is why high-frequency hearing loss (from noise damage, ototoxic drugs, aging) affects the base first — the basal hair cells receive the highest energy and are most vulnerable.

> **Key exam point:** The cochlea maps frequency spatially. Base = high frequency. Apex = low frequency. Noise-induced and age-related hearing loss begin at 4000 Hz (the basal cochlea is most vulnerable). Presbycusis is a bilateral, symmetrical, high-frequency SNHL — the audiogram slopes downward at high frequencies.

### Conductive vs Sensorineural Hearing Loss

Now that you understand the transduction pathway, the classification of hearing loss becomes logical rather than memorized. Every hearing loss is either a failure of conduction (the sound is not reaching the cochlea) or a failure of transduction/neural processing (the sound reaches the cochlea but cannot be properly converted or transmitted).

**Conductive hearing loss (CHL)** is a problem in the outer or middle ear. Sound enters the ear canal, but cannot be efficiently transferred to the oval window. Causes include: wax impaction (blocking the ear canal), tympanic membrane perforation, middle ear effusion (glue ear — fluid dampens ossicular movement), acute otitis media, otosclerosis (abnormal bone fixates the stapes footplate), ossicular chain disruption (from trauma or cholesteatoma). In all these conditions, the cochlea and auditory nerve are normal — the machinery of transduction works fine, but the sound simply isn't arriving.

**Sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL)** is a problem in the cochlea (sensory) or auditory nerve/central pathways (neural). The sound reaches the cochlea normally, but the transduction fails. Causes include: noise-induced hearing loss (hair cell damage from acoustic trauma), presbycusis (age-related), ototoxicity (aminoglycosides, cisplatin — both damage outer hair cells at the base, causing high-frequency loss), Meniere's disease (endolymphatic hydrops), sudden SNHL, acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma — actually a Schwann cell tumor on CN VIII).

The Rinne and Weber tuning fork tests make complete sense from this framework. The Rinne test compares air conduction (AC) vs bone conduction (BC) in the same ear. Air conduction uses the full pathway: outer ear → middle ear → inner ear. Bone conduction bypasses the outer and middle ear entirely — the vibrating tuning fork pressed on the mastoid vibrates the skull, which directly vibrates the cochlear fluids. 

In a normal ear and in SNHL: AC > BC (Rinne positive), because air conduction includes the amplification of the middle ear, whereas bone conduction bypasses this. In CHL: BC > AC (Rinne negative), because the middle ear amplification is lost (or the middle ear is actually attenuating sound), so bone conduction — which bypasses the broken transformer — is relatively better.

The Weber test applies a single tuning fork to the center of the forehead and asks where the sound is heard. In a normal person, it lateralizes equally. In CHL (one side), it lateralizes to the affected ear — because the middle ear problem reduces the masking effect of environmental noise on the affected side, making bone-conducted sounds seem louder there (the ear is no longer "distracted" by the ambient noise it cannot hear). In SNHL (one side), it lateralizes to the normal (better) ear, because the damaged cochlea cannot process the vibration as well.

> **Key exam point:** Weber lateralizes TO the bad ear in CHL, AWAY from the bad ear in SNHL. Rinne is negative (BC>AC) in CHL, positive (AC>BC) in SNHL. A "false negative Rinne" occurs in severe SNHL — bone conduction from the affected side crosses over to the normal cochlea, making BC seem better on the affected side. Always correlate with audiometry.

Audiometry formalizes this: pure tone audiogram plots hearing threshold (dB) on the Y-axis against frequency (Hz) on the X-axis. Air conduction thresholds are plotted as circles (right) and crosses (left); bone conduction as square brackets. In CHL, air conduction thresholds are elevated but bone conduction is normal — the "air-bone gap." In SNHL, both air and bone conduction thresholds are elevated, and there is no air-bone gap. In mixed hearing loss, both are elevated but the air-bone gap persists.

**Tympanometry** measures the compliance (flexibility) of the tympanic membrane as a function of air pressure in the sealed ear canal. Type A is normal. Type As (shallow) suggests reduced compliance — otosclerosis, tympanosclerosis. Type Ad (deep) suggests hypercompliance — ossicular discontinuity, thin/atrophic TM. Type B is a flat trace (no compliance peak) — middle ear effusion, TM perforation (large), occluded probe tip. Type C has the peak shifted toward negative pressure — eustachian tube dysfunction, early otitis media. These are logical: if the middle ear is fluid-filled, the TM cannot move, so compliance is near-zero regardless of pressure — flat type B curve.

### Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media and Cholesteatoma

Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) is a persistent infection of the middle ear cleft — the middle ear, mastoid air cells, and eustachian tube — lasting more than two to three months, typically associated with a tympanic membrane perforation. To understand CSOM, you need to understand why the ear normally stays dry and uninfected.

The eustachian tube is the pressure-equalizing ventilation system for the middle ear. In adults it is approximately 35–40 mm long, and its isthmus (narrowest point) is normally closed, opening during swallowing and yawning via the tensor veli palatini muscle. This intermittent opening allows pressure equalization and drainage of secretions from the middle ear. In children, the tube is shorter (18 mm), more horizontal, and has a more compliant lumen — which is why children get so many more middle ear infections: pathogens from the nasopharynx travel upward more easily, and drainage is less efficient.

CSOM begins typically with acute otitis media that perforates the tympanic membrane and fails to heal. Once a perforation exists, the middle ear loses its protective sealed environment. Water enters during bathing, pathogens colonize — commonly Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus in chronic disease (the gram-negatives outcompete the gram-positive organisms of acute otitis media). The mucosa of the middle ear becomes chronically inflamed, granulation tissue forms, and the perforation fails to close. Safe CSOM (tubotympanic) involves a central perforation — the annulus (the fibrous ring at the periphery of the tympanic membrane) is intact, cholesteatoma does not form, and intracranial complications are rare. Unsafe CSOM (atticoantral) involves the pars flaccida or the posterosuperior quadrant of the pars tensa — and is associated with cholesteatoma.

Cholesteatoma is the skin in the wrong place — and once you think of it that way, everything about it makes sense. Keratinizing stratified squamous epithelium has migrated into the middle ear. It does not belong there — the middle ear is normally lined with respiratory-type (cuboidal, ciliated) epithelium. This squamous epithelium is perfectly normal skin: it continuously produces keratin and desquamates. Skin cells on your forearm fall off into the environment. Skin cells in the middle ear have nowhere to go. The desquamated keratin accumulates, compressed into layers, forming an expanding "onion skin" ball of dead skin — the cholesteatoma matrix.

**Analogy:** Imagine packing a closed bag with crumpled newspapers every day but never removing any. Eventually the bag must expand, and whatever is around it gets compressed and damaged. That is exactly what cholesteatoma does in the middle ear.

The cholesteatoma matrix is not inert — it is metabolically active. Macrophages and keratinocytes within it secrete collagenases, prostaglandins, and cytokines. These enzymes erode bone. This is the defining and most dangerous property of cholesteatoma: it destroys everything it contacts. It erodes the ossicles (causing conductive hearing loss), the tegmen (roof of the middle ear — if breached, meningitis or brain abscess), the facial canal (causing facial nerve palsy — this is a surgical emergency), the lateral semicircular canal (causing labyrinthine fistula — presenting with Tullio phenomenon, vertigo on pressure), and the sigmoid sinus (causing lateral sinus thrombosis).

**Clinical connection:** The classic history of cholesteatoma is a patient with chronic ear discharge — but the discharge is characteristically foul-smelling (from the anaerobic bacteria digesting keratin), scanty (unlike the copious mucopurulent discharge of safe CSOM), and may contain white flakes. Examination reveals a retraction pocket in the pars flaccida (Shrapnell's membrane), often with cholesteatoma pearls visible. CT scan of the temporal bone shows soft tissue density in the epitympanum/mastoid, with bony erosion — the classic "soft tissue mass with bone destruction." Treatment is surgical: mastoidectomy (canal wall up or canal wall down) to exteriorize and remove all cholesteatoma matrix. If any matrix is left, it will recur.

How does keratinizing epithelium get into the middle ear in the first place? There are three main theories. The most common is the retraction pocket theory (Habermann): chronic eustachian tube dysfunction creates chronic negative middle ear pressure → the flaccid pars flaccida is sucked inward → forms a retraction pocket → this pocket deepens and migrates medially into the epitympanum → it can no longer drain its desquamated keratin → cholesteatoma develops. Less commonly, squamous metaplasia of middle ear mucosa (in response to chronic infection), or invasion through a pre-existing perforation (epithelium migrates inward from the edges of a perforation). The retraction pocket mechanism is the most important to know.

> **Key exam point:** Complications of cholesteatoma follow anatomical relationships. Facial nerve palsy = erosion of facial canal. Sensorineural hearing loss = labyrinthine fistula. Meningitis/extradural abscess = tegmen erosion. Lateral sinus thrombosis = sigmoid sinus erosion. The most common ossicle eroded is the long process of the incus. Treatment is always surgical — mastoidectomy.

### Vertigo — A Failure of Spatial Orientation

Vertigo is the illusion of movement — specifically, of rotational movement. The patient feels as if they or the room is spinning. To understand vertigo, you must understand how the vestibular system normally tells the brain where the head is in space.

The vestibular apparatus in the inner ear has two functional components: the semicircular canals (detect angular/rotational acceleration) and the otolith organs — the utricle and saccule (detect linear acceleration and head position relative to gravity). There are three semicircular canals on each side, arranged in mutually perpendicular planes: lateral (horizontal), superior (anterior), and posterior. Each canal is a fluid-filled loop with an expansion at one end — the ampulla — which contains the crista ampullaris, a ridge of hair cells topped by the cupula (a gelatinous mass in which the hair cell stereocilia are embedded). When the head rotates, inertia keeps the endolymph momentarily stationary while the canal moves — the relative movement of endolymph deflects the cupula and bends the stereocilia, generating a signal.

The two labyrinths normally fire at equal rates at rest (tonic discharge). The brain compares the two sides. Any mismatch = movement signal. Vestibular neuritis (viral inflammation of the vestibular nerve, usually the superior division) knocks out one labyrinth → massive asymmetry → the brain interprets this as continuous rotation → prolonged, severe vertigo, nausea, vomiting. The nystagmus (involuntary rhythmic eye movement) that accompanies it is horizontal-torsional, fast phase beating away from the affected side (the intact side is "firing" unopposed). This is peripheral vestibular nystagmus — it is suppressed by fixation, and the direction does not change with gaze direction.

**Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)** is the commonest cause of vertigo, and it is one of the most satisfying diagnoses in medicine because you can cure it with a simple maneuver. The problem begins with the otoconia — tiny calcium carbonate crystals that sit on the macular epithelium of the utricle. They are denser than endolymph, and their weight bends the hair cells of the macula in response to gravity and linear acceleration — that is how the utricle detects head tilt and linear movement. In BPPV, these crystals detach from the utricular macula (from head trauma, degeneration, viral labyrinthitis) and fall into a semicircular canal — most commonly the posterior SCC, which is the most dependent canal in the upright head position.

**Analogy:** Imagine putting gravel into a garden hose and then moving the hose. The gravel, being denser than water, moves under gravity and its movement pushes the water ahead of it. This is exactly what the displaced otoconia do: they are denser than endolymph, so when you move your head, they sink under gravity, pushing the endolymph as they go, which deflects the cupula.

The result is false rotational signal generated whenever the head moves into the position that causes the crystals to move. The classic trigger is lying down or turning in bed. The vertigo is brief (< 1 minute, usually 10–30 seconds — because the crystals settle quickly) but intense, and it is always position-dependent. The Dix-Hallpike maneuver confirms the diagnosis: the patient is taken rapidly from sitting to lying with the affected ear down and the head rotated 45 degrees. After a brief latency (1–5 seconds, while the crystals fall), upbeat-torsional nystagmus appears and lasts less than a minute, then fatigues with repeat testing. The Epley canalith repositioning maneuver is the treatment — you rotate the patient's head through a series of positions (usually four) designed to use gravity to walk the crystals out of the posterior SCC, around the common crus, and back into the utricle where they are reabsorbed. It is effective in >90% of cases.

> **Key exam point:** BPPV is posterior SCC in 85–90% of cases. Dix-Hallpike positive — upbeat torsional nystagmus, latency 1–5s, duration < 1 min, fatigues. Treatment: Epley maneuver. Meniere's disease: episodic vertigo + fluctuating SNHL + tinnitus + aural fullness — caused by endolymphatic hydrops. Vestibular neuritis: sudden onset severe vertigo, no hearing loss (cochlear nerve spared), nystagmus away from the lesion.

**Meniere's disease** deserves careful explanation from first principles. The endolymph is contained in the membranous labyrinth. Its ionic composition (high K+, low Na+) is carefully regulated by the stria vascularis (in the cochlea) and the dark cells (in the vestibular labyrinth). The endolymphatic sac, located in the posterior cranial fossa, reabsorbs endolymph. In Meniere's disease, there is excess endolymph — endolymphatic hydrops. The membranous labyrinth distends. When the pressure becomes high enough, Reissner's membrane ruptures → K+-rich endolymph floods the perilymph → the high-K+ environment paralyzes the hair cells and auditory nerve → sudden decrease in function → the attack. Symptoms are the four hallmarks: episodic vertigo (minutes to hours), fluctuating low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss (because the apical cochlea, which codes for low frequencies, is most distended by the hydrops), tinnitus (roaring, low-pitched), and aural fullness.

### The Nose and Sinuses

### Rhinology — The Nose as an Air Conditioner

The nose is not simply a passage for air. It is an extraordinarily sophisticated air conditioning and filtering system, designed to deliver air to the lungs that is at body temperature, 100% humidity, and largely free of particulate matter — regardless of the ambient conditions. Understanding this function makes the pathology of the nose logical.

The nasal cavity is bounded medially by the septum and laterally by three turbinates (superior, middle, inferior) — bony shelves covered by highly vascular mucosa. The turbinates dramatically increase the surface area of the nasal cavity (from about 70 cm² of flat mucosal surface to approximately 150–160 cm²). The submucosal tissue of the inferior turbinate contains a rich cavernous plexus — capable of engorgement under autonomic control — that functions as a radiator, warming the air that passes over it. The mucus layer produced by goblet cells and submucosal glands traps particles and humidifies the air; the ciliated epithelium drives the mucus blanket posteriorly toward the nasopharynx (mucociliary clearance) at a rate of about 6 mm per minute.

**Analogy:** The turbinates are the baffles in a car radiator. They break the airflow into turbulent streams that maximize contact between air and the warm, moist mucosal surface. Remove the baffles (as happens after aggressive turbinate reduction or in empty nose syndrome), and the system fails: air hits the posterior nasopharynx dry and cold, producing paradoxical nasal obstruction despite a wide-open cavity.

**Epistaxis** — nosebleed — is the most common ENT emergency. 90–95% of nosebleeds originate from Kiesselbach's plexus (Little's area) on the anterior nasal septum — a confluence of five arteries: anterior ethmoidal, posterior ethmoidal, sphenopalatine, greater palatine, and superior labial (from the facial artery). This is the most exposed and least protected area of the highly vascular nasal mucosa, which is why minor trauma, dry air, or digital trauma cause bleeding here. Posterior epistaxis (5–10%) originates from the sphenopalatine artery or its branches posterolaterally in the nose — this bleeding is often more severe, more common in the elderly and hypertensive, and is harder to control because the source cannot be visualized directly. Posterior packing or endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation is required.

**Allergic rhinitis** is a type I hypersensitivity reaction of the nasal mucosa. On first exposure to an allergen (pollens, house dust mite, animal dander), antigen-presenting cells present it to naive T-cells in the nasal mucosa, which differentiate into Th2 cells and drive B-cell production of IgE antibodies specific to that allergen. These IgE antibodies bind to mast cells on the nasal mucosa (sensitization). On re-exposure, the allergen cross-links two surface IgE molecules → mast cell degranulation → immediate release of histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes → the early phase reaction (within minutes): sneezing, watery rhinorrhea, nasal itching, lacrimation. The late phase (4–6 hours later) is driven by eosinophil recruitment and cytokine release — nasal blockage predominates, less sneezing. This explains why antihistamines work well for sneezing and rhinorrhea (histamine-driven) but less well for blockage (mediator-driven). Intranasal corticosteroids (fluticasone, mometasone) reduce the entire inflammatory response — they work on both early and late phases and are the most effective pharmacological treatment for persistent allergic rhinitis.

### The Throat — Tonsils, Pharynx, and Larynx

### Tonsils and Peritonsillar Abscess

The palatine tonsils are lymphoid organs positioned at the gateway between the oral cavity and the oropharynx — a strategic immunological checkpoint where pathogens are sampled and immune responses initiated. They are covered by stratified squamous epithelium that invaginates to form crypts, increasing the surface area for antigen sampling. The tonsils are most active immunologically in childhood (ages 4–10), which is why tonsillitis is primarily a childhood disease and tonsils physiologically enlarge during this period.

Recurrent tonsillitis — the repeated cycle of acute tonsillitis (most commonly streptococcal — Group A Strep, Streptococcus pyogenes) — leads to cryptic debris accumulation, scarring, and a poorly draining tonsil. The indication for tonsillectomy in recurrent tonsillitis follows the Paradise criteria: 7 episodes in one year, 5 per year for two years, or 3 per year for three years — each episode documented with fever, exudate, cervical adenopathy, or positive strep culture. These criteria exist because tonsillitis frequency naturally decreases with age, so the procedure must be justified by a genuine impact on quality of life and health.

**Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy)** is the most common deep-space infection of the head and neck. Understand its anatomy: pus collects in the peritonsillar space — between the tonsil capsule and the superior constrictor muscle. It is almost always unilateral. The clinical presentation is the consequence of this anatomy: severe unilateral throat pain, trismus (pterygoid muscles are irritated by adjacent pus — the patient cannot open the mouth), a "hot potato" voice (muffled because the soft palate cannot move normally), uvular deviation away from the affected side (the swelling pushes the uvula contralaterally), and pooling of saliva (too painful to swallow). Treatment is aspiration or incision and drainage — the relief for the patient is immediate and dramatic.

**Epiglottitis** — once a disease of children caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b (now rare thanks to the Hib vaccine) — is now predominantly an adult disease caused by Streptococcus pyogenes, Staphylococcus aureus, or other organisms. The epiglottis becomes acutely edematous and bright red ("cherry-red epiglottis"). Because the epiglottis sits at the entrance to the larynx, its swelling can cause complete airway obstruction with terrifying rapidity. The classic triad: dysphagia, drooling, distress (the "three D's"). The patient sits leaning forward in the "tripod" position, chin forward, mouth open — maximizing the airway diameter. The lateral neck X-ray shows the "thumbprint sign" — the swollen epiglottis resembles a thumbprint. Management priority is the airway — examination of the throat in children with suspected epiglottitis should only be done in the operating room with a skilled anesthetist present, ready to intubate or surgically secure the airway if the inspection precipitates complete obstruction.

> **Key exam point:** In epiglottitis — do NOT examine the throat with a tongue depressor in the clinic or emergency department if the patient is a child in distress. The stimulation can trigger laryngospasm and complete obstruction. Secure the airway first. Lateral neck X-ray: "thumbprint sign." Blood cultures + IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone). Contrast with croup (laryngotracheobronchitis from parainfluenza virus): barking cough, inspiratory stridor, "steeple sign" on AP neck X-ray, responds to steroids and nebulized adrenaline.

## The Throat and Larynx — Voice, Breathing, and Their Disorders

### Hoarseness — From First Principles

The human voice is produced by the vocal cords (vocal folds) acting as vibrating reeds. Each vocal cord is a layered structure of epithelium, superficial lamina propria (Reinke's space — a loose, pliable gelatinous layer critical for mucosal wave propagation), deeper lamina propria, and the vocalis muscle beneath. When you phonate, the vocal cords are adducted and subglottic air pressure builds until it overcomes the closed cords, blowing them apart. Elastic recoil and the Bernoulli effect then snap them back together. This cycle of opening and closing occurs at the fundamental frequency of the voice — approximately 100–150 Hz in adult males, 200–250 Hz in adult females. The mucosal wave — a ripple of the superficial epithelium and Reinke's space over the firmer body of the vocal cord — is essential for efficient vibration.

**Analogy:** The vocal cord is like a reed in a woodwind instrument. A reed works because it is pliable, appropriately massed, and can respond to airflow by vibrating. If you load the reed with extra mass (like sticking clay on it), it vibrates differently — at a lower pitch, more sluggishly, or not at all. If you stiffen it, it cannot vibrate properly either. Hoarseness is what happens when mass, tension, or airflow through the vibrating reed is disturbed.

Three variables govern vocal cord vibration: **mass** (more mass → lower pitch, rougher voice), **tension** (reduced tension → lower pitch, reduced control), and **airflow symmetry** (if one cord cannot fully adduct, air escapes laterally → breathy, weak voice). Any pathology that alters one or more of these will cause hoarseness.

**Vocal cord polyp** is the result of excessive vocal trauma — screaming, prolonged speaking, or singing without proper technique — causing repetitive mechanical shear on the superficial lamina propria (Reinke's space). The repeated trauma disrupts the capillaries within Reinke's space → microhemorrhage → inflammatory edema accumulates in the lax gelatinous Reinke's space → a fluid-filled swelling develops on the free edge of the vocal cord, most commonly at the junction of the anterior one-third and posterior two-thirds (the point of maximum vibratory amplitude). The polyp adds mass to the cord. It also prevents the two cords from making full, symmetric contact during the closing phase. The result is a hoarse, low-pitched, breathy voice. Importantly, the underlying mucosa is normal — the problem is mechanical accumulation. Treatment: voice rest, voice therapy (address the underlying vocal misuse), and if the polyp is persistent, microsurgical removal via direct laryngoscopy with microflap technique (carefully preserving Reinke's space to maintain the pliability of the cord after surgery).

> **Key pearl:** Vocal cord nodules ("singer's nodules," "screamer's nodules") are bilateral, at the same location on both cords — the site of maximum trauma on contact. Polyps are usually unilateral. Both present with hoarseness and are caused by vocal misuse. Nodules are typically firm and fibrous; polyps are softer and hemorrhagic. Nodules are treated primarily with voice therapy (they often resolve completely with correct vocal technique); polyps more often require surgery.

**Vocal cord palsy** results from damage to the recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN), which supplies all the intrinsic muscles of the larynx except cricothyroid (supplied by the external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve). The RLN has a remarkably tortuous course — on the right it loops around the subclavian artery; on the left it loops around the arch of the aorta. This long intrathoracic course of the left RLN is why left-sided vocal cord palsy is more common than right-sided in systemic disease, and why a newly discovered left cord palsy mandates imaging from the skull base to the aortic arch.

**Unilateral RLN palsy** causes one vocal cord to be immobile, typically in a paramedian position (partially adducted — because the unopposed cricothyroid still tenses the cord, and the lateral cricoarytenoid is paralyzed). The patient has a weak, breathy, diplophonic (two-toned) voice because the paralyzed cord cannot fully adduct to meet the normal cord, and air escapes during phonation. Over weeks to months, the contralateral normal cord may compensate by crossing the midline to approximate the paralyzed cord (compensation) — the voice improves. If compensation is inadequate, injection medialization or thyroplasty (inserting an implant to push the paralyzed cord medially) can restore a functional voice.

**Bilateral RLN palsy** is an emergency of a completely different nature. Both cords are paralyzed in a paramedian (nearly adducted) position. Paradoxically, the voice may be reasonable (the cords are near the midline and can vibrate) — but the airway is severely compromised, because the cords are also nearly touching, leaving only a narrow slit for breathing. The patient presents with stridor, dyspnea, and respiratory distress — a compromised airway, not a compromised voice. This may follow bilateral thyroid surgery (the most common cause). Management is emergency airway securement — intubation, possibly tracheotomy.

**Clinical connection:** The most important causes of RLN palsy by site: (1) Skull base/nasopharynx — nasopharyngeal carcinoma, glomus jugulare tumor. (2) Neck — thyroid surgery, thyroid malignancy, neck dissection. (3) Mediastinum — left-sided: aortic aneurysm, enlarged mediastinal lymph nodes (lymphoma, lung cancer, sarcoidosis), left hilar mass. Right-sided: subclavian artery aneurysm. (4) Idiopathic (viral) — a diagnosis of exclusion. Any new unilateral vocal cord palsy without an obvious recent surgical cause demands urgent imaging and chest X-ray to exclude a mediastinal mass.

**Laryngeal cancer** is one of the most important ENT malignancies, and understanding its two main subtypes requires understanding both causation and anatomy. The two major risk factors are smoking (the dominant factor for all laryngeal cancers — tobacco carcinogens cause mucosal DNA damage, driving squamous cell carcinoma) and HPV infection (particularly HPV-16 — associated especially with oropharyngeal cancer, but also a subset of laryngeal SCCs; HPV-driven cancers tend to occur in younger, non-smoking patients and have a better prognosis). Alcohol potentiates the carcinogenic effect of tobacco synergistically.

**Anatomical location determines clinical behavior utterly.** The larynx is divided into three regions: the supraglottis (epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, arytenoids, false cords — above the level of the true cords), the glottis (the true vocal cords and the anterior and posterior commissures), and the subglottis (below the vocal cords, down to the inferior border of the cricoid).

**Glottic cancers** present early. Because they arise on the true vocal cords — the vibrating surface — even a tiny lesion (a small irregular plaque on the vocal cord margin) immediately disrupts the mucosal wave and changes the voice. Hoarseness is the first symptom, produced by a very small tumor. In a high-risk patient (smoker, > 40 years), new progressive hoarseness lasting more than three weeks is laryngeal cancer until proven otherwise. Furthermore, the glottis has sparse lymphatic drainage — so early glottic cancers rarely metastasize to regional lymph nodes. This combination of early symptom (hoarseness from tiny tumor) and late lymphatic spread makes glottic cancers the most favorable of all laryngeal cancers in terms of prognosis. Early glottic cancer (T1-T2) is cured in >90% of cases by radiotherapy alone or endoscopic laser resection.

**Supraglottic cancers** present late. The supraglottis does not vibrate — a large tumor can grow here silently, causing only vague symptoms (throat discomfort, "something stuck in the throat," referred otalgia via the internal branch of the superior laryngeal nerve/vagus — a crucial clinical sign: ear pain without ear pathology in a smoker must raise suspicion of oropharyngeal or supraglottic malignancy). Furthermore, the supraglottis has rich bilateral lymphatic drainage — supraglottic cancers metastasize early and bilaterally to cervical lymph nodes. They therefore present at an advanced stage.

> **Key pearl:** Glottic cancer = early hoarseness, late spread, good prognosis. Supraglottic cancer = late symptoms, early bilateral nodal spread, poor prognosis. Subglottic cancer: rarest, presents even later (no symptoms until airway compromise or thyroid involvement), worst prognosis.

### Stridor — The Mechanics of Obstructed Breathing

Stridor is the harsh, high-pitched sound produced by turbulent airflow through a narrowed airway. The character of the stridor — whether it occurs during inspiration, expiration, or both — tells you precisely where the obstruction is, because the physics of breathing creates different pressure dynamics in different parts of the airway at different phases of respiration.

During **inspiration**, the intrathoracic airways are pulled open (as the chest expands, pleural pressure falls, pulling airway walls outward), but the extrathoracic airways (pharynx, larynx, upper trachea) experience a transmural pressure that tends to collapse them inward (atmospheric pressure outside > sub-atmospheric pressure inside as air rushes in). Therefore, an obstruction above the thoracic inlet (supraglottic, glottic, subglottic) is worsened by inspiration — producing **inspiratory stridor**. The supraglottis and glottis are extrathoracic.

During **expiration**, the situation reverses: positive intrathoracic pressure tends to compress the intrathoracic airways. An obstruction at the level of the subglottis or upper trachea may be worsened during expiration — though most fixed (solid) obstructions produce **biphasic stridor** (audible in both phases, but can be loudest in inspiration for glottic and in expiration for subglottic).

**Inspiratory stridor** → supraglottic obstruction (epiglottis, supraglottic mass, laryngomalacia — the most common cause of stridor in neonates, where the floppy supraglottic structures collapse inward during the negative-pressure inspiration).

**Biphasic stridor** → glottic or subglottic obstruction (bilateral cord palsy, subglottic stenosis, foreign body at the glottis).

**Expiratory wheeze/stridor** → more distal (intrathoracic) obstruction (asthma, tracheomalacia).

### Croup vs Epiglottitis — Same Stridor, Different Emergencies

**Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis)** is caused predominantly by parainfluenza virus (types 1 and 3 most common), which infects the upper and lower respiratory tract epithelium. The characteristic pathological feature is subglottic edema — swelling of the mucosa immediately below the vocal cords. In children (typically age 6 months to 3 years), this is the anatomically critical point: the subglottis is the narrowest portion of the entire child's airway (in adults, the glottis is narrowest; in children, the cricoid ring — which is a complete, non-expandable cartilaginous ring — encircles the subglottis and limits expansion). Any edema here has an outsized effect on airflow. Using Poiseuille's law (resistance ∝ 1/r⁴), halving the radius of the airway increases resistance 16-fold.

**Analogy:** Croup in a child is like trying to breathe through a straw that is being slowly pinched. A small reduction in the straw's diameter causes a dramatic increase in resistance. The seal-bark cough of croup is produced by the stiff, edematous subglottis — the cords vibrate against the edematous tissue on every cough, creating the characteristic barking sound.

The clinical picture is classic: viral prodrome (low fever, coryza) → worsening inspiratory stridor + seal-bark cough + hoarse voice, typically worse at night (upright posture during day reduces edema; lying down worsens it). AP neck X-ray shows the "steeple sign" — the normal squared-off subglottic shoulder is narrowed to a point by edema (like a church steeple). Treatment: oral dexamethasone (reduces mucosal edema — a single dose is highly effective) and nebulized adrenaline (vasoconstricts the subglottic mucosa, rapidly reducing edema — effect lasts 1–2 hours, so the child must be observed for "rebound").

**Epiglottitis** is bacterial cellulitis of the supraglottic structures — the epiglottis swells dramatically (becomes cherry-red and grossly edematous), and the aryepiglottic folds and arytenoids are also inflamed. Traditionally caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) in children; now, post-Hib vaccine, more commonly Streptococcus pyogenes or Staphylococcus aureus in adults. The swollen, stiff epiglottis obstructs the supraglottis and distorts the pharynx. The patient speaks with a "hot potato voice" (as if they have a hot object in the mouth that they dare not touch with the tongue) — because moving the tongue and pharyngeal muscles distorts the inflamed epiglottis, causing pain, so the patient holds the tongue still and speaks around it. Drooling occurs because swallowing is too painful. The patient assumes the "tripod position" — leaning forward, neck extended, hands on knees — maximizing the laryngeal inlet diameter. Lateral soft tissue neck X-ray shows the "thumbprint sign" — the swollen epiglottis projects downward from the base of the tongue like a thumbprint.

> **Key comparison:** Croup = viral, subglottic, barking cough, steeple sign, treat with steroids + nebulized adrenaline. Epiglottitis = bacterial, supraglottic, hot potato voice + drooling + tripod position, thumbprint sign, airway emergency (never examine throat in clinic with a tongue depressor — do it in OT with anesthesia present), IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone). The critical principle in epiglottitis: the airway is the priority. Everything else is secondary.

### Nasal Polyps and Sinusitis — The Mucociliary Cascade

The paranasal sinuses (maxillary, ethmoid, frontal, sphenoid) drain into the nasal cavity via ostia — small openings. The entire system depends on the mucociliary escalator: the ciliated epithelium lining the sinuses continuously moves a thin mucus blanket toward the ostium and out into the nasal cavity. The ostium of the maxillary sinus is counterintuitively located high on the medial wall of the sinus — not at the lowest point. The sinus cannot drain by gravity; it depends entirely on mucociliary transport. This is why obstruction of the ostium is catastrophic to sinus health.

The sequence leading to sinusitis is logical. An upper respiratory tract viral infection (most commonly rhinovirus) causes mucosal edema in the nasal cavity → the ostium narrows or closes → ciliary function is impaired by the viral infection itself → mucus pools in the sinus (it cannot be cleared to the obstructed ostium) → the warm, moist, nutrient-rich mucus becomes an ideal culture medium → bacterial superinfection (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis are the three most common) → acute bacterial sinusitis. The maxillary sinus is the most commonly affected because its ostium is the most easily obstructed and its gravity-dependent drainage is nil.

**Nasal polyps** are the chronic endpoint of this mucosal inflammatory cascade. They are not neoplastic — they are pale, pedunculated, gelatinous outgrowths of chronically inflamed nasal mucosa, predominantly from the ethmoid sinuses and the middle meatus (the ostiomeatal complex). They are composed of edematous stroma infiltrated with eosinophils and mast cells, covered by respiratory epithelium. Their growth obstructs the nasal cavity and ostiomeatal complex, further impairing drainage and perpetuating the cycle. Clinically: bilateral nasal obstruction (polyps are almost always bilateral; a unilateral polyp should raise suspicion of a neoplasm), anosmia (the olfactory epithelium in the roof of the nose is blocked from odorant molecules by the polyp mass), and recurrent sinusitis. The association of nasal polyps + asthma + aspirin hypersensitivity = **Samter's triad** (aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease — aspirin blocks COX-1, shunting arachidonic acid toward the leukotriene pathway → bronchoconstriction and nasal polyp flare in susceptible patients). Treatment: intranasal corticosteroids (reduce polyp size, slow regrowth), short courses of systemic steroids (dramatic reduction), and when medical treatment fails, functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS) to open the ostiomeatal complex and remove polyps. Dupilumab (IL-4/IL-13 blockade) is now licensed for severe CRS with nasal polyps (type 2 inflammation driven by eosinophils), producing dramatic polyp regression in refractory patients.

> **Key pearl:** Never forget to consider cystic fibrosis in a young patient with nasal polyps — CF is the most common cause of nasal polyps in children. The defective CFTR chloride channel produces thick, dehydrated mucus that cannot be cleared, leading to chronic infection and polyp formation throughout the respiratory tract.

> **Final clinical pearl:** The ear is the only sensory organ that develops from all three embryonic germ layers. The outer ear (auricle) from ectoderm and mesoderm (branchial arches 1 and 2), the middle ear from endoderm (first pharyngeal pouch), the inner ear from ectoderm (otic placode). This means different parts of the ear have different developmental vulnerabilities, different blood supplies, and different pathological processes. Always examine the ear systematically: auricle, EAC, tympanic membrane (all four quadrants), and always test hearing.
