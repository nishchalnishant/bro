## Ear Anatomy

### External Ear
- **Auricle (Pinna)**: Fibrocartilage (except lobule = fat); landmarks — helix, antihelix, tragus, antitragus, concha, Darwin's tubercle
- **External Auditory Canal (EAC)**: Length **24 mm** (outer 1/3 cartilaginous, inner 2/3 bony)
  - Narrowest part: **Isthmus** (junction of cartilaginous and bony parts)
  - Bony part lined by thin skin with no hair/glands (subperiosteal abscess forms here)
  - Cartilaginous part: **Ceruminous glands** (modified apocrine) + sebaceous glands + hair
  - **Fissures of Santorini**: Defects in anterior EAC cartilage → spread of parotid/EAC infections
  - **Foramen of Huschke**: In anterior bony EAC wall (in children) → communication with TMJ

### Middle Ear (Tympanic Cavity)
- **Boundaries**: Roof = tegmen tympani (temporal bone); Floor = jugular wall; Anterior = carotid wall; Posterior = mastoid wall; Medial = labyrinthine wall; Lateral = tympanic membrane
- **Contents**: Ossicles, tensor tympani, stapedius, chorda tympani nerve

#### Tympanic Membrane (TM)
- **Size**: 9-10 mm vertical, 8-9 mm horizontal
- **Layers**: Outer squamous epithelium + middle fibrous layer (Shrapnell's membrane = pars flaccida has no fibrous layer) + inner mucous layer
- **Landmarks** (on otoscopy):
  - **Handle of malleus**: Vertical white band
  - **Umbo**: Lowest point of handle of malleus
  - **Cone of light (light reflex)**: Anteroinferior; 5 o'clock right, 7 o'clock left
  - **Pars flaccida (Shrapnell's membrane)**: Superior, above malleolar folds — attic perforation site
  - **Pars tensa**: Rest of TM (most of the drum)

#### Ossicles
| Ossicle | Articulation | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Malleus** | Attached to TM at umbo (handle); articulates with incus (malleoincudal joint) | Largest ossicle |
| **Incus** | Articulates with malleus and stapes | Middle ossicle |
| **Stapes** | Articulates with incus; footplate in oval window | **Smallest bone in body**; footplate covered by annular ligament |

- **Blood supply of stapes**: No periosteum (avascular) → supplied by mucosa via stapedial artery (embryonic) — **otosclerosis** and surgical risk

#### Eustachian Tube
- **Length**: **35 mm** (adult); **17 mm** (children)
- **Angle**: 45° in adults; **10° in children** (more horizontal) → why children more prone to otitis media
- **Functions**: Pressure equalization, drainage of middle ear, mucociliary clearance
- **Opens during**: Yawning, swallowing, sneezing (tensor veli palatini and levator veli palatini)
- Closed at rest (mucosal valve at pharyngeal end)

#### Facial Nerve in Middle Ear
- Enters middle ear via internal acoustic meatus → **geniculate ganglion** (first genu — taste/lacrimation) → horizontal segment above oval window → second genu → vertical segment in mastoid → exits at stylomastoid foramen
- **Dehiscent facial nerve canal**: Exposed nerve over oval window — risk in ear surgery, chronic otitis media
- Branches: Greater petrosal nerve (lacrimation), nerve to stapedius, chorda tympani (taste anterior 2/3 tongue + submandibular salivary secretion)

### Inner Ear
- **Bony labyrinth**: Cochlea (2.5 turns), vestibule, semicircular canals (anterior/posterior/lateral)
- **Membranous labyrinth**: Cochlear duct (scala media — endolymph), utricle, saccule, membranous SCCs
- **Endolymph**: High K+, low Na+ (like intracellular fluid); produced by stria vascularis
- **Perilymph**: High Na+, low K+ (like extracellular fluid); in scala vestibuli and scala tympani
- **Organ of Corti**: On basilar membrane; inner hair cells (3,500 — afferent) and outer hair cells (12,000 — efferent/amplification)
- **Tonotopy**: High frequency at base; low frequency at apex

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Stapes = smallest bone; ET = 17mm child/35mm adult; pars flaccida = attic; cone of light = anteroinferior

## Hearing Tests

### Tuning Fork Tests (512 Hz standard)

| Test | Normal | CHL (Conductive) | SNHL (Sensorineural) |
|------|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Rinne** | AC>BC (Positive) | **BC>AC (Negative)** | AC>BC but both reduced |
| **Weber** | Central (midline) | **Lateralizes to affected ear** (better bone conduction in worse ear) | **Lateralizes to better (normal) ear** |
| Absolute Bone Conduction (ABC) | Normal | Normal | Reduced |
| Schwabach's | Normal | Prolonged | Shortened |

- **False-negative Rinne**: When SNHL is severe/profound in one ear — BC conducted across skull to better ear (cochlear reserve in normal ear perceives BC stimulus) — appears as negative Rinne in the SNHL ear; **Stenger test** differentiates
- **Bing test**: Occlusion effect — in CHL, occlusion of EAC does NOT increase BC (normally increases 15 dB)

### Audiogram Interpretation
- **Air-bone gap >15 dB = Conductive hearing loss** (air conduction worse than bone conduction)
- **No air-bone gap + elevated thresholds = SNHL**
- **Mixed hearing loss**: Both ABG and elevated bone conduction
- **PTA (Pure Tone Average)**: Average of 500, 1000, 2000 Hz (some include 4000 Hz)
  - 0-25 dB: Normal; 26-40: Mild; 41-55: Moderate; 56-70: Moderately severe; 71-90: Severe; >90: Profound

### Tympanometry (Impedance Audiometry)
| Type | Pattern | Interpretation |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Type A** | Normal peak at 0 daPa | Normal middle ear |
| **Type As** (shallow/stiff) | Low compliance peak | Otosclerosis, stiff ossicles |
| **Type Ad** (deep) | High compliance peak | Ossicular discontinuity |
| **Type B** (flat) | No peak | **Fluid in middle ear (OME)**, perforation, patent grommet |
| **Type C** | Peak at negative pressure | Eustachian tube dysfunction |

### OAE (Otoacoustic Emissions)
- Generated by **outer hair cells**; absent in SNHL (>30-35 dB HL) and CHL
- **TEOAE/DPOAE**: Used in **neonatal hearing screening** (Universal Newborn Hearing Screening — UNHS)

### BERA/ABR (Brainstem Evoked Response Audiometry)
- Records brainstem electrical activity in response to clicks
- **Waves I-V**: Wave I (CN VIII), Wave III (cochlear nucleus), Wave V (inferior colliculus)
- **Uses**: Threshold estimation (objective), **retrocochlear lesion** (acoustic neuroma — prolonged I-III and I-V interpeak latency), neonates, non-cooperative patients
- **Wave V threshold**: Approximates hearing threshold (± 10-20 dB)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Weber lateralizes to bad ear in CHL; Type B tympanogram = middle ear effusion; BERA Wave V = acoustic neuroma screen

## Otitis Media

### Acute Otitis Media (AOM)
- **Most common organism**: **Streptococcus pneumoniae** > H. influenzae > M. catarrhalis
- **Clinical**: Otalgia, fever, tugging ear, otorrhea (if perforated), reduced hearing
- **Otoscopy**: Bulging, red, opaque TM; absent light reflex; restricted mobility
- **Treatment**: Amoxicillin-clavulanate (if no improvement in 48-72h or severe); observation first for mild-moderate
- **Grommets/Ventilation tubes**: For recurrent AOM (≥3 in 6 months) or OME (persistent >3 months with hearing loss)

### Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM)

| Feature | Tubotympanic (Safe) | Atticoantral (Unsafe) |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Perforation site | **Pars tensa (central)** | **Pars flaccida (Attic/marginal)** |
| Discharge | Mucopurulent, profuse, odourless | **Scanty, foul-smelling (cholesteatoma)** |
| Cholesteatoma | Absent | **Present** |
| Hearing loss | Mild-moderate CHL | Moderate + possible SNHL |
| Prognosis | **Better (safe)** | **Dangerous (unsafe)** |
| Treatment | Medical (aural toilet + topical antibiotics) + tympanoplasty | **Mastoidectomy (cortical/modified radical/radical)** |

### Cholesteatoma
- **Definition**: Mass of **keratinizing stratified squamous epithelium** with desquamating keratin in middle ear/mastoid
- **Mechanism**: Invagination of pars flaccida (retraction pocket theory) — most common theory
- **CT scan hallmark**: **Erosion of scutum** (lateral wall of attic/epitympanum — first bony landmark eroded)
- **Bone erosion pattern**: Ossicles (incus long process first) → lateral semicircular canal → tegmen → facial canal
- **Treatment**: Surgery — **Modified radical mastoidectomy** or canal wall down/up procedures

### Complications of CSOM

**Extracranial:**
- **Mastoiditis** (most common): Postauricular swelling, pushed pinna forward
- **Bezold's abscess**: Pus tracks along sternomastoid; neck mass
- Subperiosteal abscess
- Petrositis (Gradenigo's syndrome): Apical petrositis — **VI nerve palsy + periorbital pain + otorrhea** (triad)
- Facial palsy (VII nerve involvement)
- Labyrinthitis (SNHL + vertigo)

**Intracranial:**
- Meningitis (most common intracranial complication)
- **Extradural abscess**
- **Subdural abscess**
- **Brain abscess**: Temporal lobe (from middle ear) or cerebellar (from mastoid) — **most serious**
- **Lateral sinus thrombosis** (sigmoid sinus): Picket fence/spiking fever, Tobey-Ayer test +

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Gradenigo's syndrome = VI palsy + pain + ear discharge; cholesteatoma = scutum erosion; sigmoid sinus thrombosis = picket fence fever

## Sensorineural Hearing Loss

### Presbycusis (Age-Related SNHL)
- **Most common cause of SNHL** in elderly (>65 years)
- **Bilateral, symmetrical, high-frequency SNHL** with poor speech discrimination
- Audiogram: **Downward-sloping** (high frequencies affected most)
- Types: Sensory (outer hair cell degeneration), Neural, Metabolic (stria vascularis), Mechanical (cochlear conduction)
- **Treatment**: Hearing aids, cochlear implant (if profound)

### Noise-Induced Hearing Loss
- **"C5 dip"**: **4 kHz notch** on audiogram (resonant frequency of external ear canal)
- Chronic noise: >85 dB for 8 hours continuously
- **Acoustic trauma**: Single intense exposure (blast) → immediate SNHL
- **Temporary threshold shift (TTS)**: Reversible; becomes permanent with chronic exposure

### Meniere's Disease (Endolymphatic Hydrops)
- **Triad**: 1. **Episodic vertigo** (minutes to hours), 2. **SNHL** (fluctuating), 3. **Tinnitus** (low-pitched)
- **Fourth feature**: Aural fullness/pressure
- **Pathology**: Endolymphatic hydrops (excess endolymph)
- **Audiogram**: Initially low-frequency SNHL; later affects all frequencies
- **Glycerol dehydration test**: Improvement of 15 dB after 1.5g/kg oral glycerol confirms diagnosis
- **Treatment**:
  - Medical: Low-salt diet (<2g/day), diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide), **betahistine** (vestibular suppressant)
  - Intratympanic: Gentamicin (ablation of vestibular function) or steroids
  - Surgical: Endolymphatic sac decompression, labyrinthectomy, vestibular neurectomy

### Sudden SNHL
- Hearing loss ≥30 dB in 3 contiguous frequencies over ≤72 hours
- **Idiopathic** (80%); others: viral, vascular, autoimmune, acoustic neuroma
- **Treatment**: **Oral/intratympanic corticosteroids** (prednisolone 1mg/kg/day × 7-10 days); treat within 2-4 weeks for best outcome

### Ototoxic Drugs
| Drug Class | Effect | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Aminoglycosides** | SNHL + vestibulotoxicity | Gentamicin = vestibulotoxic; amikacin/neomycin = cochleotoxic; affects outer hair cells |
| **Cisplatin** | SNHL (bilateral, high-frequency, irreversible) | Dose-dependent |
| **Loop diuretics** (furosemide) | Transient SNHL | Stria vascularis damage; reversible if stopped |
| **Quinine** | Tinnitus + SNHL | Usually reversible |
| **Aspirin** | Tinnitus | Reversible |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: 4kHz notch = noise-induced; Meniere's triad + betahistine; aminoglycosides = cochleo+vestibulotoxic

## Vertigo

### Peripheral vs Central Vertigo

| Feature | Peripheral | Central |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Onset | Sudden | Gradual |
| Severity | Severe | Less severe but persistent |
| Nystagmus | **Horizontal/rotatory; fatigable** | **Vertical/direction-changing; non-fatigable** |
| Latency (Dix-Hallpike) | **Present (2-5 sec)** | Absent |
| Fatiguability | **Yes** | No |
| Suppressed by fixation | Yes | No |
| Hearing loss | May be present | Usually absent |
| Neurological signs | **Absent** | **Present** (CN palsies, ataxia) |
| Causes | BPPV, Meniere's, vestibular neuritis, labyrinthitis | MS, brainstem TIA/stroke, tumor |

### BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)
- **Most common cause of vertigo**
- **Pathology**: Displaced **otoliths (canalith)** in semicircular canal — **posterior SCC** most common (90%)
- **Clinical**: Brief (30 seconds-1 minute) vertigo on head position change (lying down, looking up, rolling in bed)
- **Diagnostic test**: **Dix-Hallpike test** — head turned 45° to affected side + rapid recline → upbeat rotatory nystagmus (latency 2-5 sec, fatigable)
- **Treatment**: **Epley maneuver** (canalith repositioning) — 90% successful; Brandt-Daroff exercises (self-treatment)

### Vestibular Neuritis
- **Cause**: Post-viral (presumed HSV reactivation)
- **Features**: Sudden onset severe vertigo (days), nausea/vomiting; **no hearing loss, no tinnitus**
- **Examination**: Horizontal nystagmus toward normal side; Romberg positive
- **Treatment**: Symptomatic (prochlorperazine/antihistamines short-term); vestibular rehabilitation

### Ramsay Hunt Syndrome (Herpes Zoster Oticus)
- **Triad**: 1. **Facial palsy** (LMN, worse prognosis than Bell's palsy), 2. **Vesicles in EAC/auricle/pharynx**, 3. **SNHL + vertigo** (cochlear/vestibular involvement)
- **Cause**: VZV reactivation in **geniculate ganglion**
- **Treatment**: Acyclovir + prednisolone (within 72h); eye care

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Dix-Hallpike = BPPV test; Epley = BPPV treatment; Ramsay Hunt = VZV + geniculate ganglion

## Nasal Anatomy

### External Nose
- **Skeleton**: Upper 1/3 = nasal bones; Lower 2/3 = cartilaginous (upper lateral/lower lateral/septal cartilages)
- **Tip**: Defined by lower lateral cartilages (alar cartilages)
- **Ostiomeatal complex (OMC)**: Functional unit — middle meatus + uncinate process + hiatus semilunaris + infundibulum — key to chronic rhinosinusitis

### Nasal Septum
- **Bony**: Perpendicular plate of ethmoid (superoposterior) + vomer (posteroinferior) + maxillary/palatine crests (floor)
- **Cartilaginous**: Quadrilateral cartilage (anterior)
- **Kiesselbach's plexus (Little's area)**: Anteroinferior septum — anastomosis of:
  - Anterior ethmoidal artery (from ophthalmic)
  - Superior labial artery (from facial)
  - Septal branch of greater palatine artery (from maxillary)
  - **Most common site of epistaxis (90%)**
- **Woodruff's plexus**: Posterior septum/lateral wall — sphenopalatine artery — posterior epistaxis

### Lateral Wall — Turbinates and Meatuses
| Turbinate | Meatus | Structures Draining |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| Inferior | Inferior meatus | **Nasolacrimal duct** |
| Middle | Middle meatus | **Maxillary sinus** (via infundibulum), **frontal sinus** (via nasofrontal duct), **anterior ethmoid** |
| Superior | Superior meatus | **Posterior ethmoid** |
| — | Sphenoethmoidal recess | **Sphenoid sinus** |

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus; maxillary+frontal+anterior ethmoid → middle meatus; Kiesselbach's area = 90% epistaxis

## Epistaxis

### Anterior vs Posterior Epistaxis
| Feature | Anterior | Posterior |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Site | **Kiesselbach's plexus (Little's area)** | **Sphenopalatine artery** (Woodruff's plexus) |
| Frequency | **90%** | 10% |
| Age | Younger | **Elderly, hypertensive** |
| Severity | Usually mild, self-limiting | Severe, often bilateral, posterior flow |
| Visualization | Can see source | Cannot see source directly |
| Management | Direct pressure, cautery | Posterior packing (Bellocq's tampon), arterial ligation |

### Management of Epistaxis (Step-wise)
1. **First aid**: Patient seated, lean forward, pinch soft part of nose × 10-15 min
2. Ice pack on nose/neck
3. **Cauterization**: Silver nitrate or electrocautery (if source visible) — only one side at a time (risk of septal perforation)
4. **Anterior packing**: BIPP (bismuth iodoform paraffin paste) ribbon gauze or Merocel sponge × 24-48h; IV antibiotics during packing (toxic shock syndrome prevention)
5. **Posterior packing**: Bellocq's tampon (gauze balloon) or Foley catheter balloon × 48-72h
6. **Endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation**: Posterior epistaxis; preferred over ligation
7. **External carotid artery ligation / Maxillary artery ligation** (transantral approach): If endoscopic fails
8. **Embolization** (interventional radiology): Alternative

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Anterior epistaxis = Little's area; posterior = sphenopalatine; Bellocq's tampon = posterior packing

## Rhinosinusitis

### Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis
- **Organisms**: **S. pneumoniae** > H. influenzae > M. catarrhalis
- **Diagnosis**: Symptoms >10 days OR worsening after initial improvement (double worsening); purulent nasal discharge + facial pain/pressure/fullness + nasal obstruction
- **Antibiotic**: **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** 5-7 days (first-line if antibiotics indicated)

### Chronic Rhinosinusitis (CRS)
- **Definition**: Symptoms ≥12 weeks (nasal obstruction + discharge + facial pain/pressure/smell loss + endoscopic/CT findings)
- **Types**: CRS without nasal polyps (CRSsNP); CRS with nasal polyps (CRSwNP)
- **Treatment**: Topical nasal steroids (first-line for CRSwNP), saline irrigation, nasal polyp treatment (steroids/FESS), antibiotics only for acute exacerbation

### Allergic Fungal Rhinosinusitis (AFRS)
- IgE-mediated allergy to fungus; bilateral polyps; **eosinophilic mucin** (peanut butter-like material); CT: central high density (fungal concretions)
- Treatment: FESS + systemic steroids + topical steroids post-op

### Complications of Rhinosinusitis

**Orbital Complications (Chandler classification):**
1. Periorbital edema (preseptal)
2. Orbital cellulitis (postseptal)
3. Subperiosteal abscess (between periorbita and orbital wall)
4. Orbital abscess
5. **Cavernous sinus thrombosis** (most serious — septic thrombosis)

**Intracranial:** Meningitis, epidural abscess, subdural empyema, brain abscess, superior sagittal sinus thrombosis

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: CRS = >12 weeks; FESS = functional endoscopic sinus surgery; cavernous sinus thrombosis = most dangerous orbital complication

## Nasal Polyps

### Sinonasal Polyps (Bilateral)
- **Pathology**: Edematous mucosa, predominantly eosinophilic infiltrate, hypertrophy of nasal mucosa
- **Appearance**: Pale grey, glistening, insensitive (no pain on probing — differentiates from turbinate)
- **Origin**: Most commonly from **middle meatus/ethmoid** sinuses
- **Associated conditions**: Allergy, asthma (Samter's triad: aspirin sensitivity + asthma + polyps), **cystic fibrosis** (in children — always screen for CF)
- **Bernstein test**: For allergic basis of polyps (intranasal methacholine challenge)
- **Treatment**: Topical nasal steroids (shrink polyps), systemic steroids (short course for large), **FESS** (recurrence common)
- **Biologics**: Dupilumab (IL-4/IL-13 antagonist) for refractory CRSwNP

### Antrochoanal Polyp (Killian's polyp)
- **Origin**: Maxillary sinus antrum → through accessory ostium → choana → nasopharynx
- **Unilateral** (vs bilateral sinonasal polyps)
- **Young adults/children**; male > female
- **Clinical**: Unilateral nasal obstruction, post-nasal drip, absent with middle meatus polyps
- **CT**: Polyp from maxillary sinus to choana/nasopharynx
- **Treatment**: FESS — remove polyp root from maxillary sinus + avulsion of choanal portion

## Throat and Pharynx

### Tonsillitis
- **Most common organism**: **Group A Streptococcus (GAS) / Streptococcus pyogenes**
- **Clinical**: Sore throat, fever, dysphagia, exudate on tonsils, cervical lymphadenopathy
- **Centor criteria** (modified McIsaac): Exudate + tender anterior cervical nodes + fever + no cough (+ age) → score ≥3: treat empirically or swab
- **Treatment**: **Phenoxymethylpenicillin (Penicillin V) × 10 days** (amoxicillin — maculopapular rash if EBV)
- **Complications**: Peritonsillar abscess, retropharyngeal abscess, rheumatic fever (M-protein cross-reactivity), glomerulonephritis

### Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)
- **Most common complication** of tonsillitis
- Pus forms between palatine tonsil and the superior pharyngeal constrictor muscle
- **Clinical**: Severe unilateral sore throat, trismus, hot potato voice, **deviation of uvula away from the affected side**, bulging of anterior pillar
- **Treatment**: **Needle aspiration** (first-line) OR incision and drainage + IV antibiotics (penicillin + metronidazole); tonsillectomy (interval, 6-8 weeks later, or hot)

### Retropharyngeal Abscess
- **Age**: Mainly in **children <5 years** (retropharyngeal nodes atrophy after 5 years — Gillette's nodes)
- Infected lymph nodes in retropharyngeal space (between posterior pharyngeal wall and prevertebral fascia)
- **Clinical**: Fever, dysphagia, neck stiffness, stridor, bulging of posterior pharyngeal wall
- **Lateral neck X-ray**: **Prevertebral space widening** (>7mm at C2 = abnormal in children)
- **CT neck** (contrast): Confirms and differentiates cellulitis from abscess (rim-enhancing collection)
- **Treatment**: IV antibiotics; surgical drainage (transoral if without lateral extension, external if danger space involvement)

### Ludwig's Angina
- **Definition**: Bilateral submandibular space cellulitis (not true abscess); spreading to submental and sublingual spaces
- **Source**: Most commonly from **mandibular molar tooth infection** (2nd/3rd molar)
- **Clinical**: **Floor of mouth elevation/induration**, tongue displaced upward and backward, dysphagia, drooling, **airway obstruction emergency**
- **Treatment**: Airway first (**awake fiberoptic intubation** if time permits; tracheostomy if imminent obstruction), IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (penicillin + metronidazole), surgical drainage

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Quinsy = uvula deviates away; retropharyngeal abscess <5 years; Ludwig's = floor of mouth elevation

## Larynx

### Anatomy
- **Cartilages**: Thyroid (Adam's apple), cricoid (only complete ring), arytenoids (vocal process), epiglottis, corniculate, cuneiform, tritriceal
- **Vocal folds (true VF)**: **Vocalis + thyroarytenoid muscles**; covered by non-keratinizing squamous epithelium; no lymphatics (glottis)
- **False VF**: Above true VF; covered by respiratory epithelium
- **Reinke's space**: Superficial lamina propria of true vocal fold — edema here = Reinke's edema (voice change, fluid-filled VF)

### Stridor
| Type | Level | Causes |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Inspiratory** | Supraglottic | Epiglottitis, croup, laryngomalacia, abscess |
| **Biphasic (inspiratory + expiratory)** | **Glottic/subglottic** | Subglottic stenosis, bilateral VF palsy, laryngeal web |
| **Expiratory** | Trachea/bronchi | Tracheomalacia, vascular ring, tracheal foreign body |

- **Laryngomalacia**: Most common cause of stridor in infants (<6 weeks onset); inspiratory stridor worst in supine/feeding; usually resolves by 2 years; omega-shaped epiglottis on laryngoscopy

### Laryngeal Carcinoma

| Type | Frequency | Lymph Node Spread | Notes |
|------|-----------|------------------|-------|
| **Glottic** | **Most common** (60%) | **Late** (no lymphatics in true VF) | Best prognosis; early hoarseness |
| **Supraglottic** | 35% | **Early** | Late symptoms (dysphagia, foreign body sensation) |
| **Subglottic** | 5% | Early (bilateral) | Rare; worst prognosis; late hoarseness |

- **T classification**: T1 = single subsite, mobile VF; T2 = adjacent subsite, impaired mobility; T3 = fixed VF/paraglottic/inner cortex; T4 = through cartilage/extralaryngeal

**Treatment:**
- Early glottic (T1/T2): **Radiotherapy** (voice-preserving) OR **endoscopic laser excision**
- Advanced glottic/supraglottic (T3/T4): **Total laryngectomy** + neck dissection ± chemoradiation
- Larynx-preserving protocol: Induction chemotherapy + radiation for some T3 lesions

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Glottic = most common + best prognosis; early hoarseness = glottic; supraglottic = early nodal spread

## Head and Neck

### Neck Swellings

**Midline Neck Swellings:**
- **Thyroglossal cyst**: Most common midline neck swelling; moves with swallowing AND tongue protrusion; **Sistrunk's operation** (cyst + body of hyoid + tract)
- **Dermoid cyst**: Does not move with swallowing or tongue; doughy consistency
- **Submental lymph nodes**
- **Delphian lymph node**: Above isthmus of thyroid — metastatic thyroid/laryngeal carcinoma

**Lateral Neck Swellings:**
- **Branchial cyst**: Age 15-30; anterior to SCM at junction of upper 1/3 and lower 2/3 (medial to SCM, lateral to carotid); contains **cholesterol crystals** — from 2nd branchial cleft; translucent; smooth
- **Cystic hygroma (lymphatic malformation)**: **Children (<2 years)**; transilluminate brilliantly; most common in posterior triangle; soft, compressible, trans-illuminable
- **Carotid body tumor (paraganglioma/chemodectoma)**: Lateral carotid bifurcation; transmitted pulsation; **lyre sign on angiography**; moves horizontally but not vertically (Fontaine's sign)

### Lymph Node Levels (Modified AJCC)
| Level | Location |
|-------|---------|
| I | Submental (IA) + Submandibular (IB) |
| II | Upper jugular (above hyoid) |
| III | Middle jugular (hyoid to cricoid) |
| IV | Lower jugular (cricoid to clavicle) |
| V | Posterior triangle |
| VI | Central compartment (prelaryngeal, pretracheal, paratracheal) |
| VII | Superior mediastinal |

### Parotid Gland

**Tumors:**
| Type | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **Pleomorphic adenoma** (mixed parotid tumor) | **Most common benign** parotid tumor; slow-growing; risk of malignant transformation (carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma); treatment: superficial parotidectomy |
| **Warthin's tumor** (papillary cystadenoma lymphomatosum) | 2nd most common benign; **bilateral 10-15%**; **hot scan** (Tc-99m pertechnetate); in elderly males; smoking associated |
| **Mucoepidermoid carcinoma** | **Most common malignant** parotid tumor |
| Adenoid cystic carcinoma | Perineural invasion; cribriform pattern |

- **Frey's syndrome (auriculotemporal syndrome)**: After parotidectomy — sweating and flushing over cheek during eating (aberrant reinnervation of sweat glands by parasympathetic auriculotemporal nerve fibers)

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Thyroglossal cyst moves with tongue protrusion; Sistrunk's operation; pleomorphic adenoma most common benign parotid; Warthin's = hot scan; Frey's syndrome

## Tracheostomy

### Indications
- **Airway obstruction**: Upper airway obstruction (bilateral VF paralysis, epiglottitis, Ludwig's angina, trauma, tumor)
- **Prolonged ventilation**: ICU patients requiring long-term mechanical ventilation (>10-14 days)
- **Secretion management**: Poor cough, inability to clear secretions
- **Respiratory insufficiency**: Neuromuscular disease

### Procedure
- **Level**: Between **3rd and 4th tracheal rings** (avoid 1st ring → subglottic stenosis; avoid 5th+ → erosion of innominate artery)
- **Incision**: Transverse or vertical skin incision; vertical tracheal incision (adults) or flap in children (Bjork flap — prevents emergency decannulation complication)

### Types
- **Standard surgical tracheostomy**: Under GA/LA; safest
- **Percutaneous dilatational tracheostomy (PDT)**: ICU setting; Ciaglia/Griggs technique; bronchoscope guidance

### Tracheostomy Tubes
- Inner tube + outer tube + obturator + pilot balloon (cuffed)
- **Cuffed**: For ventilated patients; prevents aspiration; **High volume low pressure cuff** preferred (reduces tracheal wall ischemia)
- **Uncuffed**: For speaking; non-ventilated
- **Fenestrated**: Allows vocalization (finger occlusion or speaking valve)
- **Passy Muir valve**: One-way speaking valve

### Complications
| Timing | Complication |
|--------|-------------|
| Immediate | Hemorrhage, false passage, esophageal insertion, pneumothorax, apnea |
| Early (days) | Tube displacement/obstruction, surgical emphysema, infection |
| Late (weeks+) | **Tracheal stenosis** (most common late complication), tracheomalacia, tracheoinnominate artery fistula (massive hemorrhage), tracheo-oesophageal fistula, dysphagia |

### Decannulation Criteria
- Underlying reason for tracheostomy resolved
- Able to manage secretions, cough effectively
- Adequate oxygenation on room air
- Fenestrated tube tolerated well
- Step: Downsize tube → fenestrated tube → capping trials → decannulation

> **PYQ HOTSPOT**: Tracheostomy at 3rd-4th rings; tracheal stenosis = most common late complication; innominate artery fistula = life-threatening
