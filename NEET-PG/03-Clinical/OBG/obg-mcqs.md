## OBSTETRICS (Q1–Q15)

**Q1. A 28-year-old primigravida at 12 weeks gestation presents for her first antenatal visit. Which investigation is NOT routinely recommended at this visit?**
- A) Blood group and Rh typing
- B) VDRL
- C) Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT)
- D) HIV testing

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

OGTT is performed at 24–28 weeks gestation (or earlier if risk factors are present), not at the first antenatal visit. Routine first-visit investigations include blood group/Rh typing, VDRL, HIV, hemoglobin, urine routine, and rubella serology. The key fact: OGTT is a second-trimester screening test for gestational diabetes.

</details>

**Q2. A 32-year-old woman at 38 weeks gestation is in active labor. Her cervix is 6 cm dilated, fully effaced, and the fetal head is at 0 station. Contractions occur every 3 minutes lasting 50 seconds. Two hours later, cervical examination shows 6 cm dilation unchanged. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Latent phase arrest
- B) Active phase arrest (arrest of active phase)
- C) Prolonged latent phase
- D) Secondary arrest of descent

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Active phase arrest is defined as no cervical change for ≥4 hours with adequate contractions (or ≥6 hours with inadequate contractions). However, in the active phase (≥6 cm by current ACOG criteria), failure to progress for 2 hours with adequate uterine activity is termed arrest of active phase. The scenario describes a woman already in active labor with no dilation change — this is active phase arrest, not latent phase arrest (which occurs before 6 cm).

</details>

**Q3. A Rh-negative woman delivers a Rh-positive baby. Anti-D immunoglobulin prophylaxis should be given within:**
- A) 24 hours of delivery
- B) 48 hours of delivery
- C) 72 hours of delivery
- D) 7 days of delivery

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Anti-D immunoglobulin must be administered within 72 hours of delivery to prevent Rh isoimmunization. This is the standard window; administration beyond 72 hours is significantly less effective. The dose is 300 mcg IM. The one-liner: Anti-D within 72 hours of any sensitizing event in Rh-negative women.

</details>

**Q4. A 26-year-old primigravida at 34 weeks presents with sudden, painless, bright red vaginal bleeding. No uterine tenderness. Fetal heart rate is normal. The most appropriate next step is:**
- A) Immediate vaginal examination
- B) Immediate cesarean section
- C) Ultrasound examination
- D) Coagulation profile

<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The clinical picture — painless, bright red antepartum hemorrhage with no uterine tenderness — is classic for placenta previa. The most appropriate next step is ultrasound to confirm placental location. Vaginal examination is absolutely contraindicated until placenta previa is ruled out ("never do a vaginal examination before ultrasound in APH"). Cesarean section is not indicated immediately without confirming the diagnosis.

**Q5. A 30-year-old multigravida at 30 weeks gestation presents with severe abdominal pain, uterine tenderness, and dark vaginal bleeding. Fetal heart rate shows a sinusoidal pattern. Blood pressure is 150/100 mmHg. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Placenta previa
- B) Vasa previa
- C) Abruptio placentae
- D) Uterine rupture

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Abruptio placentae presents with painful, dark-red vaginal bleeding, a woody-hard (board-like) tender uterus, and fetal distress. It is associated with hypertension, trauma, and cocaine use. The key distinction from placenta previa: placenta previa is painless with bright red blood; abruption is painful with dark blood and uterine tenderness. Couvelaire uterus (uteroplacental apoplexy) is the pathognomonic finding on gross examination.

</details>

**Q6. A 24-year-old primigravida at 36 weeks presents with blood pressure 160/110 mmHg, severe headache, visual disturbances, and 3+ proteinuria. She has no seizures. The drug of choice to prevent seizures in this patient is:**
- A) Diazepam
- B) Phenytoin
- C) Magnesium sulfate
- D) Phenobarbitone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Magnesium sulfate (MgSO4) is the drug of choice for both prophylaxis and treatment of eclamptic seizures. It is superior to diazepam and phenytoin (shown in the Magpie Trial). The Pritchard regimen (4g IV loading + 10g IM) or Zuspan regimen (4g IV + infusion) are used. The one-liner: MgSO4 is the anticonvulsant of choice in pre-eclampsia/eclampsia — not a CNS depressant but acts by blocking NMDA receptors and causing vasodilation.

</details>

**Q7. A woman is in labor. Cardiotocography shows late decelerations with every contraction, baseline FHR of 145 bpm, and reduced variability. This pattern indicates:**
- A) Head compression
- B) Umbilical cord compression
- C) Uteroplacental insufficiency
- D) Fetal sleep cycle

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Late decelerations (onset after the peak of contraction, nadir after contraction peak) indicate uteroplacental insufficiency and fetal hypoxia — this is an ominous sign. Early decelerations (mirror image of contractions) are caused by head compression and are benign. Variable decelerations are caused by cord compression. The one-liner: Late decelerations = uteroplacental insufficiency = fetal distress.

</details>

**Q8. A 29-year-old woman at 32 weeks gestation presents with preterm labor. She is given tocolytics to delay delivery. Which tocolytic is contraindicated in a patient with gestational diabetes?**
- A) Nifedipine
- B) Indomethacin
- C) Ritodrine (beta-2 agonist)
- D) Atosiban

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Beta-2 agonists (ritodrine, terbutaline) cause hyperglycemia and hypokalemia, making them contraindicated in gestational diabetes and cardiac disease. Nifedipine (calcium channel blocker) is the most commonly used first-line tocolytic today. Atosiban (oxytocin receptor antagonist) has fewer side effects. Indomethacin is used before 32 weeks (risk of premature closure of ductus arteriosus after 32 weeks). Key fact: Beta-agonists are contraindicated in diabetes due to glycogenolysis.

</details>

**Q9. A 35-year-old woman with type 1 diabetes is pregnant. The best indicator of diabetic control during pregnancy is:**
- A) Fasting blood glucose
- B) HbA1c
- C) Fructosamine
- D) Postprandial blood glucose

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Fructosamine reflects glycemic control over the past 2–3 weeks (vs. HbA1c which reflects 8–12 weeks). During pregnancy, HbA1c is unreliable because of increased red cell turnover and physiological dilutional anemia causing lower HbA1c values. Fructosamine is the preferred marker for monitoring glycemic control in pregnancy. The one-liner: Fructosamine = 2–3 week glycemic marker, preferred in pregnancy over HbA1c.

</details>

- A) Ergometrine

<details>

The one-liner: Carboprost is the drug of choice for PPH not responding to oxytocin — most potent uterotonic.

**Q11. A 22-year-old primigravida delivers a healthy baby. On day 3 postpartum, she develops fever (38.5°C), breast engorgement, and hardness of both breasts. The most appropriate management is:**
- A) Stop breastfeeding and apply ice packs
- B) Continue breastfeeding, warm compresses, and analgesics
- C) Antibiotics and stop breastfeeding
- D) Bromocriptine to suppress lactation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Day 3 postpartum fever with bilateral breast engorgement is physiological milk fever — not infective mastitis. Management is to continue breastfeeding (or express milk), apply warm compresses, and give analgesics. Stopping breastfeeding worsens engorgement. Infective mastitis (usually after day 10, unilateral, with Staph aureus) requires antibiotics (cloxacillin/flucloxacillin) while continuing breastfeeding. Key distinction: bilateral engorgement on day 3 = milk fever; unilateral after day 10 = infective mastitis.

</details>

**Q12. A 31-year-old woman at 39 weeks is in labor. The presenting part descends but the fetal head does not rotate from the occiput posterior position. The most common complication of persistent occiput posterior (OP) position is:**
- A) Cord prolapse
- B) Prolonged labor
- C) Face presentation
- D) Placental abruption

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Persistent occiput posterior (OP) position causes prolonged labor because the occipitofrontal diameter (11.5 cm) presents instead of the smaller suboccipitobregmatic diameter. It leads to arrest of descent, increased operative delivery rates, and perineal tears. The one-liner: OP position = "face to pubis" delivery, associated with prolonged labor and increased instrumental delivery.

</details>

Intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy (obstetric cholestasis) presents with pruritus (especially palms and soles) without a primary skin rash, elevated serum bile acids (>10 μmol/L), and mildly elevated transaminases. **Q16. A 16-year-old girl presents with primary amenorrhea. Examination shows normal secondary sexual characteristics but no uterus on imaging. Karyotype is 46,XX. The most likely diagnosis is:**
- A) Turner syndrome
- B) Androgen insensitivity syndrome
- C) Mayer-Rokitansky-Küster-Hauser (MRKH) syndrome
- D) Kallmann syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

MRKH syndrome (Müllerian agenesis) presents with primary amenorrhea, normal secondary sexual characteristics (ovaries are functional, estrogen levels normal), absent uterus and upper vagina, and 46,XX karyotype. The one-liner: Primary amenorrhea + normal secondary sex characters + absent uterus + 46,XX = MRKH syndrome.

</details>

## ANTEPARTUM HEMORRHAGE (Q31–Q35)

**Q31. A 32-year-old multigravida at 36 weeks presents with sudden painless bright red vaginal bleeding. There is no uterine tenderness and the fetal heart rate is normal. Ultrasound confirms a central (major) placenta previa. She has had 3 previous cesarean sections. The most feared complication in this patient is:**
- A) Cord prolapse
- B) Placenta accreta spectrum (morbidly adherent placenta)
- C) Uterine rupture
- D) Vasa previa

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Placenta previa + previous uterine scars (cesarean sections) dramatically increases the risk of placenta accreta spectrum (PAS) — abnormal adherence of the placenta (accreta → increta → percreta). With 3 previous CS and anterior placenta previa, the risk of placenta accreta is >60%. PAS can cause catastrophic hemorrhage at delivery. Planned cesarean hysterectomy (without attempting placental removal) by an experienced team is the safest approach. The one-liner: placenta previa + previous CS scars = placenta accreta spectrum — the most dangerous combination in obstetrics.

</details>

**Q32. A 28-year-old woman at 34 weeks gestation with severe preeclampsia suddenly develops severe abdominal pain, a "woody hard" uterus, and dark vaginal bleeding. Fetal HR shows persistent late decelerations. Clotting profile shows fibrinogen 80 mg/dL and D-dimer markedly elevated. The complication responsible for the clotting abnormality is:**
- A) Placenta previa causing consumptive coagulopathy
- B) Abruptio placentae causing disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
- C) HELLP syndrome alone
- D) Antiphospholipid syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Abruptio placentae (retroplacental hemorrhage) classically presents with sudden severe abdominal pain, tender woody-hard uterus ("board-like rigidity"), and dark/concealed bleeding. Large abruption releases thromboplastin from the placenta, triggering DIC (low fibrinogen, elevated D-dimer, prolonged PT/aPTT). Management: immediate delivery (cesarean if fetal distress + unfavorable cervix), correct coagulopathy with FFP/cryoprecipitate. The one-liner: abruption = painful + tense uterus + dark blood ± DIC; previa = painless + bright red blood.

</details>

**Q35. In a case of vasa previa, what distinguishes it from placenta previa on clinical examination?**
- A) Vasa previa presents with painful bleeding and uterine tenderness
- B) Fetal blood is present in the vaginal blood (Apt test positive), and fetal bradycardia follows ROM
- C) Vasa previa is confirmed by transabdominal Doppler ultrasound in the first trimester
- D) Vasa previa is associated with maternal coagulopathy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Vasa previa — fetal vessels run over the internal os unsupported by placenta or umbilical cord — presents with painless bleeding on rupture of membranes (ROM) that is FETAL blood (not maternal). The Apt test (alkali denaturation) distinguishes fetal from maternal hemoglobin (fetal Hb resists alkalization → stays pink). Fetal bradycardia or sinusoidal pattern follows because the bleeding is from the fetus. It requires emergency cesarean section. The one-liner: vasa previa = bleeding at ROM + fetal bradycardia + Apt test + = fetal blood → emergency CS (neonatal outcome depends on speed of delivery).

</details>

The diagnosis is severe preeclampsia. The first priority before initiating antihypertensive therapy is:**
- A) Arrange emergency cesarean section

**Q41. A 25-year-old woman requests insertion of a copper IUD for long-term contraception. She has never been pregnant and has no history of PID. The copper IUD (Cu-T 380A) has a duration of effectiveness of:**
- A) 3 years
- B) 5 years
- C) 10 years
- D) 3 months

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The Cu-T 380A (Paragard) is effective for up to 10 years (some data suggest up to 12 years). It works by copper ions impairing sperm motility and viability, and preventing fertilization. It does NOT prevent ovulation. Key advantage: immediately effective, no hormonal side effects, can also be used as emergency contraception up to 5 days. The one-liner: Cu-T 380A = most effective long-term reversible contraception, works for 10 years, can be used for emergency contraception within 120 hours.

</details>

**Q42. A 30-year-old woman on combined oral contraceptive pills (COCPs) asks about failure risk. She takes the pill regularly. The mechanism by which OCPs prevent pregnancy includes all of the following EXCEPT:**
- A) Suppression of ovulation (primary mechanism)
- B) Thickening of cervical mucus
- C) Rendering endometrium unfavorable for implantation
- D) Inhibiting sperm motility in the vas deferens

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

OCPs exert contraceptive effect through: (1) suppression of ovulation via inhibition of LH surge (primary mechanism — estrogen + progesterone suppress hypothalamic GnRH → pituitary LH/FSH), (2) thickening cervical mucus (prevents sperm penetration), and (3) endometrial thinning (prevents implantation). They do NOT act on the vas deferens (a male structure). The one-liner: OCP mechanism = suppress ovulation (primary) + thick cervical mucus + thin endometrium.

</details>

**Q51. A 28-year-old primigravida at 40 weeks is in active labor. Cervix is 8 cm dilated, fully effaced. On CTG, variable decelerations are noted. <summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Cord prolapse is an obstetric emergency requiring immediate cord decompression.
