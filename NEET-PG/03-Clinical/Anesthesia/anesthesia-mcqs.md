## INTRAVENOUS ANESTHETIC AGENTS (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 45-year-old man is scheduled for elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy. The anesthesiologist plans total intravenous anesthesia (TIVA). Which agent is most appropriate, and what is an additional benefit it provides in the postoperative period?**
- A) Ketamine — provides postoperative analgesia
- B) Propofol — has intrinsic antiemetic properties
- C) Thiopentone — short duration allows rapid emergence
- D) Etomidate — maintains cardiovascular stability

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Propofol is the agent of choice for TIVA and has intrinsic antiemetic properties, making it particularly useful for laparoscopic procedures (high PONV risk). Ketamine causes emergence delirium and increases ICP/IOP. Thiopentone is ultra-short acting but has no antiemetic effect. Etomidate causes adrenal suppression with infusion.

</details>

---

**Q2. A 28-year-old patient with severe peanut allergy is posted for emergency surgery. The anesthesiologist should avoid which of the following induction agents?**
- A) Ketamine
- B) Etomidate
- C) Propofol
- D) Thiopentone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Propofol is formulated in a lipid emulsion containing soybean oil and egg lecithin — it is contraindicated in patients with egg or soy allergy. Peanut allergy carries cross-reactivity risk with soy. Ketamine, etomidate, and thiopentone do not share this contraindication.

</details>

---

**Q3. A 32-year-old trauma patient arrives in hemorrhagic shock with BP 70/40 mmHg. He requires emergency intubation. Which induction agent is the drug of choice?**
- A) Propofol
- B) Thiopentone
- C) Ketamine
- D) Etomidate

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Ketamine is the drug of choice for induction in hemorrhagic shock because it stimulates sympathetic outflow, maintaining blood pressure and heart rate. Propofol and thiopentone are potent vasodilators that worsen hypotension. Etomidate is the DOC for cardiovascular-compromised patients (not hemorrhagic shock), as it causes adrenal suppression.

</details>

**Q4. A 55-year-old patient with known ischemic heart disease requires urgent surgery. The anesthesiologist chooses etomidate for induction. Which of the following is the most important adverse effect if this agent is used as a continuous infusion in ICU?**
- A) Malignant hyperthermia
- B) Adrenocortical suppression
- C) Propofol infusion syndrome
- D) Emergence delirium

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Etomidate inhibits 11β-hydroxylase, causing adrenocortical suppression — this is clinically significant with continuous infusion (as used for ICU sedation) and is the reason it is not recommended for prolonged sedation. Propofol infusion syndrome occurs with prolonged high-dose propofol. Malignant hyperthermia is triggered by volatile agents and succinylcholine.

</details>

**Q5. A 25-year-old patient is given midazolam premedication before surgery. He develops respiratory depression and excessive sedation in the preoperative area. Which drug should be administered to reverse its effects?**
- A) Naloxone
- B) Neostigmine
- C) Flumazenil
- D) Physostigmine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Flumazenil is the specific competitive antagonist of benzodiazepines at the GABA-A receptor. Naloxone reverses opioids. Neostigmine reverses non-depolarizing neuromuscular blockers. Note: flumazenil has a shorter half-life than midazolam — re-sedation can occur, requiring repeat dosing.

</details>

## INHALATIONAL AGENTS (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A 35-year-old woman undergoes her second surgery under halothane anesthesia within 3 months. Two weeks postoperatively she develops jaundice, fever, and elevated transaminases. What is the most likely diagnosis?**
- A) Acute viral hepatitis A
- B) Halothane hepatitis
- C) Cholestatic jaundice from anesthesia
- D) Isoflurane hepatotoxicity

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Halothane hepatitis occurs on repeated exposure due to formation of trifluoroacetyl hapten triggering immune-mediated hepatocellular necrosis — risk increases with each re-exposure. Isoflurane is the least metabolized volatile agent and has minimal hepatotoxic potential. This is a classic NEET PG scenario: halothane + repeated exposure + hepatitis.

</details>

---

**Q7. During a routine appendectomy under general anesthesia, the patient develops sudden hyperthermia (40.2°C), generalized muscle rigidity, tachycardia, and a rapid rise in end-tidal CO2. The triggering agents in this condition act via which receptor?**
- A) NMDA receptor
- B) Ryanodine receptor (RYR1)
- C) Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor
- D) GABA-A receptor

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Malignant hyperthermia is triggered by volatile anesthetic agents (halothane, isoflurane, sevoflurane, desflurane) and succinylcholine acting on the mutant RYR1 (ryanodine receptor 1) — causing uncontrolled calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Treatment is dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV, which blocks this receptor. Halothane is the most potent trigger among volatile agents.

</details>

**Q8. A 6-year-old child is scheduled for elective tonsillectomy. The anesthesiologist prefers inhalational induction. Which volatile agent is most appropriate, and why?**
- A) Halothane — most potent volatile agent
- B) Isoflurane — fastest onset and offset
- C) Sevoflurane — pleasant odor, non-irritant to airways
- D) Desflurane — highest potency, fastest induction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Sevoflurane is the agent of choice for inhalational induction in children due to its pleasant, non-pungent odor and non-irritant effect on airways. Desflurane and isoflurane have pungent odors causing airway irritation, breath-holding, and laryngospasm — they are not suitable for inhalational induction. Halothane is no longer used due to hepatotoxicity risk.

</details>

**Q9. At the end of a 4-hour surgery, the anesthesiologist turns off nitrous oxide and administers 100% oxygen. The patient develops hypoxemia. What is the most likely mechanism?**
- A) Decreased hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction
- B) Diffusion hypoxia (Fink effect)
- C) Bronchospasm from extubation
- D) Pulmonary embolism

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Diffusion hypoxia (Fink effect) occurs when large volumes of nitrous oxide rapidly diffuse out of blood into alveoli on discontinuation, diluting alveolar oxygen — hence 100% O2 must be given for at least 5 minutes at the end of N2O anesthesia. N2O also expands closed air spaces and is contraindicated in pneumothorax, bowel obstruction, and middle ear surgery.

</details>

**Q10. A patient with known coronary artery disease is undergoing CABG surgery. The anesthesiologist avoids isoflurane. What is the specific concern with isoflurane in this patient?**
- A) It causes malignant hyperthermia
- B) It causes coronary steal phenomenon
- C) It triggers severe bronchospasm
- D) It is the most metabolized volatile agent

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Isoflurane causes coronary steal phenomenon — preferential vasodilation of normal coronary vessels diverts blood away from stenotic vessels, worsening ischemia in CAD patients. It is also the least metabolized volatile agent (0.2%). Halothane is the most metabolized (20%). Isoflurane's pungent odor also prevents its use for inhalational induction.

</details>

## LOCAL ANESTHETICS (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. Which sequence correctly represents the order in which nerve fibers are blocked when a local anesthetic is administered?**
- A) Motor fibers first, then pain fibers
- B) Large myelinated fibers first, then unmyelinated
- C) B > C > Aδ > Aβ > Aγ > Aα (pain before motor)
- D) Aα first (largest diameter, blocked first)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Local anesthetics block small, lightly myelinated fibers first — B fibers (preganglionic autonomic) are most sensitive, followed by C (pain/temperature), then Aδ (pain/cold), then larger fibers. Aα (motor, largest) are blocked last. Clinically: patients lose pain before motor function — this is the basis of "differential block" in obstetric epidurals.

</details>

---

**Q12. A 40-year-old man undergoes repair of a facial laceration under local anesthesia with lignocaine. The maximum safe dose of plain lignocaine (without adrenaline) is:**
- A) 1 mg/kg
- B) 3 mg/kg
- C) 7 mg/kg
- D) 10 mg/kg

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Plain lignocaine maximum dose is 3 mg/kg; with adrenaline (epinephrine), vasoconstriction reduces systemic absorption, allowing up to 7 mg/kg. Adrenaline prolongs duration and reduces toxicity risk. Lignocaine toxicity causes CNS effects (perioral tingling, seizures) followed by cardiovascular collapse — treatment is IV lipid emulsion 20%.

</details>

---

**Q13. A 30-year-old parturient receives epidural bupivacaine. She inadvertently receives an intravascular bolus and develops ventricular fibrillation. What is the treatment of choice for bupivacaine toxicity?**
- A) IV amiodarone 300 mg
- B) IV lipid emulsion 20% (Intralipid)
- C) IV sodium bicarbonate
- D) DC cardioversion alone

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic local anesthetic — it causes severe ventricular arrhythmias (including VF) due to its high lipid solubility and slow dissociation from Na+ channels ("fast-in, slow-out"). IV lipid emulsion 20% (Intralipid) acts as a "lipid sink," sequestering bupivacaine away from cardiac sodium channels. This applies to all LA toxicity.

</details>

---

**Q14. A patient undergoing nasal polypectomy is given topical cocaine solution. Apart from local anesthesia, cocaine has a unique property among local anesthetics. What is it?**
- A) It is an amide-type local anesthetic
- B) It causes vasodilation, reducing surgical bleeding
- C) It causes vasoconstriction via catecholamine reuptake inhibition
- D) It is the only LA safe in patients with porphyria

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Cocaine is the only local anesthetic that causes vasoconstriction — by inhibiting catecholamine reuptake (norepinephrine) at sympathetic nerve terminals. This property makes it ideal for nasal surgery (hemostasis + anesthesia combined). It is an ester-type LA. All other LAs cause vasodilation (except cocaine), which is why adrenaline is often added to prolong their action.

</details>

**Q15. EMLA cream is applied to a child's skin before venipuncture. The child's mother is worried about a reported side effect. Which component of EMLA cream is responsible for methaemoglobinaemia?**
- A) Lignocaine
- B) Prilocaine
- C) Benzocaine
- D) Tetracaine

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

EMLA (Eutectic Mixture of Local Anesthetics) contains lignocaine 2.5% + prilocaine 2.5%. Prilocaine is metabolized to o-toluidine, which oxidizes hemoglobin to methemoglobin — this is especially relevant in neonates (reduced methemoglobin reductase). Benzocaine (ester LA) also causes methemoglobinemia. Treatment of methemoglobinemia is IV methylene blue 1–2 mg/kg.

</details>

## NEUROMUSCULAR BLOCKING AGENTS (Q16–Q20)

**Q16. A 25-year-old burn patient from 3 weeks ago requires re-look surgery. The anesthesiologist should avoid which neuromuscular blocking agent, and why?**
- A) Vecuronium — causes histamine release
- B) Succinylcholine — risk of life-threatening hyperkalemia
- C) Atracurium — Hofmann elimination impaired in burns
- D) Rocuronium — fastest onset among non-depolarizing agents

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Succinylcholine causes massive hyperkalemia (serum K+ can rise to 10–14 mEq/L, causing cardiac arrest) in burns, crush injuries, denervation injuries, and myopathies — due to upregulation and spread of extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors releasing K+ on depolarization. This risk begins 24–48 hours post-injury and persists for up to 2 years. Safe alternative: rocuronium.

</details>

---

**Q17. A patient given succinylcholine for rapid sequence intubation remains apneic for 3 hours postoperatively. Investigations reveal the cause. What is the most likely underlying condition?**
- A) Malignant hyperthermia susceptibility
- B) Pseudocholinesterase (plasma cholinesterase) deficiency
- C) Myasthenia gravis
- D) Phase I block converting to Phase II block

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Suxamethonium (succinylcholine) is metabolized by plasma pseudocholinesterase — deficiency (genetic dibucaine-resistant enzyme variant) prolongs paralysis from the normal 5–10 minutes to hours. Dibucaine number: normal = 80, heterozygous = 60, homozygous abnormal = 20. Management: ventilate until recovery, fresh frozen plasma provides pseudocholinesterase.

</details>

---

**Q18. Which neuromuscular blocking agent undergoes Hofmann elimination and is therefore safe in patients with combined renal and hepatic failure?**
- A) Vecuronium
- B) Pancuronium
- C) Atracurium
- D) Rocuronium

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Atracurium undergoes Hofmann elimination — spontaneous non-enzymatic degradation at physiological pH and temperature, independent of renal or hepatic function. It also releases histamine (use slowly). Vecuronium has biliary excretion. Pancuronium and rocuronium depend on renal/hepatic clearance. Atracurium (and cisatracurium) are the safest NMBAs in organ failure.

</details>

---

**Q19. A patient received rocuronium for intubation. At end of surgery, the anesthesiologist wants complete reversal of neuromuscular blockade without waiting for spontaneous recovery. Which reversal agent is most appropriate?**
- A) Neostigmine + glycopyrrolate
- B) Edrophonium + atropine
- C) Sugammadex
- D) Flumazenil

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Sugammadex is a modified γ-cyclodextrin that encapsulates rocuronium (and vecuronium) in a 1:1 complex, providing complete reversal even from deep block — it does not require waiting for spontaneous recovery. Neostigmine (anticholinesterase) only reverses non-depolarizing NMBAs at moderate block and requires atropine/glycopyrrolate to block muscarinic effects. Sugammadex does NOT reverse atracurium or succinylcholine.

</details>

**Q20. During train-of-four (TOF) monitoring after succinylcholine administration, the anesthesiologist notices all 4 twitches are equally reduced (no fade). After repeated doses, fade appears. What has occurred?**
- A) Residual non-depolarizing block from vecuronium
- B) Development of Phase II block from succinylcholine
- C) Inadequate reversal with neostigmine
- D) Malignant hyperthermia affecting neuromuscular junction

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Phase I block (depolarizing) from succinylcholine shows NO fade on TOF — all twitches equally reduced, not reversed by neostigmine. With repeated doses or prolonged infusion, Phase II block (desensitization block) develops, which DOES show fade on TOF and IS reversed by neostigmine — it behaves like non-depolarizing block. This is an important distinction tested in NEET PG.

</details>

## REGIONAL ANESTHESIA (Q21–Q25)

**Q21. A 28-year-old parturient receives spinal anesthesia for elective LSCS. Two days later she complains of severe positional headache — worse on standing, relieved on lying down. What is the treatment of choice?**
- A) IV dexamethasone + bed rest
- B) Oral sumatriptan
- C) Epidural blood patch
- D) IV caffeine infusion

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Post-dural puncture headache (PDPH) — positional headache from CSF leak through the dural puncture site causing intracranial hypotension. Epidural blood patch (10–20 mL autologous blood injected epidurally) is the definitive treatment with ~90% success. Conservative management (bed rest, IV fluids, caffeine) is first-line for mild PDPH; blood patch for severe or refractory cases.

</details>

**Q22. Immediately after spinal anesthesia for a lower limb surgery, a patient develops BP 80/50 mmHg and feels dizzy. What is the first-line pharmacological treatment for hypotension following spinal anesthesia in an obstetric patient?**
- A) Epinephrine IV
- B) Dopamine IV infusion
- C) Phenylephrine or ephedrine
- D) Norepinephrine infusion

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Hypotension after spinal anesthesia results from sympathetic block causing vasodilation — treatment: IV crystalloid preloading/co-loading + vasopressors. Phenylephrine (pure α-agonist) is preferred in obstetrics as it maintains uteroplacental blood flow without fetal tachycardia. Ephedrine (mixed α/β agonist) is used if bradycardia accompanies hypotension. Spinal anesthesia is performed below L1–L2 (conus medullaris level in adults).

</details>

**Q23. A 70-year-old patient develops cauda equina syndrome after spinal anesthesia. Which feature distinguishes this complication from spinal cord injury?**
- A) Spastic paraplegia with hyperreflexia
- B) Flaccid paraplegia with bladder/bowel dysfunction and saddle anesthesia
- C) Hemisensory loss with ipsilateral motor weakness
- D) Upper motor neuron signs with Babinski positive

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Cauda equina syndrome from spinal anesthesia (concentrated local anesthetic neurotoxicity below L1–L2) presents with flaccid lower limb paralysis, bladder/bowel dysfunction, and saddle anesthesia (S3–S5 dermatomal loss) — lower motor neuron pattern. Spastic paraplegia with hyperreflexia indicates upper motor neuron (spinal cord) injury. Risk factors: hyperbaric lidocaine 5%, microcatheters.

</details>

**Q24. A patient requires brachial plexus block for shoulder arthroscopy. Which approach is preferred, and what is its most important complication?**
- A) Axillary approach — risk of pneumothorax
- B) Supraclavicular approach — most complete block, risk of pneumothorax
- C) Interscalene approach — phrenic nerve block (100% incidence)
- D) Infraclavicular approach — musculocutaneous nerve spared

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Interscalene block is the approach of choice for shoulder surgery (targets C5–C6, upper trunk) but causes ipsilateral phrenic nerve block in nearly 100% of cases — contraindicated in contralateral phrenic nerve palsy, severe COPD, or contralateral pneumonectomy. Supraclavicular gives the most complete brachial plexus block but has highest pneumothorax risk. Axillary approach misses musculocutaneous nerve.

</details>

**Q25. For a patient undergoing Pfannenstiel incision for an ovarian cystectomy, what is the minimum spinal block level required?**
- A) T10 (umbilicus level)
- B) T6 (xiphisternum level)
- C) T4 (nipple level)
- D) L1 (inguinal ligament level)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Spinal dermatome landmarks for NEET PG: T4 = nipple line, T6 = xiphisternum, T10 = umbilicus, L1 = inguinal ligament. A Pfannenstiel incision is made just above the pubic symphysis (below umbilicus) — T10 level block is sufficient. For LSCS, T6 block is standard (peritoneal traction causes referred pain to T6 level). T4 block is required for upper abdominal surgery.

</details>

## ANESTHETIC EMERGENCIES AND MONITORING (Q26–Q30)

**Q26. During induction of anesthesia with halothane and succinylcholine in a 20-year-old male, he develops masseter muscle rigidity, temperature of 41°C, metabolic acidosis, and a rapidly rising ETCO2. What is the immediate treatment?**
- A) IV dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg and stop trigger agents
- B) IV succinylcholine to relax the muscle rigidity
- C) Active cooling alone with ice packs
- D) IV propofol infusion to deepen anesthesia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) — autosomal dominant mutation in RYR1 gene causing uncontrolled sarcoplasmic reticulum calcium release. Immediate management: (1) stop all trigger agents (volatile anesthetics + succinylcholine), (2) dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV (blocks RYR1), (3) active cooling, (4) treat acidosis/hyperkalemia, (5) monitor CK. Dantrolene is the ONLY specific treatment. ETCO2 rise is the earliest sign.

</details>

**Q27. During direct laryngoscopy, a Grade IV view is obtained (no glottic structures visible). An LMA is inserted but fails to ventilate adequately. What is the next step in the "Cannot Intubate, Cannot Oxygenate" (CICO) scenario?**
- A) Attempt fibreoptic intubation through the LMA
- B) Perform emergency cricothyrotomy
- C) Increase neuromuscular blockade and retry laryngoscopy
- D) Call for senior help and wait

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

CICO (Cannot Intubate, Cannot Oxygenate) is a life-threatening emergency — emergency front-of-neck access (FONA), specifically surgical cricothyrotomy, is the last resort to establish an airway. The Cormack-Lehane grading: Grade I = full glottis, Grade II = partial glottis, Grade III = epiglottis only, Grade IV = nothing visible. LMA is the primary rescue device; if it fails, surgical airway is mandatory.

</details>

**Q28. A patient under general anesthesia develops sudden hypotension (BP 60/40), tachycardia (HR 140), generalized urticaria, and bronchospasm 5 minutes after a drug is given. What is the most common causative agent in anesthetic anaphylaxis?**
- A) Propofol
- B) Benzodiazepines
- C) Neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBAs)
- D) Volatile anesthetic agents

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

NMBAs (especially succinylcholine and rocuronium) are the most common cause of anaphylaxis during anesthesia, followed by latex and antibiotics. Treatment: (1) adrenaline 0.5 mg IM (or IV 0.1 mg boluses in severe cases), (2) stop causative agent, (3) IV fluids, (4) chlorpheniramine + hydrocortisone (adjuncts — do not replace adrenaline). Adrenaline is the only life-saving drug in anaphylaxis.

</details>

**Q29. An ICU patient reports being fully awake and aware during a surgery performed 6 months ago — they could hear conversations but could not move or communicate. This phenomenon is best prevented by which monitoring parameter?**
- A) End-tidal CO2 (ETCO2)
- B) Bispectral index (BIS) monitoring with target 40–60
- C) Train-of-four monitoring
- D) Pulse oximetry (SpO2)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Awareness under anesthesia is caused by inadequate depth of anesthesia (often due to NMBA masking movement — the primary sign of light anesthesia). BIS (Bispectral Index) monitoring processes the EEG signal to give a numerical value (0–100): target 40–60 for general anesthesia, >60 indicates light anesthesia (awareness risk). TOF monitors neuromuscular block, not depth of anesthesia.

</details>

**Q30. As per ASA monitoring standards, which of the following is mandatory during all general anesthetic procedures?**
- A) Arterial line blood pressure monitoring
- B) Pulmonary artery catheter
- C) End-tidal CO2 (capnography), SpO2, ECG, NIBP, and temperature
- D) Bispectral index monitoring and depth of anesthesia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

ASA basic monitoring standards mandate: (1) SpO2 (pulse oximetry), (2) ETCO2 (capnography — confirms ETT placement, detects disconnection/MH), (3) ECG, (4) NIBP (non-invasive blood pressure), (5) temperature (when clinically significant changes expected). BIS and arterial line are supplemental monitors used selectively. Capnography is the gold standard for confirming endotracheal intubation.

</details>
