## UPPER LIMB (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 35-year-old carpenter is brought to the emergency after falling on an outstretched hand. He is unable to extend his wrist and fingers. Sensation is lost over the dorsum of the hand. The nerve most likely injured is:**
- A) Median nerve
- B) Ulnar nerve
- C) Radial nerve
- D) Musculocutaneous nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The radial nerve is the nerve of extension; its injury causes wrist drop (loss of wrist and finger extension) and sensory loss over the dorsum of the hand. A) Median nerve injury causes loss of opposition of thumb and sensory loss over the lateral 3½ fingers (ape hand). B) Ulnar nerve injury causes claw hand and loss of sensation over medial 1½ fingers. D) Musculocutaneous nerve injury causes loss of forearm flexion and lateral forearm sensation.

</details>

**Q2. During a road traffic accident, a patient sustains injury to the medial cord of the brachial plexus. Which of the following deficits is MOST expected?**
- A) Loss of shoulder abduction
- B) Loss of elbow flexion
- C) Weakness of intrinsic muscles of the hand
- D) Loss of supination of forearm

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

The medial cord of the brachial plexus gives rise to the ulnar nerve and the medial root of the median nerve; damage causes weakness of intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, hypothenar, medial two lumbricals). A) Shoulder abduction depends on the axillary nerve (posterior cord/C5–C6). B) Elbow flexion is via musculocutaneous nerve from the lateral cord. D) Supination is by the radial nerve (posterior cord) and biceps brachii (lateral cord).

</details>

**Q3. A 28-year-old woman presents with pain and tingling in the lateral 3½ fingers that worsens at night. Symptoms are reproduced by sustained wrist flexion. The anatomical structure compressed in this condition is:**
- A) Radial nerve at the spiral groove
- B) Median nerve at the carpal tunnel
- C) Ulnar nerve at Guyon's canal
- D) Anterior interosseous nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Carpal tunnel syndrome is compression of the median nerve under the flexor retinaculum; it presents with nocturnal pain and tingling in the lateral 3½ digits (Phalen's test positive). A) Radial nerve at the spiral groove causes wrist drop. C) Ulnar nerve compression at Guyon's canal causes hypothenar wasting and claw hand. D) Anterior interosseous nerve injury causes loss of pinch grip without sensory loss.

</details>

**Q4. A patient cannot abduct the arm beyond 15°. The initial 15° of abduction is preserved. Which muscle is most likely damaged?**
- A) Deltoid
- B) Supraspinatus
- C) Infraspinatus
- D) Subscapularis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

Deltoid (axillary nerve, C5–C6) is the prime mover for arm abduction beyond 15°; the initial 0–15° is initiated by supraspinatus. If the deltoid is paralyzed, the patient can initiate abduction only up to 15° (by supraspinatus) but cannot continue. B) Supraspinatus initiates the first 15° of abduction. C) Infraspinatus is a lateral rotator. D) Subscapularis is a medial rotator.

</details>

- A) Sciatic nerve

- C) Femoral nerve
- D) Obturator nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The femoral nerve (L2–L4) supplies the quadriceps (knee extension) and gives the saphenous nerve, which provides sensation over the medial leg and foot. D) Obturator nerve injury causes loss of hip adduction.

**Q7. A patient sustains a posterior dislocation of the hip. He presents with a shortened, adducted, and internally rotated limb. Which nerve is most commonly injured in this condition?**
- A) Femoral nerve
- B) Obturator nerve
- C) Sciatic nerve
- D) Superior gluteal nerve

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Posterior hip dislocation most commonly injures the sciatic nerve (specifically the common peroneal division), leading to foot drop and sensory loss below the knee. The sciatic nerve exits through the greater sciatic foramen below piriformis and is vulnerable with posterior displacement of the femoral head. A) Femoral nerve is injured in anterior dislocation. B) Obturator nerve may be injured in obturator hernia, not posterior dislocation. D) Superior gluteal nerve is injured in deep intramuscular injections in the upper medial gluteal quadrant.

</details>

- A) Femoral canal

- C) Superficial inguinal ring only
- D) Obturator foramen

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

An indirect inguinal hernia follows the path of the processus vaginalis — entering through the deep inguinal ring (lateral to inferior epigastric vessels), traversing the inguinal canal, and exiting through the superficial inguinal ring; it lies above and medial to the pubic tubercle. A) Femoral hernia is below and lateral to the pubic tubercle. C) Direct hernia passes only through the superficial ring, not the deep ring. D) Obturator hernia presents with medial thigh pain (Howship–Romberg sign).

</details>
