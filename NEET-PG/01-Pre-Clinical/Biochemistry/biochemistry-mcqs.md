## CARBOHYDRATE METABOLISM (Q1–Q5)

**Q1. A 6-month-old infant presents with hypoglycemia, hepatomegaly, and lactic acidosis after a brief fast. Liver biopsy shows massive glycogen accumulation. Enzyme assay reveals deficiency of glucose-6-phosphatase. The diagnosis is:**
- A) McArdle disease (Type V glycogen storage disease)
- B) Von Gierke disease (Type I glycogen storage disease)
- C) Pompe disease (Type II glycogen storage disease)
- D) Cori disease (Type III glycogen storage disease)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Von Gierke disease (GSD Type I) is caused by deficiency of glucose-6-phosphatase in the liver and kidney, leading to inability to release free glucose from glycogen or gluconeogenesis — causing severe fasting hypoglycemia, hepatomegaly, lactic acidosis, hyperuricemia, and hyperlipidemia. A) McArdle disease affects muscle phosphorylase (exercise-induced cramps, no hypoglycemia). C) Pompe disease is a lysosomal disorder (acid maltase deficiency) with cardiomegaly. D) Cori disease (debranching enzyme deficiency) is milder, with normal lactate.

</details>

**Q2. The irreversible regulatory enzyme unique to glycolysis (not present in gluconeogenesis) is:**
- A) Phosphoglucose isomerase
- B) Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1)
- C) Enolase
- D) Aldolase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

PFK-1 is the key regulatory, irreversible, rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis; it is not present in gluconeogenesis (bypassed by fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase). PFK-1 is allosterically activated by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, and inhibited by ATP and citrate. A) Phosphoglucose isomerase is reversible and present in both pathways. C) Enolase is reversible; it is inhibited by fluoride (used in fluoride-oxalate tubes). D) Aldolase is reversible and functions in both pathways.

</details>

**Q3. A newborn male develops hemolytic anemia on exposure to primaquine (used for malaria prophylaxis). His RBCs are deficient in glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD). Which metabolic pathway is defective?**
- A) Glycolysis
- B) TCA cycle
- C) Hexose monophosphate (HMP) shunt
- D) Gluconeogenesis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

G6PD is the entry enzyme of the HMP shunt (pentose phosphate pathway), generating NADPH, which maintains glutathione in the reduced state to protect RBCs from oxidative damage. G6PD deficiency leaves RBCs unable to regenerate NADPH → oxidative hemolysis on exposure to primaquine, dapsone, or fava beans. A) Glycolysis does not use G6PD. B) TCA cycle is mitochondrial. D) Gluconeogenesis is independent of G6PD.

</details>

It presents when the infant starts milk feeds. A) Galactokinase deficiency causes only cataracts (galactitol accumulates in the lens) — milder disease. **Q5. During starvation, the liver converts acetyl-CoA (from fatty acid oxidation) into ketone bodies. Which enzyme is the rate-limiting step in ketogenesis?**
- A) Acetoacetyl-CoA thiolase
- B) HMG-CoA synthase (mitochondrial)
- C) HMG-CoA reductase
- D) Beta-ketothiolase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Mitochondrial HMG-CoA synthase is the rate-limiting enzyme of ketogenesis; it condenses acetyl-CoA and acetoacetyl-CoA to form HMG-CoA in the mitochondria. This is distinct from cytosolic HMG-CoA reductase, which is the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis. C) HMG-CoA reductase is the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis (cytosol), inhibited by statins. A and D) These enzymes participate in ketogenesis but are not the rate-limiting step.

</details>

## LIPID METABOLISM (Q6–Q10)

**Q6. A pharmaceutical company develops a new drug that inhibits fatty acid synthesis. The drug most likely targets which enzyme?**
- A) Carnitine acyltransferase I
- B) Acetyl-CoA carboxylase
- C) Fatty acid synthase
- D) Malonyl-CoA decarboxylase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) is the rate-limiting enzyme of fatty acid synthesis; it converts acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA (requires biotin as a cofactor). It is activated by insulin and citrate, and inhibited by glucagon, epinephrine, and palmitoyl-CoA (end-product feedback). A) Carnitine acyltransferase I is the rate-limiting step of beta-oxidation, not synthesis. C) Fatty acid synthase is a multifunctional enzyme but not the rate-limiting enzyme. D) Malonyl-CoA decarboxylase converts malonyl-CoA back to acetyl-CoA.

</details>

**Q7. A 50-year-old man is prescribed atorvastatin for hypercholesterolemia. The drug inhibits:**
- A) Pancreatic lipase
- B) HMG-CoA reductase in the liver
- C) Lipoprotein lipase
- D) Cholesterol 7-alpha-hydroxylase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Statins (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase — the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis in the liver. This reduces hepatic cholesterol, upregulates LDL receptors, and decreases plasma LDL. A) Orlistat inhibits pancreatic lipase (used in obesity). C) Gemfibrozil/fibrates activate lipoprotein lipase (lower triglycerides). D) Bile acid sequestrants indirectly upregulate 7-alpha-hydroxylase.

</details>

**Q8. Which lipoprotein is responsible for reverse cholesterol transport — carrying cholesterol from peripheral tissues BACK to the liver?**
- A) Chylomicrons
- B) VLDL
- C) LDL
- D) HDL

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: D**

HDL (high-density lipoprotein) performs reverse cholesterol transport; it accepts free cholesterol from peripheral cells (via ABCA1/ABCG1 transporters), esterifies it (via LCAT), and transports it to the liver for excretion in bile. A) Chylomicrons transport dietary (exogenous) lipids from the gut to tissues. B) VLDL carries endogenous triglycerides from the liver to peripheral tissues. C) LDL delivers cholesterol to peripheral cells (atherogenic lipoprotein).

</details>

**Q9. Which of the following is a key difference between fatty acid SYNTHESIS and beta-oxidation?**
- A) Synthesis uses NADH; beta-oxidation produces FADH2 and NADH
- B) Synthesis occurs in mitochondria; beta-oxidation occurs in cytosol
- C) Synthesis requires NADPH and occurs in the cytosol; beta-oxidation produces NADH/FADH2 and occurs in mitochondria

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Fatty acid synthesis uses NADPH (from HMP shunt) as a reducing agent and occurs in the cytosol. Beta-oxidation occurs in the mitochondrial matrix and produces NADH and FADH2 (feeding the ETC). The two pathways also use D-isomers (beta-oxidation) and L-isomers (synthesis) and are regulated reciprocally. A) This reverses the cofactors. B) This reverses the compartments. D) They use CoA but in different compartments.

</details>

## PROTEIN METABOLISM (Q11–Q15)

**Q11. A 3-month-old infant presents with eczema, fair skin with blonde hair (despite dark-haired parents), musty urine odor, and intellectual disability. Urine ferric chloride test turns olive green. The enzyme deficient is:**
- A) Homogentisate oxidase
- B) Phenylalanine hydroxylase
- C) Tyrosinase
- D) Cystathionine synthase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Phenylketonuria (PKU) is caused by deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH), which converts phenylalanine to tyrosine. Excess phenylalanine → phenylpyruvate (phenylketone) in urine. Classic features: intellectual disability, fair skin/blue eyes (reduced melanin from low tyrosine), musty/mousy odor. Guthrie test (bacterial inhibition assay) is the neonatal screening test in India. A) Homogentisate oxidase deficiency causes alkaptonuria. C) Tyrosinase deficiency causes oculocutaneous albinism. D) Cystathionine synthase deficiency causes homocystinuria.

</details>

**Q12. A child presents with dark urine that turns black on standing and stains diapers. He develops arthritis and ochronosis (dark pigmentation of cartilage) in later life. The metabolic block is in:**
- A) Tyrosine aminotransferase
- B) Homogentisate oxidase
- C) Fumarylacetoacetase
- D) Maleylacetoacetate isomerase

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Alkaptonuria is caused by deficiency of homogentisate oxidase (homogentisic acid oxidase), leading to accumulation of homogentisic acid in blood and urine. HGA is excreted in urine and oxidizes to a dark pigment on exposure to air. Long-term accumulation in connective tissue causes ochronosis (dark cartilage — ears, nose, sclerae) and ochronotic arthropathy. A) Tyrosinase deficiency causes albinism. C) Fumarylacetoacetase deficiency causes hereditary tyrosinemia type I (liver failure, risk of hepatocellular carcinoma). D) Not clinically significant.

</details>

**Q17. A point mutation changes the codon UAG to UAA. UAG normally codes for a stop signal; UAA is also a stop codon. This type of mutation is called:**
- A) Missense mutation
- B) Nonsense mutation
- C) Silent (synonymous) mutation
- D) Frameshift mutation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

A silent (synonymous) mutation changes the codon but does NOT change the amino acid (or function) — in this case, both UAG and UAA are stop codons, so there is no change in the protein produced. A) Missense mutation changes codon → different amino acid. B) Nonsense mutation changes a sense codon → stop codon (premature termination). D) Frameshift mutation inserts or deletes nucleotides, shifting the reading frame.

</details>

**Q20. A mutation in which type of gene would cause gain-of-function and lead to uncontrolled cell proliferation?**
- A) Tumor suppressor gene
- B) Proto-oncogene
- C) DNA repair gene
- D) Apoptosis effector gene (e.g., Bax)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B**

Proto-oncogenes normally promote cell growth and division; mutations (point mutation, amplification, translocation) cause gain-of-function, converting them to oncogenes that drive uncontrolled proliferation (e.g., RAS point mutation in colorectal cancer, MYC amplification in neuroblastoma, BCR-ABL translocation in CML). A) Tumor suppressor genes (p53, Rb) require loss-of-function (both alleles) — loss leads to cancer. C) DNA repair gene mutations cause genomic instability (Lynch syndrome, BRCA1/2). **Q21. A 45-year-old alcoholic man presents with confusion, ophthalmoplegia, and ataxia (Wernicke's encephalopathy). The vitamin deficient is:**
- A) Vitamin B2 (riboflavin)
- B) Vitamin B3 (niacin)
- C) Vitamin B1 (thiamine)
- D) Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Wernicke's encephalopathy (classic triad: confusion, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia) is caused by thiamine (B1) deficiency. D) B6 deficiency causes peripheral neuropathy and sideroblastic anemia.

- A) Maize has no tryptophan

**Q26. A 55-year-old man presents with chest pain. ECG shows ST elevation. Troponin I is elevated at 6 hours. After 72 hours, which enzyme marker remains elevated the LONGEST?**
- A) Troponin I
- B) CK-MB
- C) AST
- D) LDH

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A**

In acute myocardial infarction, the enzyme marker timeline is: CK-MB (rises 4–6h, peaks 18–24h, normalizes 48–72h), AST (peaks 24–48h), LDH (peaks 3–6 days, normalizes 10–14 days), Troponin I (rises 4–6h, peaks 24h, remains elevated 7–10 days). Troponin I (and T) remain elevated the longest and are also the most sensitive and specific markers for myocardial injury. B) CK-MB normalizes by 48–72 hours. C) AST normalizes by 72 hours. D) LDH remains up to 14 days but is less specific than troponin.

</details>

**Q27. A patient with obstructive jaundice has the following LFT pattern: elevated conjugated (direct) bilirubin, elevated ALP, mildly elevated AST/ALT, and elevated GGT. Which additional finding differentiates intrahepatic cholestasis from extrahepatic obstruction?**
- A) Presence of urobilinogen in urine
- B) Elevated serum cholesterol

<details>

A) Urobilinogen is absent in urine in complete obstruction (both types). B) Cholesterol is elevated in both. D) PT is elevated in severe hepatocellular disease or fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.

</details>

**Q30. A patient with alcoholic cirrhosis has the following results: elevated PT (not corrected by vitamin K injection), low serum albumin, elevated serum ammonia, and low platelet count. The finding that BEST reflects hepatocellular synthetic function failure is:**
- A) Low platelet count
- B) Elevated serum ammonia
- C) Prolonged PT not correcting with vitamin K
- D) Low serum albumin

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C**

Prolonged PT that does NOT correct after parenteral vitamin K injection directly reflects the liver's inability to synthesize clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X) — this is the most specific test of acute hepatocellular synthetic function in clinical practice. D) Low albumin reflects synthetic failure but has a long half-life (20 days) — less useful acutely.

</details>
