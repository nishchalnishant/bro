# Chapter 36: Sports medicine and sports injuries

## Learning objectives

- Review some common sports injuries
- Understand the basics of history, physical examination and imaging for common injuries
- Assess the patient and offer treatment and rehabilitation plans

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Sports injury care combines:
- Mechanism/biomechanics understanding
- Structured MSK history and exam (see Chapter 35)
- Targeted imaging
- Rehabilitation and safe return-to-sport planning tailored to the athlete’s goals

---

## 2. Diagnosis of sports injuries

### 2.1 Sports-specific history (high yield)
In addition to standard MSK history, ask:
- How was the injury sustained? (biomechanics)
- When did it happen? acute vs overuse
- What treatments/rehab have been tried?
- Training volume and recent changes
- Previous injuries and baseline function
- Competitive goals and timelines

### 2.2 Examination
Use a structured approach:
- Look/feel/move; compare sides
- Functional tests relevant to sport
- Neurovascular assessment where relevant

### 2.3 Imaging
Choose imaging that answers a clinical question:
- X-ray for suspected fracture/dislocation
- Ultrasound for soft tissue/tendon in selected contexts
- MRI for internal derangement/ligament/cartilage and occult fracture

## 3. Common regional injuries (overview)

### 3.1 Shoulder
#### Instability (common in contact sports)
- Mechanism: abduction + external rotation
- Risks: labral tears, recurrent instability in young athletes

Management principles:
- Reduce dislocation, assess neurovascular status
- Rehab focusing on rotator cuff/scapular stabilisers
- Surgical stabilisation for recurrent instability/high-demand athletes

#### Rotator cuff injury
- More common in older athletes or after dislocation in older patients
- Examine strength and impingement signs; ultrasound/MRI when needed

### 3.2 Elbow
- Lateral epicondylitis (overuse)
- Ulnar collateral ligament injury in throwing athletes

### 3.3 Wrist and hand
- Scaphoid fractures (fall on outstretched hand) – treat as fracture if tender until imaging confirms
- Tendon and pulley injuries (climbing/ball sports)

### 3.4 Hip and groin
- Muscle strains, femoroacetabular impingement, stress fractures
- Groin pain requires careful differential (adductor strain vs hernia-related vs hip joint pathology)

### 3.5 Knee
- ACL rupture (pivot injury) with immediate swelling/instability
- Meniscal tears (locking, joint line tenderness)
- Patellofemoral pain syndromes

### 3.6 Ankle and foot
- Lateral ankle sprain
- Achilles tendon rupture (sudden “kick” sensation)
- Stress fractures (metatarsals, navicular)

---

## 4. Treatment principles and rehabilitation

### Key concept
Rehabilitation is not an afterthought; it is the main treatment for many injuries and the bridge to return to sport.

Core pillars:
- Pain control and early safe movement
- Restore range of motion
- Strength and neuromuscular control
- Sport-specific conditioning
- Graduated return-to-play with clear criteria

### Return-to-sport decision-making
Should consider:
- Objective functional testing
- Psychological readiness
- Re-injury risk and sport demands

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Mechanism matters**: it often reveals the diagnosis before imaging.
2. **Overuse injuries** track training-load change; ask about recent volume spikes.
3. **Scaphoid tenderness**: treat as fracture until proven otherwise.
4. **ACL injuries** often present with pivot mechanism and instability; early swelling is common.
5. Rehab is **goal-directed and criteria-based**, not purely time-based.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Overuse injury** | Injury from repetitive microtrauma without a single acute event. |
| **Return-to-play** | Graduated progression back to training/competition guided by objective criteria. |
| **Internal derangement** | Intra-articular pathology such as meniscal tear or ligament injury. |
