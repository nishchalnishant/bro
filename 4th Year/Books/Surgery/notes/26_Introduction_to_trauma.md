# Chapter 26: Introduction to trauma

## Learning objectives

- Become familiar with the timeline concept in trauma management
- Understand how to assess a trauma problem
- Appreciate the importance of observing trends for identification of evolving conditions
- Recognise that specific criteria exist for implementation of different treatment strategies

---

## 1. Definition and scope

### Key concept
**Trauma** is injury caused by energy transfer (mechanical, thermal, chemical, radiation). The severity depends on magnitude, nature and duration of the force. **Major trauma** typically involves multiple regions/organ systems and is best managed within organised trauma systems.

### Magnitude of the problem
Trauma is a leading cause of death and disability in children and young adults worldwide. Major contributors include:
- Road traffic collisions
- Falls
- Intentional violence

---

## 2. The trauma “timeline” concept

### Key concept
Trauma care is time dependent: early recognition and intervention prevent avoidable death.

Common time-critical phases:
- **Immediate**: airway obstruction, exsanguination, tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade
- **Early (minutes–hours)**: control haemorrhage, prevent hypoxia and hypoperfusion, early imaging and decision-making
- **Later (hours–days)**: manage inflammatory response, sepsis, organ dysfunction, definitive repair and rehabilitation

### Practical implication
Teams should prioritise interventions that preserve **physiology** (oxygenation, perfusion, temperature, coagulation) over detailed anatomical perfection early on.

---

## 3. Assessing trauma severity

### 3.1 Physiological assessment (primary)
Initial severity is best captured by physiology:
- Airway/breathing status
- Haemodynamic stability, perfusion, mental status
- Lactate/base deficit trends

### 3.2 Anatomical scoring (overview)
Two commonly referenced concepts:
- **Abbreviated Injury Scale (AIS)**: assigns a severity score to individual injuries (from minor to unsurvivable).
- **Injury Severity Score (ISS)**: aggregates worst injuries across body regions; **ISS > 15** is a common definition of major trauma.

### 3.3 Trends and evolving conditions
Trauma physiology evolves. Serial assessment is essential because:
- Early compensation can mask shock
- Bleeding may be occult and progressive
- Secondary insults (hypothermia, acidosis) worsen outcomes

Monitor trends:
- HR, BP, mental state
- Lactate/base deficit
- Urine output
- Temperature

---

## 4. Trauma systems and the team approach

### Key concept
Outcomes improve when patients are managed by structured systems:
- Prehospital triage and early notification
- Trauma team activation and leadership
- Rapid imaging and access to theatre/interventional radiology
- Massive transfusion protocols and critical care support

### Team leadership
A clear team leader:
- Coordinates simultaneous ABC actions
- Maintains a “big picture” plan
- Anticipates next decisions and avoids delays

---

## 5. Strategy selection: criteria matter

### Examples of criteria-driven strategies (principles)
- **Permissive hypotension** may be used in uncontrolled haemorrhage (selected patients) to avoid dislodging clots until haemostasis is achieved.
- **Massive transfusion protocols** triggered by physiology and predicted blood loss improve speed and balance of resuscitation.
- **Damage control surgery** is chosen when physiology is failing (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy), prioritising rapid bleeding/contamination control over definitive reconstruction.

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Trauma is time critical**: early preventable death is usually airway obstruction or haemorrhage.
2. **Major trauma is defined by physiology as well as anatomy**; compensatory mechanisms can mask severity.
3. **Serial assessment** (trends) is often more valuable than single observations.
4. **AIS/ISS** provide anatomical language; **ISS > 15** commonly denotes major trauma.
5. **Choose strategy by criteria**: damage control is for failing physiology, not just “big injuries”.
