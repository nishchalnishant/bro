# Chapter 25: Nutrition and fluid therapy

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- The importance of assessment of perioperative nutritional status and fluid balance
- The nutritional requirements of surgical patients and the effects of intestinal resection on nutrition
- The causes and complications of malnutrition and their management
- The options for nutritional intervention and the indications for enteral versus parenteral nutritional support

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Nutrition and fluid balance are core determinants of surgical outcomes. Surgical pathology commonly causes malnutrition, and surgery itself can temporarily or permanently impair gastrointestinal absorption. Effective perioperative care therefore requires:
- Early **nutritional risk identification**
- Timely **enteral** support when feasible
- Safe **parenteral** support when enteral is not possible
- Accurate **fluid and electrolyte** prescribing and monitoring

### Clinical significance
Malnutrition increases:
- Infection risk and poor wound healing
- Length of stay and readmissions
- Mortality in vulnerable groups

## 2. Metabolic response to fasting and starvation (high yield)

### 2.1 Short-term fasting
Homeostasis is maintained by:
- Falling insulin, rising glucagon/catecholamines
- **Glycogenolysis** as primary glucose source initially

### 2.2 24–40 hours
Glycogen stores deplete and **gluconeogenesis** predominates:
- Substrates include lactate, glycerol, amino acids (alanine, glutamine)
- Muscle protein catabolism provides amino acids

### 2.3 Prolonged fasting (>48 hours)
Increasing reliance on **lipolysis**:
- Fatty acids and ketone bodies supply many tissues
- Ketone utilisation reduces (but does not eliminate) muscle breakdown

### Summary box 25.1: Metabolic response to starvation
- Low plasma insulin; higher glucagon
- Early glycogenolysis → later gluconeogenesis
- Increasing lipolysis and ketogenesis with prolonged fasting
- Decreased resting energy expenditure with prolonged starvation

## 3. Malnutrition: causes, consequences and assessment

### 3.1 Causes (surgical practice)
- Reduced intake: anorexia, dysphagia, obstruction, nausea, social factors
- Malabsorption: short bowel, pancreatic insufficiency, inflammatory disease
- Increased requirements: sepsis, trauma, malignancy, burns
- Losses: fistulae, stomas, diarrhoea, high NG output

### 3.2 Consequences
- Impaired immunity and infection risk
- Reduced collagen synthesis → poor wound healing, dehiscence
- Muscle wasting → weakness, respiratory compromise
- Delayed rehabilitation and increased complications

### 3.3 Assessment (practical)
Combine:
- History (weight loss, intake, GI symptoms)
- Examination (muscle/fat wasting, oedema)
- Screening tools (nutritional risk screening frameworks)
- Basic markers (interpret with caution): albumin is more inflammation/permeability than nutrition

## 4. Perioperative nutritional requirements (principles)

### Energy and protein
Major illness increases protein needs due to catabolism and repair demands. Practical goals:
- Adequate protein to limit nitrogen loss (within tolerance)
- Avoid overfeeding (hyperglycaemia, hepatic steatosis, CO₂ load)

### Micronutrients
Consider deficiency risk (e.g. thiamine, zinc, vitamins) in:
- Alcohol misuse
- Prolonged poor intake
- Malabsorption and chronic diarrhoea

## 5. Enteral vs parenteral nutrition

### 5.1 Enteral nutrition (EN)
Preferred when the gut works because it:
- Preserves gut mucosal integrity
- Reduces infectious complications relative to PN in many settings
- Is physiologically more appropriate

Routes:
- Oral supplements
- NG/NJ tube feeds
- Gastrostomy/jejunostomy (long-term needs)

### 5.2 Parenteral nutrition (PN)
Indicated when enteral feeding is not possible or unsafe, e.g.:
- Prolonged ileus/obstruction
- High-output fistula where EN not feasible
- Severe malabsorption/short bowel in acute setting

Risks:
- Line infection, thrombosis
- Electrolyte derangements, hyperglycaemia
- Liver dysfunction with prolonged PN

### Refeeding syndrome (must know)
In severely malnourished patients:
- Rapid feeding drives insulin surge → intracellular phosphate, potassium, magnesium shift
- Can cause arrhythmia, respiratory failure, neurological complications

Prevention:
- Start low, go slow; supplement thiamine; monitor and replace electrolytes.

## 6. Intestinal resection and malabsorption (overview)

### Short bowel physiology (high yield)
Consequences depend on:
- Length and segment removed (ileum vs jejunum)
- Presence of ileocaecal valve and colon continuity

Typical issues:
- Fluid and electrolyte losses
- B12 and bile salt malabsorption (ileal disease/resection)
- Fat malabsorption and diarrhoea

Management principles:
- Dietetic-led strategy, antidiarrhoeals, bile acid binders (selected), supplementation
- PN may be required initially or long-term in severe cases

## 7. Fluid therapy (surgical essentials)

### 7.1 Body water and compartments (concept)
Total body water distributes into:
- Intracellular fluid (ICF)
- Extracellular fluid (ECF): interstitial + intravascular

### 7.2 Assessing fluid status
Use combined data:
- Clinical: pulse, BP, capillary refill, JVP, mucous membranes, oedema
- Urine output and weight trend
- Labs: creatinine, lactate, electrolytes

### 7.3 Common perioperative fluid problems
- Hypovolaemia (bleeding, third spacing, sepsis)
- Fluid overload (liberal crystalloids)
- Electrolyte disturbances: sodium/potassium/magnesium/phosphate

### Practical prescribing principles
- Use balanced isotonic crystalloids for most resuscitation
- Replace measured losses (NG, fistulae, stoma output) with appropriate electrolyte solutions
- Monitor daily weights, strict input/output and U&E where indicated

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Malnutrition increases postoperative complications**—screen routinely.
2. **Enteral is preferred** when the gut works; PN is lifesaving when it doesn’t.
3. **Albumin is not a pure nutrition marker** in acute illness; inflammation drives low levels.
4. **Refeeding syndrome** can kill—start feeds cautiously and monitor phosphate.
5. **Replace measured GI losses** accurately; “maintenance-only” fluids are inadequate for high-output states.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Malnutrition** | Deficiency (or imbalance) of energy/protein that adversely affects body form and function. |
| **Enteral nutrition** | Nutrient delivery via the gastrointestinal tract (oral/tube). |
| **Parenteral nutrition** | Intravenous nutrient delivery bypassing the gut. |
| **Refeeding syndrome** | Potentially fatal electrolyte and fluid shifts on reintroducing nutrition to the severely malnourished. |
| **Short bowel syndrome** | Malabsorption state following extensive small bowel resection. |
