# Chapter 42: Musculoskeletal tumours

## Learning Objectives
- Identify symptoms and signs of musculoskeletal tumours
- Understand the 3-phase assessment and referral pathways to specialist centres
- Recognise why **staging must precede biopsy**
- Describe principles of biopsy and surgical treatment
- Evaluate the risk of pathological fracture using **Mirels' Score**

---

## 1. Introduction and Classification
- **Primary Bone Tumours (Sarcomas):** Rare. Include Osteosarcoma, Chondrosarcoma, and Ewing's sarcoma.
- **Secondary (Metastatic) Bone Disease:** Much more common. Origins: **Breast, Prostate, Lung, Kidney, Thyroid, Colon**.
- **Haematopoietic:** Multiple Myeloma (plasma cells) and Lymphoma.
- **Soft-tissue Tumours:** Extremely common, but only 1% are malignant (Sarcomas).

---

## 2. Common Bone Tumours (High Yield)

### 2.1 Benign Lesions
- **Osteoid Osteoma:** Small, painful lesion; nocturnal pain relieved by **NSAIDs/Aspirin**. CT shows a "nidus".
- **Osteochondroma:** Cartilage-capped bony outgrowth growing away from the physis. Solitary or multiple (HME).
- **Enchondroma:** Benign cartilage tumour in the marrow. Most common bone tumour in the **hand**.
- **Giant Cell Tumour (GCT):** Locally aggressive; occurs in the **epiphysis** after physis closure (ages 20–40). Requires thorough curettage.
- **Simple Bone Cyst (SBC):** Fluid-filled; common in proximal humerus/femur of children. Pathognomonic **"fallen leaf sign"** on X-ray after fracture.
- **Aneurysmal Bone Cyst (ABC):** More aggressive, blood-filled spaces; shows multiple **fluid-fluid levels** on MRI.

### 2.2 Malignant Primary Sarcomas
- **Osteosarcoma:** Most common in distal femur/proximal tibia. Bimodal age (adolescents and elderly with Paget's).
- **Ewing’s Sarcoma:** Small round cell tumour; **11:22 translocation**. "Onion-skin" periosteal reaction. Systemic symptoms (fever, high ESR).
- **Chondrosarcoma:** Malignant cartilage tumour; insensitive to chemo/radiotherapy (primary treatment is surgical).

---

## 3. Metastatic Bone Disease (MBD)
- **Spread:** Primarily haematogenous via **Batson’s venous plexus** (valveless veins) to the spine and proximal long bones.
- **Types:**
  - **Lytic:** Kidney, Lung, Thyroid. (High fracture risk).
  - **Sclerotic:** Prostate (typically osteoblast stimulation).
  - **Mixed:** Breast.
- **Mirels' Score for Pathological Fracture Risk:**
    - Evaluates: **Site** (Upper/Lower/Peritrochanteric), **Pain** (Mild/Mod/Func), **Size** (<1/3, etc.), and **Nature** (Blastic/Lytic).
    - **Score >8:** High risk; consider prophylactic internal fixation or replacement.

---

## 4. Evaluation and Staging

### 4.1 The Three Phases of Assessment
1. **Phase 1 (Referring Hospital):** History, Exam, FBC, ESR, LDH, X-ray of the **whole bone**, Chest X-ray.
2. **Phase 2:** Bone scan, CT Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis, MRI of the lesion.
3. **Phase 3 (Specialist Centre):** Review of imaging, final staging, and **Biopsy**.

### 4.2 Staging Principles
- **Enneking System (Malignant):**
  - **I:** Low grade.
  - **II:** High grade.
  - **III:** Metastases.
  - **A:** Intracompartmental.
  - **B:** Extracompartmental (has breached cortex).
- **Rule:** Stop and think before biopsy. Biopsies performed in non-specialist centres have higher complication and mismanagement rates.

---

## 5. Principles of Treatment

### 5.1 Biopsy Principles
- Always **longitudinal incision**.
- The biopsy track **must be excised** during the definitive operation.
- No transverse incisions; no crossing of compartments.

### 5.2 Surgical Margins
- **Intralesional:** Through the tumour (e.g., curettage).
- **Marginal:** Through the reactive zone.
- **Wide:** Outside the reactive zone (standard for sarcomas).
- **Radical:** Whole compartment excision.

### 5.3 Management Strategies
- **Sarcomas:** Neoadjuvant chemotherapy $\rightarrow$ Surgery (Limb salvage vs Amputation) $\rightarrow$ Adjuvant chemotherapy.
- **Metastases:** Palliative goal. Internal fixation or **Endoprosthetic replacement** (preferred for hip/epiphyseal lesions because diseased bone heals poorly).
- **Renal Metastases:** Highly vascular. Must consider **Pre-operative Embolisation** to prevent massive haemorrhage.

## Summary Boxes

### Box 42.1: Red Flags for Bone Tumours
- Non-mechanical pain (especially at night).
- Swelling around the knee in an adolescent.
- Pathological fracture through a minor injury.

### Box 42.2: Soft-tissue Sarcoma Warning (Sizes)
A lump is "Sarcoma until proven otherwise" if it is:
1. >5 cm in diameter.
2. Deep to the fascia.
3. Increasing in size.
4. Painful.

---

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **Never** biopsy a suspected primary bone tumour in a general hospital; refer to an oncology centre first.
2. ** Batson’s Plexus** explains why pelvic/abdominal cancers spread to the spine.
3. **Mirels' Score** is the key decision tool for prophylactic surgery in metastatic disease.
4. **Denosumab** is increasingly used for unresectable Giant Cell Tumours (RANK-L inhibition).
