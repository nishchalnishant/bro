# Chapter 38: The upper limb

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- Anatomy and physiology relevant to upper limb pathology

To be able to explain:
- The diagnosis and treatment of common upper limb conditions

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Upper limb disorders affect function disproportionately because the hand and shoulder are essential for work, self-care and independence. Diagnosis requires:
- Mechanism-aware history
- Structured MSK examination (Chapter 35 framework)
- Targeted imaging and specialist tests when needed

---

## 2. Shoulder girdle: anatomy and function

### Key concept
The shoulder complex comprises:
- Sternoclavicular joint (only synovial link to axial skeleton)
- Scapulothoracic motion (scapula glides/rotates on thorax)
- Glenohumeral joint (high mobility, dependent on dynamic stability)

Dynamic stabilisers:
- Rotator cuff (subscapularis, supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor)
- Deltoid and scapular stabilisers

Clinical implication:
- Scapular mechanics contribute meaningfully to elevation; cuff function prevents impingement and instability.

---

## 3. Common shoulder conditions (overview)

### 3.1 Shoulder instability
- Often traumatic in young athletes (abduction + external rotation mechanism)
- Risk of recurrent instability and labral injury

Management:
- Acute reduction and neurovascular check
- Rehabilitation; surgery for recurrent instability/high-demand cases

### 3.2 Rotator cuff disease and impingement
- Painful arc, night pain, weakness
- Imaging (US/MRI) if tear suspected or failure of conservative care

### 3.3 Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder)
- Global restriction (especially external rotation)
- Usually self-limiting but prolonged; treat with analgesia, physiotherapy ± injection

---

## 4. Elbow conditions (high yield examples)

### Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow)
- Overuse tendinopathy; pain with resisted wrist extension
- Management: activity modification, physiotherapy, bracing; injections selected

### Ulnar neuropathy (cubital tunnel)
- Numbness/tingling in ulnar digits; weakness/clawing in severe cases
- Manage with activity change/splinting; surgery for persistent deficits

---

## 5. Wrist and hand conditions

### 5.1 Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS)
- Median nerve compression; nocturnal paraesthesia, thenar weakness in severe cases
- Splinting and steroid injection; decompression surgery when indicated

### 5.2 De Quervain’s tenosynovitis
- Radial wrist pain; positive Finkelstein-type provocation
- Splinting, injection; surgery if refractory

### 5.3 Trigger finger
- Stenosing tenosynovitis causing catching/locking
- Steroid injection often effective; release if persistent

### 5.4 Dupuytren’s disease
- Palmar fascial thickening and contracture
- Treatment based on function and contracture severity (needle fasciotomy, collagenase where available, surgery)

---

## 6. Investigation and treatment principles

### Imaging (question-driven)
- X-ray for bony injury/arthritis
- Ultrasound for tendon pathology
- MRI for complex soft tissue or occult pathology

### Treatment hierarchy
- Education, activity modification and analgesia
- Physiotherapy/hand therapy
- Injections where appropriate
- Surgery for structural pathology, failure of conservative care, or neurological compromise

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **External rotation restriction** is a key sign of adhesive capsulitis.
2. **Thenar weakness** suggests advanced CTS—consider urgent decompression.
3. **Painful arc + weakness** suggests rotator cuff pathology; imaging guides severity.
4. Upper limb disorders are often best managed with **therapy first**, surgery when indicated.
5. Always distinguish **neck radiculopathy** from peripheral entrapment with careful neuro exam.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Rotator cuff** | Tendons/muscles stabilising the glenohumeral joint (SSp, ISp, TM, SubSc). |
| **CTS** | Median nerve compression at the carpal tunnel causing paraesthesia and thenar dysfunction. |
| **Trigger finger** | Stenosing tenosynovitis causing catching/locking in flexion/extension. |
| **Dupuytren’s disease** | Palmar fascial fibroproliferation causing finger flexion contractures. |
