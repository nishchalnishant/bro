# Chapter 2: Shock, Haemorrhage and Transfusion

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the pathophysiology of shock
- Understand the different patterns of shock and the principles and priorities of resuscitation
- Understand appropriate monitoring and end points of resuscitation
- Recognise and manage bleeding
- Understand the use of blood and blood products, the benefits and risks of blood transfusion

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
**Shock** is a systemic state of low tissue perfusion that is inadequate for normal cellular respiration. With insufficient delivery of oxygen and glucose, cells switch from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism → lactic acid production. If perfusion is not restored in a timely fashion, cell death ensues.

### Clinical Significance
- Shock is the **most common cause of death** in surgical patients
- Death may occur **rapidly** (profound shock) or **later** (organ ischaemia/reperfusion injury)
- Every surgeon must understand pathophysiology, diagnosis and management of shock

---

## 2. Pathophysiology of Shock

### 2.1 Cellular Level
```
Reduced tissue perfusion
    ↓
Cellular O₂ deprivation → Anaerobic metabolism
    ↓
Lactic acid accumulation → Systemic metabolic acidosis
    ↓
Intracellular glucose exhaustion → Failure of Na/K ATPase pumps
    ↓
Intracellular lysosomes release autodigestive enzymes
    ↓
Cell lysis → K⁺ released into bloodstream → Hyperkalaemia
```

### 2.2 Microvascular Level
- Hypoxia + acidosis → **complement activation** + **leukocyte priming**
- Primed leukocytes → O₂ free radicals + cytokine release
- Capillary endothelial damage → endothelium becomes **"leaky"**
- Fluid leaks into interstitium → tissue oedema → exacerbates hypoxia
- Systemic immune and coagulation systems continuously activated

### 2.3 Systemic Effects

| System | Response | Mechanism |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Tachycardia, vasoconstriction | Baroreceptor reflex → ↑ sympathetic activity → catecholamine release |
| **Respiratory** | ↑RR, ↑minute ventilation | Metabolic acidosis + sympathetic response → compensatory respiratory alkalosis |
| **Renal** | ↓ urine output, Na⁺/H₂O retention | ↓ perfusion pressure → ↓GFR; RAAS activation → vasoconstriction + reabsorption |
| **Endocrine** | Water retention, sensitisation to catecholamines | ADH release → vasoconstriction + renal H₂O resorption; cortisol → Na⁺/H₂O retention |

## 3. Classification of Shock

### Summary Box 2.1: Classification of Shock
1. Haemorrhagic/Hypovolaemic shock
2. Cardiogenic shock
3. Obstructive shock
4. Distributive shock
5. Endocrine shock

### 3.1 Hypovolaemic Shock
- **Due to reduced circulating volume**
- **Haemorrhagic:** Active bleeding
- **Non-haemorrhagic:** Dehydration, vomiting, diarrhoea, urinary losses, "third-spacing" (bowel obstruction, pancreatitis)
- Most common form; always exclude/treat hypovolaemia first regardless of shock type

### 3.2 Cardiogenic Shock
- **Primary pump failure**
- Causes: MI, dysrhythmias, valvular disease, blunt myocardial injury, cardiomyopathy, myocardial depression from sepsis/drugs
- Features: venous hypertension with pulmonary/systemic oedema coexisting with shock

### 3.3 Obstructive Shock
- **Mechanical obstruction to cardiac filling** → reduced preload → low output
- Causes: Cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax, massive PE, air embolus

### 3.4 Distributive Shock
- **Vascular dilatation** despite high or normal cardiac output → inadequate organ perfusion
- Features: hypotension, **low SVR, high cardiac output**
- Causes:
  - **Septic shock:** Endotoxin activation → microvascular maldistribution + AV shunting + cellular O₂ utilisation failure → mixed venous O₂ saturation HIGH despite shock
  - **Anaphylaxis:** Histamine → vasodilation
  - **Neurogenic shock:** High spinal cord injury → loss of sympathetic outflow → vasodilation + bradycardia
- Late septic shock can have concomitant hypovolaemia + myocardial depression

### 3.5 Endocrine Shock
- May mimic hypovolaemic, cardiogenic, or distributive shock
- Causes: Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's; relative insufficiency in sepsis), hypothyroidism (↓inotropy, bradycardia), hyperthyroidism (high-output failure)

### Haemodynamic Profiles of Shock (Table 2.1)

| Type | Cardiac Output | SVR | Venous Pressure | Mixed Venous O₂ Sat | Base Deficit |
|------|---------------|-----|-----------------|---------------------|--------------|
| Hypovolaemic | Low | High | Low | Low | High |
| Cardiogenic | Low | High | High | Low | High |
| Obstructive | Low | High | High | Low | High |
| Distributive | **High** | **Low** | Low | **High** | High |

> **Exam tip:** Distributive (septic) shock is the only one with HIGH cardiac output, LOW SVR, and HIGH mixed venous O₂ saturation.

## 4. Clinical Consequences of Shock

### 4.1 Unresuscitatable Shock
- Prolonged profound shock → systemic ischaemic injury
- Heart: myocardial cell death from poor coronary perfusion + severe acidaemia/hyperkalaemia
- Periphery: loss of ability to maintain SVR → unresponsive to vasopressors
- Once entered: death is inevitable

### 4.2 Ischaemia–Reperfusion Injury and SIRS
- During hypoperfusion: cellular and organ damage accumulates
- On reperfusion: acid, K⁺ and inflammatory mediators flushed into systemic circulation
- Activated leukocytes/complement overwhelm local anti-inflammatory defences
- Distant organ injury: **ARDS, AKI, cerebral oedema, MOF**
- Can only be attenuated by **minimising duration and extent** of hypoperfusion

### 4.3 Multiple Organ Failure (MOF)
- Defined as failure of **≥2 organ systems**
- **Summary Box 2.2: Effects of Organ Failure:**
  - Cardiac → cardiovascular failure
  - Lung → ARDS
  - Kidney → acute renal insufficiency
  - Liver → liver failure and coagulopathy
  - Brain → cerebral swelling and dysfunction
- Mortality: **60%** (no specific treatment — only supportive)
- Prevention = early, aggressive identification and reversal of shock

## 5. Recognition and Diagnosis of Shock

### 5.1 Stages of Shock (Table 2.2)

| Stage | Lactic Acidosis | Urine Output | Conscious Level | RR | Pulse | BP |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|-----|-------|----|
| Compensated | + | Normal | Mild anxiety | ↑ | ↑ | Normal |
| Mild (Uncompensated) | ++ | Reduced | Drowsy | ↑ | ↑ | Mild hypotension |
| Moderate | +++ | <0.5 mL/kg/hr | Confused | ↑ | ↑ | Falling |
| Severe | ++++ | Anuric | Comatose | Laboured | ↑↑ | Severe hypotension |

**In compensated shock:** Perfusion to skin, muscle, and gut is sacrificed to preserve brain and kidney — this is "occult hypoperfusion."

### 5.2 Clinical Pitfalls

**Capillary Refill:**
- Prolonged in hypovolaemic shock
- **Brisk** in septic (distributive) shock — peripheries are WARM despite shock

**Tachycardia:**
- May be absent with: β-blockers, pacemakers
- Paradoxical bradycardia with penetrating trauma/haemorrhage in some young patients
- A pulse of 80 in a normally bradycardic athlete = very abnormal

**Blood Pressure:**
- **Hypotension is a LATE sign** — children and fit young adults maintain BP until final stages
- Elderly "hypertensive" patients may look "normal" but be relatively hypotensive
- Blood volume must be lost by ~30–40% before BP falls

### 5.3 Occult Hypoperfusion
- Normal HR, BP, urine output — but persistent **raised lactate/base deficit**
- The gut and muscle are still underperfused
- Occult hypoperfusion for **>12 hours → 2–3× higher mortality** + infection rate + MOF

## 6. Haemorrhage

### 6.1 Types of Haemorrhage

| Type | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Revealed** | External (open wound, haematemesis, PR bleeding) |
| **Concealed** | Contained (thorax, abdomen, pelvis, retroperitoneum, long bones) |
| **Primary** | Immediate at time of injury/surgery |
| **Reactionary** | Delayed <24 hrs; clot dislodged by resuscitation/BP normalisation/vasodilatation or technical failure (ligature slip) |
| **Secondary** | 7–14 days; vessel wall sloughing from infection, pressure necrosis, malignancy |
| **Surgical** | Direct vessel injury → amenable to surgical/endovascular control |
| **Non-surgical** | Coagulopathic ooze from raw surfaces → requires coagulation correction |

### 6.2 Trauma-Induced Coagulopathy (TIC)

**Key concept: Haemorrhage ≠ Shock. Treat the bleeding, not just the shock.**

**Acute Traumatic Coagulopathy (ATC):**
- Endogenous coagulopathy developing within **minutes** of injury
- Affects up to **25%** of trauma patients
- Associated with **4-fold increase in mortality**
- Characterised by: systemic hyperfibrinolysis + low fibrinogen + platelet dysfunction

**TIC Vicious Cycle:**
```
Haemorrhage + Shock → ATC
         ↓
Fluid resuscitation → Dilution of coagulation factors
         ↓
Cold fluids/blood + open body cavities → Hypothermia
         ↓
Underperfusion + acidosis → Coagulation enzyme inhibition
         ↓
More bleeding → More dilution → Worse coagulopathy
         ↓
PHYSIOLOGICAL EXHAUSTION AND DEATH
```

**Lethal Triad:**
- **Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy** = death spiral

### 6.3 Estimation of Blood Loss
- Adult blood volume: **~5 litres** (70 mL/kg adults, 80 mL/kg neonates)
- External blood loss: can be measured (suction, weigh swabs)
- Haemoglobin: poor early indicator (concentration unchanged early as whole blood lost; later dilution occurs)

### Traditional Haemorrhagic Shock Classification (Table 2.3)

| Class | Blood Loss (%) | Clinical Findings |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **1** | <15% (<750 mL) | No signs/minimal tachycardia |
| **2** | 15–30% (750–1500 mL) | Tachycardia, tachypnoea, ↓pulse pressure, mild anxiety |
| **3** | 30–40% (1500–2000 mL) | Marked tachycardia/tachypnoea, confusion, ↓BP |
| **4** | >40% (>2000 mL) | Lethal unless immediately treated; severe BP↓, comatose |

> **Note:** This classification is conceptually useful but rarely applied clinically — individual variation is significant.

### 6.4 Dynamic Assessment: Response to Fluid Bolus

| Response Type | Haemodynamic Pattern | Implication |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Responder** | Sustained BP improvement | Bleeding likely stopped |
| **Transient Responder** | Improvement then BP drops | Still bleeding; rate < fluid rate |
| **Non-Responder** | No BP improvement | Catastrophic ongoing haemorrhage |

## 7. Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR)

**DCR = Paradigm that prioritises haemorrhage control while patients are actively bleeding.**

Rationale: No aspect of the shock state (perfusion, BP, temperature, pH) can be corrected while the patient is bleeding. Repeated large-volume resuscitation worsens coagulopathy, hypothermia and acidosis.

### Four Key DCR Principles:

#### Principle 1: Rapid Haemorrhage Control
- Direct pressure on external bleeding
- Tourniquets, balloon occlusion, junctional pressure
- Move patient urgently to OR or IR suite
- **Damage Control Surgery (DCS):**
  - **Arrest haemorrhage**
  - **Control sepsis**
  - **Protect from further injury**
  - **Nothing else** — definitive repair after stabilisation

#### Principle 2: Permissive Hypotension
- Allow the patient to set their own BP while bleeding
- Avoids "popping the clot" with high-pressure resuscitation
- Reduces blood loss and dilutional coagulopathy
- **Target:** Palpable central pulse = MAP >50 mmHg (enough for coronary perfusion)
- **Contraindication:** Traumatic brain injury (requires adequate CPP)

#### Principle 3: Avoid Dilutional Coagulopathy
- Avoid crystalloids and colloids
- Give transfusion approximating whole blood:
  - **Packed red blood cells : Fresh frozen plasma = 1:1**
  - Plus platelets to achieve 1:1:1 (RBC:FFP:Plt) in massive haemorrhage

#### Principle 4: Treat Existing Coagulation Deficits
- **Tranexamic acid (TXA):** Antifibrinolytic — given early, empirically (hyperfibrinolysis is near-universal)
- **Cryoprecipitate:** For low fibrinogen (most common coagulation deficit)
- **Platelet concentrates:** For thrombocytopenia or platelet dysfunction
- Monitor: ROTEM/TEG (point-of-care coagulation testing) to guide targeted therapy

## 8. Shock Resuscitation (After Haemorrhage Control)

### Immediate Steps
1. Ensure patent airway and adequate oxygenation/ventilation
2. Then cardiovascular resuscitation

### Fluid Therapy — Key Principles

**Access:** Short, **wide-bore** IV catheters (large lumen → low resistance → rapid infusion). Long CVP lines have too much resistance for rapid fluid delivery.

**Type of Fluid:**
- Replace with fluid approximating what was lost
- For haemorrhagic shock: whole blood (or components) first; crystalloid only while awaiting blood
- **Crystalloids vs Colloids:** No outcome difference between normal saline, Hartmann's, Ringer's lactate; colloids offer no benefit and have worse side-effect profiles
- **Hypotonic solutions** (e.g., dextrose): Poor volume expanders — do NOT use in shock unless free-water deficit or sodium overloaded

**Vasopressor and Inotropic Support:**
> ⚠️ Never give vasopressors/inotropes to an underfilled heart — depletes myocardial O₂ and reduces coronary perfusion

| Agent | Class | Indication |
|-------|-------|------------|
| **Noradrenaline** | Vasopressor | Distributive shock (sepsis/neurogenic) — low SVR states |
| **Phenylephrine** | Vasopressor | Distributive shock — pure α-agonist |
| **Vasopressin** | Vasopressor | Catecholamine-resistant shock; relative ADH deficiency |
| **Dobutamine** | Inodilator | Cardiogenic shock or myocardial depression in sepsis |

## 9. Monitoring the Shocked Patient

### Minimum Standard (Summary Box 2.4)
- Continuous HR and O₂ saturation monitoring
- Frequent non-invasive BP measurement
- Hourly urine output

### Additional Monitoring (Invasive)
- CVP (central venous pressure)
- Invasive arterial BP
- Cardiac output monitoring

### CVP Monitoring
- No single "normal" CVP in shock
- Assess **dynamically** with a fluid challenge (250–500 mL bolus over 5–10 min):
  - **No CVP rise** → patient is empty → give more fluid
  - **Rise 2–5 cmH₂O then drifts back** → normal response
  - **Large sustained rise** → fluid overloaded or cardiac insufficiency

### Systemic Perfusion Monitoring (Table 2.4)

| Monitor | Target | Significance |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| **Urine output** | >0.5 mL/kg/hr | Best clinical indicator of renal perfusion |
| **Base deficit** | <6 mmol/L | Degree of metabolic acidosis from anaerobic metabolism |
| **Serum lactate** | <2 mmol/L | Global perfusion marker; sensitive and specific for shock |
| **Mixed venous O₂ saturation (SvO₂)** | 50–70% | <50%: hypovolaemic/cardiogenic; >70%: septic shock |
| **Cardiac output** | Context-dependent | Distinguishes shock types; guides vasopressor/inotrope therapy |
| **GCS/conscious level** | Normal | Late marker of cerebral perfusion |

### Interpreting Mixed Venous O₂ Saturation (SvO₂)
- Normal: **50–70%**
- <50% → inadequate O₂ delivery (hypovolaemic or cardiogenic shock)
- >70% in shocked patient → **septic shock**: disordered cellular O₂ utilisation + AV shunting
  - Even "70%" is LOW for a septic patient → treat with fluids + inotropes (dobutamine) to get >70%

### End Points of Resuscitation
- Traditional end points (normal HR, BP, UO) monitor organs whose blood flow is preserved until late
- **Modern targets:** normalise base deficit, lactate, SvO₂ (global perfusion markers)
- Beware: aggressive crystalloid → ARDS, abdominal compartment syndrome, cerebral oedema

## 10. Blood Transfusion

### 10.1 Blood Products

| Product | Key Features | Indication |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Whole blood** | Rarely available civilian practice; coagulation factor rich | Best volume replacement |
| **Packed red cells** | ~330 mL; haematocrit 50–70%; stored in SAG-M (saline-adenine-glucose-mannitol) up to 5 weeks at 2–6°C | Replace O₂-carrying capacity |
| **Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)** | Rich in all coagulation factors; stored at −40 to −50°C; shelf life 2 years | Coagulopathic haemorrhage; 1:1 with RBC in DCR |
| **Cryoprecipitate** | Supernatant of FFP; rich in fibrinogen, Factor VIII, Factor XIII; stored at −30°C; 2 years | Low fibrinogen; Factor VIII deficiency |
| **Platelets** | Pooled concentrate ~250×10⁹/L; stored at 20–24°C on agitator; **shelf life 5 days only** | Thrombocytopenia, platelet dysfunction |
| **Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (PCC)** | Contains factors II, IX, X (± VII) | Emergency warfarin reversal |
| **Autologous blood** | Pre-donation or cell saver | Elective surgery to reduce allogeneic transfusion |

### 10.2 Transfusion Triggers

| Haemoglobin (g/dL) | Recommendation |
|---------------------|----------------|
| <6 | Will probably benefit from transfusion |
| 6–8 | Unlikely to benefit in absence of bleeding, impending surgery, or symptoms |
| >8 | No indication unless other risk factors |

> Historical trigger of Hb >10 g/dL is **no longer appropriate** — associated with increased morbidity and mortality compared to restrictive strategy.

### 10.3 Blood Groups and Cross-Matching

**ABO System:**

| Phenotype | Antigens on Red Cells | Antibodies in Serum | Frequency (UK) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| **O** | None | Anti-A, Anti-B | 46% |
| **A** | A | Anti-B | 42% |
| **B** | B | Anti-A | 9% |
| **AB** | A and B | None | 3% |

- **Group O:** Universal donor (no antigens to react with recipient antibodies)
- **Group AB:** Universal recipient (no antibodies to react with donated antigens)
- ABO incompatibility → **potentially fatal complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis**

**Rhesus System:**
- Rh(D) antigen: present in **85%** of UK population (Rh-positive)
- Rh-negative individuals can become sensitised by exposure to Rh-positive blood
- Acquired antibodies can cross placenta → **hydrops fetalis** (haemolytic disease of newborn)
- Emergency: Give **O-negative** to females of childbearing age; O-positive acceptable for males

**Cross-Matching Timeline:**
- Full cross-match: up to **45 minutes**
- Type-specific (ABO/Rh only): **10–15 minutes**
- Emergency group O: **Immediate**

**Safety Checks:** Two healthcare personnel must check patient details, prescription, and blood serial number before transfusion.

### 10.4 Transfusion Reactions

**Acute Haemolytic Reaction (ABO Incompatibility):**
- **Potentially fatal** complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis
- Back pain, fever, hypotension, haemoglobinuria
- Stop transfusion immediately; aggressive supportive care

**Febrile Non-Haemolytic Reaction:**
- Graft-versus-host response from leukocytes in donor blood
- Fever, chills, rigors — usually self-limiting
- Stop transfusion; reassess; restart with different unit if appropriate
- Now rare with leukodepleted blood

**Other Reactions:**
- Allergic (urticaria → anaphylaxis)
- Transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI) — usually from FFP
- Infection transmission (bacterial, hepatitis B/C, HIV, malaria)

### 10.5 Complications of Massive Transfusion

| Complication | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Coagulopathy** | Dilution of coagulation factors |
| **Hypocalcaemia** | Citrate (preservative) binds Ca²⁺ — most important with rapid infusion |
| **Hyperkalaemia** | Lysed red cells in stored blood release K⁺ |
| **Hypokalaemia** | Can develop as transfusion alkalosis ensues |
| **Hypothermia** | Cold stored blood products |
| **Iron overload** | Repeated transfusions (thalassaemia); ~250 mg elemental iron per unit |

### 10.6 Management of Coagulopathy

**During Active Haemorrhage:**
1. Balanced transfusion (1:1:1 RBC:FFP:Platelets — approximates whole blood)
2. Tranexamic acid (TXA) — empirical antifibrinolytic (most patients are hyperfibrinolytic)
3. Cryoprecipitate — for low fibrinogen (most common specific deficit)
4. Guide with **ROTEM/TEG** (viscoelastic point-of-care clotting tests) for targeted therapy
5. Avoid crystalloids/colloids

- Still in experimental/early clinical trials for most agents

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. **Shock definition:** Low tissue perfusion inadequate for cellular respiration — not just "low BP"
2. **Septic/Distributive shock** = WARM peripheries, brisk capillary refill, HIGH SvO₂ (>70%), HIGH cardiac output, LOW SVR
3. **Hypotension is a LATE sign** of shock — children and young adults especially compensate well
4. **Occult hypoperfusion:** Normal HR/BP/UO but persistent lactate >2 mmol/L; >12 hours = 2–3× mortality
5. **DCR priority:** Stop the bleeding FIRST — resuscitation cannot fix the shock state while bleeding continues
6. **Permissive hypotension** target = palpable central pulse (MAP ~50 mmHg); CONTRAINDICATED with TBI
7. **Tranexamic acid:** Give early and empirically in major haemorrhage — hyperfibrinolysis is near-universal in trauma
8. **Lethal triad:** Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy — breaks DCR protocol when allowed to develop
9. **1:1 RBC:FFP ratio** in massive haemorrhage prevents dilutional coagulopathy
10. **Transfusion trigger:** Hb >10 g/dL is outdated; Hb >6 g/dL is acceptable in non-bleeding patients
11. **ABO incompatibility** = potentially fatal complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis
12. **Citrate toxicity** (hypocalcaemia) from rapid massive transfusion → replete Ca²⁺
13. **CVP** should be assessed dynamically (fluid challenge response), not as a single value
14. **Vasopressors before adequate preload** = dangerous; depletes myocardial O₂ and reduces coronary perfusion
15. **MOF mortality = 60%** — prevention by early shock reversal is the only strategy

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Shock** | Systemic state of low tissue perfusion inadequate for cellular respiration |
| **Occult hypoperfusion** | Normal HR/BP/UO with persistent lactate >2 mmol/L — tissue still underperfused |
| **ATC** | Acute traumatic coagulopathy; endogenous coagulopathy developing within minutes of injury |
| **TIC** | Trauma-induced coagulopathy; complex multifactorial coagulopathy amplified by resuscitation, hypothermia and acidosis |
| **DCR** | Damage control resuscitation; paradigm prioritising haemorrhage control and haemostatic resuscitation |
| **DCS** | Damage control surgery; staged surgical approach: haemorrhage/contamination control then ICU stabilisation then definitive repair |
| **Permissive hypotension** | Accepting low BP during active haemorrhage to avoid dislodging clots and worsening coagulopathy |
| **Lethal triad** | Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy — vicious cycle leading to death in trauma |
| **TRALI** | Transfusion-related acute lung injury; acute lung injury from donor antibodies (usually from FFP) |
| **Transient responder** | Temporary BP improvement with fluid bolus then BP drops — indicates ongoing active bleeding |
| **SvO₂** | Mixed venous oxygen saturation; normal 50–70%; guides assessment of perfusion adequacy |
