# Chapter 23: Anaesthesia and pain relief

## Learning objectives

To gain an understanding of:
- Techniques of anaesthesia and airway maintenance
- Methods of providing pain relief
- Local and regional anaesthesia techniques
- The management of chronic pain and pain from malignant disease

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Safe surgery depends on close collaboration between surgical and anaesthetic teams. Modern anaesthetists function as **perioperative physicians**, contributing to:
- Risk assessment and optimisation (pre-op)
- Safe anaesthesia and physiological homeostasis (intra-op)
- Pain control and complication prevention (post-op)

### Summary box 23.1: Ground rules for anaesthesia
- Safe surgery is achieved by close teamwork between surgeon and anaesthetist
- Safety checklists ensure that things are not forgotten
- Risk assessments allow the best strategy to be chosen
- Anaesthetists extend care into pre- and postoperative phases

---

## 2. Preparation for anaesthesia (shared responsibilities)

### 2.1 Preoperative assessment
Key elements:
- Comorbidity review and optimisation (see Chapter 21)
- Airway assessment and aspiration risk
- Medication plan (anticoagulants, insulin, steroids, antihypertensives)
- Fasting strategy and aspiration prevention
- Consent for anaesthetic technique(s), including discussion of risks

### 2.2 Safety systems
Operating theatre safety is improved by:
- WHO checklist (sign-in, time-out, sign-out)
- Team brief and explicit plan for anticipated difficulties (airway, bleeding, positioning)

---

## 3. General anaesthesia (GA)

### 3.1 Definition and the “triad”
GA is commonly described as a combination of:
- **Hypnosis** (unconsciousness/amnesia)
- **Analgesia** (pain suppression)
- **Muscle relaxation/immobility** (where needed)

### 3.2 Airway maintenance (high-yield)
Airway strategies include:
- Face mask ventilation
- Supraglottic airway devices
- Endotracheal intubation (cuffed tubes common in modern practice with appropriate technique)

Core principles:
- Anticipate difficult airway and plan with backup options
- Confirm ventilation and oxygenation continuously (capnography)
- Protect airway in aspiration risk situations

### 3.3 Physiological management during GA
Anaesthesia must preserve:
- Oxygenation and ventilation
- Circulation (avoid hypotension and organ hypoperfusion)
- Temperature (avoid hypothermia)
- Glucose and electrolytes (selected cases)

---

## 4. Regional and local anaesthesia

### 4.1 Advantages
- Reduced opioid requirement
- Better early mobilisation and respiratory mechanics
- May attenuate stress response in selected operations

### 4.2 Common techniques (overview)
- Local infiltration and field blocks
- Peripheral nerve blocks (single shot or catheter)
- Neuraxial: spinal and epidural (with careful patient selection)

### 4.3 Safety considerations
- Anticoagulation timing is critical for neuraxial techniques
- Monitor for local anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)
- Document block type, site, dose and monitoring

---

## 5. Acute postoperative pain management

### 5.1 Multimodal analgesia (core concept)
Use multiple complementary agents/techniques to reduce opioid dose and side effects:
- Paracetamol/acetaminophen
- NSAIDs (when safe)
- Local/regional anaesthesia
- Opioids (titrated) ± patient-controlled analgesia (PCA)
- Adjuncts in selected contexts (ketamine, gabapentinoids, lidocaine infusions, etc.)

### 5.2 Consequences of poor pain control
- Atelectasis and pneumonia (poor breathing/cough)
- Delayed mobilisation → VTE risk
- Ileus and prolonged LOS
- Risk of chronic postsurgical pain

---

## 6. Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV)

### Key concept
PONV is common and can prevent discharge and impair recovery.

Risk factors (typical):
- Female sex, nonsmoker, history of PONV/motion sickness, opioid use

Prevention:
- Risk stratify and use prophylaxis (multimodal antiemetics + opioid-sparing)

---

## 7. Chronic pain and malignant pain (principles)

### 7.1 Chronic non-malignant pain
Requires:
- Biopsychosocial approach
- Avoid escalating opioids without clear benefit
- Multidisciplinary pain services

### 7.2 Cancer pain
Principles:
- Stepwise pharmacological approach (opioids + adjuvants)
- Consider nerve blocks, neuraxial infusions, palliative radiotherapy and specialist input
- Prioritise function and comfort

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Teamwork is safety**: surgeon + anaesthetist planning reduces adverse events.
2. **GA requires airway + physiology control**, not just unconsciousness.
3. **Regional techniques** can transform recovery but require anticoagulation awareness.
4. **Multimodal analgesia** reduces opioid complications and improves mobilisation.
5. **PONV prevention** is a major determinant of day-case success and patient experience.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **General anaesthesia** | Drug-induced reversible unconsciousness with analgesia and autonomic control, often with airway support. |
| **Regional anaesthesia** | Blockade of nerve conduction to provide surgical anaesthesia/analgesia without unconsciousness. |
| **PCA** | Patient-controlled analgesia: patient-administered opioid dosing within safety limits. |
| **LAST** | Local anaesthetic systemic toxicity: CNS/cardiac toxicity from local anaesthetic absorption/overdose. |
