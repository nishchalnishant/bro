# Chapter 15: Human factors, patient safety and quality improvement

## Learning objectives

To learn:
- The importance of understanding human behaviour, quality and value in healthcare delivery
- The importance of human factors and teamworking in reducing and rectifying error
- Medical error and its definitions, including adverse events and near misses
- The importance of patient safety, strategies and application in clinical practice
- Quality improvement as an overarching activity
- The need for system thinking and leadership

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Modern healthcare is complex: many handovers, teams, technologies, and time-critical decisions. Harm often arises not from lack of knowledge, but from **system design interacting with human limitations**. Human factors and quality improvement aim to:
- Reduce preventable harm
- Improve reliability of care
- Increase value (outcomes relative to cost and patient experience)

### Clinical significance
- Surgical adverse events are frequently related to **communication, teamwork, workflow, fatigue, cognitive overload**, and **process variability**.
- High performance requires both technical skill and **safe systems**.

## 2. Value, quality and safety in healthcare

### 2.1 Value-based healthcare (high-yield overview)
**Value** can be framed as: outcomes important to patients / resources used.

Common system-level principles to support value (illustrative examples):
- Organise care around conditions and pathways (not departments)
- Measure outcomes and costs
- Align incentives with value
- Integrate services across sites
- Concentrate complex care in centres of excellence
- Use information technology to support continuity and measurement

### 2.2 Quality domains (classic framing)
High-quality care is commonly described as:
- Safe
- Effective
- Patient-centred
- Timely
- Efficient
- Equitable

## 3. Human factors (HF)

### Definition
**Human factors** is the study of how humans interact with elements of a system (tasks, tools, technology, environment, teams). In surgery, HF aims to design work so that:
- The system is resilient to predictable human limitations (attention, memory, fatigue)
- Errors are **detected early** and **contained**
- Teams perform reliably under stress

### 3.1 Why errors happen (systems thinking)
Most harmful events reflect a combination of:
- **Active failures** (frontline errors: slips, lapses, mistakes, violations)
- **Latent conditions** (poor design, staffing gaps, confusing equipment, inadequate training)

#### “Swiss cheese” model (high yield)
Multiple system barriers each have weaknesses; harm occurs when gaps align.

### 3.2 Types of human error (exam-friendly)
- **Slip**: correct plan, wrong execution (e.g. picking wrong syringe)
- **Lapse**: memory failure (e.g. forget to restart anticoag plan)
- **Mistake**: wrong plan due to knowledge/judgement (e.g. misdiagnosis)
- **Violation**: deviation from protocol (routine or exceptional)

### 3.3 Cognitive biases in surgery (common)
- Anchoring, premature closure, confirmation bias
- Availability bias (recent cases dominate thinking)
- Authority gradient (junior staff hesitate to challenge)

## 4. Patient safety: definitions and measurement

### 4.1 Key definitions
- **Adverse event**: harm caused by healthcare rather than disease
- **Preventable adverse event**: harm that could have been avoided by different actions/systems
- **Near miss**: event that could have caused harm but did not (by chance or interception)
- **Never event**: serious, largely preventable incident (e.g. wrong-site surgery)

### 4.2 Safety culture (what good looks like)
Features of a strong safety culture:
- Psychological safety (anyone can speak up)
- Learning orientation (blame-free analysis; accountability for reckless behaviour)
- Reliable reporting and feedback loops
- Standardised processes where appropriate

---

## 5. Strategies to improve safety in surgery

### 5.1 Standardisation and checklists
- WHO surgical safety checklist / sign-in, time-out, sign-out
- Structured briefings and debriefings
- Standard operating procedures for common emergencies (sepsis, haemorrhage)

### 5.2 Communication and handover
- Use structured handover tools (e.g. SBAR)
- Closed-loop communication in theatre/trauma/ICU
- Clear escalation pathways (“call criteria”)

### 5.3 Teamworking and leadership (theatre & ward)
- Shared mental model: “what are we doing and why?”
- Role clarity (who leads airway, haemorrhage control, documentation)
- Flatten authority gradients: invite challenge and confirm understanding

### 5.4 Designing for reliability
- Reduce reliance on memory (forcing functions, defaults, prompts)
- Simplify and standardise equipment layout where possible
- Fatigue management (staffing, breaks, rostering, task switching)

## 6. Quality improvement (QI)

### 6.1 QI vs audit vs research (high yield)
- **Audit**: are we meeting a standard? (measure vs benchmark)
- **QI**: how do we improve performance? (iterative change + measurement)
- **Research**: what is the best standard? (generate generalisable knowledge)

### 6.2 Core QI methods
#### Model for improvement (PDSA cycles)
Plan → Do → Study → Act:
- Small tests of change
- Rapid feedback
- Scale-up when successful

#### Process mapping and root cause analysis
- Map the pathway to reveal failure points
- Use structured analysis (e.g. fishbone/Ishikawa) to identify contributing factors

#### Measurement in QI
Use three types:
- **Outcome measures** (e.g. SSI rate, LOS)
- **Process measures** (e.g. antibiotics within 60 min)
- **Balancing measures** (e.g. C. difficile from antibiotic changes)

---

## 7. System thinking and leadership

### Key concept
Surgeons influence both bedside care and the system in which care is delivered. Effective leadership means:
- Seeing the whole pathway (pre-op → intra-op → post-op → discharge)
- Aligning teams around shared goals and reliable processes
- Using data to drive improvement without distorting clinical priorities

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Most errors are system problems**: focus on latent conditions, not just individual blame.
2. **Near misses are gold**: they reveal weak points before harm occurs—capture and learn from them.
3. **Checklists work** when paired with team culture (speak-up, briefings), not as a tick-box.
4. **QI needs balancing measures** to ensure improvement in one domain doesn’t cause harm elsewhere.
5. **Audit measures compliance; QI changes the system; research creates the standard.**
