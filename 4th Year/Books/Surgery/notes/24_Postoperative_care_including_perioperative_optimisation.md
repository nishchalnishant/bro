# Chapter 24: Postoperative care including perioperative optimisation

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- The integrated approach to caring for patients in the perioperative period
- Common postoperative problems seen in the immediate postoperative period
- How to predict, recognise, prevent and treat common postoperative complications
- The principles of enhanced recovery

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
**Perioperative care** is integrated multidisciplinary care delivered before, during and after surgery to improve outcomes and reduce cost. It includes:
- Pre-op risk assessment and optimisation (including prehabilitation)
- Intra-op harm prevention (checklists, physiology, temperature, haemostasis)
- Post-op complication prevention, early detection and rapid response

### Clinical significance
Many postoperative complications are predictable and preventable (pain-related respiratory compromise, fluid overload, delirium, VTE, infection). Early recognition is critical.

---

## 2. Multidisciplinary perioperative optimisation

### Team roles (typical)
- Surgery + anaesthesia/perioperative medicine
- Acute/elderly medicine, cardiology (selected)
- Nursing, physiotherapy, occupational therapy
- Dietetics, pharmacy

### Risk assessment and fitness
Approaches may include:
- Targeted blood tests and physiology review
- Functional reserve quantification (e.g. CPET in selected high-risk cases)

---

## 3. Immediate postoperative care (PACU / recovery)

### 3.1 Core priorities (ABCDE)
- **Airway**: patency, aspiration risk, OSA vigilance
- **Breathing**: oxygenation, ventilation, atelectasis prevention
- **Circulation**: haemorrhage, fluid status, perfusion, arrhythmias
- **Disability**: consciousness, pain control, glucose, temperature
- **Exposure**: wound/drains, pressure area care

### 3.2 Postoperative destination
Patients may require:
- Ward care (routine)
- PACU/overnight recovery unit for enhanced monitoring
- HDU/ICU when organ support or close monitoring is needed

---

## 4. Common postoperative problems (early)

### 4.1 Pain
Poorly controlled pain causes:
- Shallow breathing → atelectasis/pneumonia
- Delayed mobilisation → VTE and deconditioning
- Ileus, poor oral intake, stress response amplification

Management: multimodal analgesia and early reassessment (see Chapter 23).

### 4.2 Nausea and vomiting
- Treat and prevent with risk-based antiemetic strategies
- Protect wounds and prevent aspiration/dehydration

### 4.3 Hypoxia and respiratory complications
Common contributors:
- Atelectasis, aspiration, opioid-induced hypoventilation
- Pulmonary oedema from fluid overload
- Pulmonary embolism

Prevention:
- Early mobilisation, incentive spirometry, adequate analgesia
- Judicious fluids and VTE prophylaxis

### 4.4 Haemorrhage and shock
Red flags:
- Tachycardia, hypotension, falling Hb, rising lactate
- Increased drain output, expanding wound swelling

Management: prompt review, resuscitation, reversal of coagulopathy, return to theatre when needed.

### 4.5 Fluid and electrolyte disturbances
Common patterns:
- Hyponatraemia (inappropriate hypotonic fluids)
- Hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia (GI losses)
- AKI from hypoperfusion/nephrotoxins

Principles: daily weights, strict input/output, U&E monitoring, early AKI recognition and treatment.

### 4.6 Delirium
Risk factors:
- Age, frailty, dementia, infection, pain, sleep deprivation, polypharmacy

Prevention:
- Reorientation, mobilisation, sleep hygiene, pain control, avoid deliriogenic drugs where possible

## 5. Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) principles

### Key concept
ERAS reduces the physiological stress of surgery and accelerates functional recovery using multimodal measures across the pathway.

Core components:
- Minimal fasting and carbohydrate loading where appropriate
- Minimally invasive techniques
- Opioid-sparing analgesia; regional techniques where helpful
- Goal-directed fluid therapy (avoid overload)
- Early enteral feeding
- Early mobilisation
- Standardised pathways and auditing

---

## 6. Complication prevention bundles (high yield)

### VTE prevention
- Risk assessment + mechanical prophylaxis
- Pharmacological prophylaxis when safe
- Mobilisation

### Surgical site infection prevention
- Antibiotic timing/dosing, normothermia, glycaemic control
- Good wound care and asepsis

### “Failure to rescue” prevention
- Early warning scores
- Clear escalation criteria and rapid response teams

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **PACU is an ABCDE environment**: treat threats early; re-assess frequently.
2. **Pain control is respiratory care**: good analgesia prevents atelectasis and pneumonia.
3. **Fluid overload is harmful**: it worsens pulmonary function and delays gut recovery.
4. **Delirium is common and preventable**: mobilise, reorient, treat triggers.
5. **ERAS is multimodal**: single interventions rarely achieve the benefit alone.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Perioperative care** | Integrated multidisciplinary care before, during and after surgery to improve outcomes. |
| **PACU** | Post-anaesthesia care unit providing enhanced monitoring immediately post-op. |
| **ERAS** | Enhanced recovery after surgery: multimodal pathway to reduce stress response and speed recovery. |
| **Delirium** | Acute fluctuating disturbance of attention and cognition, often triggered by illness, drugs or environmental factors. |
