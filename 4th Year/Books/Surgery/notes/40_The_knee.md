# Chapter 40: The knee

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- The anatomy and biomechanics of the knee and their clinical implications
- The clinical presentation, aetiology and management of common knee pathologies
- The principles of joint replacement, including important complications
- The advances in surgical practice in this field

---

## 1. Applied anatomy

### Key concept
The knee is a synovial hinge joint comprising:
- Tibiofemoral compartments (medial and lateral)
- Patellofemoral joint

It is inherently less stable by bony shape and relies on:
- **Static stabilisers**: ligaments (ACL, PCL, MCL, LCL and posterolateral complex)
- **Dynamic stabilisers**: muscles (quadriceps, hamstrings, gastrocnemius, hip abductors)

### Menisci (high yield)
Medial and lateral menisci:
- Shock absorption and load distribution
- Contribute to stability

Loss or major disruption of meniscal hoop function accelerates degenerative change.

## 2. History and examination

### 2.1 History
Ask about:
- Pain location (medial joint line, anterior knee, diffuse OA pain)
- Swelling timing (immediate haemarthrosis suggests ACL rupture)
- Mechanical symptoms (locking/catching suggests meniscal pathology)
- Instability (“giving way”)
- Functional limits (stairs, squatting, walking distance)

### 2.2 Examination
- Inspect alignment, effusion, quadriceps wasting
- Palpate joint line tenderness
- ROM and crepitus
- Ligament tests (ACL/PCL/MCL/LCL)
- Meniscal provocation tests (interpret with context)

---

## 3. Common knee pathologies

### 3.1 Osteoarthritis (OA)
Features:
- Activity-related pain and stiffness
- Reduced function

Management:
- Weight management, physiotherapy, analgesia
- Injections selected
- Arthroplasty when severe disability persists

### 3.2 Meniscal tears
- Joint line pain, swelling, mechanical symptoms
- Degenerative tears common in older adults; traumatic tears in younger

Management:
- Physiotherapy and activity modification often first-line
- Arthroscopy for selected mechanical symptoms/repairable tears

### 3.3 ACL injury
- Pivot mechanism, “pop”, immediate swelling, instability
Management:
- Prehab and rehab; reconstruction for instability/high-demand athletes

### 3.4 MCL/LCL injuries
- MCL often heals with conservative care
- LCL/posterolateral injuries more complex; may require reconstruction

### 3.5 Patellofemoral pain and instability
- Anterior knee pain, worse with stairs/squats
- Manage with physiotherapy (hip and quad control), bracing/taping selected

---

## 4. Investigations

### Imaging (question-driven)
- X-ray: OA changes, alignment
- MRI: ligaments, menisci, cartilage, occult fracture

---

## 5. Knee replacement principles

### Indications
- Severe pain and functional loss despite maximal conservative management

### Important complications (must know)
- Infection
- VTE
- Stiffness
- Loosening/wear
- Periprosthetic fracture
- Neurovascular injury (rare but serious)

### Advances (overview)
- Enhanced recovery pathways
- Improved implant design and alignment techniques

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Immediate swelling after pivot injury** suggests ACL tear (haemarthrosis).
2. **Locking** may indicate meniscal tear; differentiate true locking from pain inhibition.
3. **MCL injuries** often heal conservatively; **LCL/PLC** injuries are less forgiving.
4. OA management starts with **weight loss and physiotherapy**; surgery is for function-limiting disease.
5. Knee arthroplasty complications to memorise: **infection, VTE, stiffness, loosening**.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **ACL** | Anterior cruciate ligament; key restraint to anterior tibial translation and rotational stability. |
| **Meniscal hoop stress** | Circumferential fibre tension distributing compressive load across meniscus. |
| **TKA** | Total knee arthroplasty. |
| **PLC** | Posterolateral corner structures contributing to lateral knee stability. |
