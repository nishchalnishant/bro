## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Paediatric urology covers congenital and acquired disorders of the urinary tract and genitalia in children. Good outcomes depend on:
- Correct diagnosis using age-appropriate imaging
- Timing of intervention to protect renal function and future continence/sexual function
- Gentle tissue handling and specialist follow-up with transition to adult services when needed

### Clinical significance
Unrecognised obstruction or recurrent infection can cause irreversible renal damage. Many conditions are congenital and require longitudinal care.

## 3. The penis: foreskin disorders and circumcision

### 3.1 Normal foreskin development
The foreskin is often non-retractile at birth and gradually separates from the glans in early childhood.

### 3.2 Common presentations
- Physiological non-retractability (often reassurance)
- Balanitis/balanoposthitis
- Pathological phimosis (e.g. scarring)
- Paraphimosis (emergency)

### 3.3 Indications for circumcision (typical)
- Pathological phimosis with scarring and symptoms
- Recurrent severe balanitis (selected cases)
- Recurrent UTIs in selected high-risk patients (context-specific)
- Cultural/religious indications (not medical, but requires safe practice and counselling)

### 3.4 Complications (must know)
- Bleeding, infection
- Meatal stenosis
- Excessive/insufficient skin removal, cosmetic issues
- Adhesions
- Rare: urethral injury

## 4. Urinary tract obstruction (three levels)

### 4.1 Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) obstruction
- Often presents as antenatal hydronephrosis
- Evaluate with ultrasound and functional imaging as indicated
- Manage based on severity, function, symptoms and infection risk

### 4.2 Ureterovesical junction (UVJ) obstruction / reflux spectrum
- Can present with hydronephrosis or UTI
- Imaging: ultrasound ± VCUG (voiding cystourethrogram) ± isotope scans
- Management ranges from observation/antibiotic prophylaxis to surgery depending on grade and renal impact

### 4.3 Posterior urethral valves (PUV)
- Male infants; bladder outlet obstruction
- Can cause severe bilateral hydronephrosis and renal impairment
- Requires urgent decompression and definitive valve ablation in specialist care

## 5. Hypospadias and epispadias

### Hypospadias
- Ventral urethral meatus displacement; may include chordee
- Surgical repair aims: straight penis, functional urethra, acceptable cosmesis

### Epispadias / exstrophy spectrum (overview)
- Dorsal meatus; may be associated with bladder exstrophy
- Specialist multidisciplinary management

---

## 6. Urolithiasis (stones) in children

### 6.1 Risk factors (high yield)
- Dehydration and low urine volume
- Metabolic disorders (hypercalciuria, cystinuria, uric acid disorders)
- Infection stones (urease-producing organisms)
- Anatomical abnormalities and urinary stasis

### 6.2 Management options (three common modalities)
- Conservative/medical: hydration, analgesia, metabolic evaluation and prevention
- Endoscopic: ureteroscopy and laser lithotripsy
- Shockwave lithotripsy (selected cases)
- Percutaneous approaches for large/complex stones (specialist)

## 7. Differences/disorders of sex development (DSD)

### Key concept
DSD involves variation in chromosomal, gonadal or anatomical sex development. Management requires:
- Urgent safeguarding of the child’s health (salt-wasting CAH is an emergency)
- Multidisciplinary specialist team (endocrinology, surgery, psychology, genetics)
- Careful communication and long-term support

Common broad categories:
- Sex chromosome DSD
- 46,XX DSD
- 46,XY DSD

## 8. Neuropathic bladder and reconstruction

### Problem
Neurogenic bladder can cause:
- High pressures → reflux and renal damage
- Incontinence and recurrent infections

### Surgical strategy (overview)
When conservative measures fail, reconstructive options can include:
- **Ileocystoplasty** (bladder augmentation)
- **Appendicovesicostomy** (continent catheterisable channel; Mitrofanoff principle)

Goals:
- Low-pressure storage
- Reliable emptying (often clean intermittent catheterisation)
- Renal protection and continence

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Most foreskins are normally non-retractile** in early childhood—reassurance is often correct.
2. **PUV is a renal emergency**: suspect in male infants with bilateral hydronephrosis/poor stream/UTIs.
3. **Antenatal hydronephrosis** needs structured postnatal follow-up imaging and renal protection strategy.
4. **DSD needs MDT care**; always consider life-threatening CAH in virilised 46,XX infants.
5. **Neurogenic bladder management** is kidney-protection first; reconstruction supports low-pressure storage and catheterisation.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Phimosis** | Inability to retract foreskin; physiological in young boys, pathological when scarred/symptomatic. |
| **UPJ obstruction** | Functional/anatomical blockage at ureteropelvic junction causing hydronephrosis. |
| **PUV** | Posterior urethral valves causing bladder outlet obstruction in boys. |
| **Hypospadias** | Ventral displacement of urethral meatus, often with chordee. |
| **Mitrofanoff** | Continent catheterisable channel (often appendix) to facilitate bladder emptying. |
