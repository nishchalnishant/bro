# Chapter 43: Infection of the bones and joints

## Learning Objectives
- Differentiate between septic arthritis, acute/chronic osteomyelitis, and implant infections
- Understand diagnostic principles, including the role of biomarkers and imaging
- Learn management strategies for native and prosthetic joint infections

---

## 1. Pathophysiological Concepts
- **Biofilm:** A complex community of bacteria enveloped in a polysaccharide matrix on dead bone or implants. Highly resistant to antibiotics and host immunity.
- **Sequestrum:** A fragment of dead, devascularised bone that acts as a reservoir for infection.
- **Involucrum:** A sheath of new bone laid down by the periosteum around a sequestrum.
- **Sinus:** A tract communicating the infected bone/joint with the skin surface.

---

## 2. Microbiology (High Yield)
- **Staphylococcus aureus:** Most common pathogen across all orthopaedic infections.
- **Coagulase-negative Staphylococci (e.g., S. epidermidis):** Common in implant-related infections.
- **Kingella kingae:** Common cause of septic arthritis in children **under 4 years**.
- **Salmonella spp.:** Classically associated with osteomyelitis in **Sickle Cell Disease**.
- **Cutibacterium acnes:** Frequent in shoulder implant infections (slow-growing; requires 10-14 days of culture).

---

## 3. Diagnostic Principles

### 3.1 Biomarkers
- **ESR and CRP:** Characteristically raised in acute infection but not specific enough to rule infection in or out.
- **Synovial Markers:** $\alpha$-defensin and calprotectin are highly accurate for PJI diagnosis.

### 3.2 Imaging
- **X-rays:** Often normal in the first few days. Later shows lysis, sequestration, or implant loosening.
- **MRI:** Investigation of choice for native bone/joint (highly sensitive for marrow oedema).
- **CT:** Best for identifying sequestra and planning surgical excision.

### 3.3 The "Gold Standard" Biopsy
- **Technique:** At least **5 tissue samples** must be taken, each using a **separate, sterile set of instruments** to avoid cross-contamination.
- **Histology:** Presence of $\ge$ 5 neutrophils per high-power field (HPF) is diagnostic for PJI.

---

## 4. Septic Arthritis (Native Joint)
- **Clinical Feature:** A **medical emergency**. Single hot, swollen, painful joint with "pseudoparalysis" (immobility due to pain).
- **Diagnosis:** Aspiration for WCC and Gram stain.
- **Management:** 
  - **Surgical:** Prompt drainage (Arthroscopic or Open washout).
  - **Medical:** 3-6 weeks of targeted antibiotics.

---

## 5. Prosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

### 5.1 Classification by Timing
1. **Early (<3 months):** acquired during surgery; virulent (*S. aureus*).
2. **Delayed (3–24 months):** acquired during surgery; low-virulence (*S. epidermidis*).
3. **Late (>24 months):** haematogenous spread from a distant focus (UTI, dental, etc.).

### 5.2 Surgical Strategies
- **DAIR (Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention):** Only for **well-fixed** implants. Involves exchange of modular parts (liners) and thorough debridement.
- **One-Stage Revision:** Removal and reimplantation in the same session.
- **Two-Stage Revision:** Gold standard for many. 1st stage: Removal + Antibiotic-loaded Spacer. 2nd stage: Reimplantation after 6 weeks of antibiotics.
- **Excision Arthroplasty (Girdlestone):** Permanent removal of the joint; palliative for frail patients.

---

## 6. Osteomyelitis

### 6.1 Acute Osteomyelitis
- Common in children. Presents as systemic upset and local pain.
- Requires prompt antibiotics to prevent transition to chronic disease.

### 6.2 Chronic Osteomyelitis
- Characterised by the presence of dead bone (**sequestrum**).
- **Management:** Radical excision of all dead tissue ("Cierny-Mader" classification guides this) and management of the resulting dead space (e.g., muscle flap or antibiotic beads).

---

## Summary Boxes

### Box 43.1: The "Don'ts" of Orthopaedic Infection
- **Do not** rely on superficial swabs (they show skin contaminants, not deep pathogens).
- **Do not** give antibiotics before sampling in stable patients (masking effect).
- **Do not** assume a negative X-ray rules out early acute osteomyelitis.

---

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **Biofilm** is why infected metalwork often must be removed; antibiotics cannot penetrate the matrix effectively.
2. **Sickle cell + bone pain/fever** = think **Salmonella** osteomyelitis.
3. **PJI in any painful joint replacement:** Assume infection until proven otherwise (check CRP/ESR).
4. **OVIVA Trial:** High-quality evidence that oral antibiotics can be as effective as IV for bone/joint infections.
