# Chapter 33: Disaster surgery

## Learning objectives

To recognise and understand:
- The common features of various disasters
- The principles behind the organisation of the relief effort and of triage in treatment and evacuation
- The role and limitations of field hospitals in disaster
- The features of conditions peculiar to disaster situations and their treatment

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Disasters can be natural (earthquake, flood, tsunami) or man-made (industrial accidents, terrorism, conflict-related). They share common characteristics: **mass casualties** and disruption of infrastructure and communication. Surgical care must shift from individual-optimised practice to population-focused practice: **“do the best for the most.”**

---

## 2. Common features of major disasters

### Summary box 33.1: Common features of major disasters
- Massive casualties
- Damage to infrastructure
- Large numbers requiring shelter
- Panic and uncertainty
- Limited access to the area
- Breakdown of communication

### Practical implications
- Hospitals and emergency services may themselves be damaged/overwhelmed
- Supply chains (oxygen, blood, drugs) may be unreliable
- Surge capacity and coordination become core determinants of survival

---

## 3. Organisation of relief and medical aid

### 3.1 Command and coordination
Effective response requires:
- Clear incident command structure
- Rapid situational awareness (information gathering, mapping)
- Logistics for water, sanitation, shelter and security

### 3.2 Communication
Communication failure is expected; redundancy (radio, satellite systems) improves coordination and patient flow.

### 3.3 Patient flow
Key principle: triage and evacuation are continuous processes, not one-time events.

---

## 4. Triage in disasters

### Key concept
Triage sorts patients by urgency and resource needs to maximise survival.

### Typical triage categories (conceptual)
- Immediate (life-saving intervention required now)
- Delayed (can wait)
- Minimal (walking wounded)
- Expectant (unlikely to survive given available resources)

### Pitfalls
- Over-triage consumes scarce resources
- Under-triage increases preventable death
- Re-triage is necessary as patients and resources change

---

## 5. Field hospitals: role and limitations

### Role
Field hospitals can provide:
- Basic resuscitation and stabilisation
- Essential surgery (damage control)
- Basic post-op care

### Limitations
- Restricted diagnostics (imaging/lab)
- Limited blood products and ICU capability
- Sterility, staffing and supply constraints

Key principle: perform **staged, stabilising surgery** and evacuate where definitive care is required.

---

## 6. Conditions peculiar to disaster settings

### 6.1 Crush syndrome
After entrapment:
- Rhabdomyolysis → hyperkalaemia, AKI, shock

Management principles:
- Early fluids and monitoring
- Treat hyperkalaemia and renal failure promptly

### 6.2 Wound contamination and infection
High contamination rates; delayed presentation common.
- Early debridement and delayed primary closure often appropriate
- Tetanus prophylaxis and antibiotics where indicated

### 6.3 Hypothermia and exposure
Particularly in floods/cold environments; worsens coagulopathy and shock.

## Summary Boxes

### Box 33.2: Disaster surgery priorities
- Command + communication
- Triage and patient flow
- Damage control surgery where appropriate
- Infection prevention (debridement, tetanus)
- Evacuation and continuity of care

### Box 33.3: Triage principle
- Allocate scarce resources to maximise overall survival, with frequent re-triage.

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Disasters overwhelm systems**: planning and coordination are as important as surgical skill.
2. **Triage is dynamic**: reassess continuously as patient status and resources change.
3. **Field hospitals are limited**: prioritise stabilisation and damage control, not complex definitive reconstruction.
4. **Crush syndrome kills after rescue**: anticipate hyperkalaemia and AKI early.
5. **Debridement and infection control** are central in contaminated disaster wounds.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Disaster** | Event causing mass casualties and disruption of societal response capacity. |
| **Triage** | Sorting patients by urgency and resource need to maximise survival. |
| **Crush syndrome** | Systemic effects of muscle breakdown after compression (rhabdomyolysis → hyperkalaemia, AKI). |
| **Field hospital** | Temporary medical facility providing stabilisation and essential surgical care with limited resources. |
