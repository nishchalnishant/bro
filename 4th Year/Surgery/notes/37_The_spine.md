# Chapter 37: The spine

## Learning objectives

To learn:
- The salient features relating to the history and examination of the spine
- The investigations commonly used in the field of spinal disorders
- The treatment principles for common conditions affecting the spine
- The global issues in spinal surgery

---

## 1. Epidemiology

### Key concept
Back pain is extremely common, while true radicular sciatica is less frequent. Most acute episodes resolve, but a minority develop chronic disability.

### Summary box 37.1: Epidemiology
- Low back pain is extremely common
- ~90% of acute low back pain episodes settle within 6 weeks
- Sciatica is much less frequent
- ~70% of acute sciatic episodes settle within 3 months

---

## 2. Clinical anatomy (high yield)

### Curvatures and sagittal balance (concept)
- Cervical lordosis and lumbar lordosis balance thoracic kyphosis for efficient posture.

### Spinal canal and nerve roots
- Central canal contains the thecal sac; spinal cord typically ends around L1 with cauda equina below.
- Intervertebral foramina transmit nerve roots; degenerative changes can cause nerve compression.

---

## 3. History and examination of the spine

### 3.1 History
Key domains:
- Pain location (neck vs thoracic vs lumbar), onset, trauma, progression
- Mechanical vs inflammatory features
- Radicular symptoms: dermatomal pain, numbness, weakness
- Red flags: cancer, infection, fracture, cauda equina
- Impact on function: walking distance, sitting tolerance, sleep, work

### Red flags (must ask)
- Fever, immunosuppression, IV drug use (infection)
- Unexplained weight loss, night pain (malignancy)
- Significant trauma/osteoporosis (fracture)
- Saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention/incontinence (cauda equina)

### 3.2 Examination
- Inspect posture, gait, scoliosis/kyphosis
- Palpate for focal tenderness and step deformity
- Range of motion (pain-limited patterns)
- Neurology: motor, sensory, reflexes
- Special tests: straight leg raise, femoral stretch, myelopathy signs

---

## 4. Investigations

### Key principle
Investigate based on clinical question and red flags.

Common investigations:
- X-ray: alignment, gross degenerative change, fracture suspicion
- MRI: discs, nerve roots, cord, infection, tumour (most informative soft tissue)
- CT: bony detail, complex fractures, pre-op planning
- Labs (when indicated): inflammatory markers, infection workup

---

## 5. Common spinal conditions (overview)

### 5.1 Non-specific low back pain
- Most common; treat with reassurance, activity, analgesia and physiotherapy focus.

### 5.2 Disc herniation and radiculopathy (sciatica)
- Dermatomal pain ± neurological deficit
- Many improve with conservative care; surgery considered for persistent deficit/pain or red flags.

### 5.3 Spinal stenosis
- Neurogenic claudication: leg pain/heaviness with walking, relieved by sitting/flexion
- Management: physio/analgesia; decompression surgery in selected cases.

### 5.4 Spondylolisthesis and instability
- Mechanical back pain; may cause radicular symptoms
- Imaging-guided management; surgery in selected instability/neurological compromise.

### 5.5 Infection and tumour (do not miss)
- Constant pain, systemic symptoms, elevated inflammatory markers (often)
- Urgent imaging and specialist referral required.

---

## 6. Treatment principles

### Conservative care
- Education, maintain activity, targeted physiotherapy
- Analgesia escalation judiciously
- Address psychosocial contributors (fear avoidance, work factors)

### Interventional/surgical (selected)
- Decompression for stenosis with significant disability
- Microdiscectomy for persistent radiculopathy with correlating imaging
- Stabilisation/fusion in selected instability contexts

### Rehabilitation
Functional recovery depends on:
- Early mobilisation
- Core strengthening and graded return to activity

---

## 7. Global issues in spinal surgery (overview)

### Key themes
- High prevalence makes access and resource allocation important
- Variation in practice; need for evidence-based indications
- Balancing surgery benefits against risk, cost and long-term outcomes

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Most acute low back pain resolves**; avoid unnecessary imaging without red flags.
2. **Cauda equina symptoms are emergencies**: saddle anaesthesia and urinary dysfunction require urgent MRI.
3. **Neurogenic claudication** improves with flexion and sitting—classic stenosis pattern.
4. **MRI is the key** for disc, cord, infection and tumour evaluation.
5. Always document a **full neurological exam** when radicular symptoms are present.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Sciatica** | Radicular pain radiating along a dermatomal distribution, commonly from lumbar nerve root irritation. |
| **Neurogenic claudication** | Leg symptoms on walking from spinal canal stenosis, relieved by rest/flexion. |
| **Cauda equina syndrome** | Compression of lumbosacral nerve roots causing saddle anaesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction and weakness. |
| **Stenosis** | Narrowing of spinal canal or foramina leading to neural compression. |
