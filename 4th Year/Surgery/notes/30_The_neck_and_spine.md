# Chapter 30: The neck and spine

## Learning objectives

To be familiar with:
- The accurate assessment of spinal trauma
- The basic management of spinal trauma and the major pitfalls
- The pathophysiology and types of spinal cord injury
- The prognosis of spinal cord injury, factors affecting functional outcome and common associated complications

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Spinal trauma threatens:
- **Mechanical stability** of the spine
- **Neurological function** (spinal cord/nerve roots)

Outcome is strongly influenced by early recognition, immobilisation, safe imaging, and prevention of secondary cord injury from hypoxia, hypotension and ongoing compression.

---

## 2. Anatomy essentials (exam-oriented)

### 2.1 Motion segment
A spinal **motion segment** consists of:
- Two adjacent vertebrae
- Intervertebral disc
- Ligamentous restraints (anterior/posterior longitudinal, interspinous, supraspinous, facet capsule)

### 2.2 Regional variations and injury-prone junctions
- Upper cervical spine: highly mobile; stability depends heavily on ligaments.
- Transitional zones (common injury sites):
  - Cervicothoracic junction
  - Thoracolumbar junction

---

## 3. Assessment of spinal trauma

### 3.1 Clinical priorities
Assume spinal injury in high-risk mechanisms until excluded.

Key steps:
- Immobilisation (balanced against airway and haemorrhage priorities)
- Full neurological assessment and documentation
- Identify red flags: midline tenderness, neurological deficit, altered mental state, distracting injuries

### 3.2 Neurological examination (must document)
- Motor power by myotomes
- Sensory levels
- Reflexes and tone
- Perianal sensation and anal tone (when indicated)
- Bladder function (retention)

### 3.3 Imaging approach (principles)
- CT is common for bony injury assessment in significant trauma
- MRI evaluates spinal cord, ligaments, discs and haematoma; critical when neurological deficit with normal CT or suspected ligament injury

Pitfall: inadequate imaging of junctional areas can miss unstable injuries.

---

## 4. Pathophysiology of spinal cord injury (SCI)

### 4.1 Primary vs secondary injury
- Primary: mechanical damage at impact (not reversible)
- Secondary: evolving injury from oedema, ischaemia, inflammation, hypotension/hypoxia (preventable)

### 4.2 Types of SCI (overview)
SCI patterns reflect mechanism and level. Clinically important considerations:
- Complete vs incomplete injury
- High cervical injuries risk ventilatory failure

### 4.3 Neurogenic shock (high yield)
In high spinal cord injury:
- Loss of sympathetic tone → hypotension, bradycardia, warm peripheries
Management requires:
- Volume assessment and fluids as appropriate
- Vasopressors to maintain perfusion targets per protocol
- Temperature management and intensive monitoring

---

## 5. Initial management principles

### 5.1 General trauma priorities
- Airway and oxygenation first (prevent hypoxia)
- Treat haemorrhage and avoid hypotension (protect spinal cord perfusion)
- Maintain normothermia

### 5.2 Spine-specific measures
- Immobilise and handle with log-roll precautions when feasible
- Early specialist referral for unstable injuries or neurological deficit
- Urgent decompression/stabilisation when indicated (time-sensitive in selected cases)

---

## 6. Prognosis and complications

### Factors affecting outcome
- Level and completeness of injury
- Speed of decompression/stabilisation when indicated
- Prevention of secondary insults (hypoxia/hypotension)
- Quality of rehabilitation

### Common complications
- Respiratory failure (high cervical injury)
- Pressure injuries
- VTE
- Autonomic dysreflexia (chronic high SCI)
- Neurogenic bladder/bowel dysfunction
- Chronic pain and spasticity

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Document neurology early**—it defines baseline and guides urgency.
2. **Hypoxia and hypotension worsen SCI**: preventing secondary injury is as important as immobilisation.
3. **Junctional injuries are often missed**—ensure adequate imaging of C-spine and junctions.
4. **Neurogenic shock** is hypotension with bradycardia; don’t confuse with haemorrhagic shock (tachycardia).
5. **MRI** is key when neurology is abnormal but CT is “normal”.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SCI** | Spinal cord injury. |
| **Motion segment** | Functional spinal unit of two adjacent vertebrae + disc + ligaments. |
| **Neurogenic shock** | Shock from loss of sympathetic tone after high SCI causing hypotension and bradycardia. |
| **Secondary cord injury** | Ongoing ischaemic/inflammatory damage after the primary insult, worsened by hypoxia/hypotension. |
