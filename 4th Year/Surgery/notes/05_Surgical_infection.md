# Chapter 5: Surgical infection

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the characteristics of common surgical pathogens and their sensitivities

## 1. History of Surgical Infection

Early civilisations identified the importance of avoiding putrefaction (e.g. Egyptians) and the use of antimicrobials (e.g. wine and vinegar by Hippocrates). Ignaz Semmelweis proved the value of handwashing. Louis Pasteur established the germ theory, and Joseph Lister applied it to prevent infection via antiseptics.

### Box 5.1: Koch’s Postulates
To prove a given organism is the cause of a given disease:
1. It must be found in every case.
2. It should be possible to isolate it from the host and grow it in culture.
3. It should reproduce the disease when injected into another healthy host.
4. It should be recovered from the experimentally infected host.

## 2. Microbiology of Surgical Infection

### 2.1 Streptococci
- Gram-positive, chain-forming.
- **Group A (*Streptococcus pyogenes*):** Most pathogenic. Capable of causing spreading cellulitis through enzymatic release (streptokinase, hyaluronidase).
- *Streptococcus faecalis* (Group D) is an enterococcus often found in synergy during bowel infections.
- Most remain sensitive to penicillin and erythromycin.

### 2.2 Staphylococci
- Gram-positive, clump-forming.
- ***Staphylococcus aureus*:** Major cause of localised suppurative wound infections and prosthetic infections. 
- **MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant *S. aureus*):** A major concern in hospitals. Often managed via 'search and destroy' screening policies prior to elective surgery.
- ***Staphylococcus epidermidis*:** Coagulase-negative. Forms *biofilms* on prostheses and indwelling vascular cannulae.

### 2.3 Clostridia
- Gram-positive, obligate anaerobic, spore-bearing bacilli.
- ***C. perfringens*:** Gas gangrene.
- ***C. tetani*:** Tetanus.
- ***C. difficile*:** Pseudomembranous colitis (follows antibiotic destruction of normal colonic flora, particular risk with quinolones like ciprofloxacin). Treated with metronidazole or oral vancomycin.

### 2.4 Aerobic Gram-Negative Bacilli
- Normal large bowel inhabitants (*E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Proteus*).
- Act in synergy with *Bacteroides* to cause Surgical Site Infections (SSIs) post-bowel surgery.
- *Pseudomonas* often colonises burns, tracheostomies, and urinary tracts (treated with aminoglycosides or quinolones).

### 2.5 Bacteroides
- Non-spore-bearing strict anaerobes.
- ***B. fragilis*:** Principal organism acting in synergy with Gram-negatives in intra-abdominal abscesses. Sensitive to metronidazole.

## 3. Sources of Infection

SSIs are classified by source:
- **Endogenous:** Acquired from the host's own flora (e.g., perforated appendix). Most common type of SSI today.
- **Exogenous:** Acquired from outside the body (e.g., theatre environment, staff hands). Cause of Hospital-Acquired Infections (HAIs).

### Box 5.4 & 5.5: Risk factors for an infected wound
- **Localised Surgical Factors:** Poor technique, devitalised tissue, dead space, haematoma, foreign bodies, poor perfusion.
- **Systemic Host Factors:** Malnutrition, obesity, metabolic disease (diabetes, uraemia, jaundice), immunosuppression (steroids, chemo), systemic shock.

### 3.1 The 'Decisive Period'
There is a 4-hour window before bacterial growth becomes established after a tissue breach. Prophylactic antibiotics are highly effective at preventing infection *during* this period, but become ineffective thereafter.

## 4. Presentation of Surgical Infection

### 4.1 Abscess
- Presents with *calor, rubor, dolor, tumor, functio laesa*.
- Walled off by granulation and fibrous tissue. Extremely hyperosmolar, drawing in fluid and causing intense pain under pressure.
- **Treatment:** Abscesses *must* be drained. If left open to granulate/drain freely, antibiotics are generally not required. If closed or if systemic signs are present, use antibiotics.

### 4.2 Cellulitis and Lymphangitis
- Cellulitis: Non-suppurative, highly invasive spreading infection of tissues (commonly Streptococcal).
- Lymphangitis: Painful red streaks tracking along lymphatics.
- Requires aggressive antibiotic treatment.

### 4.3 Specific Local Infections
- **Gas Gangrene (*C. perfringens*):** Thin, sweet-smelling, brown exudate. Crepitus (tissues bubbling with gas). High mortality. Treated with radical debridement and high-dose IV penicillin.
- **Necrotising Fasciitis (Synergistic spreading gangrene):** Fascial necrosis via mixed synergistic microbes. Known as Meleney's gangrene (abdominal wall) or Fournier's gangrene (scrotum). Tested via the "**finger test**" (lack of bleeding and unresisted finger sweep along fascia under local anaesthesia). Requires aggressive and wide operative excision immediately.

### 4.4 Systemic Infections
- **Bacteraemia:** Organisms in blood. Dangerous if patient has prosthetics.
- **SIRS (Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome):** Needs 2/3 of: Temp >38°C/<36°C, HR>90 or RR>20, WBC >12/<4. Unchecked SIRS progresses to MODS (Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome).
- **Sepsis Six (Resuscitation Bundle):** 
  - GIVE: Oxygen, IV fluids, IV antibiotics.
  - TAKE: Blood cultures, lactate, full blood count / urine output monitoring.

## 5. Prevention of Infection

### 5.1 Operating Environment
- Preoperative skin shaving should occur *immediately* before surgery in theatre, not the night before (which doubles SSI risk).
- Skin preparation with alcohol-based chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine.
- Intraoperative temperature maintenance (normothermia) and supplemental oxygen strongly reduce SSI rates.

### 5.2 Prophylactic Antibiotics
Not required in "Clean" surgery unless a prosthesis is placed. Strongly indicated in "Clean-contaminated" and "Contaminated" procedures.
- Must be administered IV around induction of anaesthesia to be effective centrally during the *decisive period*.
- Only repeat the dose if there is extreme blood loss or a very long operation. Continued postoperative prophylaxis is generally not beneficial.

## 6. Antimicrobial Treatments

1. **Penicillins:** Act by breaking the bacterial cell wall synthesis. Benzylpenicillin is excellent against Streptococcus and Clostridium.
2. **Flucloxacillin:** Beta-lactamase resistant penicillin. First-line for non-MRSA Staphylococcal skin infections.
3. **Co-amoxiclav:** Amoxicillin paired with clavulanic acid (which binds beta-lactamase). Good broad Gram-negative/positive cover.
4. **Cephalosporins (e.g. Cefuroxime, Ceftazidime):** Useful broad-spectrum, but enterococci are inherently resistant.
5. **Aminoglycosides (Gentamicin):** Gram-negative activity. Inactive against anaerobes/strep. Require regular trough/peak serum monitoring to prevent *ototoxicity* and *nephrotoxicity*.
6. **Vancomycin / Teicoplanin:** Glycopeptide agents of choice against MRSA. Also requires therapeutic drug monitoring.
7. **Metronidazole:** Gold-standard against strict anaerobes like *Bacteroides fragilis* and *Clostridium*.

### A Note on Antibiotic Regimens for the Abdomen
For perforated bowel, empirical "triple therapy" historically covered all bases: 
- Amoxicillin (Gram Positive / Enterococci)
- Gentamicin (Gram Negatives)
- Metronidazole (Anaerobes)

Modern variations might simply use a carbapenem or combined broad-spectrum agents (e.g. Tazosin), depending on local hospital guidelines.

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **Never close an abscess cavity** unless absolutely necessary or explicitly managed for specific small lesions. Allowing open drainage dramatically lowers recurrence.
2. **The "Finger Test":** If you suspect necrotising fasciitis, sweep a gloved finger along the deep fascia through a small incision. If it slides freely without resistance, there is fascial necrosis, and you must radically debride immediately.
3. **Clostridium Difficile:** The classic trigger is broad-spectrum antibiotics (especially Cephalosporins and Ciprofloxacin). Diagnosed via Toxins in stool or GDH antigen; treated strictly with oral Vancomycin or Metronidazole.
4. **Shaving Patients:** Tell the nurse NOT to shave the patient on the ward the night before surgery. Hair should be clipped/shaved in theatre right before the prep to prevent bacterial proliferation in micro-abrasions.

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SIRS** | Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome. |
| **MODS** | Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome resulting from excessive, damaging SIRS. |
| **Decisive period** | Time window (up to 4 hours) post-tissue breach where bacteria try to establish growth. |
| **Sepsis** | SIRS coupled with a documented, definitive source of infection. |
