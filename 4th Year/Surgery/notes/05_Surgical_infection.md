## 1. History of Surgical Infection

### Ancient Understanding
- Egyptians: Prevented putrefaction; used salves and antiseptics (documented in papyruses)
- Assyrians/Greeks/Romans: Independent recognition of antiseptic principles
- Hippocrates: Used wine and vinegar to irrigate infected wounds; delayed primary or secondary closure for contaminated wounds
- Galen: Recognised that localised pus formation (suppuration) often heralded recovery — *pus bonum et laudabile*

### 19th Century Milestones

| Person | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| **Ignaz Semmelweis** | Proved value of handwashing — reduced puerperal sepsis from >10% to <2% |
| **Louis Pasteur** | Germ theory — microorganisms cause disease |
| **Joseph Lister** | Applied germ theory to surgery — antiseptic technique with carbolic acid (1860s) |
| **Robert Koch (1882)** | Stated Koch's Postulates — defined proof of a causative organism |
| **Alexander Fleming (1928)** | Discovered penicillin (*Penicillium notatum*) |
| **Florey & Chain (1941)** | Isolated penicillin for clinical use |

### Koch's Postulates (Summary Box 5.1)
To prove a given organism is the cause of a given disease, it must:
1. Be found in **every case** of the disease
2. Be possible to **isolate it from the host and grow it in culture**
3. **Reproduce the disease when injected** into another healthy host
4. Be **recovered from the experimentally infected host**

### Advances in Control of Surgical Infection (Summary Box 5.2)
- Aseptic operating theatre techniques + antiseptics
- Antibiotics reduced postoperative infection rates
- Delayed primary or secondary closure remains useful in heavily contaminated wounds
- Faecal peritonitis: previously inevitably fatal; now >90% heal primarily with antibiotics

## 2. Microbiology of Surgical Infection

### 2.1 Streptococci
- **Gram-positive cocci**, arrange in chains
- Classified by Lancefield carbohydrate antigens (A–G)

| Type | Key Species | Features |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Group A (β-haemolytic)** | *Streptococcus pyogenes* | Most pathogenic; spreads via enzymes (streptolysin, streptokinase, streptodornase, hyaluronidase); causes cellulitis, necrotising fasciitis |
| **Group D (γ-haemolytic enterococcus)** | *Streptococcus faecalis* | Synergistic infections with other organisms; bowel surgery SSIs |
| **α-haemolytic (Viridans)** | *Streptococcus viridans* | NOT associated with wound infections; but risk for endocarditis |

**Sensitivity:** All streptococci remain **sensitive to penicillin and erythromycin**. Cephalosporins are suitable alternatives in penicillin allergy.

### 2.2 Staphylococci
- **Gram-positive cocci**, form clumps

#### *Staphylococcus aureus*
- Found in nasopharynx of up to **15%** of population (carriers)
- Causes: **suppurative, localised wound infections**; prosthetic infections; bacteraemia
- Most hospital strains are **β-lactamase producers** → resistant to benzylpenicillin
- Sensitive to: **flucloxacillin**, vancomycin, aminoglycosides, some cephalosporins
- Newer agents: linezolid (good oral activity), teicoplanin (wide spectrum), daptomycin (bacteraemia)

#### MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant *S. aureus*)
- Found in nasopharynx of asymptomatic carriers (patients AND hospital workers)
- Major concern in hospital practice
- **Management:** "Search and destroy" screening before elective surgery
- Requires: widespread swabbing, ward closure if outbreak, patient isolation, deep cleaning
- **Treatment:** Only vancomycin/teicoplanin are reliable (glycopeptide antibiotics)

#### *Staphylococcus epidermidis* (Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus)
- Previously thought non-pathogenic
- Now a **major threat** in:
  - Vascular and orthopaedic prosthetic surgery
  - Indwelling vascular cannulae/catheters
- Mechanism: **Biofilm formation** on prosthetic surfaces → limits antibiotic effectiveness
- Biofilm makes it very difficult to eradicate without removing the prosthesis

### 2.3 Clostridia
- **Gram-positive, obligate anaerobic, spore-bearing bacilli** (Figure 5.3)

| Species | Disease | Features |
|---------|---------|---------|
| ***C. perfringens*** | Gas gangrene | Proteases (hyaluronidase, lecithinase, haemolysin, collagenase) → spreading gangrene; gas + sweet-smelling brown exudate |
| ***C. tetani*** | Tetanus | Exotoxin tetanospasmin → trismus ("lockjaw"), opisthotonus, generalised spasms, respiratory arrest |
| ***C. difficile*** | Pseudomembranous colitis | Follows antibiotic-induced destruction of normal colonic flora; fibrinous exudate; highest risk with **quinolones (ciprofloxacin)** |

**C. difficile details:**
- Diagnosed by: **GDH antigen** rapid test + **Toxin A/B** rapid test (results in <30 minutes)
- Treatment: Metronidazole or oral vancomycin (empirical while awaiting results)
- Most severe form: Toxic megacolon → perforation → emergency colectomy + high mortality
- ⚠️ Hand gels with 70% alcohol do NOT destroy C. difficile spores — handwashing with soap and water is required

### 2.4 Aerobic Gram-Negative Bacilli
- Normal large bowel inhabitants
- **Species:** *E. coli* (lactose-fermenting), *Klebsiella* (lactose-fermenting), *Proteus* (non-lactose fermenting)
- **Role in SSI:** Act in synergy with *Bacteroides* to cause SSIs after bowel operations (appendicitis, diverticulitis, peritonitis)
- *E. coli*: major cause of UTIs (especially with catheterisation)
- **Increasing concern:** ESBL (extended-spectrum β-lactamases) → resistance to many antibiotics, particularly cephalosporins

**Pseudomonas:**
- Colonises burns, tracheostomy wounds, urinary tract
- Hard to eradicate from wards/ICUs once established
- Resistance transferred by plasmids
- Treatment: aminoglycosides, quinolones, some carbapenems (meropenem)

### 2.5 Bacteroides
- **Non-spore-bearing, strict anaerobes** — colonise large bowel, vagina, oropharynx
- ***Bacteroides fragilis*:** Principal organism acting in synergy with aerobic Gram-negatives
- Causes: Intra-abdominal abscesses after colorectal/gynaecological surgery
- **Sensitivity: Metronidazole** (gold standard) + some cephalosporins (cefotaxime)

## 3. Sources of Infection

### Classification (Summary Box 5.3)

| Source | Definition | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Endogenous** | Organisms present ON or IN the patient at time of surgery | SSSI following contamination from perforated appendix |
| **Exogenous** | Acquired from a source OUTSIDE the patient | HAI from theatre (poor air filtration, poor antisepsis) or ward (poor handwashing) |

> In modern hospital practice, **endogenous organisms** are by far the most common source of SSI.

### Host Defence Mechanisms
1. **Physical barriers:** Intact skin and epithelium
2. **Chemical:** Low gastric pH
3. **Humoral:** Antibodies, complement, opsonins
4. **Cellular:** Macrophages, PMNs, killer lymphocytes

### Factors Determining Whether a Wound Becomes Infected (Summary Box 5.4)
1. **Host response** (immune status)
2. **Virulence and inoculum** of infective agent
3. **Vascularity and health** of invaded tissue (local ischaemia, systemic shock)
4. **Presence of dead or foreign tissue**
5. **Presence of antibiotics during the 'decisive period'**

### Risk Factors for Increased Wound Infection (Summary Box 5.5)
- Malnutrition (obesity AND weight loss — both impair healing)
- Metabolic disease: diabetes, uraemia, jaundice
- Immunosuppression: cancer, AIDS, steroids, chemotherapy, radiotherapy
- Colonisation and translocation in the GI tract
- Poor perfusion: systemic shock or local ischaemia
- Foreign body material
- Poor surgical technique: devitalised tissue, dead space, haematoma

### The Decisive Period
- There is a **4-hour window** after a tissue breach before bacterial growth becomes established
- Prophylactic antibiotics are highly effective **within** this window
- Become **ineffective** once bacterial growth is established
- Tissue antibiotic levels must be above **MIC90** (minimum inhibitory concentration for 90% of expected pathogens)

### Gut Translocation and SIRS/MODS (Figure 5.6)
```
Starvation + sepsis + immunosuppression
      ↓
Normal upper GI sterility lost
      ↓
Aerobic Gram-negative bacilli colonise upper GI tract
      ↓
Translocation to mesenteric nodes
      ↓
Release of endotoxins (LPS from Gram-negative cell walls)
      ↓
Macrophage activation → Cytokine release (IL-6, TNF-α)
      ↓
SIRS → MODS
```

### Opportunistic Infection
- In states of reduced host resistance, normally non-pathogenic organisms become pathogenic
- Common opportunistic infections: Fungi (especially with prolonged/changing antibiotic regimens), CMV, PCP in HIV

## 4. Presentation of Surgical Infection

### 4.1 Surgical Site Infection (SSI) — Classification

**By location:**
- **Superficial SSI (SSSI):** Wound itself
- **Deep SSI:** Deeper musculofascial layers
- **Organ/space SSI:** Intra-abdominal abscess (e.g., anastomotic leak, perforated appendix)

**By severity:**

| Category | Features |
|----------|---------|
| **Major SSI** | Significant pus (spontaneous or requiring drainage); patient systemically ill (tachycardia, pyrexia, raised WBC); delayed discharge |
| **Minor SSI** | Small pus/infected serous fluid; not associated with excessive discomfort or systemic signs or delayed discharge |

**Important timing:** 75% of SSIs present **after the patient has left hospital** — often missed by the surgical team.

Suppurative wound infections: take **7–10 days** to develop.
Cellulitis (e.g., β-haemolytic Streptococcus): takes **3–4 days** to develop.

### 4.2 Abscess

**Definition:** Localised collection of pus surrounded by granulation tissue.

**Classic signs (Celsus):** Calor (heat), Rubor (redness), Dolor (pain), Tumor (swelling), Functio laesa (loss of function)

**Pathogenesis:**
```
Pyogenic organism (usually S. aureus)
      ↓
Tissue necrosis + suppuration
      ↓
Pus = dead neutrophils succumbed to bacterial toxins
      ↓
Surrounded by acute inflammation (fibrinous exudate + oedema)
      ↓
Granulation tissue (macrophages + fibroblasts + new vessels) → collagen deposition
      ↓
Hyperosmolar contents draw in fluid → ↑ pressure → pain
      ↓
Tracks along planes of least resistance → Points to skin surface
```

**Summary Box 5.7: Key Principles of Abscess Management**
- **Abscesses MUST be drained** — incision and drainage or needle aspiration
- Open abscess cavity heals by **secondary intention** — no antibiotics needed if left open
- Antibiotics indicated **only if abscess cavity is closed** after drainage
- Do NOT close if risk of retained loculi or foreign material

**Special abscesses:**
- **Perianal abscess:** Can be incised, drained, curetted, and closed with antibiotics (good results)
- **Pilonidal abscess:** Higher recurrence risk with closure — hair nidus may remain
- **Breast abscess (small):** Needle aspiration + antibiotics may be sufficient
- **Intra-abdominal abscess:** US/CT/MRI guided aspiration (avoids surgery)

**Complications of untreated abscess:**
- Sinus or fistula formation (especially with *Mycobacterium*, *Actinomyces*)
- Chronic abscess with lymphocytes and plasma cells
- Calcification may occur

### 4.3 Cellulitis and Lymphangitis

**Cellulitis:**
- Non-suppurative, invasive spreading infection of tissues
- **Poor localisation** (diffuse, not walled off)
- Organisms: β-haemolytic streptococci (most common), staphylococci, *C. perfringens*
- Systemic signs (toxaemia): chills, fever, rigors from toxin release into circulation
- Blood cultures often **negative** (toxaemia without bacteraemia)
- Treatment: **Aggressive antibiotics** (penicillin for streptococcal; flucloxacillin for staphylococcal)

**Lymphangitis:**
- Painful red streaks tracking along affected lymphatics
- Accompanied by painful, enlarged regional lymph nodes (cervical, axillary, inguinal)
- Part of same spreading infection process

### 4.4 Gas Gangrene (*C. perfringens*)

- Gram-positive, anaerobic, spore-bearing bacilli — found in soil and faeces
- Ideal conditions: devitalised tissue, necrotic/foreign material, anaerobic environment (military wounds, vascular amputations)

**Virulence factors:** Collagenase, hyaluronidase, lecithinase (alpha toxin), haemolysin — all allow spread through tissues

**Clinical Features (Summary Box 5.9):**
- Severe **local wound pain** (early)
- **Crepitus** (gas in tissues — bubbling on palpation; may show on X-ray)
- **Thin, brown, sweet-smelling exudate** (characteristic)
- Gas visible on plain radiographs
- Oedema + spreading gangrene
- Rapid systemic: circulatory collapse, organ failure

**Treatment:**
1. **Large doses IV benzylpenicillin** (high-dose)
2. **Aggressive surgical debridement** of all affected tissue
3. Hyperbaric oxygen (adjunct in some centres)
4. Antibiotic prophylaxis for amputations for peripheral vascular disease (risk of contamination from open necrotic ulceration)

### 4.5 Tetanus (*C. tetani*)

- Anaerobic, terminal spore-bearing, Gram-positive bacterium
- Spores widespread in soil and manure
- **Exotoxin: Tetanospasmin** — causes disease

**Clinical Sequence:**
```
Contaminated wound → Clostridium tetani implanted
      ↓
Tetanospasmin released
      ↓
Spasms in short motor nerves (face first) → Trismus (lockjaw)
      ↓
Severe generalised motor spasms → Opisthotonus
      ↓
Respiratory arrest → Death
```

**Management:**
- Prevention: **Tetanus toxoid vaccination** (most important)
- Treatment of established infection:
  1. Wound debridement
  2. High-dose benzylpenicillin
  3. Muscle relaxants
  4. Ventilation (in severe forms — high mortality)

### 4.6 Necrotising Fasciitis (Synergistic Spreading Gangrene)

**Synonyms:** Subdermal gangrene, Meleney's gangrene (abdominal wall), Fournier's gangrene (scrotum)

**Polymicrobial** (most common): Coliforms + staphylococci + *Bacteroides* + anaerobic streptococci + peptostreptococci (aerobic bacteria destroy tissue → anaerobic bacteria thrive)

**Risk factors:** Immunocompromised, diabetes mellitus; minor contaminated wound can be initiating event.

**The Finger Test (Diagnostic):**
```
1. Infiltrate suspected area with local anaesthesia
2. Make 2-cm incision down to deep fascia
3. Insert gloved index finger along deep fascia
4. Positive test = Tissue dissects with MINIMAL RESISTANCE (no bleeding + unresisted sweep)
5. Dishwater-coloured fluid may seep from wound
6. Tissue biopsies for frozen section: obliterative vasculitis + acute inflammation + subcutaneous necrosis
```
> **Positive finger test OR progressive clinical features = IMMEDIATE OPERATIVE TREATMENT**

**Treatment:**
1. **Urgent wide excision** of necrotic tissue (same day — do NOT delay)
2. **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics** + aggressive circulatory support
3. Laying open of all affected areas
4. Second-look in 24–48 hours; multiple debridements required
5. Later: Large area skin grafting needed for survivors
6. **ICU support**

### 4.7 Bacteraemia

- Unusual after superficial SSI (drains through wound)
- **Common** after deep space SSI (anastomotic breakdown, bile/urine instrumentation)
- Usually transient; follow instrumenting infected tissues
- Indwelling IV cannulae → replace regularly to avoid colonisation
- **Dangerous if prosthesis present** → haematogenous seeding of prosthesis
- Organisms: aerobic Gram-negatives, *S. aureus*, fungi

**Summary Box 5.10:** Bacteraemia is common after anastomotic breakdown; dangerous with prosthetics; associated with systemic organ failure.

### 4.8 SIRS, Sepsis, Severe Sepsis, MODS, MSOF (Table 5.1)

| State | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| **SIRS** | ≥2 of: Temp >38°C or <36°C; HR >90/min; WBC >12×10⁹/L or <4×10⁹/L |
| **Sepsis** | SIRS + **documented source of infection** |
| **Severe sepsis** | Sepsis + **evidence of failure of one or more organs** |
| **Septic shock** | Severe sepsis + haemodynamic compromise despite fluid resuscitation |
| **MODS** | Systemic effect of unchecked SIRS — dysfunction of multiple organs |
| **MSOF** | Multiple System Organ Failure — end stage of uncontrolled MODS |

**Pathophysiology of SIRS → MODS:**
```
Serious infection → LPS endotoxin from dying Gram-negative bacilli
      ↓
Macrophage activation → Cytokine release (IL-1, TNF-α)
      ↓
Neutrophil activation → Adherence to distant vascular endothelium
      ↓
Respiratory burst → Lysosomal enzymes + oxidants + free radicals
      ↓
Remote organ endothelial damage → ↑ vascular permeability
      ↓
Organ dysfunction → MODS → MSOF → Death
```

**Summary Box 5.11: Definitions of Infected States**
- SSI: infected wound or deep organ space
- SIRS: body's systemic response to severe infection
- MODS: effect that SIRS produces systemically
- MSOF: end stage of uncontrolled MODS

### 4.9 Surviving Sepsis Campaign — Sepsis Six

**Sepsis Bundle:** Evidence-based objectives to be completed **within 6 hours** of diagnosis of severe sepsis/septic shock/lactate >4 mmol/L.

**Sepsis Six (developed by UK's Sepsis Trust):**

| Give (3) | Take (3) |
|----------|---------|
| 1. **Oxygen** | 4. **Blood cultures** (before antibiotics) |
| 2. **IV fluid challenge** | 5. **Full blood count** |
| 3. **IV antibiotics** | 6. **Lactate** |

Also: **Monitor urine output**

> Mnemonic: **"3 in, 3 out"** — Give O₂, fluids, antibiotics; Take cultures, FBC, lactate

## 5. Viral Infections Relevant to Surgery

### 5.1 Hepatitis B and C

- Both are **blood-borne pathogens** — risk of transmission from patient to surgeon AND vice versa
- Usual mode: **Needle-stick injury or cut**
- Hepatitis B: Many carriers are asymptomatic (surgeon may not know their status)
  - **Effective vaccine available** — surgeons should know their immune status and be vaccinated
- Hepatitis C: Often becomes chronic; risk of liver damage; potentially curable with antiviral therapy

### 5.2 HIV

- Retrovirus; transmitted via body fluids (especially blood), sexual contact, IV drug use, contaminated blood products
- Most surgical risk: **Needle-stick injury during operations**
- Greatest transmission risk during **early seroconversion phase**

**AIDS complications:** Opportunistic infections (PCP, TB, CMV) and neoplasms (Kaposi's sarcoma, lymphoma)

**Treatment:** HAART (Highly Active Antiretroviral Therapy) — inhibits reverse transcriptase and protease

### 5.3 Universal Precautions (CDC/NHS)
- **Full face mask** (ideally) or protective spectacles
- Fully waterproof disposable gowns and drapes (especially during seroconversion)
- **Boots** (not clogs) — protection from dropped sharps
- **Double gloving** (larger size inside for comfort)
- Minimal theatre personnel; minimal movement
- Respect for sharps — **pass in kidney dish**
- Slow, meticulous operative technique

**Post-exposure protocol:** Wash under running water → Report immediately → Local policy for PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) → Occupational health advice + HIV/hepatitis testing

### 5.4 COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2)

- Aetiology: SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus
- Symptoms: Fever, cough, fatigue, dyspnoea, loss of smell/taste; incubation 1–14 days
- Severe complications: ARDS (cytokine storm), multiorgan failure, septic shock, hypercoagulable states
- Surgical implications:
  - Elective surgery paused during peaks
  - Emergency and cancer surgery continued with PPE
  - Preoperative cocooning 10–14 days + COVID-19 swab test 2–3 days before elective surgery

## 6. Prevention of Surgical Infection

### 6.1 Preoperative Preparation

**Hospital admission:** Short stay before surgery → lower risk of acquiring MRSA and resistant organisms.

**Hand hygiene:**
- Handwashing between ALL patient contacts (most important)
- 70% alcohol hand gels: good substitute for handwashing BUT do NOT destroy *C. difficile* spores
- Staff with open infected skin lesions must NOT enter operating theatres

**Hair removal:**
- **Do NOT shave the night before** — micro-abrasions enhance bacterial colonisation → **doubles SSI rate in clean surgery**
- Shave/clip in the operating theatre **immediately before surgery**

**Skin preparation:**
- Alcohol-based chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine (concentrated)
- One application → **>95% reduction in bacterial count**
- ⚠️ Do not pool alcohol on skin — risk of ignition with diathermy

### 6.2 Operating Theatre Technique

- Minimise theatre staff; minimise movement in/out of theatre
- **Laminar flow:** Directs clean filtered air over operative field; any contaminated air directed away from patient
- Careful, gentle tissue handling — dead space and haematomas increase infection risk
- No evidence that drains, incision drapes, or wound guards reduce wound infection
- **High-level evidence:** Perioperative **normothermia** and **supplemental oxygen** significantly reduce SSI rates

### 6.3 Prophylactic Antibiotics (Summary Box 5.12)

**Infection rates by wound class (Table 5.2):**

| Surgery Type | With Prophylaxis | Without Prophylaxis |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Clean (no viscus opened) | 1–2% | 1–2% |
| Clean-contaminated (viscus opened, minimal spillage) | 3% | 6–9% |
| Contaminated (open viscus with spillage/inflammatory disease) | 6% | 13–20% |
| Dirty (pus, perforation, incision through abscess) | 7% | 40% |

**Key Principles:**
- **NOT required** in clean surgery (unless prosthesis is implanted — even small infection risk is catastrophic)
- **Strongly indicated** in clean-contaminated and contaminated surgery
- **When to give:** At **induction of anaesthesia** (IV) — maximises tissue levels during decisive period; avoids timing uncertainty
- **Single dose** is sufficient; no evidence for continuing post-operatively
- **Repeat dose if:** Long operation (>4 hours), excessive blood loss, unexpected contamination (4-hourly intervals during surgery)
- Continued post-op prophylaxis only encourages antibiotic resistance

**Special groups requiring antibiotic prophylaxis:**
- Patients with **valvular heart disease** or **implanted prostheses** undergoing dental, urological, or open viscus surgery — risk of haematogenous prosthesis/valve seeding

## 7. Antimicrobial Treatment of Surgical Infection

### 7.1 Principles (Summary Box 5.14)
- **Antibiotics do NOT replace surgical drainage** — abscess requires drainage; antibiotics alone insufficient
- Only spreading infections or systemic signs justify antibiotics
- **Culture before starting antibiotics** whenever possible (don't miss the opportunity)
- Empirical choice initially; modify based on culture and sensitivity
- If poor response at 3–4 days: re-evaluate, review cultures, repeat investigations
- Changing antibiotics promotes resistance and risks complications (C. diff)

### 7.2 Antibiotic Reference Guide

#### A. Penicillins

| Antibiotic | Spectrum | Key Uses | Notes |
|-----------|---------|---------|-------|
| **Benzylpenicillin** | Gram-positive (Streptococcus, Clostridium, Actinomyces) | Streptococcal cellulitis; gas gangrene; spreading infection | High-dose IV for gas gangrene |
| **Flucloxacillin** | β-lactamase resistant; Staphylococcal | Non-MRSA staphylococcal skin infections, osteomyelitis | First-line non-MRSA staph; good tissue penetration |
| **Amoxicillin** | Enterobacteriaceae, Enterococcus, Group D Strep | Oral/IV; not Klebsiella or Pseudomonas | |
| **Co-amoxiclav** | Amoxicillin + clavulanate (β-lactamase inhibitor) | MRSA-negative S. aureus, E. coli, Haemophilus, Bacteroides, Klebsiella | Broad; covers β-lactamase producers |
| **Piperacillin-tazobactam (Tazocin)** | Very broad: Gram-pos, Gram-neg, anaerobes, Pseudomonas | Septicaemia, hospital-acquired pneumonia, complex UTI | NOT against MRSA; synergistic with aminoglycosides |

#### B. Cephalosporins

| Antibiotic | Spectrum | Key Uses |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Cefuroxime** | Gram-positive + most Enterobacteriaceae | Intra-abdominal, skin and soft-tissue infections; prophylaxis |
| **Cefotaxime** | Similar to cefuroxime + better Gram-negative | Intra-abdominal infections |
| **Ceftazidime** | Gram-negative + *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* | Pseudomonas infections; + aminoglycoside if needed |

> ⚠️ **Enterococci (S. faecalis) are inherently resistant** to ALL cephalosporins
> ⚠️ ESBLs confer resistance to cephalosporins in many Gram-negative organisms

#### C. Aminoglycosides (Gentamicin, Tobramycin)

- **Spectrum:** Gram-negative Enterobacteriaceae; Pseudomonas (gentamicin)
- **NOT active against:** Anaerobes, Streptococci
- **Monitoring:** Serum levels — **peak (1 hour post-dose)** and **trough (pre-dose)** at start and 48 hours
- **Toxicity:** **Ototoxicity** and **Nephrotoxicity** — dose-related; single daily dosing may be safer
- Combined with: cephalosporins (Gram-negative cover) + metronidazole (anaerobic cover)

#### D. Glycopeptides (Vancomycin, Teicoplanin)

- **Spectrum:** Gram-positive aerobes and anaerobes; **MRSA** (drug of choice)
- **Also effective against:** *C. difficile* pseudomembranous colitis
- **Monitoring required:** Serum levels (ototoxic and nephrotoxic)
- Used as prophylaxis when high MRSA risk
- Teicoplanin: wider spectrum; oral availability (unlike vancomycin in some formulations)

#### E. Metronidazole

- **Spectrum:** Active against ALL anaerobic bacteria
- **Organisms:** Bacteroides, Clostridia, anaerobic cocci
- **Routes:** Oral, rectal (PR), intravenous — safe and flexible
- **Uses:** Prophylaxis + treatment of anaerobic infections after abdominal, colorectal, pelvic surgery; *C. difficile* colitis
- **Key drug** for any abdominal/pelvic anaerobic cover

#### F. Carbapenems (Meropenem, Ertapenem, Imipenem)

- **Spectrum:** Stable to β-lactamase; broad-spectrum — anaerobes + Gram-positive
- **Uses:** Resistant organisms (ESBL Gram-negatives), serious mixed abdominal infections (peritonitis)
- Reserved for resistant/complex infections

#### G. Quinolones (Ciprofloxacin)

- **Spectrum:** Broad — Gram-positive and Gram-negative; especially Pseudomonas
- **Caution:** Many hospitals restrict use — highest risk of triggering *C. difficile* colitis (especially in elderly/immunocompromised)

#### H. Linezolid (Oxazolidinone)

- **Spectrum:** Gram-positive, MRSA
- **Advantage:** Good **oral bioavailability** (unlike vancomycin)
- Expensive; risk of bone marrow toxicity with prolonged use

### 7.3 Abdominal/Peritonitis Antibiotic Regimens

**Triple therapy (classical):**
- Amoxicillin → Gram-positive/Enterococcal cover
- Gentamicin → Gram-negative cover
- Metronidazole → Anaerobic cover

**Modern alternatives:**
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam (Tazosin)** + metronidazole (broad spectrum)
- **Meropenem** (carbapenem — single agent, very broad) ± metronidazole
- Choice guided by local microbiological guidelines and sensitivities

## 8. Antibiotic Resistance

### Mechanisms of Resistance
- **β-lactamase production:** Breaks β-lactam ring of penicillins/cephalosporins
- **ESBL (Extended-spectrum β-lactamases):** Confer resistance to cephalosporins in Gram-negatives
- **Plasmid-mediated transfer:** Resistance transferred between bacteria
- **MRSA:** Altered penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a) → methicillin/penicillin resistance

### Prevention of Resistance
- Targeted (narrow-spectrum) antibiotics when organism known
- Avoid unnecessary antibiotic prescribing
- Single-dose prophylaxis (not prolonged)
- Microbiological guidance for complex infections
- Antibiotic rotation in resistant Pseudomonas/Klebsiella units
- Hand hygiene and barrier precautions to prevent spread of resistant organisms

- Not needed for clean surgery (unless prosthesis); give IV at induction; single dose; repeat only if long/bloody operation

### Box 5.14: Antibiotic Therapy Principles
- Doesn't replace surgical drainage; culture first; empirical initially; modify on sensitivities

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. **The decisive period is 4 hours** — prophylactic antibiotics must be given before bacterial growth establishes
2. **Prophylaxis timing:** IV at induction of anaesthesia (NOT the day before; NOT continued post-op)
3. **Clean surgery without prosthesis:** Prophylactic antibiotics NOT required (low infection rate, benefit doesn't outweigh resistance risk)
4. **Clean surgery WITH prosthesis:** Prophylaxis IS required (catastrophic consequences if infected)
5. **Shaving:** Never the night before — doubles SSI rate. Clip/shave in theatre immediately before surgery
6. **Normothermia + supplemental O₂:** High-level evidence for reducing SSI rates
7. **MRSA treatment:** ONLY glycopeptides (vancomycin/teicoplanin) are reliable
8. **MRSA prevention:** Screen and decolonise before elective surgery ("search and destroy")
9. **S. epidermidis:** Pathogenic on prosthetic surfaces via biofilm — key risk in orthopaedic/vascular prosthetic surgery
10. **C. difficile:** Triggered by antibiotics (especially ciprofloxacin/quinolones); diagnosed by GDH antigen + toxin A/B; treated with metronidazole or oral vancomycin; alcohol gel does NOT kill spores
11. **Abscess:** MUST be drained — antibiotics alone are insufficient. If left open: no antibiotics needed. If closed: give antibiotics
12. **Necrotising fasciitis:** Finger test is diagnostic; DO NOT delay surgery for investigations
13. **Gas gangrene:** Sweet-smelling brown exudate + crepitus + pain = C. perfringens → high-dose IV penicillin + aggressive debridement
14. **Sepsis Six (give 3, take 3):** O₂ + fluids + IV antibiotics; Blood cultures + FBC + lactate
15. **75% of SSIs present AFTER discharge** — careful outpatient follow-up is essential
16. **Aminoglycosides:** Monitor peak and trough levels; risk of ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity; not active against anaerobes or streptococci
17. **Metronidazole:** Gold standard for anaerobic cover; covers Bacteroides + Clostridium; multiple routes of administration
18. **Endotoxin (LPS):** From Gram-negative bacterial cell walls → macrophage activation → cytokine storm → SIRS

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SSI** | Surgical site infection — infected wound or deep organ space |
| **SIRS** | Systemic inflammatory response syndrome: ≥2 of Temp >38/<36°C, HR>90, WBC>12/<4 |
| **Sepsis** | SIRS + documented source of infection |
| **MODS** | Multiple organ dysfunction syndrome — systemic effect of uncontrolled SIRS |
| **MSOF** | Multiple system organ failure — end-stage uncontrolled MODS |
| **HAI** | Hospital-acquired infection — from theatre environment, staff, or ward sources |
| **Decisive period** | 4-hour window after tissue breach before bacterial growth establishes; prophylaxis effective only within this period |
| **Endogenous infection** | Infection from patient's own flora (most common SSI source in modern practice) |
| **Exogenous infection** | Infection from outside the patient (cause of HAI) |
| **Bacteraemia** | Organisms in the bloodstream — transient or sustained |
| **MRSA** | Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus — resistant to all penicillins; treated with glycopeptides |
| **ESBL** | Extended-spectrum β-lactamases — enzymes in Gram-negative bacteria that destroy most cephalosporins |
| **Biofilm** | Layer of bacteria on prosthetic surfaces (especially S. epidermidis) — limits antibiotic penetration |
| **Meleney's gangrene** | Necrotising fasciitis of the abdominal wall |
| **Fournier's gangrene** | Necrotising fasciitis of the scrotum/perineum |
| **Opportunistic infection** | Infection by normally non-pathogenic organisms in immunocompromised host |
| **MIC90** | Minimum inhibitory concentration that inhibits 90% of expected pathogen strains |
| **Antibioma** | Chronic abscess partly sterilised by antibiotics but not fully resolved — palpable but not fully drainable |
