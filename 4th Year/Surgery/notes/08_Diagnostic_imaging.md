# Chapter 8: Diagnostic imaging

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the advantages of a good working relationship with the imaging department.
- Command the basic principles of radiation protection and the laws regarding ionising radiation.
- Review the principles, advantages, and disadvantages of different imaging modalities (Radiography, US, CT, MRI, Nuclear Medicine).
- Understand the role of imaging in directing treatment across various surgical scenarios, especially trauma, acute abdomen, and oncology.

---

## 1. Requesting and Interpreting Images

### 1.1 Criteria for a Useful Investigation
Before requesting imaging, ensure the result will actively inform clinical management. Ask these five questions:
1. **Has it been done already?** Avoid duplicating tests.
2. **Is it needed?** Will the result alter management?
3. **Is it needed now?** Investigate when the disease could have progressed/resolved appropriately.
4. **Is this the best investigation?** Choose the modality most suited for the pathology.
5. **Has the problem been properly explained?** Give the radiologist an exact clinical question and past history (e.g., allergies, renal function).

### 1.2 Systematic Checking of Radiographs
Always use a system: 
- **Label:** Check patient name, date, and Left/Right markers.
- **Site:** Is the correct region centered? Are there adequate views (usually minimum two at right angles)?
- **Quality:** Adequate penetration.
- **Compare:** Look at previous films.
- **Conclude:** Make a diagnosis; decide if further imaging is needed.

## 2. Hazards of Imaging

### 2.1 Contrast Media
- **Iodinated Contrast (for CT/Radiography):** 
  - *Allergic reactions:* Serious reactions occur in ~1:2500 cases. Low-Osmolality Contrast Media (LOCM) are extremely safe. Keep high-risk patients observed for 30 minutes with IV access intact.
  - *Nephrotoxicity:* Danger is high in severe renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²). Pre-hydrate patients and use minimum LOCM doses. Stop Metformin if eGFR <30 (intravenous) due to the risk of lactic acidosis.
- **Gadolinium (for MRI):**
  - Extremely rare allergic reactions. However, it is contraindicated in severe renal failure due to the risk of **Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis (NSF)**, a severe hardening of skin and connective tissues.

### 2.2 Ionising Radiation
- *Deterministic (predictable) effects:* e.g., cataracts (from chronic exposure).
- *Stochastic (random) effects:* e.g., Cancer. Has no safe threshold; probability increases with dose.
- **Dose Context:** 
  - A CT of the Abdomen/Pelvis delivers a radiation dose equivalent to approximately **500 chest x-rays** (Lifetime excess cancer risk ~1:1000).
- **Current Legislation:** In the UK, IR(ME)R places a legal duty on the radiologist to ensure investigations are justified, avoiding unnecessary radiation exposure.

## 3. Imaging Modalities

### 3.1 Conventional Radiography & Fluoroscopy
- Relies on differential X-ray absorption (Bone/Metal > Soft tissue/Fluid > Fat > Air).
- **Strengths:** Cheap, universal, highly effective for bone, lung, and foreign bodies. 
- **Limitations:** Poor soft-tissue differentiation. 
- **Fluoroscopy:** Real-time imaging used heavily for interventional guidance and examining bowel motility (Barium studies). 

### 3.2 Ultrasound (US)
- Echoes of high-frequency sound waves. *Doppler* measures frequency shifts to calculate blood flow.
- **Strengths:** No radiation, cheap, highly dynamic (can interact with patients). Best first-line method for **biliary tract, female pelvis, testes, thyroid, and soft tissue cystic masses.**
- **Limitations:** Cannot see through bone or bowel gas. Highly operator-dependent. Deep structures are hard to see in obese patients.

### 3.3 Computed Tomography (CT)
- Fast spinning X-ray tube creating multi-planar slice reconstructions.
- **Strengths:** Global assessment of the thorax, abdomen, and pelvis in seconds. High spatial and contrast resolution (utilising arterial, venous, and delayed contrast phases). 
- **Limitations:** Very high radiation dose. Poor differentiation of certain superficial soft tissues.

### 3.4 Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
- Uses a strong magnetic field and radiofrequency pulses to align and flip odd-numbered protons. 
  - **T1-weighted:** Best for defining anatomy (Fat is bright).
  - **T2-weighted:** Best for pathology (Water/fluid is bright).
- **Strengths:** Exquisite soft-tissue contrast, multiplanar capability. Excellent for neurology, spine, musculoskeletal, and cancer staging (e.g., rectum). MRCP for the biliary tree.
- **Limitations:** Expensive, long scan times (motion artifact prone). **Contraindicated** in patients with pacemakers, cochlear implants, or certain aneurysm clips. Severe claustrophobia issues.

### 3.5 Nuclear Medicine and PET/CT
- Patients are injected with an active radionuclide (e.g., Technetium-99m) and evaluated using a gamma camera. This provides **functional**, rather than just anatomical, data.
- **PET/CT:** Uses ¹⁸F-Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) to image high glucose metabolism (tumours/infection). Superb for staging lymphoma, lung cancer, and assessing liver metastases resectability.

## 4. Imaging in Orthopaedic Surgery

- **Trauma:** First-line is plain radiographs (minimum 2 perpendicular views). If occult or involving the spine/complex joints, proceed to CT.
- **Joint Disease (Synovitis/Cartilage):** Early synovitis is best seen on Ultrasound or Gadolinium-enhanced MRI. MR Arthrography is the gold standard for labral/articular surface defects.
- **Bone Tumours:** Plain films dictate whether it is benign/aggressive. MRI is mandatory next for local staging of both soft tissue and bone margins before biopsy. 
- **Infection:** X-rays exclude gross erosion. US is highly sensitive for joint effusions (and guiding aspiration). **MRI** is the modality of choice for diagnosing early/acute osteomyelitis showing subperiosteal oedema.

## 5. Imaging in Major Trauma (ATLS Protocol)

- **Plain Radiographs:** Supine CXR and Pelvic X-ray. (Cervical spine X-ray has largely been replaced by CT in the polytrauma patient).
- **Ultrasound (FAST):** Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma looks for free intraperitoneal fluid and haemopericardium. It is a rapid triage tool for the unstable patient.
- **Trauma CT ("Doughnut of Truth"):** Due to multidetector speeds, a "Pan Scan" (Head to Pelvis) is performed within 5 minutes on stable patients. 
  - Excellent for rapidly diagnosing extradural/subdural hematomas, visceral lacerations (spleen/liver/kidneys), and complex spinal fractures.
- **Vascular Interventional Radiology:** Endovascular embolisation using spongy gelatin or coils is increasingly used for solid organ hemorrhage or massive pelvic fracture bleeding.

## 6. Imaging the Acute Abdomen

- **Bowel Obstruction:** Small bowel vs Large bowel. Standard modern diagnostic test is **CT**, which identifies the **transition point** between dilated and collapsed bowel, defines the cause (e.g., adhesion, tumor, closed-loop), and assesses for evolving bowel ischemia.
- **Perforation:** An **Erect Chest X-ray** is the classic first test (detecting as little as 10-20 mL of subdiaphragmatic gas). **CT** is definitive if the X-ray is equivocal. (*Note: Never use oral barium if perforation is suspected!*)
- **Ischaemia:** Diagnosed via **Contrast CT**. Shows bowel wall thickening, submucosal edema, and in grave cases, pneumatosis intestinalis (air in the bowel wall) continuing into the portal vein.
- **Appendicitis / Diverticulitis:** 
  - *Appendicitis:* US is first-line in children and young females (to rule out gynae conditions). **CT is the gold standard in adults**. Look for an appendix >7mm with periappendiceal fat stranding.
  - *Diverticulitis:* Best diagnosed with CT showing pericolic inflammation/abscessation securely ruling out perforation.
- **Pancreatitis:** US confirms gallstones but **CT with IV contrast (done at 48-72 hours)** is required to establish severity, specifically assessing for necrosis (areas of non-enhancing parenchyma).
- **Biliary Tract:** **Ultrasound is the absolute first-line investigation** for cholecystitis, biliary colic, and jaundice. MRCP accurately rules out Common Bile Duct stones pre-cholecystectomy.
- **Renal Colic:** **Non-contrast CT KUB** is the gold standard (superior to IVU/US). Identifies highly hyperdense calculi and secondary signs of obstruction.
- **Leaking AAA:** **CT Angiogram** provides definitive diagnosis and delineates anatomical requirements if EVAR/open repair is planned.

## 7. Imaging in Oncology (TNM Staging)

The MDT dictates precision staging determining respectability vs. neoadjuvant modes.
- **Tumour (T):** Local staging. *Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS)* is extremely accurate for T1/T2 luminal penetrating depth in the upper GI tract. MRI excels at defining margin planes in rectal, pelvic, and musculoskeletal tumors.
- **Nodes (N):** A size criterion (>8-10mm) is mostly used on CT/MRI, limiting the ability to spot micrometastases in small nodes. EUS can biopsy mediastinal/celiac nodes directly. PET/CT reveals hypermetabolic nodes.
- **Metastases (M):** **Contrast-enhanced CT** is the workhorse for screening lung/liver mets. **MRI Liver** specific-agents now exceed the accuracy of CT for solid focal hepatic deposits. **PET/CT** uncovers systemic distant disease altering the paradigm of "curative" intent operations.
