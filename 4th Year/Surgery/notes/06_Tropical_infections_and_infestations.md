# Chapter 6: Tropical infections and infestations

## Learning Objectives

- List the common surgical infections and infestations that occur in the tropics.
- Appreciate that many patients do not seek medical help until late in the course of the disease because of socioeconomic reasons.
- Describe the emergency presentations of the various conditions, as patients may not seek treatment until they are very ill.
- Diagnose and treat these conditions, particularly as emergencies. Global travel connects areas where tropical infections are common to areas where they are not.
- Realise that optimal management involves a multidisciplinary approach (surgeon, physician, radiologist, pathologist, microbiologist).

---

## 1. Amoebiasis

### 1.1 Pathogenesis
Caused by *Entamoeba histolytica*. It is transmitted via the faecal-oral route in areas with substandard hygiene. 
- In the small intestine, cysts hatch into trophozoites. 
- Trophozoites invade the colon submucosa to produce **flask-shaped ulcers**.
- After entering the portal circulation, the parasite causes liquefactive necrosis in the liver, leading to an **amoebic liver abscess** (the most common extraintestinal manifestation, predominantly affecting the right lobe).

### 1.2 Clinical Features
- **General:** Bloody mucoid diarrhoea (history or active). Occurs in patients from/visiting endemic areas. Insidious onset of abdominal pain, anorexia, fever, night sweats, weight loss.
- **Liver Abscess:** Pain in right upper quadrant and right shoulder tip. Tender hepatomegaly, bulging intercostal spaces, overlying skin oedema. Can rupture into pleural, peritoneal, or pericardial cavities. The abscess contains chocolate-colored, odourless "anchovy sauce"-like fluid.
- **Amoeboma:** A chronic granuloma in the large bowel (commonly caecum). Presents as a mass in the right iliac fossa (RIF) mimicking a colonic carcinoma. Colonoscopy and biopsy are mandatory to exclude cancer. 

### 1.3 Investigations
- Raised inflammatory markers, deranged LFTs (elevated Alk Phos).
- **Serology:** Highly sensitive/specific outside endemic areas (IHA, ELISA).
- **Ultrasound / CT:** Best imaging mode. Shows hypoechoic liver lesions.

### 1.4 Treatment
- **Medical:** Highly effective. Metronidazole or tinidazole followed by diloxanide furoate (to clear luminal cysts).
- **Intervention:** Large or threatened-rupture abscesses (especially in the left lobe) require aspiration or pigtail catheter drainage.
- **Surgery:** Reserved for complications (rupture) or toxic megacolon (requires subtotal colectomy). An amoeboma unresponsive to medication should be resected to rule out cancer.

## 2. Roundworm (Ascariasis)

### 2.1 Pathogenesis
Caused by *Ascaris lumbricoides*, the most common intestinal nematode in humans. Transmission is faecal-oral.
- **Life cycle:** Ingested eggs hatch in jejunum, larvae migrate to liver and then to lungs (causing Löffler's syndrome). Larvae are coughed up, swallowed, and mature into adults in the small intestine.

### 2.2 Clinical Features
- **Pulmonary:** Dry cough, chest pain, dyspnoea, fever (**Löffler's syndrome**).
- **Intestinal:** Bolus of worms in the terminal ileum can cause small bowel obstruction (especially in children).
- **Biliary/Pancreatic:** Adult worms can migrate into the common bile duct (causing ascending cholangitis/jaundice) or pancreatic duct (causing acute pancreatitis).

### 2.3 Diagnosis
- Eosinophilia.
- **Radiology:** Barium meal shows worms scattered in the small bowel. Ultrasound/MRCP may show worms in the CBD or pancreatic duct. Plain X-rays/CTs demonstrate tubular/curvilinear structures.

### 2.4 Management
- **Conservative:** First-line treatment is anthelminthic drugs. Obstruction in children is given a trial of conservative therapy (IV fluids, NGT suction, hypertonic saline enemas to disentangle the bolus).
- **Surgery:** Last resort for acute abdomen (unresolving obstruction, perforation, peritonitis). Bolus is ideally milked into the colon. If bowel is healthy, enterotomy to remove worms.

## 3. Asiatic Cholangiohepatitis

### 3.1 Pathogenesis
Caused by *Clonorchis sinensis* (or *Opisthorchis*, *Fasciola*). Acquired by eating raw/undercooked infected freshwater fish/crabs.
- The organism resides in the intrahepatic biliary radicles.
- Produces bile duct hyperplasia, intrahepatic duct dilatation, mucin-rich bile, and stone formation.
- **Greatest Risk:** High incidence of progressing to **cholangiocarcinoma**.

### 3.2 Diagnosis & Treatment
- Presents with biliary tract symptoms (obstructive jaundice, cholangitis).
- Diagnosed by stool examination (eggs) or Ultrasound/ERCP showing uniform dilatation of small peripheral intrahepatic bile ducts.
- **Treatment:** Medical (praziquantel, albendazole). Surgery involves cholecystectomy, CBD exploration, and biliary-enteric bypass (choledochoduodenostomy or Roux-en-Y choledochojejunostomy with access loop).

## 4. Filariasis

### 4.1 Pathogenesis & Clinical Features
Primarily caused by *Wuchereria bancrofti*, transmitted by mosquitoes. Adult worms colonise and obstruct the lymphatic system.
- Leads to gross lower limb oedema, thickening of skin (**elephantiasis**).
- Promotes secondary streptococcal infection.
- Can cause massive scrotal/penile elephantiasis with underlying hydroceles.

### 4.2 Management
- Eosinophilia, microfilariae in a nocturnal peripheral blood smear.
- **Medical:** Diethylcarbamazine is highly effective early on.
- **Surgery:** Generally unproven and ineffective for reducing limb size. A hydrocele can be treated via standard excision/eversion.

## 5. Hydatid Disease

### 5.1 Pathogenesis & Classification
Caused by *Echinococcus granulosus* (dog tapeworm). Humans (and sheep/cattle) are intermediate hosts. Ingested eggs hatch and form cysts.
- **Cyst layers:** Pericyst (host organ compression), ectocyst (hyaline, non-infective), and endocyst (germinal layer containing active protoscolices). 
- Classified by ultrasound into groups based on activity (Active, Transitional, Inactive/Calcified).

### 5.2 Clinical Presentation
- Commonly asymptomatic until mass effect occurs. 
- **Liver:** Painful right upper quadrant lump, jaundice if communicating with biliary tree. Rupture causes severe anaphylactic shock.
- **Lungs:** Next most common site. Ruptured cysts can be coughed up.
- **Imaging Highlights:**
  - CT shows septated, smooth-walled cysts.
  - Lung X-ray shows the "meniscus/crescent sign" (impending rupture).
  - CT lung shows the "water-lily sign" (ruptured cyst).

### 5.3 Management
- Must be treated in a specialized tertiary unit. 
- Inactive asymptomatic cyst: Leave alone.
- Active cysts: Give Albendazole. Options include PAIR (Puncture, Aspiration, Injection of scolicidal agent, Reaspiration) or Surgical excision (pericystectomy, segmentectomy). **Povidone-iodine or hypertonic saline** are used as scolicidal agents.

## 6. Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)

### 6.1 Pathogenesis
Caused by *Mycobacterium leprae*. Transmitted through nasal secretions. Bacilli prefer colder parts of the body (nose, ear lobes, superficial nerves).
- **Lepromatous:** Poor immune response, widespread dissemination.
- **Tuberculoid:** Strong immune response, localized granulomas.

### 6.2 Clinical Presentation
- "Leonine facies" (wrinkled skin, collapsed nasal bridge).
- Ocular changes (paralysis of orbicularis oculi, blindness).
- Anaesthetic hypopigmented macules.
- Thickened, tender peripheral nerves (ulnar, median, posterior tibial, common peroneal) leading to **claw hands, foot drop, and trophic ulcers/autoamputation**.

### 6.3 Management
- Multidrug therapy (Dapsone, Rifampicin, Clofazimine) for 12 months.
- Multidisciplinary surgical reconstruction (tendon transfers) to regain grasp/pinch grip and treat foot drop.

## 7. Mycetoma

### 7.1 Pathogenesis & Presentation
A chronic, destructive granulomatous disease caused by fungi (*Eumycetoma*) or bacteria (*Actinomycetoma*). The organism enters via unnoticed trauma (bare feet on thorns). Endemic in the "mycetoma belt".
- **Pathognomonic Triad:** Painless subcutaneous mass, multiple sinuses, and seropurulent discharge containing grains (black, yellow, white, red).
- Destroys soft tissue and bone, but spares tendons and nerves until late. It is NOT contagious.

### 7.2 Diagnosis
- Clinically deceptive; often extends much deeper than apparent.
- **X-Ray:** "Sun-ray" appearance, Codman's triangle, multiple punched-out cavities. May mimic osteosarcoma.
- **MRI:** Highly specific "**dot-in-circle**" sign.
- Open deep biopsy or FNAC required for definitive identification of causative grains.

### 7.3 Management
- Long-term antimicrobial therapy. Fungal type is extremely difficult to cure entirely via medication alone and often requires surgical debulking.
- Amputation is a life-saving measure in severe, refractory disease with massive secondary bacterial infection.

## 8. Tuberculosis
Particularly relevant in the tropics are TB of the cervical lymph nodes and small intestine.

### 8.1 Tuberculous Cervical Lymphadenitis
- Matted, painless lymph nodal mass with "evening fever" and night sweats.
- Due to caseating necrosis, it forms a **cold abscess** (no signs of inflammation).
- Abscess breaks through the deep fascia forming a **collar-stud abscess**.
- Rupture results in an ulcer/sinus with characteristic **undermined/overhanging edges**.
- Diagnosed by FNAC/Excision biopsy. Treated mostly medically.

### 8.2 Tuberculosis of the Small Intestine
- **Hyperplastic Type:** Strong immune response causes a mass in the right iliac fossa (often mimicking Crohn's or Cancer). The caecum is pulled into a subhepatic position.
- **Ulcerative Type:** Poor immune response. Forms transverse ulcers in the ileum with undermined edges. Resolves via fibrosis leading to **multiple small bowel strictures**.
- **Clinical Presentation:**
  - **Elective:** Weight loss, night sweats, doughy abdomen (loculated ascites), alternating bowel habits.
  - **Emergency:** Complete distal small bowel obstruction due to strictures, or severe peritonitis due to a perforated transverse ulcer.
- **Diagnosis:** Barium meal shows strictures and subhepatic caecum. CXR to rule out pulmonary TB. Sterile pyuria indicates genitourinary TB.
- **Management:** 
  - Medical first. Later, elective resection or strictureplasty for obstructive strictures.
  - Emergency obstruction requires resuscitation, laparotomy, and often resection with temporary ileostomy. Primary anastomosis is avoided if the patient is nutritionally depleted and highly infected.

## 9. Typhoid

### 9.1 Pathogenesis
Caused by *Salmonella Typhi*. Affects **Peyer's patches** in the terminal ileum causing lymphoid hyperplasia, necrosis, and subsequent ulceration.

### 9.2 Clinical Presentation
- Patient is toxic with a high fever for 2–3 weeks.
- General abdominal pain, distension (paralytic ileus), and melaena.
- **Typhoid Perforation:** Emergency presentation! Sudden deterioration and signs of peritonitis in the second/third week. Plain X-ray will show free gas.

### 9.3 Management
- Vigorous ICU resuscitation. Intravenous broad-spectrum antibiotics.
- Operative choices: simple closure, wedge resection, or extensive resection with exteriorisation (ileostomy/mucous fistula) if peritoneal soiling is heavy.
- **Critical rule:** When closing an ileal perforation, the surgeon must explicitly look over the rest of the small and large bowel for other perforations or impending necrotic patches. The main abdominal wound is often left open for delayed primary closure due to inevitable infection.

## 10. Tropical Chronic Pancreatitis

### 10.1 Pathogenesis & Presentation
Occurs almost exclusively in resource-poor countries due to malnutrition and ingestion of cassava (tapioca), which contains cyanide derivatives (detoxification fails due to lack of dietary sulphur-containing amino acids).
- It is NOT alcohol-related.
- Present in young males (<40 years) with Type 1 diabetes (referred to as **fibro-calculous pancreatic diabetes**).
- Gross exocrine insufficiency resulting in malnutrition and steatorrhoea.
- High incidence of subsequent pancreatic cancer.

### 10.2 Diagnosis & Management
- Plain abdominal X-ray: Pathognomonic large, discrete stones along the main pancreatic duct (staghorn-like).
- Medical: Insulin, pancreatic enzyme replacement, analgesia.
- Surgical: Only for intractable pain. Side-to-side pancreaticojejunostomy is the primary option; resection is a final resort.
