# Chapter 31: Maxillofacial trauma

## Learning objectives

- Understand and identify potentially life-threatening injuries to the face, head and neck
- Safely perform a systematic examination of facial injuries, and describe the basic classification of soft-tissue and bony injuries
- Describe the principles of management of facial soft-tissue injuries
- Understand and describe the principles of management of fractures of various facial bones, and appreciate the initial management of dental injuries

---

## 1. Emergency assessment and management

### Key concept
Facial trauma is common and can be **immediately life-threatening** due to:
- Airway obstruction (blood, vomit, displaced midface/mandible, tongue)
- Massive haemorrhage (midface vascular injury; neck injury)
- Associated head/c-spine injury

Therefore, assessment must start with **ATLS ABC** while simultaneously protecting the cervical spine.

### Airway threats unique to facial trauma (high yield)
- Midface impaction displacing maxilla posteriorly
- Bilateral mandibular fractures allowing tongue to fall back
- Oedema of tongue/soft palate

Immediate temporising manoeuvres:
- Suction blood/debris aggressively
- Pull maxilla forward (disimpaction) in obtunded patient if impacted
- Pull tongue forward (suture/towel clip) if posterior displacement
- Prepare for definitive airway (intubation); be ready for emergency surgical airway if needed

---

## 2. Haemorrhage control

### Key concept
Life-threatening haemorrhage may occur with:
- Large facial soft-tissue lacerations
- Penetrating neck injury
- Ballistic injury

Principles:
- Direct pressure to visible bleeding points where possible
- Positioning: conscious patient leaning forward/side to reduce airway obstruction from blood (with c-spine caution)
- Rapid escalation to definitive control (packing, surgical/IR support) when uncontrolled

## 3. Systematic examination of facial injuries

### 3.1 History (mechanism-focused)
- Mechanism and force (assault, fall, RTA, sports)
- Symptoms: malocclusion, diplopia, numbness, epistaxis, CSF leak symptoms, vision changes
- Dental injury history and tetanus status

### 3.2 Examination (structured)
- General: airway, breathing, circulation; neurological status
- Inspect: asymmetry, swelling, lacerations, bruising, periorbital signs
- Palpate: bony step deformities, tenderness, mobility/instability
- Eyes: visual acuity, pupils, EOM, diplopia, globe injury signs
- Nose: septal haematoma (must drain), deformity, epistaxis
- Mouth: mucosal lacerations, teeth, malocclusion, mandibular movement
- Sensation: infraorbital/mental nerve deficits suggest fracture patterns

### 3.3 Imaging (principles)
- CT facial bones for complex/bony injuries
- Dental X-rays/OPG for mandible and dentoalveolar injury (as appropriate)
- Consider CT head/c-spine based on mechanism and symptoms

---

## 4. Soft-tissue injuries

### Principles of management
- Prioritise haemostasis and decontamination
- Copious irrigation; remove foreign bodies
- Restore anatomy: careful layered closure; align vermilion border in lip injuries
- Consider prophylactic antibiotics for contaminated bites/through-and-through oral lacerations (protocol dependent)
- Tetanus prophylaxis where indicated

Special considerations:
- Parotid duct injury, facial nerve injury in cheek lacerations
- Eyelid/canalicular injuries require specialist input

---

## 5. Facial fractures (overview principles)

### 5.1 Mandible fractures
Clinical clues:
- Malocclusion, trismus, step deformity, dental injury

Management principles:
- Airway vigilance (floor of mouth swelling)
- Analgesia, soft diet, antibiotics if open fracture/through oral mucosa
- Definitive fixation (e.g. ORIF) based on fracture pattern and displacement

### 5.2 Midface fractures (Le Fort concept)
High yield:
- Midface fractures can compromise airway and cause significant bleeding.
- Look for mobility of maxilla, malocclusion, infraorbital numbness, epistaxis.

### 5.3 Zygomaticomaxillary complex and orbital fractures
Clinical clues:
- Flattened malar eminence, trismus (coronoid impingement)
- Diplopia, restricted eye movements, enophthalmos

Urgent issues:
- Orbital compartment syndrome (vision threat)
- Entrapment (particularly in children; “white-eye” blowout)

---

## 6. Dental injuries (initial management)

### Key concept
Dental trauma is part of facial trauma care and affects function and cosmesis.

Principles:
- Account for missing teeth (aspiration/swallowed)
- Reposition and splint displaced teeth where appropriate (specialist)
- Avulsed permanent tooth: time-critical reimplantation principles (if feasible and safe); store in appropriate medium if not immediately replanted

## Summary Boxes

### Box 31.1: Life-threatening issues in facial trauma
- Airway obstruction (blood, displaced fragments, tongue)
- Massive haemorrhage
- Associated head/c-spine injury

### Box 31.2: “Do not miss” findings
- Septal haematoma
- Globe injury/vision loss
- CSF leak suspicion
- Malocclusion suggesting mandibular/midface fracture

### Box 31.3: Initial steps
- ATLS ABC with c-spine protection
- Suction and haemostasis
- Structured exam + CT where indicated
- Early specialist involvement for complex fractures/ocular issues

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Facial trauma is an airway problem first**—blood and displaced anatomy obstruct quickly.
2. **Septal haematoma is an emergency**: drain to prevent cartilage necrosis and saddle nose deformity.
3. **Malocclusion is a key sign** of mandibular/midface fracture.
4. **Diplopia and restricted eye movement** can indicate orbital fracture/entrapment.
5. Always consider **c-spine and TBI** with high-energy facial trauma.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Septal haematoma** | Blood collection between septal cartilage and mucoperichondrium; requires urgent drainage. |
| **Le Fort fractures** | Patterns of midface fractures involving maxillary separation at different levels (conceptual classification). |
| **ORIF** | Open reduction and internal fixation. |
| **Orbital blowout fracture** | Orbital wall fracture with possible muscle entrapment and diplopia. |
