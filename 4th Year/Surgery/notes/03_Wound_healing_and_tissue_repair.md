# Chapter 3: Wound healing and tissue repair

## Learning Objectives

- Understand normal wound healing and how it can be adversely affected
- Understand types of healing and how to classify wounds
- Understand the principles of wound management
- Understand the principles of scar management

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
**Wound healing** is a complex and dynamic biological process. In human adults, the normal response across all organ systems typically results in fibrosis and scar formation, which causes tissue dysfunction. This contrasts with early gestation when fetal tissues can heal without fibrosis. 

---

## 2. Normal Wound Healing in Skin

Historically described in three overlapping stages (Inflammation, Proliferation, Remodelling). **Haemostasis** is the immediate phase before inflammation.

### 2.1 Haemostasis
- **Vascular Endothelium Disruption** → Vasoconstriction + Exposure of subendothelial extracellular matrix.
- **Platelet Plug Formation** → Platelets adhere, activate, and aggregate. 
- **Release of Granules** → Alpha granules contain cytokines & growth factors (TGF-β, PDGF, FGF, EGF, VEGF). Essential for extracellular matrix deposition, chemotaxis, and angiogenesis.
- **Coagulation Cascade** → Initiated by tissue factor, yielding thrombin → fibrin generation (stabilises platelet plug).

### 2.2 Inflammation
- **Early Phase (Days 1-2)**: Platelet activation causes influx of polymorphonuclear leukocytes (**neutrophils**) to minimise bacterial contamination. Platelets/injured tissue release vasoactive amines (histamine, serotonin) causing vascular permeability. (Classic signs: rubor, tumor, calor, dolor).
- **Late Phase (Days 2-3)**: Monocytes arrive and differentiate into **macrophages** (vital role in healing: phagocytosis, debridement via proteolytic enzymes, primarily produce cytokines/growth factors for fibroblasts & angiogenesis).

### 2.3 Proliferation
- Starts around **day 3** and lasts for **2-4 weeks**.
- Maintained by **fibroblast** activity: production of ground substance, collagen, angiogenesis, and re-epithelialisation.
- **Granulation Tissue** is formed (pink, granular appearance).
- Myofibroblasts (contractile cells) help bring wound edges together (contraction).

### 2.4 Remodelling
- Begins **2-3 weeks** after injury; lasts for **a year or more**.
- Type III collagen (from proliferation phase) is replaced by stronger **Type I collagen** (normal skin ratio 4:1 typical).
- Tensile strength is maximal at **12 weeks** post-injury (~80% of uninjured skin strength).

## 3. Healing in Other Specific Tissues

### 3.1 Bone
- Follows similar phases to skin.
- **Secondary (Indirect) Healing:** Includes haematoma → soft callus (fibrocartilage) → endochondral ossification to hard callus (woven bone) → remodelling to lamellar bone.
- **Primary Bone Healing:** Direct bone union via intramembranous ossification without callus. Requires absolute stability (e.g. rigid internal fixation).

### 3.2 Nerve
- **Wallerian degeneration** occurs distal to the injury. Proximally degenerates to nearest node of Ranvier.
- **Neurotropism:** Regenerating fibres are guided by growth factors.
- **Neuroma:** Disorganised nerve regeneration yielding a painful lump.

### 3.3 Tendon
- **Intrinsic Healing:** Vincular blood flow and synovial diffusion (desired, promoted by early active mobilisation).
- **Extrinsic Healing:** Fibrous adhesions to tendon sheath (limits range of motion).

## 4. Abnormal Wound Healing

Aberrations in healing lead to excessive scarring or non-healing chronic wounds. 

### 4.1 Hypertrophic vs Keloid Scars

| Feature | Hypertrophic Scar | Keloid Scar |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Boundaries** | Confined to original wound boundaries | Expands BEYOND original boundaries |
| **Collagen** | Parallel pattern | Disorganised pattern |
| **Regression** | Eventually regresses | Does NOT spontaneously regress |
| **Aetiology** | Increased tension areas, delayed healing | Minor trauma, genetic, darker pigmentation |

### Box 3.1: Factors influencing wound healing
- **Local:** Skin tension, hypoxia/ischaemia, vascular insufficiency, lymphoedema, contamination, infection, foreign bodies, radiotherapy.
- **Systemic:** Advancing age, obesity, malnutrition, smoking, diabetes, immunocompromised, medications (steroids, immunosuppressants, chemo).

---

## 5. Types of Wound Healing

### Box 3.2: Classification of wound closure & healing
- **Primary (1st intention):** Wound edges opposed directly. Normal healing, minimal scar.
- **Secondary (2nd intention):** Wound left open. Heals by granulation, contraction, re-epithelialisation. Poor scar.
- **Tertiary (Delayed primary):** Wound initially left open (e.g. for cleaning), edges opposed later when conditions favourable.

---

## 6. Classification of Wounds

Wounds are classified based on aetiology, depth, contamination, complexity, and chronicity.

### Table 3.1: US CDC Surgical Wound Classification
1. **Clean (Class I):** Uninfected, no inflammation. Respiratory, alimentary, genital, or urinary tracts NOT entered.
2. **Clean-contaminated (Class II):** Above tracts entered under controlled conditions without unusual contamination.
3. **Contaminated (Class III):** Open, fresh, accidental. Major break in sterile technique or gross GI spillage.
4. **Dirty (Class IV):** Old traumatic wounds with devitalised tissue, existing infection, or perforated viscera.

**NNIS Score:** Used to predict Surgical Site Infections (SSIs). Ranges from 0-3 based on dirty wound, ASA score ≥ 3, and operative time > 75th percentile.

## 7. Wound Management

### 7.1 Principles
- Prepare patient (Antibiotics, Tetanus prophylaxis, Analgesia, Irrigation).
- Early debridement and irrigation. Explore & repair structures. Achieve haemostasis.
- Closure without tension. Consider reconstructive ladder. Drain and dress.

### 7.2 Debridement Types
- **Surgical:** Using scalpel/scissors until healthy bleeding edges.
- **Mechanical:** Irrigation, wet-to-dry dressings (non-selective, removes viable tissue too).
- **Autolytic:** Dressings to retain moisture, allowing wound enzymes to liquefy necrotic tissue.
- **Enzymatic:** Topical chemical agents (collagenase).
- **Biological:** Larvae (*Lucilia sericata*) releasing proteolytic/antimicrobial substances.

### Box 3.4: Reconstruction Options (The Reconstructive Ladder)
- Primary/Secondary/Tertiary closure → NPWT → Split-thickness graft → Full-thickness graft → Dermal matrices → Local/Regional flap → Tissue expansion → Free flap.
*Note: Skin grafts rely on recipient site vasculature. Flaps carry their own intrinsic blood supply.*

### 7.3 Tetanus-Prone Wounds
- Puncture injuries in contaminated environment, bites, compound fractures, foreign bodies, sepsis.
- **High-Risk:** Heavy contamination (soil/manure), >6 hour delay, extensive devitalised tissue.

## 8. Acute Wounds

### 8.1 Bites
- Treat wounds over the metacarpophalangeal joint as a human bite (punch) until proven otherwise. Joint infection is a surgical emergency.

### 8.2 Degloving (Avulsion of skin/fat from fascia/muscle/bone)
- **Morel-Lavallée lesion:** A closed degloving from shearing forces creating a haemolymphatic collection between fascial planes.
- **Management:** Excision until bleeding edges (can use intravenous fluorescein or indocyanine green to check viability).

### 8.3 Acute Compartment Syndrome
- Increased interstitial pressure within closed osteofascial compartment compromising microvasculature.
- **Signs:** Pain out of proportion, pain on passive stretching, paraesthesia. Absent pulses are a VERY late sign.
- **Treatment:** Emergency Fasciotomy (e.g. 2 lower limb longitudinal incisions to release 4 compartments).

### 8.4 Necrotising Fasciitis
- Rapidly progressing fascia/soft tissue infection. 26-40% mortality.
- **Pathogens:** Polymicrobial or monomicrobial (*Streptococcus pyogenes*).
- **Signs:** 'Dishwater pus', crepitus, blisters, shock out of proportion, fixed staining.
- **Treatment:** Urgent radical surgical debridement (often requiring multiples) + IV antibiotics. DO NOT DELAY.

---

## 9. Chronic Wounds

Failure to progress properly, marked by prolonged inflammation.

### 9.1 Leg Ulcers
- Aetiology: Venous, Arterial, Mixed, Trauma, Infection, Diabetes.
- **Marjolin’s ulcer:** Squamous cell carcinoma arising in a non-healing chronic wound/scar. Suspect if unresponsive to simple treatments.

### 9.2 Pressure Ulcers (Injuries)
- Over bony prominences (Ischium, Trochanter, Sacrum, Heel, Malleolus, Occiput).
- Regarded as *preventable*. Risk assessed via Braden/Waterlow/Norton scales.
- Reposition every 2-4 hours. Prioritise optimising nutrition & preventative measures. Surgery is NOT first-line.

---

## 10. Scar Management

- Scars take 1-2 years to fully mature. 
- **Prevention:** Tension relief, suturing along relaxed skin tension lines, avoiding joints, avoiding tattoo-scarring by early scrubbing.
- **Treatments for Hypertrophic/Keloid:** Silicone sheeting/gel (1st line), Intralesional corticosteroid injection (Triamcinolone acetonide), Pressure therapy, Laser therapy. Surgery with adjuvant therapy for major keloids.
- **Contractures:** Differential growth or scar shortening across a joint limiting movement. Treated by replacing scar tissue with healthy tissue (e.g., Z-plasty local flaps). Skin grafts contract more, so full-thickness grafts or flaps are preferred over split-thickness.

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **Collagen Types:** In normal skin healing, Type III is prevalent in the proliferative phase, which then matures to Type I in the remodelling phase (making a 4:1 ratio of I to III).
2. **Keloids vs Hypertrophic:** A scar strictly within the boundary = hypertrophic. Extending outward like a tumour = keloid.
3. **Compartment Syndrome key indicator:** Pain completely out of proportion to injury, especially upon passive stretch of the involved muscles. Do NOT wait for absent pulses to diagnose!
4. **Marjolin's Ulcer:** When evaluating chronic leg ulcers, always consider a biopsy for squamous cell carcinoma if unresponsive to therapy.
5. **Human Bites to the hand:** Must be urgently evaluated for septic arthritis.
