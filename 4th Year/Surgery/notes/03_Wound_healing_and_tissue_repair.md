# Chapter 3: Wound Healing and Tissue Repair

## Learning Objectives

- Understand normal wound healing and how it can be adversely affected
- Understand types of healing and how to classify wounds
- Understand the principles of wound management
- Understand the principles of scar management

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
**Wound healing** is a complex, dynamic, and highly regulated biological process. In adult humans, the normal response across all organ systems results in **fibrosis and scar formation** — not true regeneration. This causes tissue dysfunction and is often underappreciated. This contrasts with early fetal wound healing, which can occur without fibrosis — a model that regenerative medicine seeks to replicate.

### Clinical Significance
- Abnormal healing leads to: hypertrophic/keloid scars, chronic non-healing wounds, contractures
- Understanding the process guides wound management, timing of closure, antibiotic prophylaxis, and scar management
- Clinical judgement and multidisciplinary approach are key

## 2. Normal Wound Healing in Skin

Classically described in **4 overlapping but distinct phases:**
1. Haemostasis (immediate)
2. Inflammation (days 1–5)
3. Proliferation (day 3 → 2–4 weeks)
4. Remodelling (week 3 → 1+ year)

### 2.1 Haemostasis (Immediate)

**Trigger:** Disruption of vascular endothelium

**Sequence:**
```
Vascular endothelium disruption
      ↓
Vasoconstriction (immediate, transient)
      ↓
Exposure of subendothelial extracellular matrix (collagen, fibronectin)
      ↓
Platelet ADHESION (via vWF and GPIb)
      ↓
Platelet ACTIVATION → release of alpha granules
      ↓
Alpha granule contents: TGF-β, PDGF, FGF, EGF, VEGF
(→ ECM deposition, chemotaxis, epithelialisation, angiogenesis)
      ↓
Platelet AGGREGATION
      ↓
Tissue factor activates coagulation cascade
      ↓
Thrombin generated → Fibrin production → Stabilises platelet plug
      ↓
Fibrin scaffold formed for infiltrating cells
```

**Key Growth Factors from Platelets:**
| Growth Factor | Abbreviation | Action |
|--------------|--------------|--------|
| Transforming Growth Factor-β | TGF-β | ECM deposition, fibrosis, wound closure |
| Platelet-Derived Growth Factor | PDGF | Fibroblast and smooth muscle chemotaxis |
| Fibroblast Growth Factor | FGF | Angiogenesis, fibroblast proliferation |
| Epidermal Growth Factor | EGF | Epithelialisation |
| Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor | VEGF | Angiogenesis |

### 2.2 Inflammation (Days 1–5)

**Classic signs:** Rubor (redness), Tumor (swelling), Calor (heat), Dolor (pain), Functio laesa (loss of function)

**Early phase (Days 1–2): Neutrophil infiltration**
- Platelet activation + vasoactive amines (histamine, serotonin) → ↑ vascular permeability
- Polymorphonuclear leukocytes (**neutrophils**) flood the wound
- Role: Phagocytosis of bacteria; minimise bacterial contamination
- Proteases released: debride wound of non-viable material

**Late phase (Days 2–3): Monocyte → Macrophage differentiation**
- Monocytes recruited and differentiate into **macrophages** (the master orchestrators)
- Macrophage roles:
  - Phagocytosis and debridement (proteolytic enzymes)
  - **Primary producers of cytokines and growth factors** for:
    - Fibroblast proliferation
    - Angiogenesis
    - Continued wound cleaning
- Without macrophages, healing is severely impaired

### 2.3 Proliferation (Day 3 → 2–4 weeks)

**Dominated by fibroblast activity:**
- Production of **ground substance** (glycosaminoglycans, proteoglycans)
- **Collagen synthesis** (initially Type III)
- **Angiogenesis** (VEGF-driven new capillary formation)
- **Re-epithelialisation** (keratinocytes migrate from wound edges)

**Granulation tissue:**
- Formed in early proliferative phase
- Pink, granular appearance
- Consists of: fibroblasts + collagen + new capillaries + macrophages
- Highly vascular → bleeds easily on contact

**Myofibroblasts:**
- Fibroblasts differentiate into myofibroblasts (contractile cells)
- Cause **wound contraction** → bringing edges together (important in open wounds)

**Tensile strength build-up:**
- Fibroblasts synthesise increasing collagen → tensile strength rises
- Not yet maximum at this stage

### 2.4 Remodelling (Week 3 → Year+)

**The longest phase — takes 1–2 years to complete**

**Key process:**
```
Type III collagen (week 3–) → replaced by → Type I collagen
Normal skin ratio: Type I : Type III = 4:1
```

**Changes in collagen:**
- More cross-linked (stronger)
- More uniformly aligned
- Parallel fibre arrangement (more functional)

**Tensile Strength:**
- Maximum at **12 weeks** post-injury
- Reaches approximately **80% of uninjured skin strength** (never 100%)

**Visible changes:**
- Scar becomes: less red, less raised, less itchy, softer, paler
- Most external changes within 3 months; full maturation 1–2 years

## 3. Normal Healing in Specific Tissues

### 3.1 Bone Healing

**Two types:**

#### Secondary (Indirect) Bone Healing — Most Common
Used in **non-operative fracture management** (conservative treatment):
```
Fracture → Haematoma formation + Inflammation
    ↓
Haematoma organised → Soft callus (fibrocartilage)
    ↓
Endochondral ossification → Hard callus (woven bone)
    ↓  [+ Intramembranous ossification at fracture ends]
Remodelling → Woven bone → Lamellar bone (normal bone structure)
```

#### Primary (Direct) Bone Healing — Requires Surgery
- Direct bone-to-bone union via **intramembranous ossification** WITHOUT callus
- Requirements: **Absolute rigid stability** + Direct apposition of fracture ends (no gap)
- Achieved by: ORIF (Open Reduction Internal Fixation) with absolute stability
- If a gap exists → secondary healing may lead to delayed union, non-union, or malunion
- Primary healing is the **AIM of rigid internal fixation surgery**

### 3.2 Nerve Healing

**Peripheral nerve injury and regeneration (Figure 3.4):**

```
Nerve injury (neurotmesis = complete division)
    ↓
DISTAL stump: Wallerian degeneration
(axon + myelin debris breaks down; Schwann cells clear debris)
    ↓
PROXIMAL end: degeneration back to nearest node of Ranvier
    ↓
Schwann cells proliferate → form Bands of Büngner (guidance channels)
    ↓
Regenerating axons guided by: Neurotropism
(growth factors, hormones, ECM guidance)
    ↓
Axonal regrowth + Remyelination
Rate: ~1 mm/day
```

**Complications:**
- **Neuroma:** Disorganised nerve regeneration → painful lump (perineurium injured + inflammation)
- Poor outcomes if: large gap, perineurium torn, or infection

### 3.3 Tendon Healing

**Two mechanisms compete:**

| Mechanism | Pathway | Outcome |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Intrinsic** | Vincular blood flow + synovial diffusion | Preferred — no adhesions |
| **Extrinsic** | Fibrous adhesions between tendon and sheath | Leads to restricted range of motion |

**Clinical management:**
- **Early active mobilisation** promotes intrinsic healing and prevents adhesions
- Simultaneous **splintage** protects the repair from rupture
- Balance is critical: too much movement = rupture; too little = adhesions

## 4. Abnormal Wound Healing

### 4.1 Factors Adversely Affecting Healing (Summary Box 3.1)

**Local Factors:**
- Skin tension (high tension → more scarring)
- Hypoxia and ischaemia (impairs fibroblast function, collagen synthesis)
- Vascular insufficiency (arterial or venous)
- Lymphoedema (tissue swelling → oedema impairs diffusion)
- Contamination and infection (prolongs inflammation)
- Foreign bodies (perpetuates inflammation)
- Radiotherapy (damages microcirculation, impairs regeneration)

**Systemic Factors:**
- Advancing age (↓ cellular responses, ↓ collagen synthesis)
- Obesity (poor tissue perfusion, increased wound tension, infection risk)
- Malnutrition (↓ protein, vitamins C/A/zinc → impaired collagen synthesis)
- Smoking (vasoconstriction → tissue hypoxia; carbon monoxide)
- Diabetes mellitus (↓ fibroblast function, ↓ angiogenesis, neuropathy, infection)
- Connective tissue diseases (e.g. Ehlers-Danlos — intrinsic collagen abnormality)
- Immunosuppression (HIV, steroids, chemotherapy)
- Medications: steroids (↓ inflammation → impaired early healing), immunosuppressants, cytotoxics

### 4.2 Hypertrophic vs Keloid Scars

Both result from **excess collagen deposition** during/after the remodelling phase.

| Feature | Hypertrophic Scar | Keloid Scar |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Boundary** | Confined WITHIN original wound | Extends BEYOND original wound |
| **Collagen arrangement** | Parallel/nodular | Disorganised (whorled) |
| **Regression** | Eventually regresses spontaneously | Does NOT regress spontaneously |
| **Aetiology** | Increased tension, deep dermal burns, wounds >3 wks to heal | Minor trauma, genetic predisposition |
| **Risk factors** | Areas of high tension, crossing skin lines | Darker skin pigmentation, genetic, any age |
| **Recurrence after excision** | Low | HIGH |

**Management of Hypertrophic Scars (Algorithm):**
1. Silicone gel/sheeting (first-line, 2 months minimum)
2. Pulsed-dye laser (PDL) or fractional laser therapy
3. Intralesional corticosteroid injection (triamcinolone 10–40 mg/mL every 4–6 weeks)
4. Pressure/compression therapy
5. Combination therapies

**Management of Keloid Scars:**
- **Minor keloid:** Intralesional corticosteroids ± pulsed-dye laser; 5-FU + corticosteroids
- **Major/high-risk keloid:** Surgical excision + adjuvant (silicone + corticosteroids OR radiotherapy)
- Patient counselling: high recurrence rate; set realistic expectations

## 5. Types of Wound Healing (Summary Box 3.2)

| Type | Description | Outcome |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Primary (1st intention)** | Wound edges directly apposed; normal healing when clean | Best scar, minimal scarring |
| **Secondary (2nd intention)** | Wound left open; heals by granulation + contraction + re-epithelialisation | Prolonged, worse scar, more contraction |
| **Tertiary / Delayed primary (3rd intention)** | Initially left open (contaminated/untidy wound), edges apposed later when clean | Good outcomes when correctly timed |

**When to use delayed primary closure:**
- Contaminated wounds
- Battlefield/disaster wounds
- Wounds with significant swelling or tissue loss
- Closure performed when: swelling resolved, clean granulating base present

## 6. Classification of Wounds

Wounds are diverse — multiple classification systems are needed:

### CDC Surgical Wound Classification (Table 3.1)

| Class | Name | Definition |
|-------|------|-----------|
| **Class I** | Clean | Uninfected; no inflammation; respiratory/GI/GU tracts not entered |
| **Class II** | Clean-contaminated | Above tracts entered under controlled conditions; no unusual contamination |
| **Class III** | Contaminated | Open fresh accidental wounds; major break in sterile technique; gross GI spillage |
| **Class IV** | Dirty | Old traumatic wounds with devitalised tissue; existing infection; perforated viscera |

### NNIS (National Nosocomial Infections Surveillance) Score
**Predicts SSI risk — score 0–3:**
- 1 point: Contaminated or dirty wound (Class III or IV)
- 1 point: ASA score ≥3
- 1 point: Operative time > 75th percentile for that procedure

Higher score = higher SSI risk

### Other Wound Classification Dimensions (Summary Box 3.3)

| Dimension | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Aetiology** | Clean surgical, shearing, crush, blast, burn, bite, avulsion |
| **Depth** | Epidermal, dermal (superficial/deep), full thickness |
| **Contamination** | Clean → dirty |
| **Complexity** | Simple, complex, open fracture/joint, visceral involvement |
| **Chronicity** | Acute vs chronic (vascular ulcers, pressure ulcers, diabetic ulcers) |

## 7. Wound Management

### 7.1 Assessment
1. **ATLS principles** — rule out life/limb-threatening conditions first
2. Assess: site, size, geometry, contamination, infection, neurovascular status
3. Assess mechanism: high-energy injuries can look innocuous but be devastating (e.g., high-pressure injection injury of hand)
4. Assess motor and sensory function **before** local anaesthesia
5. Imaging: exclude FB, fractures, dislocations where appropriate

### 7.2 General Principles (Table 3.2)

| Phase | Actions |
|-------|---------|
| **Preparation** | Antibiotic prophylaxis, tetanus prophylaxis, analgesia, wound irrigation |
| **Wound care** | Early debridement + irrigation; explore and repair structures; haemostasis |
| **Closure** | Skin closure without tension; consider reconstruction; suture choice; drains; dressings |
| **Follow-up** | Suture/splint removal; physiotherapy; monitor complications; scar management |

### 7.3 Debridement

**Principle:** Excise all devitalised tissue and foreign material until healthy, bleeding wound edges are seen.

| Type | Method | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Surgical** | Scalpel/scissors/curette until punctate bleeding | Gold standard; most reliable |
| **Mechanical** | Irrigation, wet-to-dry dressings, hydrotherapy | Non-selective; removes viable tissue too |
| **Autolytic** | Hydrocolloid/transparent film dressings → moisture retains wound enzymes | Selective; slow; no pain |
| **Enzymatic** | Topical collagenase or papain-urea | Chemically liquefies necrotic tissue |
| **Biological** | Larvae of *Lucilia sericata* (maggots) | Release proteolytic + antimicrobial substances; directly promote healing |

**Assessing tissue viability:**
- Healthy subcutaneous fat: yellow, soft
- Muscle viability: colour, contractility, capacity to bleed (the "4 Cs": Colour, Consistency, Contractility, Capacity to bleed)

### 7.4 Reconstruction Options — The Reconstructive Ladder (Summary Box 3.4)

Listed from simplest to most complex:
1. Primary closure
2. Secondary closure (granulation)
3. Tertiary (delayed primary) closure
4. Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)
5. Split-thickness skin graft (STSG)
6. Full-thickness skin graft (FTSG)
7. Dermal matrices (acellular)
8. Local flap
9. Regional / Pedicled flap
10. Tissue expansion
11. Free flap (microsurgical)

**Skin Grafts:**
- No intrinsic blood supply
- Dependent on recipient site vasculature
- **STSG:** Epidermis + small portion of dermis; meshed for larger defects; more contraction
- **FTSG:** Epidermis + majority of dermis; better texture; less contraction; preferred for aesthetically important areas

**Flaps:**
- Carry their **own intrinsic blood supply** — do not depend on recipient site
- **Free flap:** Tissue with vascular pedicle detached and anastomosed microsurgically at recipient site

**Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT):**
- Applied to wound surface (foam/gauze + airtight seal + suction)
- Mechanisms: draws wound edges together; removes exudate; reduces oedema; promotes granulation
- NOT suitable for: exposed vessels, malignancy, untreated osteomyelitis, unexplored fistulae

### 7.5 Tetanus Prophylaxis (Table 3.3)

**Tetanus-prone wounds:**
- Puncture injuries in contaminated environment
- Animal/human bites
- Compound fractures
- Wounds containing foreign bodies
- Burns/wounds with systemic sepsis

**High-risk tetanus-prone wounds (any tetanus-prone wound with):**
- Heavy contamination (soil, manure)
- >6 hour delay before surgery
- Extensive devitalised tissue

**Management depends on immunisation status** (Figure 3.6):
- Fully immunised (within 10 years): No treatment needed for most
- Partially immunised or >10 years since last dose: Reinforcing vaccine dose
- Unimmunised/unknown + high-risk wound: Vaccine + tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG)

## 8. Acute Wounds

### 8.1 Bites

- Treat any wound over **metacarpophalangeal joint** as a **human bite** (punch-to-mouth) until proven otherwise
- **Joint infection = surgical emergency** → can cause articular cartilage destruction
- Presents: hot, swollen, tender joint with limited range of motion
- High risk of: *Eikenella corrodens* (human bites), *Pasteurella multocida* (animal bites)
- Management: urgent irrigation, exploration, appropriate antibiotics (co-amoxiclav)

### 8.2 Degloving Injuries

**Definition:** Avulsion of skin and subcutaneous fat from underlying fascia, muscle, or bone.

**Open degloving:** Visible loss of skin (e.g., finger avulsion)

**Closed degloving:** Shearing force → skin separated from fascia but skin still present (may be non-viable)
- Common in motor vehicle collisions
- **Morel-Lavallée Lesion:** Haemolymphatic collection between fascial planes from disruption of perforating vessels and lymphatics
  - Classically over greater trochanter but can occur anywhere
  - Management: surgical debridement of non-viable tissue

**Assessing viability:**
- Fixed staining, thrombosis of subcutaneous veins = non-viable
- Excise until punctate dermal bleeding seen
- IV fluorescein or indocyanine green fluorescence to delineate non-viable tissue

**Classification of Degloving in Limb Trauma (Summary Box 3.5):**
1. Limited degloving with abrasion/avulsion
2. Non-circumferential degloving
3. Circumferential single-plane degloving
4. Circumferential multiplanar degloving (→ may need amputation)

### 8.3 Acute Compartment Syndrome

**Definition:** Increased interstitial pressure within a closed osteofascial compartment → microvascular compromise → muscle ischaemia

**This is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY — delayed treatment = irreversible muscle damage**

**Common Causes:**
- Long bone fractures (most common — both open AND closed)
- Soft tissue trauma
- Arterial injuries (vascular repair → reperfusion)
- Burns
- Prolonged limb compression

**Classic Signs (in order of appearance):**
1. **Pain out of proportion to the injury** (most important early sign)
2. **Pain with passive stretching** of muscles within affected compartment (early sign)
3. **Paraesthesia** (pressure on sensory nerves — early)
4. **Pallor, Poikilothermia** (loss of vasoregulation)
5. **Absent pulses** — LATE, UNCOMMON sign; suggests vascular injury, not just compartment syndrome

> ⚠️ "The 6 Ps" of vascular/compartment syndrome: Pain, Pressure, Paraesthesia, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paralysis — but **absent pulses are VERY LATE**

**Diagnosis:**
- Primarily **clinical**
- Intracompartment pressure (ICP) monitoring can help if clinical uncertainty
- Fasciotomy threshold: **ΔP (diastolic BP − ICP) <30 mmHg**

**Treatment:** Emergency fasciotomy
- Lower limb: **2 longitudinal incisions**
  - **Medial** (1–2 cm posterior to tibia) → decompresses superficial + deep posterior compartments
  - **Lateral** (2 cm lateral to anterior tibial border) → decompresses anterior + peroneal compartments
- If delayed fasciotomy (late diagnosis): risk of rhabdomyolysis, infection, amputation, death

### 8.4 Necrotising Fasciitis

**Definition:** Severe, rapidly progressing infection of soft tissue AND fascia with significant morbidity/mortality.

**Mortality:**
- 30-day mortality: **~26%**
- 1-year mortality: **~40%**

**Microbiology:**
- **Polymicrobial** (most common): mixed aerobic/anaerobic synergy
- **Monomicrobial** (frequent): *Streptococcus pyogenes* (Group A Streptococcus)
- Others: *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, *Pseudomonas*, *Clostridium*, *Bacteroides*

**Risk factors:**
- Trauma or surgery with wound contamination
- **Diabetes mellitus** (most common comorbidity)
- Immunosuppression
- ~30% have NO comorbidities

**Signs and Symptoms (Summary Box 3.6):**

Local:
- Unusual pain (severe, disproportionate)
- Erythema, oedema, warmth
- **Crepitus** (gas in tissues)
- Blisters, bullae
- **"Dishwater pus"** (grey, thin, non-purulent discharge)
- Fixed staining of skin
- Necrosis, gangrene

Systemic:
- Fever, tachycardia, tachypnoea
- Shock
- Coagulopathy
- Multi-organ failure

**Diagnosis:**
- Primarily **clinical** — SURGICAL TREATMENT SHOULD NOT BE DELAYED if suspicion is high
- **The "Finger Test":** Small incision, pass gloved finger along deep fascia under local anaesthesia
  - If finger slides freely without resistance + no bleeding = fascial necrosis → DEBRIDE IMMEDIATELY
- CT can help identify gas tracking along fascial planes, but should NOT delay surgery
- LRINEC score exists but performance is limited

**Treatment:**
1. **Urgent radical surgical debridement** (first priority — same day)
2. IV antibiotics (broad-spectrum: e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin + vancomycin)
3. **Second-look operation** planned in 24–48 hours
4. Multiple debridements often required
5. ICU support, nutritional support

**Eponymous forms:**
- **Meleney's gangrene:** Abdominal wall necrotising fasciitis (synergistic bacterial gangrene)
- **Fournier's gangrene:** Perineum/scrotal necrotising fasciitis

## 9. Chronic Wounds

**Definition:** Wounds that fail to progress through normal healing phases in a timely, orderly manner. Characterised by a **prolonged inflammatory phase** and persistent infection.

### 9.1 Leg Ulcers

**Most common chronic wounds in developed countries.**

**Aetiology (Summary Box 3.7):**
- Vascular: venous (most common), arterial, mixed
- Trauma: bites, self-inflicted, burns
- Infection: bacterial, fungal, mycobacterial, syphilis
- Metabolic: diabetes mellitus, gout, calciphylaxis
- Autoimmune: vasculitis, systemic sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis
- Neoplastic: squamous cell carcinoma, BCC

**Marjolin's Ulcer:**
- Squamous cell carcinoma arising in a **chronic non-healing wound or scar**
- **Suspect and biopsy** any chronic ulcer unresponsive to standard treatments

**Assessment:**
- Assess arterial AND venous circulation
- Check ABPI (ankle-brachial pressure index)
- Assess sensation (neuropathy assessment)
- Treatment is directed at the underlying cause

### 9.2 Pressure Ulcers (Pressure Injuries)

**Definition:** Localised injury to skin/underlying tissue over bony prominences or under medical devices.

**Staging (Table 3.5):**
| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Stage 1** | Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin |
| **Stage 2** | Partial-thickness skin loss with exposed dermis |
| **Stage 3** | Full-thickness skin loss |
| **Stage 4** | Full-thickness skin AND tissue loss |
| **Unstageable** | Obscured full-thickness loss (can't see base) |
| **Deep tissue injury** | Persistent non-blanchable deep red/maroon/purple discoloration |

**Common Sites (Summary Box 3.8):**
- Ischium, Greater trochanter, Sacrum, Heel, Malleolus, Occiput

**Prevention:**
- Risk assessment: **Braden scale, Waterlow score, Norton score**
- Skin assessment
- Repositioning every **2–4 hours**
- Pressure-redistributing devices (specialised mattresses)
- Nutritional support

**Treatment:**
- Patient optimisation (nutrition, glycaemic control)
- Debridement and appropriate dressings
- Surgery: **NOT first-line** (only if conservative measures fail and patient is motivated/compliant)
- Surgical options: Large fasciocutaneous or musculocutaneous flaps (NOT primary closure or skin grafts — will fail)

## 10. Scar Management

### 10.1 Normal Scar Maturation
- **1–2 years** to fully mature
- Initially: pink, hard, raised, itchy
- Over time: paler, softer, flatter, less itchy
- Tensile strength maximal at **12 weeks** (~80% of normal skin)

### 10.2 Prevention of Adverse Scarring
- Place incisions along **relaxed skin tension lines** (RSTL)
- Avoid straight-line incisions across flexion creases
- Early debridement (prevents infection → reduces scarring)
- Scrub wounds immediately to prevent tattooing
- Close without tension
- Layered closure (deep dermal absorbable + subcuticular or skin sutures)
- **Suture removal times:** Face 5 days; scalp 7–10 days; trunk 10 days; lower limb 10–14 days

### 10.3 Scar Treatments

| Treatment | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Silicone gel/sheeting** | First-line for hypertrophic and keloid scars | Apply daily; widely accepted; mechanism unclear but effective |
| **Intralesional corticosteroids** (triamcinolone acetonide) | Hypertrophic and keloid scars | 10–40 mg/mL; repeat every 4–6 weeks until flat |
| **Pressure/compression therapy** | Hypertrophic scars (especially burns) | Custom garments; worn 23 hrs/day |
| **Pulsed-dye laser (PDL)** | Linear hypertrophic scars; vascular scars | Preferred initial laser for most scars |
| **Fractional laser therapy** | Keloids, hypertrophic scars | Ablative or non-ablative |
| **Surgical excision** | Major keloids, significant hypertrophic scars | + adjuvant therapy to prevent recurrence |
| **5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)** | Keloids | Often combined with corticosteroids |

### 10.4 Scar Contractures

**Definition:** Scar shortening (or differential growth of scar vs surrounding tissue) across a joint → limits range of movement → deformity, impairment.

**Causes:**
- Full-thickness burns crossing joints
- Traumatic wounds with significant tissue loss
- Improper wound management

**Treatment:**
- Principle: **Replace scar tissue with healthy tissue** (not just scar revision)
- Options:
  - **Z-plasty** (and variants: Y-V, V-Y, W-plasty): Local transposition flaps; lengthen and transpose scar
  - Other local/regional flaps
  - Free flaps (for severe contractures)
  - Skin grafts (least preferred — graft contraction common)

**Graft choice for contractures:**
- **Flaps preferred over grafts** (due to graft contracture)
- If grafts needed: **FTSG > STSG** (better texture, less contraction)

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. **Wound healing phases:** Haemostasis → Inflammation → Proliferation → Remodelling. Know timing: inflammation Day 1–5; proliferation Day 3 to 2–4 weeks; remodelling Week 3 to 1+ year.
2. **Collagen types:** Type III in proliferative phase → replaced by Type I in remodelling; normal ratio **Type I : Type III = 4:1**
3. **Maximum tensile strength:** Reached at **12 weeks** post-injury; only **80%** of uninjured skin — NEVER returns to 100%
4. **Macrophages are essential** for wound healing — without them, healing fails. Key cytokine producers.
5. **Keloid vs Hypertrophic:** Keloid extends BEYOND wound boundary; hypertrophic stays within. Keloid does NOT regress; hypertrophic does.
6. **Compartment syndrome hallmark:** Pain OUT OF PROPORTION to injury + pain on passive stretch. DO NOT wait for absent pulses — this is a late, uncommon sign.
7. **Fasciotomy threshold:** ΔP (Diastolic BP − ICP) <30 mmHg
8. **Necrotising fasciitis:** Do NOT delay surgery for investigations if clinical suspicion is high. "Finger test" is diagnostic.
9. **Marjolin's ulcer:** Always biopsy a chronic ulcer that fails to respond to standard treatment (SCC in scar/ulcer)
10. **Pressure ulcers:** Surgery is NOT first-line; primary closure/skin grafts will fail — use flaps for surgical reconstruction
11. **Tetanus prophylaxis:** >10 years since last dose = reinforcing vaccine; unimmunised + high-risk wound = vaccine + TIG
12. **First-line scar treatment:** Silicone gel/sheeting for both hypertrophic and keloid scars
13. **Wound over MCP joint:** Always suspect human bite; joint infection = surgical emergency
14. **Primary bone healing** requires ABSOLUTE stability and direct apposition — only achievable with rigid internal fixation
15. **Z-plasty** lengthens and transposes scar tissue — fundamental technique for contracture release

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Granulation tissue** | Newly formed tissue in proliferative phase: fibroblasts + new capillaries + macrophages + Type III collagen |
| **Myofibroblast** | Contractile fibroblast that mediates wound contraction (actin-containing) |
| **Wallerian degeneration** | Anterograde (distal) degeneration of axon and myelin after nerve injury |
| **Neurotropism** | Directional guidance of regenerating nerve fibres by growth factors and ECM |
| **Neuroma** | Disorganised, painful nerve regeneration into a lump — occurs when perineurium is disrupted |
| **Hypertrophic scar** | Excess scar tissue within wound boundaries; eventually regresses |
| **Keloid scar** | Excess scar tissue extending beyond wound boundaries; does not regress; high recurrence |
| **Morel-Lavallée lesion** | Haemolymphatic collection between fascial planes from closed degloving shear injury |
| **Marjolin's ulcer** | SCC arising in chronic non-healing wound or scar |
| **Primary bone healing** | Direct intramembranous ossification without callus; requires rigid fixation and direct apposition |
| **Secondary bone healing** | Indirect healing via callus formation (haematoma → fibrocartilage → woven bone → lamellar bone) |
| **NNIS score** | National Nosocomial Infection Surveillance risk score for SSI (0–3 points) |
| **NPWT** | Negative pressure wound therapy; promotes granulation and wound closure by suction |
| **Z-plasty** | Transposition of two triangular tissue flaps to lengthen and redirect a scar or contracture |
| **Fasciotomy** | Surgical incision of fascia to relieve raised compartment pressure |
