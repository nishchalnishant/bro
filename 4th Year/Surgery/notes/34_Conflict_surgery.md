# Chapter 34: Conflict surgery

## Learning objectives

To understand and appreciate:
- Fundamental differences of war surgery
- Injury patterns of modern warfare
- Principles of war surgical management
- Blast and ballistic injury

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Conflict (war) surgery shares principles with civilian trauma but differs fundamentally in:
- **Austere, hazardous environments**
- **Mass casualty situations**
- **Unique injury mechanisms** (blast and high-energy ballistics)
- **Staged care** across echelons with evacuation between levels

The operative philosophy often prioritises **damage control** and rapid stabilisation to enable onward transfer.

---

## 2. How war surgery differs from civilian trauma

### Environment and logistics
- Limited diagnostics, blood products and ICU capability may exist
- Supply chains and sterility may be compromised
- Protection of personnel and patients is a real constraint

### Injury patterns
- High-energy transfer causing extensive tissue destruction
- Combined injury mechanisms (blast + fragmentation + burns + crush)

### Mass casualty and triage
Ethical focus shifts:
- “Do the best for the most” rather than maximal care for each individual
- Triage and resource allocation dominate decision-making

### Staged care
Care is often delivered in stages across forward teams, field hospitals and definitive centres:
- Rapid resuscitation and haemorrhage control near point of injury
- Damage control surgery
- Evacuation to higher-level facilities for definitive reconstruction

---

## 3. Injury mechanisms: blast and ballistics

### 3.1 Blast injury (high-yield framework)
Blast causes injury via multiple mechanisms:
- Primary (pressure wave): lungs, ears, bowel
- Secondary (fragmentation): penetrating wounds from shrapnel
- Tertiary (body displacement): blunt trauma
- Quaternary (other): burns, inhalation, toxins

### 3.2 Ballistic injury (principles)
Tissue damage depends on:
- Projectile velocity and energy
- Yaw, fragmentation and cavitation
- Tissue type and elasticity

Clinical implication: wounds often appear small externally but have large deep devitalised zones.

---

## 4. Principles of war surgical management

### 4.1 Haemorrhage control
- Tourniquets and junctional haemorrhage control
- Damage control resuscitation and early balanced transfusion where possible
- Rapid operative/adjunct control (packing, vascular control, shunts)

### 4.2 Wound management
Core principles:
- Meticulous debridement of devitalised tissue
- Copious irrigation
- Leave wounds open initially (delayed primary closure common)
- Fracture stabilisation often with external fixation in austere settings

### 4.3 Infection and antibiotics
High contamination rates and delayed evacuation increase infection risk:
- Early antibiotics according to protocol
- Tetanus prophylaxis
- Repeated debridement and staged closure

---

## 5. Ethical and legal considerations (practical)

### Key concept
Conflict creates competing duties:
- Duty to treat the injured regardless of affiliation (medical neutrality)
- Resource scarcity and triage decisions
- Security and protection constraints

Documentation, governance and adherence to ethical frameworks remain essential despite austere conditions.

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **War surgery is resource-limited and staged**; damage control dominates.
2. **Blast injuries are multi-mechanism**: look beyond the obvious wounds (lungs/bowel/ears).
3. **Ballistic wounds need debridement**, not just closure—deep tissue damage is often extensive.
4. **Triage principles change** in mass casualty scenarios; re-triage is continuous.
5. **Delayed primary closure** is common and safer in contaminated conflict wounds.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Damage control surgery** | Rapid haemorrhage/contamination control with staged definitive repair after stabilisation. |
| **Primary blast injury** | Pressure-wave injury classically affecting air-containing organs (lung, bowel, ear). |
| **Cavitation** | Temporary cavity formation in tissue from ballistic energy transfer, contributing to deep injury. |
| **Echelon of care** | Level/stage of medical facility in conflict system with escalation/evacuation. |
