## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Neonatal surgeons manage life-threatening non-cardiac congenital anomalies and key acquired neonatal conditions (notably **necrotising enterocolitis**, NEC). Successful outcomes depend heavily on:
- Early recognition (antenatal + postnatal)
- Stabilisation (temperature, glucose, fluids, ventilation)
- Safe transfer to appropriate centres
- Meticulous perioperative physiology management

### Clinical significance
Neonates have limited physiological reserve. Small errors in heat loss, fluid balance, ventilation, or infection control can rapidly become catastrophic.

---

## 2. Aetiology of congenital anomalies (high-yield categories)

Structural anomalies can arise from:
- **Single-gene defects**
- **Aneuploidy** (abnormal chromosome number)
- **Infections** (TORCH-type exposures)
- **Teratogens** (drugs, smoking, alcohol)
- **Mechanical/late effects** (e.g. volvulus, constriction phenomena)

Early developmental insults can produce multi-system syndromes (e.g. association patterns with vertebral/anorectal/cardiac/TEF/renal/limb anomalies).

## 3. Newborn physiology and principles of neonatal surgery

### 3.1 Thermoregulation
Neonates (especially preterm) lose heat rapidly:
- High surface area:weight ratio
- Thin skin, little subcutaneous fat
- Limited shivering thermogenesis

**Practical:** warm environment, active warming, warmed fluids, minimise exposure.

### 3.2 Fluid balance and nutrition
- Small absolute volumes → small errors become big problems
- Limited glycogen/fat stores → hypoglycaemia risk
- Early nutrition planning (enteral if possible; parenteral when needed)

### 3.3 Respiratory considerations
Neonates may need ventilatory support; many congenital anomalies compromise lungs (e.g. diaphragmatic hernia).

### 3.4 Infection risk
Immature immunity + invasive lines → high sepsis risk.

### 3.5 Transfer and pre-op stabilisation
If bowel obstruction suspected:
- Insert nasogastric tube for decompression (free drainage with regular aspiration)
- IV fluids and correction of electrolytes
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics where indicated
- Oxygen/ventilation as needed

## 4. Congenital anomalies commonly managed by neonatal surgeons (examples)

### 4.1 Oesophageal atresia ± tracheo-oesophageal fistula (TOF)
- Drooling, choking, inability to pass NG tube; aspiration risk
- Stabilise airway, suction pouch, avoid bag-mask insufflation if fistula suspected

### 4.2 Intestinal atresias (duodenal/jejunoileal)
- Bilious vomiting, abdominal distension
- “Double bubble” for duodenal atresia

### 4.3 Malrotation and volvulus
- Bilious vomiting is volvulus until proven otherwise
- Rapid progression to bowel ischaemia → urgent surgery after stabilisation

### 4.4 Abdominal wall defects
- **Gastroschisis**: exposed bowel; fluid/heat loss; protect bowel, resuscitate
- **Omphalocele**: sac-covered; associated anomalies common

### 4.5 Anorectal malformations / Hirschsprung disease
- Failure to pass meconium, distension, vomiting
- Decompression, contrast studies, staged surgery as needed

### 4.6 Congenital diaphragmatic hernia (overview)
- Pulmonary hypoplasia and hypertension drive risk
- Stabilise cardiorespiratory status before repair (often after initial ICU optimisation)

## 5. Necrotising enterocolitis (NEC)

### Key concept
NEC is an acquired inflammatory/ischaemic condition of the preterm bowel with risk of:
- Perforation
- Sepsis
- Strictures

### Presentation
- Feeding intolerance, abdominal distension, bloody stools
- Systemic deterioration (apnoea, temperature instability, shock)

### Management principles
- NPO, NG decompression
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics
- Fluids, ventilatory/ICU support
- Surgery when perforation, worsening despite maximal medical therapy, or necrosis suspected

---

## 6. Newborn tumours (examples)

### Sacrococcygeal teratoma
- Large mass, haemorrhage risk, high-output cardiac failure
- Requires stabilisation, imaging and planned excision (often with coccyx)

### Congenital neuroblastoma (example framework)
- Can present with abdominal mass; variable biology
- MDT staging and tailored therapy

---

## 7. Why adult surgeons should know neonatal surgery
- Adult surgeons may be involved in **transfer, stabilisation, and counselling** when specialist support is distant.
- Survivors of neonatal conditions may present later with complications or require transition care.

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Bilious vomiting = surgical emergency** in neonates until volvulus excluded.
2. **Preterm physiology is fragile**: heat loss and hypoglycaemia are common avoidable harms.
3. **NEC**: treat early; surgery for perforation or deterioration despite maximal medical therapy.
4. **Antenatal diagnosis matters**: delivery planning and early specialist involvement improve outcomes.
5. **Transfer safety** is part of neonatal surgery: decompression and thermoregulation during transport are critical.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **NEC** | Necrotising enterocolitis; acquired inflammatory/ischaemic bowel disease in neonates, mainly preterm. |
| **Malrotation/volvulus** | Abnormal midgut rotation with risk of twisting and bowel ischaemia. |
| **Gastroschisis** | Abdominal wall defect with exposed bowel (no protective sac). |
| **Omphalocele** | Abdominal wall defect with herniated viscera covered by a sac; associated anomalies common. |
| **TOF** | Tracheo-oesophageal fistula, often with oesophageal atresia. |
