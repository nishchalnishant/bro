# Chapter 7: Basic surgical skills

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the importance of safe patient positioning on the operating table.
- Learn about the steps involved in surgical site preparation (hair removal, skin antisepsis, draping).
- Understand the principles of surgical exposure, including open and laparoscopic access.
- Describe the concepts of wound closure, including types of sutures, needle characteristics, and suturing techniques.
- Appreciate basic haemostatic methods and the principles and safe use of electrosurgery.
- Understand the classification, role, and indications for surgical drains.

---

## 1. Patient Safety and Positioning

Successful outcomes depend on knowledge, judgement, and technical/non-technical skills (e.g., communication). The safe transfer and positioning of a patient is a shared team responsibility to prevent falls and other injuries, particularly in complex or bariatric cases.

### 1.1 Objectives of Correct Positioning
- Facilitate safe anaesthesia and surgery.
- Reduce adverse physiological insults and optimize surgical exposure/ergonomics.
- Maintain the patient's dignity by avoiding unnecessary exposure.

### 1.2 Common Surgical Positions
1. **Supine:** The most common. Can be modified (e.g., Rose’s position for neck surgery).
   - *Trendelenburg:* Head tilted down, knees slightly flexed (for pelvic procedures, shock resuscitation).
   - *Reverse Trendelenburg:* Head tilted up, feet down.
   - *Potential complications:* Brachial plexus injury (if arm abducted >90°), ulnar/peroneal neuropraxia, pressure necrosis, compromised respiration in steep Trendelenburg.
2. **Prone:** Used in spinal and certain rectal surgeries (e.g., jack-knife modification). Requires padded support from axillae to iliac crest for free breathing.
3. **Lateral:** Useful for thoracotomy, kidney positioning, or pilonidal sinus. Requires stirrups/straps to prevent rolling.
4. **Lithotomy / Lloyd-Davies:** Used for gynaecological, perineal, and urological access. Hips flexed and legs placed simultaneously in stirrups.
   - *Potential complications:* Compartment syndrome (venous/arterial insufficiency during long procedures), pressure on peroneal and saphenous nerves.

## 2. Preparation of the Surgical Site

Correct site preparation minimizes Surgical Site Infection (SSI).

- **Removal of Foreign Bodies:** Rings and piercings should be removed to prevent necrosis (postoperative oedema) or thermal burns (diathermy).
- **Hair Removal:** Should only be done if it interferes with surgery. Clipped (using disposable clipper blades) *on the operating table after pre-op antibiotics*. Shaving with a razor is discouraged as it creates micro-abrasions and increases SSI risk.
- **Skin Antisepsis:** An alcohol-based solution is recommended (e.g., chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine). The area is painted avoiding pooling (which can cause chemical burns or ignite during diathermy use).
- **Draping:** Sterile boundary formation. Uses fluid-resistant, anti-static drapes.

## 3. Surgical Exposure and Wound Approximation

### 3.1 Skin Incisions
- The scalpel should be pressed firmly, at right angles to the skin, avoiding oblique cuts (which cause ischemia).
- Natural skin creases (Relaxed Skin Tension Lines, Langer's lines) guide cosmetically favourable incisions. 
- Circular lesions should be excised using an elliptical incision (length 3 times the width to enable tension-free closure). If a "dog ear" remains, it is picked up with a skin hook and excised for cosmetic closure.

### 3.2 Abdominal Surgical Access (General Surgery)
- Both scalpel and diathermy can be used; there is no significant difference in SSI, but diathermy uses less operative time and results in a lower need for post-operative analgesia.
- **Midline laparotomy:** The standard approach for emergency procedures as it provides excellent, versatile exposure. 
  1. Incise skin via midline landmarks (xiphisternum to pubis).
  2. Subcutaneous tissue divided to expose the linea alba.
  3. Sharp incision of the linea alba near the umbilicus.
  4. Precise opening of the peritoneum to prevent visceral injury.

### 3.3 Laparoscopic Access and Port Placement
Laparoscopic surgery benefits include less pain, better cosmesis, shorter hospital stays, and magnified views.

- **Open (Hasson’s) Technique:** Evert the umbilicus, incise the stalk to the fascial decussation, and sharply open the fascia. A blunt trocar is directly placed into the peritoneum. Considered very safe.
- **Closed (Veress Needle) Technique:** Most often placed at Palmer's point (3cm below the left costal margin in the midclavicular line). A spring-loaded needle is advanced, resulting in a distinct 'pop/give'. Intraperitoneal presence is verified (e.g., negative aspiration, low pressure measurement) before insufflating CO₂.
- **Rules for Secondary Ports:** Must be inserted under direct vision, properly planned to avoid the inferior epigastric vessels (usually found within the lateral rectus sheath border), and placed perpendicularly to prevent instrument torque. All large fascial defects (>5mm) should be closed to prevent port-site herniation.

## 4. Wound Closure and Suturing

### 4.1 Types of Wound Healing
1. **Primary Intention:** Clean wounds with good blood supply directly opposed.
2. **Secondary Intention:** Wounds left open. Healing depends on the production of granulation tissue (capillaries, fibroblasts), contraction, and epithelialisation.
3. **Tertiary Intention:** Delayed primary closure. Wounds left open temporarily to resolve infection and subsequently sutured.

### 4.2 Suture Materials
- **Monofilament:** Glides through tissue easily but is difficult to knot.
- **Multifilament (Braided):** Knots well but has capillary properties (attracts bacteria into its interstices). Often coated to mitigate this.
- **Absorbable:** Degrades over a predicted timeframe (e.g., Polyglactin / Vicryl, Polydioxanone / PDS). Ideal for subcutaneous tissue and bowel anastomoses. 
- **Non-absorbable:** Polypropylene (Prolene), Nylon, Silk (unpredictable reaction, now mostly avoided). Essential for vascular anastomoses and prolonged tension.

### 4.3 Suturing and Knotting Techniques
- **Needles:** Round-bodied (separates tissue; used for bowel/vascular) vs Cutting (used for tough skin/fascia).
- **Interrupted:** Individual stitches perpendicular to the wound edge. Distance apart = 2 times the bite distance.
- **Continuous:** Tension is evenly distributed but breaking the suture unspools the entire line. 
- **Mattress:** Useful for ensuring eversion (or inversion) of irregular wound edges.
- **Subcuticular:** Cosmetically ideal for skin edges under no tension.
- **Knots:** The "Standard surgical knot" is a reef knot with an extra throw for security. Knots must be bedded down firmly but must not saw/strangulate tissue securely. Use instrument tying with precision.

### 4.4 Abdominal Wall Closure
The objective is tension-free closure preventing early dehiscence or late incisional hernia. 
The modern recommendation for abdominal wall mass closure involves using a delayed absorbable monofilament (e.g., PDS) in a continuous fashion. The current favored technique utilizes **small bites, placed closer together (0.5 cm)** with a thinner needle, resulting in better tissue preservation and a lower risk of 'cheese-wiring' compared to the older 'big needle, big bite' (1 cm) rule.

## 5. Haemostasis and Electrosurgery

Surgical bleeding requires immediate management using varied techniques: Mechanical (digital pressure, clips, ligatures, packing), Thermal (Electrosurgery), and Chemical/Topical agents (Fibrin, Thrombin, oxidized cellulose/Surgicel).

### 5.1 Principles of Electrosurgery
Electrosurgical Units (ESUs) convert direct wall current to high-frequency (>500,000 Hz) Alternating Current (AC) to heat human tissue (preventing muscle/nerve stimulation, which happens at <10,000 Hz).
- **"Cut" Mode:** Lower voltage, continuous 100% current flow. Heat causes tissue vaporization/spark arcing with minimal collateral spread.
- **"Coagulation" Mode:** Higher voltage, heavily modulated/interrupted flow. Results in cellular dehydration and protein denaturation, sealing vessels with wider lateral spread. 
- **Monopolar vs. Bipolar:** Monopolar involves passing energy through the active electrode and completing the circuit via a large patient return pad. Bipolar surgery passes the energy solely between the two active electrode prongs (e.g., forceps); no return pad is required. Highly preferred for delicate work (e.g., near the recurrent laryngeal nerve).

### 5.2 Hazards of Diathermy 
- Patient burns due to faulty, poorly attached return pads or short-circuiting to metal operating tables.
- "Channelling" injuries (Current concentrated through narrow stalks like the penis/spermatic cord leading to massive necrosis). Bipolar diathermy is mandatory in these scenarios.
- Ignition risks if allowed to react with pooled alcohol preps.
- Implantable Devices: May alter the program of pacemakers or ICDs.

### 5.3 Advanced Vessel-Sealing Devices
Allows safe ligation of vessels up to 7mm outside of conventional tying.
- *Bipolar systems* (LigaSure) deliver precisely controlled compressive pressure and energy to fuse collagen directly into a seal. 
- *Ultrasonic shears* (Harmonic) use an ultrasonically vibrating mechanical blade that denatures proteins through friction instead of electric current passing through the patient. It generates virtually no smoke.

## 6. Drains in Surgery

The routine prophylactic use of surgical drains (Lawson Tait's "when in doubt, drain") is largely historical, as it has been proven that drains generally do NOT prevent complications or anastomotic leakage. However, they may change the nature of a complication from an unmanageable deep abscess to a manageable external fistula.

### 6.1 Classification 
- **Open Drains:** (e.g., Corrugated, Penrose). A simple path of least resistance draining passively depending on gravity. Risk of retrograde bacterial contamination.
- **Closed Active/Suction Drains:** (Jackson-Pratt, Redon). Maintains a tight vacuum/negative pressure. Helps obliterate dead space (axillary/neck dissections, massive ventral hernias).
- **Closed Passive Drains:** (Robinson, Foley, NGT). Prevent suction against visceral wall mucosa but provide clean, contained gravity drainage.

### 6.2 Specialized Cases
- **Nasogastric Tube (NGT):** Largely eliminated from routine prophylactic placement in elective enhanced recovery (ERAS) pathways due to complications like reflux, aspiration, and airway trauma. Reserved mostly for therapeutic gastric decompression during acute bowel obstruction.
- **T-Tube Drainage:** A silicone 'T'-shaped tube placed directly into the Common Bile Duct after surgical exploration. Stays in place 2–3 weeks to encourage a robust fistulous tract. Its removal enables minor, self-limiting biliary drainage to the skin, bypassing spasm at the sphincter of Oddi. 

Drains should be promptly removed exactly when their primary goal of volume/purulence depletion has been met, as they contribute to pain, limit mobility, and act as ascending tracts for infection.
