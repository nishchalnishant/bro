# Chapter 9: Gastrointestinal Endoscopy

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the role of endoscopy as a diagnostic and therapeutic tool.
- Be familiar with the basic organization of an endoscopy unit and its equipment.
- Appreciate the critical aspects of consent and safe sedation.
- Understand the management of endoscopy in at-risk patients (e.g., diabetics, anticoagulated patients, and those needing antibiotic prophylaxis).
- Know the indications and techniques for OGD, ERCP, small bowel endoscopy, and colonoscopy, including EUS.
- Recognise and manage complications.

---

## 1. The Modern Endoscopy Unit and Preparation

### 1.1 Organisation and Equipment
A unit must ensure rigorous clinical governance, strict endoscopy training, and regular audits of key performance indicators (e.g., caecal intubation rate, adenoma detection, sedation use). Equipment includes forward- and lateral-viewing scopes, enteroscopes, colonoscopes (adult and paediatric), and electrosurgical units (including Argon Plasma Coagulation [APC]). 

### 1.2 Decontamination
- Endoscopes cannot be autoclaved and require high-level chemical disinfection.
- Single-use accessories are best practice.
- **Prion Risk:** Endoscopes used for 'invasive' procedures in patients at high risk of vCJD require separate decontamination with a single-use disinfectant and strict traceability, though full quarantine is usually no longer required just based on receipt of pooled plasma.

### 1.3 Consent and Safe Sedation
- **Consent:** Must cover the nature, purpose, risks, and alternatives. Minor adverse risks >10% and serious events >0.5% must be stated. Key risks include cardiopulmonary complications, perforation, and hemorrhage.
- **Sedation:** Uses *conscious sedation*, not general anaesthesia. Sedation-induced respiratory depression is the main cause of endoscopy-related mortality, especially in the elderly.
  - Supplemental oxygen and continuous pulse oximetry are mandatory.
  - Opiates (given first) and benzodiazepines have a synergistic effect, so titrate doses carefully.

### 1.4 Endoscopy in Special Risk Groups
1. **Diabetes:** Aim for early morning lists. Protocols should manage oral hypoglycemics or insulin depending on the procedure and preparation required.
2. **Antibiotic Prophylaxis:** Routine prophylaxis for endocarditis is *no longer recommended* for low-risk groups. Administer antibiotics for Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy (PEG) insertion (e.g., co-amoxiclav) and obstructed biliary systems at ERCP.
3. **Anticoagulation:** 
   - **Low-risk procedures** (Diagnostic OGD/Colonoscopy, biliary stenting): Continue most standard therapies.
   - **High-risk procedures** (Polypectomy, ERCP + Sphincterotomy, EMR/ESD, stricture dilatation): Warfarin often requires cessation for 5 days (bridging with LMWH for high-risk conditions like mechanical valves or recent VTE). Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs) are omitted 1–2 days prior. Clopidogrel is typically stopped 7 days prior.
   - **Aspirin:** generally continued for all procedures.

## 2. Upper Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (OGD)

OGD successfully visualizes the esophagus, stomach, duodenal bulb, and second part of the duodenum. An essential step is the 'retroversion' in the stomach to visualize the cardia and fundus.

### 2.1 Diagnostic Indications
- Investigation of upper GI bleeding, iron deficiency anemia, unexplained weight loss, persistent vomiting, dysphagia, or refractory dyspepsia.
- Surveillance of premalignant conditions (e.g., Barrett’s esophagus).
- Mucosal biopsies (for histology or rapid urease *H. pylori* testing).

### 2.2 Therapeutic OGD
- **Variceal bleeding:** Band ligation (esophageal) or Thrombin glue injection (gastric varices).
- **Peptic ulcer bleeding:** Dual therapy (e.g., epinephrine injection + thermal coagulation or hemoclip placement) followed by 72-hour IV proton pump inhibitors.
- **Vascular Ectasias (e.g., GAVE):** Treated via Argon Plasma Coagulation (APC).
- **Strictures & Achalasia:** Dilated with through-the-scope (TTS) balloons. Malignant strictures palliated with self-expanding metal stents (SEMS). Achalasia can also be treated with POEM (Peroral Endoscopic Myotomy).
- **Malignancy/Dysplasia:** Endoscopic Mucosal Resection (EMR) or Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD) for early neoplastic lesions. Barrett's high-grade dysplasia can be eradicated using Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA).
- **Nutrition:** PEG tube insertion for enteral feeding.

### 2.3 Complications
- Perforation (rare diagnostically; more common in therapeutic dilatations or EMR/ESD). Symptoms include chest/neck pain, tachycardia, subcutaneous emphysema, and dysphagia. Manage with strict NBM, IV antibiotics/fluids, CT, and urgent surgical review.

## 3. Endoscopic Assessment of the Small Bowel

Primarily indicated for obscure GI bleeding, confirmed iron-deficiency anemia (with normal OGD/Colonoscopy), or assessment of Crohn's disease extent.

1. **Capsule Endoscopy:** A pill-sized camera giving high-yield diagnostic mucosal views. 
   - *Contraindication:* Stricturing disease (risk of impaction). A dissolving 'patency capsule' trial should be used if a stricture is suspected. Avoid in severe gastroparesis. No biopsy or therapeutic capability.
2. **Single / Double-Balloon Enteroscopy:** An enteroscope with an overtube uses balloon inflation to pleat/accordion the small bowel. Can reach the entire small intestine from oral or rectal approaches under sedation/GA, allowing full therapeutics and biopsies.

## 4. Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)

Uses a side-viewing duodenoscope. Radiographic contrast is injected into the selected duct (biliary or pancreatic) under fluoroscopy. Because of the rise of Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP), ERCP has shifted from a diagnostic to a purely **therapeutic** modality.

### 4.1 Therapeutic Interventions
- **Biliary Sphincterotomy:** Eases the extraction of common bile duct stones using balloon trawlers or wire baskets (or lithotripsy for large stones).
- **Stenting:** Placement of plastic or covered metal stents to relieve malignant or benign biliary strictures.
- **Pancreatic Therapy:** Dilation of strictures, stone retrieval, and transgastric drainage of pseudocysts.

### 4.2 Complications
- High complication rate vs. other endoscopies. Includes duodenal perforation, haemorrhage post-sphincterotomy, and post-ERCP pancreatitis.
- **Post-ERCP Pancreatitis:** Mild to fatal necrotising pancreatitis. 
  - Risk factors: Young females, normal bilirubin, suspected sphincter of Oddi dysfunction, difficult cannulation.
  - Prophylaxis: **PR Indometacin or Diclofenac** administered peri-procedurally.

## 5. Colonoscopy

Colonoscopies use highly flexible scopes to examine the entire colon and terminal ileum. High Adenoma Detection Rates (ADR) are crucial quality markers that directly decrease interval colorectal cancer (CRC) incidence.

### 5.1 Technique Pearls
- Caecal intubation (required in >90% of procedures) relies on minimizing looping. Achieved via applying torque, reducing the scope, changing patient position, and abdominal counter-pressure.
- Carbon Dioxide insufflation is preferred over air for rapid resorption and lower perforation risk.
- **Optical Diagnosis:** *Chromoendoscopy* (indigo carmine dye) and *Narrow Band Imaging* (NBI) enhance capillary/mucosal pit patterns to differentiate hyperplastic vs. adenomatous vs. malignant polyps.

### 5.2 Therapeutic Colonoscopy
- **Polypectomy:**
  - Small non-pedunculated polyps (<15mm): Cold snare excision.
  - Stalked polyps: Hot snare (diathermy/coagulation).
- **EMR and ESD:** Used for large, flat, or sessile neoplasia (>20mm). Fluid is injected into the submucosa to lift the lesion safely away from the muscularis propria before excision (snare or knife), mitigating deep thermal injury or perforation.
- **Strictures:** TTS balloons for benign strictures; SEMS for palliative decompression in malignant obstruction (often used as a bridge to surgery).

### 5.3 Complications
- Perforation (0.1%) and Haemorrhage (0.3%), usually post-polypectomy. 
- *Post-polypectomy syndrome:* Transmural thermal burn (without frank perforation) causing localized abdominal pain and peritonism.
- Management involves immediate NBM, IV antibiotics, CT confirmation, and urgent colorectal surgical opinion.

## 6. Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS)

EUS combines mucosal imagery with high-frequency ultrasound to assess submucosal and extraintestinal structures.
- **Radial Echoendoscope:** For diagnostic local tumour staging (e.g., T-staging esophageal or gastric tumors).
- **Linear Echoendoscope:** Uses a working channel to conduct real-time Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) or Core Biopsy of mediastinal nodes, pancreatic masses, or submucosal lesions. Also enables drainage of pancreatic pseudocysts or difficult biliary systems.
