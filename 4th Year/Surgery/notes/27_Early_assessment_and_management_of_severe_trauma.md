# Chapter 27: Early assessment and management of severe trauma

## Learning objectives

- How to identify and assess the severely injured patient
- Early treatment goals for multiply injured patients
- Understand the role of permissive hypotension, tranexamic acid and massive transfusion protocols
- Understand the principles of damage control surgery (DCS) versus early total care (ETC)

---

## 1. Identification of severe trauma

### Key concept
Severe trauma is not always obvious. Mechanism helps, but **physiology** determines urgency. Early identification begins prehospital with clear communication that triggers appropriate hospital response.

### Prehospital pre-alert (typical essentials)
- Age/sex
- Mechanism of injury
- Suspected injuries
- Vital signs (RR, HR, BP, SpO₂, GCS)
- Interventions performed (airway, tourniquet, pelvic binder, TXA, fluids)

---

## 2. The trauma team

### Key concept
Trauma team activation enables **simultaneous** (not sequential) assessment and treatment using ATLS principles.

### Summary box 27.1: Role of the trauma team
- Allows simultaneous and efficient application of ATLS principles to identify/treat life-threatening pathology
- Should be led by the most senior clinician
- Senior clinicians from key specialties should attend “code red” calls
- Team leader anticipates next decisions and coordinates time-critical steps

### Code red / enhanced activation (concept)
Triggered for haemodynamic instability or airway compromise; aims to ensure:
- Senior decision-makers present early
- Massive transfusion protocol initiated early when indicated
- Immediate access to imaging/theatre/IR as needed

---

## 3. Early assessment: ATLS with parallel processing

### 3.1 Primary survey (ABCDE)
Priorities:
- Control catastrophic bleeding
- Secure airway with C-spine protection
- Treat breathing threats (tension pneumothorax, massive haemothorax)
- Restore circulation and oxygen delivery

### 3.2 Adjuncts and early imaging
- FAST/ultrasound in selected patients
- Portable X-rays (chest/pelvis) where helpful
- CT trauma series in stable patients to define injuries and plan definitive care

### 3.3 Reassessment
After each intervention, reassess physiology (vitals, perfusion, lactate, mental state).

---

## 4. Resuscitation goals in the multiply injured patient

### Key concept
The goal is **physiological stabilisation** and prevention of secondary injury:
- Oxygenation and ventilation
- Haemorrhage control
- Normothermia
- Correction of coagulopathy and acidosis

---

## 5. Haemorrhage control and damage control resuscitation (DCR)

### 5.1 Permissive hypotension (selected patients)
Concept: avoid high BP before haemostasis in uncontrolled bleeding to reduce clot disruption.

Cautions/contraindications (exam framing):
- Traumatic brain injury requiring adequate cerebral perfusion
- Prolonged hypotension risks organ ischaemia

### 5.2 Tranexamic acid (TXA)
Early TXA in major haemorrhage can reduce mortality when given promptly (follow local trauma protocols).

### 5.3 Massive transfusion protocols (MTP)
MTP supports rapid delivery of:
- Balanced blood products (RBC, plasma, platelets) in predefined ratios/pack structures
- Laboratory-guided correction (fibrinogen, calcium, pH, temperature)

Core goals:
- Stop bleeding and treat coagulopathy early
- Avoid the lethal triad: **hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy**

---

## 6. DCS versus early total care (ETC)

### 6.1 Damage control surgery (DCS)
Indicated when physiology is failing. Features:
- Rapid haemorrhage control (packing, vascular control)
- Contamination control (staple/clip bowel ends, temporary closure)
- Temporary closure and transfer to ICU for rewarming and correction

### 6.2 Early total care (ETC)
Definitive repair early may be appropriate when physiology is stable and resources allow.

### Decision principle
Choose the strategy that best matches **current physiology**, anticipated time burden, and the risk of “second hit” from prolonged surgery.

## Summary Boxes

### Box 27.2: Indicators of severe trauma (physiology-led)
- Hypotension or rising lactate/base deficit
- Altered mental state not explained by intoxication alone
- Respiratory compromise/hypoxia
- Need for major transfusion or ongoing haemorrhage control measures

### Box 27.3: Lethal triad
- Hypothermia
- Acidosis
- Coagulopathy

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Physiology overrides anatomy**: unstable patients need haemostasis and resuscitation first.
2. **Trauma teams save time** by parallel tasks; leadership is critical.
3. **TXA is time sensitive**—give early per protocol in major bleeding.
4. **MTP** prevents delays and treats coagulopathy early; monitor calcium and temperature.
5. **DCS** is for failing physiology; definitive repair waits until the patient is rewarmed and corrected.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **DCR** | Damage control resuscitation: integrated haemostatic resuscitation strategy in major trauma bleeding. |
| **MTP** | Massive transfusion protocol: predefined system for rapid balanced blood product delivery. |
| **DCS** | Damage control surgery: staged operative approach prioritising rapid control over definitive repair. |
| **ETC** | Early total care: definitive repair strategy early when physiology allows. |
