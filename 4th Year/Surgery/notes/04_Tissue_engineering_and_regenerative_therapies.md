## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
**Tissue engineering and regenerative medicine** are relatively new but rapidly expanding multidisciplinary fields that harness the biology at the site of tissue damage to mediate **regeneration** — not just fibrotic repair. They achieve this through the localised delivery of **cells, materials, and molecules**.

### Goal
Actual **tissue regeneration** (as in fetal healing), rather than the scar formation that normally occurs in adult wound healing.

### Why Relevant to Surgeons
- Surgeons are involved in assessment and treatment of conditions that could benefit
- Surgeons are integral to multidisciplinary translational research teams
- Tissue engineering may transform how diseases are treated in the operating theatre

### Opportunities (Table 4.1)

| Tissue | Conditions Treated |
|--------|-------------------|
| Skin | Burns, skin defects after excision/trauma |
| Eye | Retinal and corneal disease |
| Cardiac muscle | Heart failure |
| Heart valves | Congenital and acquired valvular heart disease |
| Cartilage and bone | Degenerative and traumatic bone/joint disorders |
| Trachea and bronchus | Congenital/acquired stenosis, resection for malignancy |
| Bladder | Congenital malformation, cystectomy |
| Anal/bladder sphincter | Incontinence |
| Pancreatic islets | Insulin-dependent diabetes |
| Large blood vessels | Aneurysmal, atheromatous, traumatic arterial disease |
| Oesophagus | Benign stenosis, resection for malignancy |
| Small intestine | Intestinal failure after resection for Crohn's/cancer/ischaemia |

**Longer term goal:** Whole organ replacement (liver, kidney) — but technical challenges enormous.

**Additional opportunity:** Tissue engineering can provide **in vitro models** to test therapeutic efficacy and drug toxicity (without animal testing).

### Summary Box 4.1: Tissue Engineering Provides Potential For
- Treatment for a wide range of diseases
- Clinical applications — underpinned by translational research, delivery strategies, clinical evidence
- In vitro models to test drug efficacy and toxicity

## 2. The Tissue Engineering Paradigm

Three fundamental components work together — the "triad":

```
         CELLS
        /      \
       /        \
   MATERIALS ── MOLECULES
```

| Component | Role | Examples |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Cells** | Biological building blocks; carry out regeneration | Somatic cells, stem cells |
| **Materials (Scaffolds)** | Structural framework; support cell growth; deliver cells/molecules | Polymers, ceramics, hydrogels |
| **Molecules** | Modulate biological environment; direct cell differentiation; control inflammation | Growth factors, anti-inflammatory agents, gene editing tools |

## 3. Cells in Tissue Engineering

### 3.1 Somatic (Differentiated) Cells

- Fully differentiated specialised cells obtained from normal tissues
- Used directly for tissue engineering
- **Examples:**
  - **Keratinocytes:** Skin regeneration for burns
  - **Chondrocytes:** Cartilage repair (ACI)
  - **Smooth muscle cells + uroepithelial cells:** Bladder wall engineering
- **Autologous** advantage: no immune rejection, no immunosuppression needed
- **Limitation:** Not readily available in sufficient numbers; limited proliferative ability in vitro

### 3.2 Stem Cells

**Definition:** Undifferentiated/non-specialised cells that can:
1. **Self-renew** indefinitely (through cell division)
2. **Differentiate** into one or more specialised cell types (potency)

**Classification by Potency (Figure 4.2):**

| Potency Level | Cell Type | Capability |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Totipotent** | Zygote (fertilised egg), early cleavage cells | Can form ALL body cells + extraembryonic tissues (placenta) |
| **Pluripotent** | ESCs, iPSCs | Can form ALL body cells but NOT extraembryonic tissues |
| **Multipotent** | Adult/somatic stem cells (SSCs) | Can form a limited range of specialised cells |
| **Nullipotent** | Terminally differentiated cells | Cannot form other cell types |

---

### 3.3 Adult/Somatic Stem Cells (SSCs)

- Resident in different tissues and organs
- Responsible for replacing senescent or damaged cells
- **High activity in:** Bone marrow, gut
- **Lower activity in:** Heart, central nervous system
- **Characteristics:** Multipotent (limited potency compared to pluripotent cells)

#### Mesenchymal Stromal/Stem Cells (MSCs)
The most clinically relevant SSC population in tissue engineering.

**Sources:**
- Bone marrow (iliac crest aspiration)
- Subcutaneous fat (liposuction/lipoaspiration)

**Minimum Criteria for MSCs (ISCT 2006 Criteria):**
1. Adherence to plastic (in standard culture)
2. Expression of CD105, CD73, CD90 (positive markers)
3. Lack of CD45, CD34, CD14, CD11b, CD79α, CD19, HLA-DR (negative markers)
4. **Trilineage differentiation:** Can differentiate into **osteoblasts, adipocytes, chondroblasts** in vitro

**How MSCs work clinically:**
```
MSC mechanism (two proposed pathways):
A) Direct differentiation → becomes the desired cell type
B) Paracrine/secretory mechanism (more important) →
   Interacts with inflammatory + immune processes
   Releases cytokines, growth factors
   Modulates macrophage behaviour, T-cells, NK cells, B-cells
```

> **Key exam point:** MSCs largely work via **paracrine and secretory mechanisms** rather than direct differentiation into tissue — this is why the term "mesenchymal stromal cell" is preferred over "stem cell"

**DOSES nomenclature for MSC reporting:**
- **D** — Donor
- **O** — Origin tissue
- **S** — Separation method
- **E** — Exhibited characteristics
- **S** — Site of delivery

---

### 3.4 Embryonic Stem Cells (ESCs)

- Derived from the **inner cell mass of the early human blastocyst** (days 4–5)
- Embryos from IVF that are surplus to those needed for reimplantation
- First isolated in culture by **James Thomson in 1998**
- **Pluripotent** with excellent proliferative capacity

**Advantages:**
- Pluripotent — can differentiate into any cell type
- Self-renewing indefinitely

**Disadvantages:**
| Limitation | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| **Major ethical concerns** | Destruction of an embryo required; major political/ethical debate |
| **Risk of rejection** | Allogeneic cells → immunological rejection possible → immunosuppression needed |
| **Risk of teratoma** | Pluripotent cells can form teratomas if undifferentiated cells are implanted |

> **Exam note:** The ability to form a teratoma (containing tissue from all 3 germ layers) is a hallmark of TRUE pluripotency.

---

### 3.5 Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs)

**Breakthrough discovery (2006) by Shinya Yamanaka** (Nobel Prize 2012, shared with John Gurdon):

```
Adult somatic cell (e.g., skin fibroblast or peripheral blood mononuclear cell)
      ↓
Viral transfection of reprogramming factors
(OSKM factors: OCT3/4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC — or NANOG and LIN28)
      ↓
Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell (iPSC)
      ↓
In vitro differentiation with specific growth factors/chemicals
      ↓
Desired specialised cell type for therapy
```

**Advantages over ESCs:**

| Feature | iPSC Advantage |
|---------|----------------|
| **Ethics** | No embryo destruction needed |
| **Autologous** | Patient's own cells → no immune rejection |
| **Personalised** | Patient-specific cells |
| **Availability** | Easy cell source (skin, blood) |

**Autologous iPSC Workflow (Figure 4.4):**
```
Patient skin biopsy/blood sample
     ↓ Reprogramming factors (OSKM)
iPSCs generated (takes weeks)
     ↓ Differentiation factors
Desired cell type (chondrocytes, neurones, myocytes, pancreatic islet cells)
     ↓
Cell therapy delivered to patient
```

**Alternative iPSC strategy:** HLA-matched iPSC banks from volunteer donors → choose closest match → less immunosuppression needed

**Risks and mitigation:**

| Risk | Mechanism | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Insertional mutagenesis → tumorigenesis** | Retroviral vectors integrate into genome → may activate oncogenes | Use non-integrating vectors: adenovirus, Sendai virus, episomal plasmids, synthetic RNA; small molecules instead of viral vectors |
| **Teratoma formation** | Residual undifferentiated pluripotent cells implanted | Ensure full in vitro differentiation; confirm absence of undifferentiated cells before use |
| **Genetic/epigenetic abnormalities** | Reprogramming process can introduce mutations | Rigorous characterisation before clinical use |

### 3.6 Cell Comparison Table (Table 4.2)

| Feature | Somatic Cells | SSCs | ESCs | iPSCs |
|---------|--------------|------|------|-------|
| Ease of availability | Limited | Good | Moderate | Good |
| In vitro expansion | Limited | Good | Excellent | Excellent |
| Potency | None | Limited (multipotent) | Excellent (pluripotent) | Excellent (pluripotent) |
| Ethical concerns | None | None | YES (major) | Limited |
| Risk of malignancy | None | Low | Moderate | Moderate |
| Autologous possible | YES | YES | NO | YES |
| Future use | Limited | High | Limited | High |

## 4. Exemplars: Cells as Therapy

### 4.1 Skin Regeneration

**Historical note:** Skin was one of the first tissues engineered with human cells (Rheinwald and Green).

**Process:**
```
Harvest keratinocytes from patient's healthy skin
      ↓
Culture in vitro with mitotically inhibited fibroblasts (provide paracrine factors)
      ↓
Keratinocytes grown on collagenous membrane
      ↓
Air-liquid interface → Stratification of epidermal layer
      ↓
Tissue-engineered skin applied to wound (takes ~1 month)
```

**Why fibroblasts are needed:** Provide paracrine factors for keratinocyte phenotype maintenance and maturation. Must be mitotically inhibited (otherwise fibroblasts overgrow keratinocytes).

**Limitations:**
- Process takes ~1 month
- Burn wound must be protected/covered during this time
- Lacks features of normal skin (colour, appendages)
- Not in widespread clinical use

### 4.2 Articular Cartilage Regeneration

**Challenge:** Articular cartilage has very limited intrinsic regenerative capacity (avascular, aneural).

#### Method 1: Microfracture / Microdrilling

**Mechanism:**
```
Drill into subchondral bone at base of cartilage defect
      ↓
Creates communication between intra-articular space + subchondral bone
      ↓
Blood + bone marrow stromal cells enter defect
      ↓
Clot forms → Environment for cartilage reformation
      ↓
Fibrocartilage forms (mechanically inferior to hyaline cartilage)
```

**Used clinically for >30 years**

**Limitation:** Forms **fibrocartilage** — mechanically inferior to native hyaline articular cartilage; lacks long-term durability.

#### Method 2: Autologous Chondrocyte Implantation (ACI)

**Two-stage procedure (Figure 4.5):**
```
Stage 1: Arthroscopy — harvest small cartilage sample from healthy area of patient's knee
      ↓
Lab expansion of chondrocytes (GMP quality standards)
      ↓ (weeks to months)
Stage 2: Open surgery — inject expanded cells into defect
      ↓
Secured under surgically positioned patch/roof
      ↓
Hyaline cartilage forms (superior to microfracture)
```

**UK NICE recommendation (2017):** ACI recommended for NHS use in patients with symptomatic articular cartilage defects of the knee (cost-effective at <£20,000/QALY).

**Advantage of ACI over Microfracture:** Forms **hyaline cartilage** (mechanically superior, closer to normal articular cartilage).

## 5. Materials (Scaffolds)

Materials serve as:
1. Structural scaffold for cell attachment and growth
2. Delivery vehicles for cells and/or molecules
3. Osteoinductive or chondroinductive substrates

### 5.1 Synthetic and Engineered Polymers

**Common materials:**
- **Poly(caprolactone) [PCL]** — resorbable; widely used in scaffolds and sutures
- **Poly(lactic acid) [PLA]** — resorbable; used in scaffolds (Figure 4.6)
- **Poly(glycolic acid) [PGA]** — resorbable sutures
- **PLGA (copolymer)** — widely used combination

**Processing methods:**

| Method | Product | Application |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Foaming with supercritical CO₂** | Porous monoliths (Figure 4.6) | Bone scaffolds; pore size tailored by process conditions |
| **Electrospinning** | High-surface-area fibrous mats (Figure 4.7) | Mimics ECM; used in tendon repair (rotator cuff), vascular grafts |
| **Emulsion processing** | Microspheres | Drug delivery |

**Electrospinning detail:**
- An electric field draws charged polymer threads → deposits them onto a surface
- Creates fibrous mats with very high surface area
- Fibres mimic the dimensions of the extracellular matrix
- Used to create patches for rotator cuff repair, tendon reconstruction

**Other important synthetic polymers (used clinically):**
- PMMA (polymethyl methacrylate): intraocular lenses, bone cement in joint replacement
- Dacron™: vascular grafts

### 5.2 Bioceramics

**Bioglass® (most important):**
- Composition: Calcium, silicate, phosphate, sodium oxide (Na₂O–CaO–P₂O₅–SiO₂)
- In body fluids: surface degrades → releases ions → deposits bone-like mineral on surface
- This mineral encourages:
  - Material to **bond to surrounding bone and soft tissue**
  - Recruitment and differentiation of **mesenchymal cell populations**
- Forms: granules (most common), foamed structures, monoliths
- Incorporated into polymer matrices for bone integration
- Also used in **remineralising toothpaste** (as Novamin®)
- Addition of strontium or lithium ions → further enhances bone formation

### 5.3 Hydrogels

**Composition:** Hydrophilic polymers dispersed in aqueous medium → form a gel-like structure (~99% water)

**Properties:**
- Highly hydrated → nutrients, O₂, metabolic by-products can diffuse through
- Biocompatible, tunable mechanical properties
- Formed by: temperature change, ion addition, or chemical cross-linking

**Applications:**

| Application | Example |
|------------|---------|
| **Cell encapsulation** | Alginate hydrogel encapsulating pancreatic islets → protects from immunological attack |
| **Tissue scaffolding** | Structures from bone to brain |
| **3D Bioprinting** | Complex geometries (blood vessels, osteochondral plugs) |

**Materials for hydrogels:**
- Natural: Alginate, chitosan, collagen, hyaluronic acid, gellan
- Synthetic: Poly(ethylene glycol) — can be chemically modified for specific biological stimuli

**3D Printing with Hydrogels — Technical Challenge:**
- Hydrogels are extremely weak when liquid → slump and lose shape when cast
- **Solution:** Suspended printing methods

**Suspended Layer Additive Manufacturing (SLAM):**
```
Print hydrogel (± cells) into a supportive particulate medium
      ↓
Hydrogel is supported while it develops mechanical integrity
      ↓
Wash supporting medium away with water
      ↓
Complex hydrogel structure retained (e.g., bifurcating carotid artery [Figure 4.9])
```

**FRESH method (Free-form Reversible Embedding of Suspended Hydrogels):**
- Uses gelatin-based support matrix
- Hydrogel printed within gelatin bed
- Gelatin melted at 37°C → complex structure revealed

**Current clinical relevance:** Most useful for disease modelling and drug testing; longer-term potential for tissue regeneration.

## 6. Molecules

### 6.1 Role of Molecules
Molecules can:
- Modulate the inflammatory environment (enhance or inhibit specific pathways)
- Direct angiogenesis, fibrosis, cell differentiation
- Prevent pathological scarring

### 6.2 Healing Skin Without Scarring

**The Problem:**
```
Severe skin damage/infection
      ↓
Inflammatory "overshoot"
      ↓
Excessive TGFβ1 release
      ↓
Excess myofibroblast recruitment
      ↓
Overproduction + overcontraction of collagen matrix
      ↓
Classic scar formation
```

**Molecular intervention — TGFβ3:**
- **TGFβ1 and TGFβ2:** Drive fibrosis and scar formation
- **TGFβ3:** Antagonist — regulates ECM formation; blocks TGFβ1/2 receptors; modulates cell penetration into new tissue
- **TGFβ3 as therapy:** Developed as anti-scarring agent; showed significant potential in Phase I/II trials but not ultimately adopted
- **Lesson:** Exemplar of translational approach — from mechanism → preclinical → clinical trials → evidence

### 6.3 Preventing Ocular Fibrosis (Corneal Scarring)

**The Problem:**
```
Corneal infection
      ↓
Potent inflammatory response
      ↓
Rapid, disordered collagenous tissue deposition across corneal surface
      ↓
Cornea becomes cloudy → Blindness
```

**Normal corneal healing** (clean wound): Re-epithelialisation within ~7 days.

**Molecular solution — Decorin:**
- **Decorin:** A proteoglycan that decorates normal ECM (high affinity for collagen I)
- In solution: Decorin has affinity for 7 different cytokines, including **TGFβ1** → modulates fibrosis
- **Decorin eye drop:** Delivers sustained dose to corneal surface
- **Result:** Effective in preclinical models — rapid restoration of transparent ocular surface; significant reduction in scar markers (α-SMA, fibronectin, laminin)

**Challenge:** Large biomolecules (proteins) are sensitive to environmental changes; lose bioactivity rapidly; expensive; sustained delivery is technically difficult.

### 6.4 Challenges in Molecular Delivery

| Challenge | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Small molecules** | Diffuse rapidly through delivery materials → burst release rather than sustained delivery |
| **Large biomolecules (proteins)** | Very sensitive to environmental changes (pH, temperature); high activity at low concentrations; expensive; difficult to formulate |
| **Sustained delivery** | Required to avoid repeated administration; achieved by implantable degradable polymer systems |
| **Targeting** | Getting the molecule to the right tissue at the right time and in the right amount |

**Solution:** Implantable degradable dense polymer systems → slow localised release as polymer degrades.

**Emerging Technologies:**
- **Extracellular vesicles (EVs):** Cell-derived vesicles for therapeutic signalling
- **CRISPR-Cas9:** Gene editing of cells before transplantation to correct genetic disease or modify cell behaviour

## 7. Safety Concerns

Cell-based therapies carry unique risks that require stringent regulation and GMP standards.

### 7.1 Safety Risks (Table 4.3)

| Risk | Cell Types at Risk | Detail | Mitigation |
|------|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| **Tumour formation** | ESCs, iPSCs (NOT SSCs) | Pluripotent cells form teratomas; hallmark of pluripotency; risk if undifferentiated cells implanted | Ensure full in vitro differentiation; confirm absence of undifferentiated cells; use non-integrating viral vectors |
| **Genetic/epigenetic abnormalities** | iPSCs (viral reprogramming) | Retroviral integration → insertional mutagenesis → oncogene activation → malignant transformation | Use non-retroviral vectors (adenovirus, Sendai virus, episomal plasmids, synthetic RNA); small molecules to avoid viral vectors |
| **Transmission of infection** | All allogeneic cells | Viral/bacterial contamination during cell culture | GMP standards; screen donor cells; bacterial-free culture conditions |
| **Poor viability / loss of function** | All | Cells may not survive or function after transplantation | Rigorous quality assurance; characterise function before use |
| **Differentiation to undesired cell types** | Pluripotent cells | Cells may differentiate along wrong lineage | Defined differentiation protocols; cell characterisation |
| **Rejection** | Allogeneic cells | Recipient immune system attacks donor cells | Autologous cells (iPSCs, SSCs); HLA-matched banks; immunosuppression |
| **Side effects of immunosuppression** | Allogeneic cells | Required to prevent rejection | Use autologous cells where possible; HLA matching to reduce immunosuppression needed |

### 7.2 Transdifferentiation — An Alternative Approach
- Directly reprogramming one somatic cell type into another somatic cell type
- **Without** passing through a pluripotent intermediate
- **Advantage:** No teratoma risk
- **Example:** Fibroblasts reprogrammed into cardiomyocytes
- Currently experimental

## 8. Future Directions

The field is evolving rapidly, with several emerging technologies:

| Technology | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Extracellular vesicles (EVs)** | Cell-derived vesicles used for therapeutic signalling without transplanting cells directly |
| **CRISPR-Cas9** | Precise gene editing of cells before transplantation; corrects genetic disease; modifies cell behaviour |
| **Gene therapies** | Deliver therapeutic genes directly to target tissues |
| **Patient stratification** | Molecular phenotyping/genotyping → more targeted, personalised therapy |
| **Point-of-care manufacturing** | New regulatory frameworks being developed for bedside manufacturing of cell therapies |

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. **Tissue engineering triad:** Cells + Materials + Molecules — all three work together
2. **MSCs work primarily via paracrine mechanisms** (not direct differentiation) — hence preferred term is "stromal" not "stem"
3. **MSC minimum criteria (ISCT):** Plastic adherence + CD105/73/90 positive + CD45/34 negative + trilineage differentiation (osteo, adipo, chondro)
4. **iPSC OSKM factors:** OCT3/4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC (Yamanaka factors); Nobel Prize 2012
5. **Teratoma formation = hallmark of pluripotency** (ESCs and iPSCs) — NOT SSCs
6. **Microfracture produces fibrocartilage** (inferior); **ACI produces hyaline cartilage** (superior)
7. **NICE approved ACI (2017)** for knee cartilage defects on NHS
8. **Bioglass® mechanism:** Ion release in body fluids → bone-like mineral deposition → tissue bonding + MSC recruitment
9. **Electrospinning:** High-surface-area fibrous scaffold mimicking ECM; used in rotator cuff, tendon repair
10. **TGFβ1/2 → scarring; TGFβ3 → anti-scarring** (blocks TGFβ1/2 receptors)
11. **Decorin:** Proteoglycan; binds TGFβ1; delivered as eye drop → prevents corneal fibrosis
12. **Retroviral reprogramming risk:** Insertional mutagenesis → oncogene activation → tumorigenesis
13. **Autologous iPSCs** avoid rejection; autologous SSCs avoid rejection
14. **GMP standards** (Good Manufacturing Practice) essential for all cell-based therapies
15. **Transdifferentiation:** Direct conversion of one somatic cell to another without pluripotent intermediate → avoids teratoma risk

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Totipotent** | Can give rise to ALL body cells PLUS extraembryonic tissues (e.g., placenta) — zygote |
| **Pluripotent** | Can give rise to all body cells but NOT extraembryonic tissues — ESCs, iPSCs |
| **Multipotent** | Can give rise to a limited range of specialised cells — SSCs, MSCs |
| **Nullipotent** | Terminally differentiated; cannot give rise to other cell types |
| **Self-renewal** | Ability of stem cells to divide and produce more stem cells indefinitely |
| **MSC** | Mesenchymal stromal cell; multipotent adult cell from bone marrow or fat; trilineage differentiation |
| **ESC** | Embryonic stem cell; derived from inner cell mass of blastocyst (day 4–5); pluripotent |
| **iPSC** | Induced pluripotent stem cell; adult cell reprogrammed to pluripotent state via Yamanaka/OSKM factors |
| **OSKM factors** | OCT3/4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC — transcription factors used to reprogram adult cells to iPSCs |
| **Teratoma** | Tumour containing tissue from all 3 embryonic germ layers; hallmark of true pluripotency |
| **Trilineage differentiation** | Ability to differentiate into osteoblasts, adipocytes, and chondroblasts (defines MSC in vitro) |
| **Electrospinning** | Manufacturing technique using electric field to draw charged polymer threads into high-surface-area fibrous mats |
| **Bioglass®** | Calcium-silicate-phosphate ceramic; bonds to bone; osteoinductive; recruits MSCs |
| **Hydrogel** | Highly hydrated (≤99% water) polymer network; allows nutrient diffusion; used for cell encapsulation |
| **ACI** | Autologous chondrocyte implantation; two-stage procedure; produces hyaline cartilage |
| **Microfracture** | Drilling into subchondral bone; allows bone marrow cells to form fibrocartilage; mechanically inferior |
| **Transdifferentiation** | Direct reprogramming of one somatic cell into another without pluripotent intermediate |
| **Decorin** | Proteoglycan; binds collagen I + TGFβ1; delivered as eye drops to prevent corneal fibrosis |
| **SLAM** | Suspended Layer Additive Manufacturing; 3D printing method for complex hydrogel structures |
| **GMP** | Good Manufacturing Practice; regulatory standards for manufacturing cell-based therapies |
| **CRISPR-Cas9** | Gene editing tool; used to modify cells for therapeutic purposes; Nobel Prize 2020 |
