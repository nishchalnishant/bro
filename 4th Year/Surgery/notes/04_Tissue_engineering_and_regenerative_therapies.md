# Chapter 4: Tissue engineering and regenerative therapies

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the potential opportunities afforded by tissue engineering and regenerative therapy
- Understand the nature of stem cells, including somatic and adult stem cells, embryonic stem cells and induced pluripotent stem cells
- Understand the role and range of materials and scaffolds for tissue engineering

- Understand the main challenges, safety issues and future directions

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
**Tissue engineering and regenerative medicine** harness the biology at the site of tissue damage to mediate regeneration through the localised delivery of **cells, materials, and molecules**. The goal is *actual tissue regeneration*, not merely fibrotic repair.

### 1.1 The Tissue Engineering Paradigm
The field relies on a triad:
1. **Cells:** Providing the biological building blocks (e.g., somatic cells, stem cells).
2. **Materials:** (Scaffolds) Providing the structural framework (e.g., polymers, ceramics, hydrogels).
3. **Molecules:** Modifying the biological response (e.g., growth factors, osteogenic/anti-inflammatory regulators).

## 2. Cells in Tissue Engineering

### 2.1 Somatic Cells
- Fully differentiated cells isolated from normal tissues.
- Examples include **keratinocytes** for skin burns, and **autologous chondrocytes** for cartilage defects.
- **Limitation:** Hard to obtain in sufficient numbers and have limited proliferative ability in vitro.

### 2.2 Stem Cells
- Unspecialised cells that can self-renew indefinitely and differentiate into specialized cell types (**potency**).
- **Totipotent:** Can form all body & extraembryonic cell types (zygote).
- **Pluripotent:** Can form all cell types of the body (e.g., embryonic stem cells).
- **Multipotent:** Can form a limited range of specialised cells (e.g., adult somatic stem cells).

#### A. Somatic (Adult) Stem Cells (SSCs)
- Resident in adult tissues (bone marrow, gut) to replace senescent/damaged cells.
- **Mesenchymal Stromal Cells (MSCs):** A key type of multipotent cell isolated from bone marrow or fat. 
  - Show adherence to plastic.
  - Can differentiate into osteoblasts, adipocytes, and chondroblasts in vitro (trilineage differentiation).
  - Act largely via *paracrine and secretory behaviour* (interacting with inflammatory and immune processes) rather than purely direct cellular replacement.

#### B. Embryonic Stem Cells (ESCs)
- Derived from the inner cell mass of the early blastocyst (days 4-5).
- **Pluripotent**, but fraught with major ethical concerns and a risk of immunological rejection (allogeneic) and teratoma formation.

#### C. Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs)
- Somatic cells (e.g., skin fibroblasts) reprogrammed back to an embryonic-like pluripotent state.
- Triggered by specific transcription factors (e.g., the OSKM reprogramming factors: OCT3/4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC).
- **Advantages:** Circumvents embryonic ethical issues and allows for *autologous* generation (no immune rejection).
- **Risks:** The initial use of retroviral vectors presented a risk of insertional mutagenesis and tumourigenesis (oncogene activation).

## 3. Exemplars of Cell Therapy

### 3.1 Skin Regeneration
- Engineered skin is primarily generated using **keratinocytes** expanded in culture over a collagenous membrane alongside mitotically inhibited fibroblasts (which provide necessary paracrine factors). 
- Allows for the treatment of severe burns.

### 3.2 Articular Cartilage Regeneration
- **Microfracture/Microdrilling:** Targeting endogenous cells by drilling into subchondral bone, allowing bone marrow stem cells to form a clot in the defect. (Limitation: forms mechanically inferior fibrocartilage).
- **Autologous Chondrocyte Implantation (ACI):** Harvesting healthy chondrocytes, expanding them in vitro, and reimplanting them under a patch in the defect space to specifically regenerate *hyaline* cartilage.

## 4. Materials

Materials serve as scaffolds and delivery vehicles. They can be natural or synthetic.

### 4.1 Synthetic and Engineered Polymers
- Includes resorbable polymers like poly(caprolactone) (PCL) and poly(lactic acid) (PLA).
- Can be processed into:
  - **Porous monoliths** via foaming (using supercritical CO2).
  - **Fibrous mats** via **electrospinning** (creating high surface area patches that mimic extracellular matrix, used in tendon repair).

### 4.2 Bioceramics
- Used for regenerating mineralised tissues (bone).
- **Bioglass®:** A glassy ceramic containing calcium, silicate, and phosphate ions. Placed in body fluids, it degrades and deposits bone-like mineral, encouraging tissue bonding and recruiting mesenchymal cells.

### 4.3 Hydrogels
- Highly hydrated networks of hydrophilic polymers (up to 99% water) that allow nutrient/oxygen diffusion.
- Extremely useful for **cell encapsulation**. For example, alginate used to encapsulate pancreatic islets to protect them from immunological attack.
- Used heavily in **Additive Layer Manufacturing (3D Printing)**: Methods like SLAM (Suspended Layer Additive Manufacturing) allow printing complex geometries (like bifurcating blood vessels) by supporting the weak hydrogel inside a secondary medium while it sets.

## 5. Molecules

Molecules (like growth factors or inhibitors) modulate the inflammatory environment and direct healing.

### 5.1 Healing Skin Without Scarring
- Normal wound healing upregulates cytokines like **TGF-β1**, driving excessive myofibroblast recruitment and collagen deposition, yielding a scar.
- **TGF-β3** acts as an antagonist and regulates extracellular matrix formation. It was explored heavily as an anti-scarring agent to promote scarless healing.

### 5.2 Preventing Ocular Fibrosis
- Damage + infection on the cornea leads to collagenous fibrosis and blindness.
- **Decorin**, a proteoglycan, binds and neutralises TGF-β1. Delivered via eye drops, it successfully prevents intense scarring and restores ocular transparency in preclinical models.
- **Challenge:** Sustained delivery of large, unstable biomolecules is very difficult without them diffusing away too rapidly.

## 6. Safety Concerns

Cell-based therapies are highly regulated due to severe potential risks:

| Risk | Description / Mitigation |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Tumour Formation** | The biggest concern for pluripotent stem cells (ESCs, iPSCs). Pluripotent cells naturally form **teratomas**. Must ensure full in vitro differentiation prior to implantation. |
| **Genetic Abnormalities** | Retroviral vectors for iPSCs can cause insertional mutagenesis. Shift to using non-integrating vectors (adenovirus, episomal plasmids). |
| **Infection Transmission** | GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) standards are strictly required. |
| **Rejection** | An issue for allogeneic cells. Solved by using autologous iPSCs or closely HLA-matched banks. |

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **MSCs mechanisms:** Mesenchymal Stromal Cells largely work via *paracrine signalling* to modulate the immune response rather than strictly differentiating into the tissue themselves.
2. **Pluripotency rule:** An indicator of true pluripotency in vivo is the ability of the cell line to form a **teratoma** (tumours containing tissues from all three embryonic germ layers).
3. **iPSC induction:** Specialised adult cells are forced back into pluriopotency (iPSCs) via the insertion of specific transcription factors known as the OSKM (Yamanaka) factors.
4. **Articular cartilage repair:** Microfracture heals defects with inferior 'fibrocartilage', whereas ACI (Autologous Chondrocyte Implantation) aims to regenerate normal 'hyaline' cartilage.

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Totipotent** | A stem cell capable of giving rise to ALL cell types of the body PLUS extraembryonic tissues (placenta). |
| **Pluripotent** | Capable of giving rise to all cell types of the body, but not extraembryonic tissues. |
| **Electrospinning** | A manufacturing technique using an electric field to draw charged polymer threads, creating high-surface-area fibrous scaffolds mimicking the extracellular matrix. |
| **Transdifferentiation** | Directly reprogramming one somatic cell type into a different somatic cell type without passing through a pluripotent intermediate state. |
| **Bioglass** | Processed ceramic which rapidly exchanges ions with human fluid to bond directly with bone and stimulate osteogenesis. |
