# Chapter 1: Metabolic Response to Injury

## Learning Objectives

- Understand how the body responds to accidental injury and surgery
- Understand physiological and biochemical changes during injury and recovery
- Understand mediators and pathways of the metabolic response to injury
- Recognise avoidable factors that compound the metabolic response
- Understand how the metabolic response influences surgical outcomes
- Understand concepts behind optimal perioperative care

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
The **metabolic response to injury** is a systemic, predictable, and graded cascade of physiological and biochemical changes triggered by tissue trauma (surgical, accidental, burns, sepsis). It involves three interacting systems:
- **Neural circuits** (nociceptive afferents → hypothalamic–pituitary axis)
- **Hormones** (counter-regulatory hormones and their downstream effects)
- **Inflammation-related mediators** (cytokines, DAMPs, PAMPs)

### Why It Matters Clinically
- The severity of the response dictates surgical outcomes
- Uncontrolled metabolic response → **sepsis → MODS (Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome)**
- MODS mortality: **~25%** in modern trauma systems
- Understanding and attenuating the response is the purpose of ERAS, damage control and perioperative care

---

## 2. Homeostasis and the Two Phases of Metabolic Response

### Definition
Homeostasis = maintaining a constant internal environment for optimal cellular function. Injury disrupts this and the body's response attempts to restore it.

### The Two Phases

| Phase | Timing | Core Features | Dominant Mediators |
|-------|--------|---------------|--------------------|
| **Catabolic ('ebb') phase** | 0–48 hours post-injury | Hypovolaemia, ↓BMR, ↓cardiac output, hypothermia, lactic acidosis, muscle catabolism, hyperglycaemia | Catecholamines, cortisol, aldosterone |
| **Hypermetabolic ('flow') phase** | After resuscitation, days–weeks | Tissue oedema, ↑BMR (15–25%), ↑cardiac output, raised temperature, leukocytosis | IL-6, TNF-α, counter-regulatory hormones |
| **Anabolic phase** | Weeks–months | Tissue repair, rebuilding, return to anabolism | Growth hormone, insulin, IGF-1 |

### Surgical Goals
- Minimise primary insult (minimal access surgery, ERAS)
- Return patient to homeostasis rapidly
- Avoid avoidable compounding factors (hypothermia, starvation, oedema)

---

## 3. Magnitude of the Metabolic Response

### Key Principle: Graded Response
> **The more severe the injury, the greater the metabolic response**

| Injury Type | Response Characteristics |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| Minor elective surgery | Transient modest rise in temperature, HR, RR, energy expenditure |
| Major elective surgery | Moderate SIRS, significant catabolism |
| Major trauma / emergency surgery | SIRS, hypermetabolism, marked catabolism, shock, possible MODS |
| Burns / sepsis | Maximum response, prolonged hypermetabolism |

### Individual Variability
- Genetic polymorphisms in cytokine receptors affect intensity
- Age, frailty, malnutrition, and medications modify the response
- Children often have an exaggerated inflammatory response

## 4. Mediators of the Metabolic Response

### 4.1 Damage-Associated Molecular Patterns (DAMPs) — The Initiators

#### What They Are
Released from **damaged or dying cells** (aseptic injury); also called **alarmins**. They signal "danger" to the innate immune system.

**Important DAMPs:**
- **HMGB1** (High Mobility Group Box 1) — nucleus-to-extracellular
- Heat shock proteins
- S100 proteins
- Nucleic acid fragments (free DNA/RNA)

#### How They Work
```
Tissue Damage → DAMPs released from dying cells
       ↓
Pattern Recognition Receptors (PRRs) on innate immune cells
(Toll-like Receptors, NOD-like Receptors)
       ↓
Inflammasome activation (NLRP3) → Caspase-1 activation
       ↓
Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines: IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-8
       ↓
SIRS + local inflammation
```

#### Consequences of DAMP Activation
- **Sterile systemic inflammatory cascade**
- Capillary leak (endothelial activation)
- Coagulopathy (platelet and complement activation)
- Local ischaemia → more DAMP release → **vicious cycle**
- Risk of: AKI, ALI/ARDS, coagulopathy, MODS, secondary brain injury

### 4.2 Secondary Triggers (Amplifiers of Response)

| Secondary Trigger | Mechanism |
|-------------------|-----------|
| **Sepsis** | PAMPs (bacterial products) amplify innate immune activation |
| **Haemorrhage** | Tissue hypoperfusion → more DAMPs, prolongs catabolism |
| **Massive transfusion** | Stored blood components trigger inflammatory response |
| **Acidosis** | Inhibits coagulation enzymes, impairs cellular function |
| **Surgery** | Additional tissue trauma and DAMP release |
| **Crush syndrome** | Massive rhabdomyolysis → DAMP flood |
| **Ischaemia–reperfusion** | Oxidative stress, complement activation on reperfusion |

### 4.3 Neuroendocrine Response

#### Afferent Pathway
```
Injury (pain/haemorrhage/hypoxia)
    ↓
Nociceptive neurones → Spinal cord
    ↓
Hypothalamus (CRF release)
    ↓
Anterior pituitary (ACTH release) + Posterior pituitary (ADH release)
    ↓
Target organs: Adrenal cortex (cortisol), Adrenal medulla (adrenaline), Pancreas (glucagon)
```

#### Hormonal Changes: Acute Phase (Hours)

| Hormone | Source | Key Actions |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| **CRF** | Hypothalamus | Stimulates ACTH |
| **ACTH** | Anterior pituitary | Stimulates adrenal cortex |
| **Cortisol** | Adrenal cortex | Gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism, immunosuppression, anti-inflammatory |
| **Adrenaline** | Adrenal medulla | Glycogenolysis, lipolysis, tachycardia, vasoconstriction |
| **Glucagon** | Pancreatic alpha cells | Gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis |
| **ADH (vasopressin)** | Posterior pituitary | Water retention, vasoconstriction |
| **Aldosterone** | Adrenal cortex (via renin) | Na⁺ and water retention |

#### Hormonal Changes: Chronic Phase (Days)
After the initial surge, there is **hypothalamic suppression** and LOW levels of:
- Thyroid hormones (T3 inactive; peripheral conversion blocked)
- Gonadal hormones (testosterone, oestrogen)
- IGF-1 (despite elevated GH = growth hormone resistance)
- This drives ongoing catabolism and wasting

#### Combined Metabolic Effects of Counter-Regulatory Hormones
- Liberate glucose from carbohydrate stores (glycogenolysis)
- Break down fat for energy (lipolysis → free fatty acids)
- Break down protein for gluconeogenesis and repair
- Cause insulin resistance → hyperglycaemia
- Suppress anabolism (low IGF-1, hypogonadism)

### 4.4 Cytokine Response

#### Pro-Inflammatory Cytokines (First 24 hours)
| Cytokine | Main Effect |
|----------|-------------|
| **IL-1** | Pyrexia, hypothalamic stimulation, endothelial activation |
| **IL-6** | Hepatic acute-phase protein synthesis (main stimulus), pyrexia |
| **TNF-α** | Muscle proteolysis, endothelial activation, apoptosis |
| **IL-8** | Neutrophil chemotaxis to wound |
| **IL-12** | NK cell and T-cell activation |

#### Other Key Mediators
- **Nitric oxide (NO)** — produced via iNOS → vasodilatation, microvascular dysfunction
- **Prostanoids** — via COX-2 → prostaglandins contribute to fever, inflammation
- **Endothelin-1** → vasoconstriction, endothelial damage
- **Complement pathways** — C3a, C5a: anaphylatoxins, neutrophil chemotaxis
- **Kinin pathways** — bradykinin: vasodilation, pain, oedema

### 4.5 Counter-Regulatory (Anti-inflammatory) Response

The body cannot sustain unchecked pro-inflammation — endogenous inhibitors emerge:

**Endogenous Antagonists:**
- **IL-1Ra** (IL-1 receptor antagonist) — blocks IL-1 signalling
- **TNF-sR-55 and TNF-sR-75** (soluble TNF receptors) — mop up circulating TNF

**Counter-inflammatory Cytokines:**
- IL-4, IL-5, IL-9, IL-10, IL-13
- **TGF-β** (also promotes fibrosis)

**Specialised Pro-resolving Mediators (SPMs):**
- Lipoxins, Resolvins, Protectins, Maresins
- Derived from omega-3 fatty acids
- Function: clear apoptotic neutrophils, promote macrophage efferocytosis, reduce inflammation without immunosuppression

### 4.6 SIRS vs CARS vs PICS

| Syndrome | Full Name | Characteristics | Consequences |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| **SIRS** | Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome | Pro-inflammatory dominance; defined by ≥2 criteria (Temp >38 or <36°C, HR>90, RR>20, WBC>12 or <4) | Organ damage, MODS |
| **CARS** | Compensatory Anti-inflammatory Response Syndrome | Anti-inflammatory dominance; immune suppression | Nosocomial infection, opportunistic pathogens |
| **PICS** | Persistent Inflammation, Immunosuppression and Catabolism | Chronic critical illness; prolonged SIRS + CARS simultaneously | High mortality, prolonged ICU stay |

## 5. Metabolic Changes After Surgery and Trauma

### 5.1 Ebb Phase — Catabolic Phase (0–48 hours)

**Purpose:** Conserve circulating volume and energy stores

**Features:**
- Hypovolaemia → reduced preload
- Decreased BMR (body "conserves")
- Reduced cardiac output
- Hypothermia
- Lactic acidosis (anaerobic metabolism)
- Muscle catabolism begins
- Insulin levels may fall; glucagon and catecholamines dominate

**Dominant Hormones:** Catecholamines, cortisol, aldosterone

### 5.2 Flow Phase — Hypermetabolic Phase (After Resuscitation)

**Purpose:** Provide energy for healing and immune response

**Features:**
- Tissue oedema (from vasodilatation and capillary leak)
- **BMR 15–25% above normal** (burns: can be 2× normal)
- Increased cardiac output
- Raised body temperature (1–2°C)
- Leukocytosis
- Increased O₂ consumption
- Increased gluconeogenesis and glucose turnover

### 5.3 Substrate Metabolism

#### Carbohydrate
- **Hyperglycaemia** is universal (stress hyperglycaemia)
- Mechanism: ↑ glucose production (gluconeogenesis) + ↓ peripheral uptake (insulin resistance)
- Insulin levels may initially fall then rise, but resistance persists
- Tight glycaemic control reduces morbidity but avoid hypoglycaemia

#### Fat
- Lipolysis stimulated by catecholamines, cortisol, glucagon
- Free fatty acids (FFAs) mobilised → main energy source
- Adipose tissue mass reduced
- Ketone formation during prolonged starvation

#### Protein
- **Net negative nitrogen balance** — catabolism exceeds synthesis
- Muscle protein (myofibrillar) broken down via ubiquitin–proteasome pathway
- Amino acids used for gluconeogenesis, acute-phase protein synthesis, wound repair
- Urinary nitrogen excretion: 10–20 g N/day (normal: ~14 g N/day)

## 6. Changes in Body Composition

### 6.1 Normal Body Composition (70 kg Male)

| Component | Mass |
|-----------|------|
| Total fat | 13 kg |
| Fat-free mass | 57 kg |
| — Protein | 12 kg |
| — Water (ICF 28L, ECF 14L) | 42 kg |
| — Minerals | 3 kg |

### 6.2 Protein Turnover

**Normal:**
- Whole body protein turnover: 150–200 g/day
- Net: in balance with dietary intake (~70–100 g protein/day)
- Urinary nitrogen: ~14 g N/day

**After Major Injury:**
- Urinary nitrogen: 10–20 g N/day (obligatory loss)
- **1g nitrogen loss = 36g wet weight lean tissue loss**
- Can lose **500 g lean tissue/day** in severe injury/sepsis
- Critical threshold: loss of **30–40% total body protein = incompatible with life**

### 6.3 Skeletal Muscle Wasting

#### Primary Mechanism: Ubiquitin–Proteasome Pathway
```
Pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1) → Ubiquitin ligase activation
      ↓
Myofibrillar proteins (actin, myosin) tagged with ubiquitin chains
      ↓
26S proteasome degrades tagged proteins
      ↓
Amino acids released into bloodstream
```

#### Secondary Mechanisms
- **Lysosomal cathepsins** — protein degradation within lysosomes
- **Calcium–calpain pathway** — Ca²⁺ activated proteases

#### Why Wasting Occurs
- Amino acids preferentially redirected to liver (acute-phase proteins) and immune system
- Sepsis prolongs and worsens wasting
- Wasting **cannot be fully prevented** by nutrition alone

#### Clinical Consequences
- Weakness and fatigue
- Respiratory muscle weakness → hypoventilation, pneumonia
- Gut motility impairment → ileus
- Poor wound healing (substrate deficiency)
- Increased morbidity and mortality

### 6.4 Insulin Resistance

**Mechanism:**
- Pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) impair insulin receptor signalling (IRS-1 phosphorylation)
- Glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4) translocation inhibited
- Proportional to injury magnitude

**Duration:**
- Routine abdominal surgery: up to **~2 weeks**
- Prolonged sepsis: months (contributing to wasting)

**Clinical Consequences:**
- Hyperglycaemia → osmotic diuresis, infection risk, impaired wound healing
- Hyperglycaemia → endothelial dysfunction

**Management:**
- IV insulin infusion protocol in ICU
- Target glucose: moderate control (avoid hypoglycaemia, which is dangerous)
- Pre-op carbohydrate loading reduces post-op insulin resistance

### 6.5 Hepatic Acute-Phase Response

Stimulated by **IL-6** (main driver), IL-1, TNF-α.

**Purpose:** Prioritise hepatic defence protein synthesis over transport proteins.

| Positive Reactants (↑) | Negative Reactants (↓) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| **C-reactive protein (CRP)** | Albumin |
| Fibrinogen | Transferrin |
| Serum amyloid A | Prealbumin |
| Complement proteins | Retinol-binding protein |
| α1-antitrypsin | |

**Key Points:**
- **Albumin falls NOT because of reduced synthesis** but because of:
  - Increased capillary permeability (leakage into interstitium)
  - Dilutional effect from fluid resuscitation
  - Reprioritisation of hepatic protein synthesis
- CRP rises sharply (can reach >200 mg/L) and is a useful clinical marker
- Fibrinogen supports coagulation and wound healing

### 6.6 Body Weight Changes

| Condition | Early | Later |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Starvation (uncomplicated)** | Gradual weight loss (adaptive) | Continued slow loss |
| **Uncomplicated surgery** | Minimal change | Small recovery |
| **Major trauma/sepsis** | Weight gain (+6–10L ECF expansion in 24hrs) | Weight loss as oedema resolves + catabolism |

**Critical illness pattern:**
- Immediate: +6–10 L ECF expansion
- Days 1–10: –15% total body protein
- Weight eventually reaches negative balance as oedema resolves

## 7. Avoidable Factors That Compound the Metabolic Response

These are factors that **amplify** the metabolic response beyond what is necessary and can be prevented.

### 7.1 Volume Loss / Hypovolaemia
- Triggers ADH and aldosterone → sodium and water retention
- Visceral oedema → delayed gastric emptying and ileus
- Tissue hypoperfusion → ischaemia → more DAMPs
- **Solution:** Careful balanced fluid management, early haemostasis

### 7.2 Hypothermia
- **2–3× increase** in cardiac arrhythmias
- Increases catabolism and metabolic demand
- Impairs coagulation (enzyme inhibition)
- Worsens trauma-induced coagulopathy
- **Solution:** Forced-air warming devices → reduces wound infections, bleeding, transfusion requirements

### 7.3 Tissue Oedema
- Impairs oxygen diffusion (increased diffusion distance)
- Impairs renal tubular function
- Mediated by: cytokines, prostanoids, bradykinin, NO → capillary leak
- **Solution:** Avoid excess IV fluids, maintain oncotic pressure

### 7.4 Tissue Underperfusion
- Endothelial dysfunction → barrier failure
- Cellular hypoxia → anaerobic metabolism → acidosis
- Organ failure risk (AKI, hepatic dysfunction)
- **Solution:** Targeted resuscitation; appropriate vasopressors; insulin control (protects endothelium)

### 7.5 Starvation
- **Cerebral glucose requirement:** ~100 g/day (brain cannot use fat)
- Glycogen stores depleted within **24 hours** of fasting
- Then gluconeogenesis from amino acids (muscle wasting accelerates)
- Prolonged: lipolysis and ketosis
- **Solution:**
  - Avoid unnecessary pre-operative fasting
  - Clear fluids up to **2 hours before surgery** (ERAS)
  - Pre-op carbohydrate drink (reduces insulin resistance post-op)
  - Early post-op enteral feeding

### 7.6 Immobility
- Prevents anabolic stimulation of protein synthesis by meal-derived amino acids
- Accelerates muscle atrophy
- **Solution:** Early mobilisation; pre-habilitation programmes

### 7.7 Pain
- Increases sympathoadrenal activation → cortisol, catecholamines
- Amplifies SIRS response
- **Solution:** Adequate multimodal analgesia; epidural for open surgery

## 8. Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS)

### Philosophy
ERAS uses multimodal perioperative interventions to **attenuate the metabolic response**, minimise catabolism, and accelerate functional recovery.

### Core ERAS Elements and Their Benefits

| Intervention | Mechanism of Benefit |
|--------------|---------------------|
| **Minimal access surgery** (laparoscopic) | Reduces tissue trauma and DAMP release |
| **Epidural analgesia** (open surgery) | Blocks nociceptive afferents → less neuroendocrine response; reduces insulin resistance |
| **Pre-op carbohydrate loading** | Reduces insulin resistance post-op; avoids fasted state |
| **Minimal fasting periods** | Reduces catabolism and preserves glycogen stores |
| **Early enteral feeding** | Preserves gut mucosal integrity; prevents translocation |
| **Early mobilisation** | Restores anabolic stimulation; prevents DVT and pneumonia |
| **Careful fluid management** | Avoids oedema and organ dysfunction |
| **Normothermia** | Reduces cardiac events, wound infections, coagulopathy |
| **Laparoscopic access** | Less pain, faster recovery, earlier feeding and mobilisation |

### Outcomes
- **30–50% reduction** in hospital stay
- Reduced healthcare costs
- Improved patient outcomes (complications, QoL)

### Analgesia in ERAS (Specific Notes)
- **Open surgery:** Thoracic epidural strongly recommended (best stress response blockade)
- **Laparoscopic surgery:** Epidural NOT recommended (fluid shifts, hypotension risk)
  - Instead: patient-controlled analgesia (PCA)
  - Adjuncts: single-shot spinal diamorphine, IV lidocaine infusion (6–12 hrs post-op)

## 9. Mediator Pathways — Integrated Summary

```
INJURY (surgery/trauma/burns/sepsis)
          ↓
┌──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
│ NEURAL PATHWAY       │ INFLAMMATORY PATHWAY │
│                      │                      │
│ Nociceptors          │ DAMPs released       │
│ Spinal cord          │ PRR activation       │
│ Hypothalamus (CRF)   │ Inflammasome         │
│ Pituitary (ACTH)     │ IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α    │
│ Adrenal (cortisol,   │ Neutrophils,         │
│ adrenaline)          │ macrophages          │
└──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘
          ↓
INTEGRATED SYSTEMIC EFFECTS:
  • Pyrexia                    • Protein catabolism
  • Hypermetabolism            • Fat mobilisation
  • Hyperglycaemia             • Acute-phase protein ↑
  • Insulin resistance         • Albumin ↓
  • Fluid retention            • Immune modulation
          ↓
OUTCOME (if poorly managed):
  SIRS → Sepsis → MODS → Death (25% mortality)
          ↓
OUTCOME (with ERAS/optimal care):
  Attenuated response → Early recovery
```

## Summary Boxes

### Box 1.1: Basic Concepts
- Homeostasis is the foundation of normal physiology
- 'Stress-free' perioperative care (ERAS) preserves homeostasis
- Metabolic response influences outcomes through catabolism, MODS, impaired immunity

### Box 1.2: Neuroendocrine Response (Biphasic)
- **Acute (hours):** Elevated counter-regulatory hormones (beneficial short-term: mobilise energy)
- **Chronic (days):** Hypothalamic suppression → low thyroid, gonadal hormones, IGF-1 → prolonged wasting

### Box 1.3: Key Characteristics of the Metabolic Response
- Rapid onset (cytokine-driven within minutes)
- Proportional to injury severity
- Individual variation (genetic, age, comorbidity)
- Causes catabolism, immunosuppression, organ dysfunction
- Counterbalanced by anti-inflammatory responses (balance may be imperfect → SIRS or CARS)

### Box 1.4: Purpose of Neuroendocrine Changes
- Provide essential substrates from tissue breakdown
- Postpone anabolism (healing deferred until survival is secured)
- Optimise host defence (redirect energy to immune function and wound)

### Box 1.5: Skeletal Muscle Wasting
- Provides amino acids for central organs and wound
- Mediated primarily by ubiquitin–proteasome pathway
- Inevitable but prolonged by sepsis
- Leads to immobility, poor healing, pneumonia if severe
- **Cannot be fully prevented by artificial nutrition**

### Box 1.6: Hepatic Acute-Phase Response
- Positive reactants (CRP, fibrinogen): ↑ plasma concentration
- Negative reactants (albumin, transferrin): ↓ plasma concentration
- Main driver: IL-6

### Box 1.7: Body Composition Changes
- Catabolism → ↓ fat mass, ↓ skeletal muscle mass
- Paradoxical weight **gain** from ECF expansion in early critical illness
- Late critical illness: profound weight loss

### Box 1.8: Avoidable Compounding Factors (THISSPI mnemonic)
- **T**issue underperfusion
- **H**ypothermia
- **I**mmobility
- **S**tarvation
- **S**epsis
- **P**ain
- **I**nappropriate fluids (excess volume)

### Box 1.9: ERAS Approach
- Minimal access techniques
- Blockade of afferent nociceptive stimuli (epidural)
- Pre-op carbohydrate loading
- Minimal fasting
- Early enteral feeding
- Early mobilisation
- Careful fluid management

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. **MODS mortality remains ~25%** despite modern critical care — prevention is key
2. **DAMPs** (not PAMPs) are the key initiators of **sterile** post-injury inflammation
3. **IL-6** is the main driver of the hepatic acute-phase response (not IL-1 or TNF-α)
4. **Ubiquitin–proteasome pathway** is the PRIMARY mechanism of post-injury muscle wasting
5. **Insulin resistance** is proportional to injury magnitude and persists for ~2 weeks after routine abdominal surgery
6. **1g nitrogen loss = 36g wet weight lean tissue loss** — this is a key number
7. **Critical threshold:** 30–40% total body protein loss is incompatible with life
8. **ERAS** reduces hospital stay by **30–50%**
9. **Normothermia** reduces wound infections, cardiac arrhythmias AND coagulopathy
10. **Pre-op carbohydrate loading** reduces post-op insulin resistance (avoids fasted state)
11. **Albumin falls** due to capillary leak and reprioritisation — NOT due to reduced synthesis
12. **Tight glucose control** is beneficial in ICU but avoid hypoglycaemia (target ~6–10 mmol/L)
13. **Epidural analgesia** (for open surgery) blocks afferent pathways and is the most potent attenuator of surgical stress response
14. **SPMs** (lipoxins, resolvins, protectins) resolve inflammation — disruption leads to chronic non-healing wounds

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SIRS** | Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (≥2: Temp >38/<36°C, HR>90, RR>20, WBC>12/<4) |
| **MODS** | Multiple organ dysfunction syndrome; failure of ≥2 organ systems |
| **Sepsis** | SIRS with a documented/suspected infective source |
| **DAMPs** | Damage-associated molecular patterns; endogenous molecules released from injured cells that activate PRRs |
| **PAMPs** | Pathogen-associated molecular patterns; microbial molecules (LPS, peptidoglycan) |
| **PRRs** | Pattern recognition receptors (Toll-like, NOD-like receptors) on innate immune cells |
| **CARS** | Compensatory anti-inflammatory response syndrome; anti-inflammatory dominance |
| **PICS** | Persistent inflammation, immunosuppression and catabolism syndrome; chronic critical illness |
| **ERAS** | Enhanced recovery after surgery; multimodal protocol to attenuate metabolic response and accelerate recovery |
| **Ebb phase** | Early catabolic phase of metabolic response (0–48 hrs) |
| **Flow phase** | Hypermetabolic phase after resuscitation |
| **SPMs** | Specialised pro-resolving mediators (lipoxins, resolvins, protectins, maresins) |
