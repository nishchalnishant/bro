# Chapter 1: Metabolic Response to Injury

## Learning Objectives

- Understand how the body responds to accidental injury and surgery
- Understand physiological and biochemical changes during injury and recovery
- Understand mediators and pathways of the metabolic response to injury
- Recognise avoidable factors that compound the metabolic response
- Understand how the metabolic response influences surgical outcomes
- Understand concepts behind optimal perioperative care

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
The **metabolic response to injury** is a series of systemic changes affecting vital organ functions, triggered by tissue injury (surgical or accidental). It involves:
- Hormones
- Inflammation-related cytokines
- Neural circuits

### Effects
- Alterations in body metabolism
- Changes in wound healing
- Immunity modifications
- Specific organ function changes

### Clinical Significance
- Can lead to complications, especially **sepsis**
- May cause/prolong **Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS)**
- MODS mortality: ~25% even in modern trauma systems
- Successful management improves outcomes

---

## 2. Homeostasis and the Metabolic Response

### Homeostasis Definition
Maintaining a constant internal environment for optimal cellular function.

### Phases of Metabolic Response

| Phase | Timing | Characteristics | Purpose |
|-------|--------|-----------------|---------|
| **Catabolic** | Immediate (0-48 hrs) | Hypovolaemia, ↓BMR, ↓cardiac output, hypothermia, lactic acidosis, muscle breakdown, hyperglycaemia | Conserve circulating volume and energy stores |
| **Anabolic** | Days to weeks | Tissue repair, rebuilding | Recovery and tissue healing |

### Modern Surgical Care Goals
- Work alongside metabolic effects of injury
- Return patient to homeostasis
- Minimise primary insult via:
  - Minimal access surgery
  - 'Stress-free' perioperative care (ERAS)

---

## 3. Magnitude of Response

### Graded Response Principle
**The more severe the injury, the greater the metabolic response**

| Injury Type | Response |
|-------------|----------|
| Minor elective surgery | Transient modest rise in temperature, HR, RR, energy expenditure |
| Major trauma/emergency surgery/sepsis/burns | SIRS, hypermetabolism, marked catabolism, shock, MODS |

### Individual Variability
- Genetic factors influence response intensity
- Some patients respond more dramatically to similar injuries

## 4. Mediators of Metabolic Response

### 4.1 Tissue Damage and Inflammation (DAMPs)

#### Damage-Associated Molecular Patterns (DAMPs)
Released from damaged cells; also called **alarmins**

**Important DAMPs:**
- Heat shock proteins
- HMGB1 (High Mobility Group Protein B1)
- S100 proteins
- Nucleic acid fragments

#### Pattern Recognition Receptors (PRRs)
- **Toll-like receptors**
- **NOD-like receptors** (NLRs)

**Location:** Innate immune cells (macrophages, neutrophils, dendritic cells)

#### Inflammasome Activation
```
Tissue Damage → DAMPs released → PRRs activated → Inflammasomes formed
→ Caspases activated → Cytokines released (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)
→ SIRS → Local inflammation + Systemic effects
```

#### Consequences of DAMP Activation
- Sterile systemic inflammatory cascade
- Capillary leak (endothelial cell activation)
- Coagulopathy (platelet activation)
- Local ischaemia → More DAMPs (vicious cycle)
- Risk factors for:
  - Acute kidney injury
  - Acute lung injury
  - Coagulopathy
  - MODS/organ failure
  - Secondary brain injury

### 4.2 Secondary Triggers (Amplify Response)

| Secondary Trigger | Effect |
|-------------------|--------|
| Sepsis | Major amplifier |
| Haemorrhage | Prolongs catabolism |
| Massive transfusion | Inflammatory response |
| Acidosis | Cellular dysfunction |
| Surgery | Additional tissue trauma |
| Crush syndrome | Massive DAMP release |
| Ischaemia-reperfusion | Oxidative stress |

### 4.3 Neuroendocrine Response

#### Pathway
```
Injury → Nociceptive neurones → Spinal cord → Hypothalamus → Pituitary
```

#### Hormonal Changes (Acute Phase - Hours)

| Hormone | Source | Effect |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **CRF** | Hypothalamus | Stimulates ACTH |
| **ACTH** | Anterior pituitary | Stimulates cortisol |
| **Cortisol** | Adrenal cortex | Gluconeogenesis, immunosuppression |
| **Adrenaline** | Adrenal medulla | Glycogenolysis, lipolysis |
| **Glucagon** | Pancreas | Gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis |

#### Metabolic Effects of Counter-Regulatory Hormones
- Liberate glucose from carbohydrate stores
- Breakdown fat and protein for energy/repair
- Alter insulin release and sensitivity
- Hypersecretion of prolactin and GH
- Low IGF-1
- Inactivation of peripheral thyroid hormones
- Suppressed gonadal function

### 4.4 Cytokine Response

#### Pro-inflammatory Cytokines (First 24 hours)
- **IL-1** → Pyrexia, hypothalamic stimulation
- **IL-6** → Hepatic acute-phase protein synthesis
- **TNF-α** → Muscle proteolysis
- **IL-8** → Neutrophil chemotaxis

#### Other Mediators
- **Nitric oxide** (via iNOS)
- **Prostanoids** (via COX-2)
- **Endothelin-1** → Vasoconstriction
- **Complement and kinin pathways**

### 4.5 Counter-Regulatory (Anti-inflammatory) Response

#### Endogenous Antagonists
- **IL-1Ra** (IL-1 receptor antagonist)
- **TNF soluble receptors** (TNF-sR-55, TNF-sR-75)

#### Counter-inflammatory Cytokines
- IL-4, IL-5, IL-9, IL-13
- TGF-β

#### Specialised Pro-resolving Mediators (SPMs)
- Lipoxins
- Resolvins
- Protectins
- Maresins

**Function:** Clear apoptotic neutrophils, reduce inflammation, enhance debris removal

### 4.6 CARS vs SIRS

| Condition | Full Name | Characteristics | Consequences |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| **SIRS** | Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome | Pro-inflammatory dominance | Organ damage |
| **CARS** | Compensatory Anti-inflammatory Response Syndrome | Anti-inflammatory dominance | Immunosuppression, nosocomial infection |

#### PICS (Persistent Inflammation, Immunosuppression and Catabolism)
- Chronic critical illness syndrome
- Prolonged catabolism
- Poor outcomes

## 5. Metabolic Changes After Surgery and Trauma

### 5.1 Catabolic Phase (0-48 hours)

**Characteristics:**
- Hypovolaemia
- Decreased BMR
- Reduced cardiac output
- Hypothermia
- Lactic acidosis

**Dominant Hormones:**
- Catecholamines
- Cortisol
- Aldosterone (via renin-angiotensin)

### 5.2 Hypermetabolic Flow Phase (After Resuscitation)

**Characteristics:**
- Tissue oedema (vasodilatation, capillary leak)
- Increased BMR (15-25% above normal)
- Increased cardiac output
- Raised body temperature
- Leukocytosis
- Increased oxygen consumption
- Increased gluconeogenesis

### 5.3 Substrate Changes

#### Carbohydrate Metabolism
- **Hyperglycaemia** due to:
  - Increased glucose production
  - Decreased peripheral uptake (insulin resistance)
- Insulin levels may fall initially, then rise with resistance

#### Fat Metabolism
- Lipolysis stimulated by catecholamines, cortisol, glucagon
- Free fatty acids mobilised for energy

#### Protein Metabolism
- Muscle protein breakdown
- Increased urinary nitrogen excretion
- Negative nitrogen balance

## 6. Changes in Body Composition

### 6.1 Normal Body Composition (70 kg male)

| Component | Mass |
|-----------|------|
| Fat | 13 kg |
| Fat-Free Mass | 57 kg |
| - Protein | 12 kg |
| - Water | 42 kg (ICF: 28L, ECF: 14L) |
| - Minerals | 3 kg |

### 6.2 Protein Turnover

**Normal:**
- Whole body turnover: 150-200 g/day
- Dietary intake: 70-100 g protein/day
- Urinary nitrogen: ~14 g N/day

**After Major Injury:**
- Urinary nitrogen: 10-20 g N/day
- 1 g nitrogen loss = 36 g wet weight lean tissue loss
- Can lose 500 g lean tissue/day

### 6.3 Skeletal Muscle Wasting

#### Mechanism
**Ubiquitin-Proteasome Pathway** (primary)
- ATP-dependent
- Myofibrillar protein degradation

**Accessory pathways:**
- Lysosomal cathepsins
- Calcium-calpain pathway

#### Consequences
- Weakness, fatigue
- Reduced functional ability
- Decreased quality of life
- Increased morbidity/mortality
- Respiratory muscle weakness → hypoventilation
- Gut motility reduction

#### Clinical Note
- Cannot be fully inhibited by artificial nutrition
- Hyperalimentation is harmful (metabolic stress)
- Treat underlying sepsis

### 6.4 Insulin Resistance

**Mechanism:**
- Pro-inflammatory cytokines impair glucose transporters
- Proportional to injury magnitude

**Duration:**
- Routine abdominal surgery: ~2 weeks
- Prolonged sepsis: extended period

**Management:**
- IV insulin infusion in ICU
- Target reasonable glucose control (avoid hypoglycaemia)

### 6.5 Hepatic Acute-Phase Response

| Positive Reactants (↑) | Negative Reactants (↓) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| CRP | Albumin |
| Fibrinogen | Transferrin |
| Serum amyloid A | Prealbumin |

**Key Points:**
- IL-6 is the main stimulus
- Albumin fall is due to increased capillary permeability, not decreased synthesis
- Represents reprioritisation of protein metabolism toward liver

### 6.6 Body Weight Changes

| Condition | Weight Change Pattern |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Starvation** | Gradual weight loss (adaptive) |
| **Uncomplicated surgery** | Minimal change |
| **Major trauma/sepsis** | Initial weight gain (fluid) → Later loss |

**Critical Illness Pattern:**
- Immediate: +6-10 L ECF expansion (24 hrs)
- Day 1-10: -15% total body protein
- Weight reaches negative balance as oedema resolves

## 7. Avoidable Factors Compounding Response

### 7.1 Volume Loss
- ADH and aldosterone release
- Sodium and water retention
- Visceral oedema → delayed gastric emptying
- **Solution:** Careful fluid balance, balanced crystalloids

### 7.2 Hypothermia
- 2-3x increase in cardiac arrhythmias
- Increased catabolism
- **Solution:** Forced-air warming (reduces wound infections, bleeding, transfusion needs)

### 7.3 Tissue Oedema
- Impairs oxygen diffusion
- Renal function impairment
- Mediated by: cytokines, prostanoids, bradykinin, NO

### 7.4 Tissue Underperfusion
- Endothelial dysfunction
- Cellular hypoxia
- Organ failure risk
- **Solution:** Appropriate insulin control (protects endothelium)

### 7.5 Starvation
- Cerebral glucose requirement: 100 g/day
- Glycogen stores depleted in 24 hrs
- Then gluconeogenesis from amino acids
- **Solution:**
  - Avoid unnecessary fasting
  - Clear fluids up to 2 hrs before surgery
  - Pre-op carbohydrate drink reduces insulin resistance
  - Early post-op feeding

### 7.6 Immobility
- Impairs meal-derived amino acid stimulation of protein synthesis
- **Solution:**
  - Early mobilisation
  - Pre-habilitation programmes

## 8. Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS)

### Principles

| Intervention | Benefit |
|--------------|---------|
| **Minimal access surgery** | Reduced tissue trauma |
| **Epidural analgesia** (open surgery) | Blocks stress response, reduces insulin resistance |
| **Minimal fasting** | Reduces catabolism |
| **Early mobilisation** | Prevents muscle wasting |
| **Careful fluid management** | Avoids oedema |
| **Early enteral feeding** | Preserves gut function |

### Outcomes
- 30-50% reduction in hospital stay
- Reduced healthcare costs
- Improved patient outcomes

### Analgesia Notes
- **Open surgery:** Epidural recommended
- **Laparoscopic surgery:** Patient-controlled analgesia sufficient
  - Epidural not recommended (fluid shifts, hypotension)
- **Adjuncts:**
  - One-shot spinal diamorphine
  - IV lidocaine infusion (6-12 hrs)

## Summary Boxes

### Box 1.1: Basic Concepts
- Homeostasis is the foundation of normal physiology
- 'Stress-free' perioperative care preserves homeostasis
- Metabolic response influences outcomes through catabolism, MODS, impaired immunity

### Box 1.2: Neuroendocrine Response (Biphasic)
- **Acute (hours):** Elevated counter-regulatory hormones (beneficial short-term)
- **Chronic (days):** Hypothalamic suppression, low target organ hormones (contributes to wasting)

### Box 1.3: Key Characteristics
- Rapid onset (cytokine-driven)
- Related to injury severity
- Individual variation (genetic)
- Causes catabolism, immunosuppression, organ dysfunction
- Counterbalanced by antagonists (balance may be imperfect)

### Box 1.4: Purpose of Neuroendocrine Changes
- Provide essential substrates from tissue breakdown
- Postpone anabolism
- Optimise host defence

### Box 1.5: Skeletal Muscle Wasting
- Provides amino acids for central organs
- Mediated by ubiquitin-proteasome pathway
- Inevitable but prolonged by sepsis
- Results in immobility, poor healing, pneumonia if severe

### Box 1.6: Hepatic Acute-Phase Response
- Positive reactants (CRP): ↑ plasma concentration
- Negative reactants (albumin): ↓ plasma concentration

### Box 1.7: Body Composition Changes
- Catabolism → ↓ fat mass, ↓ skeletal muscle mass
- Paradoxical weight gain from ECF expansion

### Box 1.8: Avoidable Factors
- Haemorrhage/volume loss
- Hypothermia
- Tissue oedema
- Tissue underperfusion
- Starvation
- Immobility

### Box 1.9: ERAS Approach
- Minimal access techniques
- Blockade of painful stimuli
- Minimal fasting periods
- Early mobilisation

- Major trauma: Highest response

```
Injury → Afferent nociceptive pathways → Hypothalamus → Pituitary → Adrenal
                                              ↓

                                              ↓

                                    - Pyrexia

                                    - Lipolysis
                                    - Gluconeogenesis
```

Peripheral tissues (muscle, fat, skin) → Amino acids → Central tissues (liver, immune system, wound)

Multiple factors amplify each other: pain, hypothermia, starvation, immobility, sepsis

Multimodal intervention → Faster functional recovery vs traditional care

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. **MODS mortality** remains ~25% despite modern care
2. **DAMPs** are the key initiators of sterile inflammation
3. **IL-6** is the main driver of hepatic acute-phase response
4. **Ubiquitin-proteasome pathway** is the primary mechanism of muscle wasting
5. **Insulin resistance** is proportional to injury magnitude
6. **1g nitrogen loss = 36g lean tissue loss**
7. **Critical threshold:** 30-40% protein mass loss is incompatible with life
8. **ERAS** reduces hospital stay by 30-50%
9. **Normothermia** reduces wound infections, cardiac complications, bleeding
10. **Pre-op carbohydrate loading** reduces post-op insulin resistance
