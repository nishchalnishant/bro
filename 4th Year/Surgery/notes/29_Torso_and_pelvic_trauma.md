# Chapter 29: Torso and pelvic trauma

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- The importance of physiology over anatomy in the management of trauma
- The gross surgical anatomy of the chest and abdomen
- The pathophysiology of torso injury
- The clinical assessment in the injured patient
- The use of special investigations and their limitations
- The operative approaches to the thoracic cavity
- The special features of an emergency department thoracotomy for haemorrhage control
- The indications for, and techniques of, the trauma laparotomy
- The philosophy of damage control resuscitation
- The management of trauma to the pelvis

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Torso trauma (chest + abdomen + pelvis) drives a large proportion of trauma deaths, largely through **major haemorrhage** and physiological collapse. Injury patterns cross anatomical boundaries, so management is best organised around:
- Physiology and haemorrhage control
- Junctional zones and surgical access planning
- Damage control resuscitation and surgery when unstable

---

## 2. Physiology over anatomy

### Key concept
Early survival depends on preserving physiology:
- Oxygenation and ventilation
- Perfusion and haemostasis
- Normothermia and correction of coagulopathy/acidosis

Detailed anatomical repair is secondary when the patient is unstable.

---

## 3. Mechanisms and junctional zones

### 3.1 Why zones matter
Injury traverses between regions; these “junctional zones” create diagnostic and access challenges.

Key junctional zones:
- Neck–thorax (thoracic inlet)
- Thorax–upper limb
- Thorax–abdomen (diaphragm)
- Abdomen/pelvis–groin

### 3.2 Clinical implication
Penetrating injury near junctions demands high suspicion for major vascular/visceral injury even with initially subtle signs.

---

## 4. Assessment and investigations

### 4.1 Primary survey priorities
Identify and treat immediate threats:
- Airway compromise
- Tension pneumothorax / massive haemothorax
- Cardiac tamponade
- Exsanguinating abdominal/pelvic bleeding

### 4.2 Adjuncts and limitations
- FAST: useful for free fluid, but false negatives occur
- CT: excellent anatomical definition, but only for sufficiently stable patients
- Plain films: chest/pelvis for rapid information in unstable patients

---

## 5. Operative approaches to the thoracic cavity (overview)

### Indications (conceptual)
Thoracic surgery in trauma may be needed for:
- Massive haemothorax
- Cardiac tamponade
- Major vascular injury
- Persistent air leak / bronchial injury

Approaches (principle level):
- Anterolateral thoracotomy (resuscitative access)
- Posterolateral thoracotomy (definitive access in stable setting)
- Median sternotomy (central access in selected injuries)

---

## 6. Emergency department thoracotomy (EDT)

### Key concept
EDT is an extreme, time-critical procedure for selected patients with:
- Peri-arrest arrest from penetrating thoracic trauma (best evidence)
- Potentially reversible causes (tamponade, controllable bleeding)

Goals:
- Relieve tamponade
- Control intrathoracic haemorrhage
- Cross-clamp descending aorta (selected) to prioritise cerebral/coronary perfusion

It requires trained teams and appropriate setting; outcomes are highly selection dependent.

---

## 7. Trauma laparotomy

### 7.1 Indications (high yield)
Consider when:
- Haemodynamic instability with suspected intra-abdominal bleeding
- Peritonitis or evisceration
- Free air or clear hollow viscus injury signs
- CT evidence of injuries requiring surgery (in stable patients)

### 7.2 Principles
- Rapid haemorrhage control (packing, vascular control, splenic/liver control strategies)
- Contamination control (bowel control, temporary closures)
- Damage control approach when physiology is deranged

---

## 8. Damage control resuscitation (DCR) in torso trauma

### Core elements
- Early haemorrhage control (surgery/IR)
- Balanced transfusion strategy when indicated
- Avoid the lethal triad (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy)
- Permissive hypotension in selected patients without TBI until haemostasis

---

## 9. Pelvic trauma

### 9.1 Why pelvic injuries kill
Pelvic fractures can cause massive bleeding from:
- Venous plexuses
- Fracture surfaces
- Arterial injury (less common but critical)

### 9.2 Immediate management (high yield)
- Pelvic binder (early, correct position)
- Haemostatic resuscitation and temperature control
- Rapid imaging as appropriate

Definitive haemorrhage control options include:
- Interventional radiology embolisation (arterial bleeding)
- Preperitoneal pelvic packing (selected unstable cases)
- External fixation/C-clamp (stabilisation reduces bleeding)

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Torso haemorrhage is a leading cause of trauma death**—think bleeding early.
2. **CT is for stable patients**; unstable patients need haemorrhage control decisions, not long scans.
3. **ED thoracotomy is for selected cases** (especially penetrating peri-arrest) with reversible pathology.
4. **Pelvic binders save lives** when applied early and correctly.
5. **Damage control** is physiology-driven: stop bleeding/contamination fast, fix later.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Junctional zone** | Region where injuries cross anatomical boundaries and surgical access is complex (e.g. thoracic inlet). |
| **ED thoracotomy** | Resuscitative thoracotomy performed in ED for selected near-arrest trauma patients. |
| **Trauma laparotomy** | Emergency abdominal surgery for haemorrhage/contamination control in trauma. |
| **Pelvic binder** | External stabilisation device reducing pelvic volume and bleeding in pelvic fractures. |
