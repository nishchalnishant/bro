# Chapter 13: Surgical audit and research

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish between audit and clinical research.
- Understand the cyclic process of surgical audit and service evaluation.
- Grasp the core elements of research design, including sample size, power calculation, and ethics.
- Understand the role of surgical research collaboratives (e.g., WMRC, STARSurg).
- Appreciate the standard structures for presenting logic (IMRAD) and analyzing papers (CONSORT, PRISMA).

---

## 1. Audit vs. Research
The distinction between an audit and research dictates the entire regulatory framework of a project. The UK Health Research Authority (HRA) specifies that true research exists when:
- Participants are subjected to **randomization**.
- The study protocol alters the accepted standard of care.
- The findings are distinctly designed to produce newly **generalisable** (extrapolatable) or **transferable** knowledge.

If a project simply evaluates contemporary practice without randomization or protocol deviation, it is an audit or service evaluation.

---

## 2. Surgical Audit and Service Evaluation

### 2.1 The Audit Cycle
Clinical audit seeks to improve patient care by continually comparing local practice metrics against heavily scrutinized national/international standards. It is integral to surgical revalidation in the UK.

**Steps of the Audit Cycle:**
1. Define the question within a multidisciplinary context.
2. Identify current evidence-based standards (e.g., NICE guidelines).
3. Design and collect performance data across an agreed timeframe.
4. Analyze data and compare performance against the standard.
5. Undertake robust **gap analysis**: implement interventions (e.g., a new protocol, training, funding).
6. **Re-audit** to close the loop.

### 2.2 Multicentre Snapshot Audits
A highly successful modern trend whereby collaborative groups simultaneously collect prospective, anonymised patient data over a short 6–8 week window across dozens of hospitals to capture "real-world" variances and generate rapid hypotheses.

## 3. Planning and Designing Surgical Research

Research endeavors to answer the unknown. A well-designed premise prevents catastrophic delays later in the process. Excellent sources of high-yield surgical research questions originate from "recommendations for research" blocks in NICE Guidelines or via Priority Setting Partnerships (e.g., James Lind Alliance).

### 3.1 Establishing a Team (Surgical Collaboratives)
It is remarkably difficult to complete robust surgical research independently. Developing a massive paradigm shift in 2008, the **West Midlands Research Collaborative (WMRC)** revolutionized research by pooling training registrars as co-authors across an entire region to systematically recruit patients into trials simultaneously. 
- *Success Story:* The **ROSSINI trial** (wound-edge protection) recruited 760 patients 2 months ahead of schedule via this network.
- The highly inclusive model has since spread globally (Australia, Italy, Canada) and vertically into student networks (STARSurg).

### 3.2 Key Research Designs
- **Observational - Retrospective:** Looking back on historical cohorts.
- **Observational - Prospective:** Contemporaneous data collection.
- **Cross-Sectional:** Measurements taken securely at a single fixed occasion across a target subset.
- **Case-Control:** Comparing patients with a disease against matched healthy controls.
- **Experimental (RCT):** Randomizing patients to multiple tightly controlled intervention groups against a placebo/standard therapy wing. Considered the zenith of clinical evidence structure.

### 3.3 Statistical Pillars and Power
Calculations mapping target requisite sample sizes must occur *before* starting a study to prevent catastrophic failure known as statistical errors. 

- **Type I Error (Alpha, false-positive):** Reporting a true benefit when there really isn't one. Allowed max is 5% ($p < 0.05$).
- **Type II Error (Beta, false-negative):** Missing a genuine clinical benefit because the study lacked statistical power. Requisite power is almost exclusively set at 90%.

### 3.4 Eliminating Investigator Bias
- **Single blind:** Patient unaware of allocation arm.
- **Double blind:** Neither the patient *nor* the examining researcher knows the therapeutic allocation, neutralizing unconscious assessment bias.

## 4. Conduct and Regulation

Robust protocols are universally required. Flow diagrams tracking study attrition are heavily recommended. Open online secure databases (like **REDCap**) make large international data collation seamless. 

**Regulatory Steps:**
1. **Sponsorship:** Must be backed by an institution accepting legal responsibilities and financial probity.
2. **Peer Review:** External independent vetting.
3. **Ethics:** In the UK, handled by the NHS Research Ethics Committee (REC), processed efficiently by the online Integrated Research Application System (IRAS). Trial animal research mandates strict Home Office licensing and adherence to **ARRIVE guidelines**.
4. **Registration:** Mandatory entry of RCTs into public registries (ClinicalTrials.gov, ISRCTN) prior to recruiting the first patient.
5. **Research Integrity:** An atmosphere of unquestionable honesty and accountability.

## 5. Standard Statistical Distributions

Correct analysis choices depend exclusively on the underlying distribution of the numerical data sequence.
- **Normally Distributed (Parametric):** The curve is a symmetrical bell. The mean, mode, and median equal exactly the same number. 
  - Compare two distinct groups: *Unpaired t-test*
  - Compare the exact same people over two different times: *Paired t-test*
- **Non-Normally Distributed (Non-Parametric):**
  - Compare two distinct groups: *Mann-Whitney U-test*
  - Compare the exact same people over two different times: *Wilcoxon signed rank test*
- **Categorical / Nominal Data (Non-numerical):** Utilize the *Chi-squared* test.

## 6. Analyzing, Presenting and Publishing

### 6.1 Critical Analysis of Papers
Surgical trainees must learn to aggressively vet published evidence. 
- **CONSORT Checklist:** Heavily used guideline checking if an RCT possesses fatal statistical loopholes.
- **PRISMA:** Specific guidelines formalizing systematic reviews and meta-analyses. 

### 6.2 The IMRAD Publication Structure
To structure a submission correctly, editors rigidly demand conformity to the structure below formatting: 
- **Introduction:** Extremely concise background and explicit aim statements. 
- **Methods:** Detailed sufficiently to allow another institution to flawlessly reproduce the study.
- **Results:** Emphasizing non-duplicated tabulated tables and visual figures. 
- **Discussion:** Contextualize the findings without restating Results. Posit suggestions for practice change or next steps without speculative fantasy. 

> [!TIP] 
> **Evidence-Based Surgery:** Centralizing disparate research findings to map clinical truths requires powerful databases like the Cochrane Collaboration Library, smoothing out individual idiosyncratic surgeon variations via heavily curated systemic meta-analyses.
