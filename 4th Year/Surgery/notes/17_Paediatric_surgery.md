# Chapter 17: Paediatric surgery

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Children are not “small adults”. Paediatric surgical care differs due to:
- Developmental physiology (airway, circulation, metabolism, thermoregulation)
- Age-dependent anatomy and disease patterns
- Communication, safeguarding and family-centred decision-making

### Clinical significance
- Many paediatric surgical problems are time-critical (torsion, pyloric stenosis, intussusception, appendicitis).
- Errors in fluid prescribing, temperature control, analgesia dosing, and delayed recognition of deterioration can cause major harm.

---

## 2. Organisation and subspecialisation

### Subspecialisation trend (high-income settings)
Paediatric surgery has branched into subspecialties (e.g. neonatal surgery, paediatric urology, trauma). Some conditions have migrated to other specialties (e.g. clefts → plastics; shunts → neurosurgery).

### Implication for non-specialist hospitals
Surgeons who manage general surgical conditions in children should be confident with:
- Inguinoscrotal pathology (hernia/hydrocele, torsion)
- Acute abdominal presentations by age
- Safe perioperative fluid/temperature/analgesia management
- Early referral and stabilisation for specialist conditions

---

## 3. Age, anatomy and environment

### Age terminology
- Neonate: <4 weeks
- Infant: <1 year
- Adolescence: 10–19 years (WHO)

### Environment and thermoregulation
Children, especially infants, lose heat rapidly:
- Higher surface area:weight ratio
- Lower reserves

Practical: warm rooms, warmed fluids, active warming devices, minimise exposure time.

---

## 4. Perioperative fluids in children (safe prescribing)

### Key risks
- **Hypoglycaemia** (limited glycogen stores in infants)
- **Hyponatraemia** (especially with hypotonic fluids)
- Rapid shifts in intravascular volume

### Practical approach (high-yield)
- Use **isotonic maintenance** fluids in most hospital settings unless specific indication otherwise.
- Add glucose when needed (age-dependent), and monitor blood glucose.
- Monitor: weight, urine output, electrolytes, acid–base, clinical perfusion.

### Targets
- Urine output: typically \( \ge 1 \) mL/kg/hr (age/context dependent)
- Avoid fluid overload and pulmonary oedema in small infants.

## 5. Inguinal hernia vs hydrocele

### Embryological basis (concept)
Persistence of a patent processus vaginalis underlies:
- **Indirect inguinal hernia** (abdominal contents can protrude)
- **Communicating hydrocele** (fluid communication)

### Key clinical differences
- **Hernia**: reducible groin/scrotal swelling; increases with crying/straining; risk of incarceration/strangulation.
- **Hydrocele**: typically painless scrotal swelling; transilluminates; often resolves in infants (non-communicating).

### Management
- Hernia: early elective repair (urgent if incarcerated).
- Hydrocele: observe in infancy if uncomplicated; repair if persistent/communicating/symptomatic.

## 6. Acute scrotum (time-critical)

### Key concept
Assume **testicular torsion until proven otherwise**.

### Differential diagnosis (high yield)
- Testicular torsion
- Torsion of appendix testis
- Epididymo-orchitis (less common pre-puberty)
- Incarcerated hernia
- Idiopathic scrotal oedema / trauma

### Management principle
- If torsion cannot be excluded clinically → urgent exploration (time-sensitive salvage).

---

## 7. Pyloric stenosis

### Presentation
- Progressive, non-bilious projectile vomiting (typically 2–8 weeks)
- Hungry after vomiting; weight loss/dehydration
- Palpable “olive” (epigastrium), visible peristalsis

### Metabolic consequence (classic)
- Hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis

### Management
- Resuscitate first: correct dehydration and electrolytes
- Definitive: pyloromyotomy (after optimisation)

---

## 8. Abdominal pain in children (age-based approach)

### Infants
- Intussusception, incarcerated hernia, malrotation/volvulus, NEC (neonates), UTI

### School-age
- Appendicitis, mesenteric adenitis, constipation, gastroenteritis

### Adolescents
- Appendicitis, inflammatory bowel disease, ovarian/testicular torsion, ectopic pregnancy (if relevant), PID

### Practical approach
- ABC assessment and analgesia early
- Consider dehydration and sepsis
- Use targeted imaging (US first-line in many conditions)

---

## 9. Solid abdominal tumours of childhood (examples)

### Wilms tumour (nephroblastoma)
- Renal mass; haematuria or hypertension may occur
- Management involves MDT staging, surgery and chemo ± radiotherapy

### Neuroblastoma
- Adrenal/sympathetic chain origin
- May present with abdominal mass, systemic symptoms, or metastatic disease

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Children decompensate late**: hypotension is a late sign—use tachycardia, perfusion and lactate/urine output.
2. **Acute scrotum = torsion until proven otherwise**; time is testis.
3. **Pyloric stenosis**: correct metabolic alkalosis before surgery.
4. **Paediatric fluids**: avoid hypotonic maintenance fluids; monitor sodium and glucose.
5. **Hernia in infants**: high incarceration risk → early referral/repair.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Acute scrotum** | Sudden scrotal pain/swelling requiring urgent evaluation to exclude torsion. |
| **Testicular torsion** | Twisting of spermatic cord causing ischaemia; surgical emergency. |
| **Pyloric stenosis** | Hypertrophy of pylorus causing gastric outlet obstruction in early infancy. |
| **Hydrocele** | Fluid around testis; communicating if linked to peritoneal cavity. |
