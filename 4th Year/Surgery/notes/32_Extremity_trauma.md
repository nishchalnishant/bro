# Chapter 32: Extremity trauma

## Learning objectives

To gain an understanding of:
- How to identify whether an injury exists
- The important injuries not to miss
- The principles of the description and classification of fractures
- The range of available treatments
- How to select an appropriate treatment

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Extremity trauma ranges from isolated injuries to components of polytrauma. While seldom immediately fatal, limb injuries are a major source of lifelong disability. Management must integrate:
- ATLS priorities (life before limb)
- Limb-threatening injury recognition (ischaemia, compartment syndrome, open fractures)
- Appropriate timing of fixation (damage control orthopaedics vs definitive care)

---

## 2. Diagnosis: is there an injury?

### 2.1 History
Determine:
- Mechanism and energy (twist vs high-energy RTA, crush)
- Timing and progression of pain/swelling
- Functional ability immediately after injury (weight bearing/hand function)
- Comorbidities affecting bone/soft tissue (diabetes, steroids, anticoagulants)

### 2.2 Examination (structured)
- Inspect: deformity, swelling, bruising, wounds
- Palpate: tenderness, crepitus, bony step
- Move: active and passive range (avoid excessive manipulation if unstable)
- **Neurovascular**: pulses, capillary refill, sensation, motor function
- Compare with contralateral side

### 2.3 Imaging
- X-ray in at least two planes including joints above/below (fracture principle)
- CT for complex articular fractures
- Ultrasound/Doppler/CT angiography for vascular injury suspicion

## 3. Important injuries not to miss (limb-threatening)

### 3.1 Open fractures
Key concept: an open fracture is contaminated until proven otherwise.

Immediate actions:
- Cover with sterile dressing
- IV antibiotics early (protocol dependent)
- Tetanus prophylaxis
- Splint and elevate
- Early debridement and specialist orthoplastic planning for severe soft tissue injury

### 3.2 Compartment syndrome (must know)
Clues:
- Pain out of proportion
- Pain on passive stretch
- Tense compartments
- Late signs: sensory loss, paralysis, pulselessness (very late)

Management:
- Urgent fasciotomy; do not wait for late signs

### 3.3 Vascular injury and acute limb ischaemia
Hard signs:
- Absent pulses, pallor/cool limb, expanding haematoma, active bleeding

Principles:
- Immediate senior review
- Restore perfusion urgently (temporary shunts, vascular repair) with fracture stabilisation as needed

### 3.4 Dislocations
Dislocations are joint emergencies with:
- Neurovascular risk
- Cartilage injury risk

Reduce promptly (after documentation of neurovascular status), then reassess and image.

## 4. Describing fractures (communication tool)

### Core descriptors
- Bone and segment (proximal/mid/distal)
- Laterality
- Pattern (transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted)
- Displacement and angulation
- Intra-articular vs extra-articular
- Open vs closed
- Neurovascular status and soft tissue envelope condition

---

## 5. Classification (high-yield examples)

### Open fractures (concept)
Severity grading incorporates:
- Wound size/contamination
- Soft tissue damage
- Periosteal stripping and vascular injury

### Articular fractures
Often categorised by:
- Involvement of joint surface
- Degree of comminution and displacement

Purpose: guide management and prognosis; standardise communication.

---

## 6. Treatment options

### 6.1 Non-operative
Used when stable/acceptable alignment and low displacement:
- Splints/casts
- Functional bracing
- Early range of motion where safe

### 6.2 Operative fixation (overview)
Options include:
- Internal fixation (plates/screws)
- Intramedullary nailing
- External fixation (temporary or definitive)

Goals:
- Restore alignment and length
- Stabilise to permit soft tissue recovery and early function
- Minimise complications (infection, non-union, stiffness)

---

## 7. Timing: damage control orthopaedics (DCO) vs definitive fixation

### Key concept
In polytrauma, long operations can deliver a “second hit” to physiology. DCO uses temporary stabilisation (often external fixation/traction) until the patient is stable for definitive fixation.

Choose based on:
- Physiological status (shock, acidosis, hypothermia, coagulopathy)
- Associated injuries (TBI, chest injury)
- Soft tissue condition

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Life before limb**, but limb-threatening injuries must be recognised early.
2. **Open fracture = antibiotics early + sterile dressing + splint + tetanus**.
3. **Compartment syndrome is a clinical diagnosis**; fasciotomy is time-critical.
4. Always document and reassess **neurovascular status** before and after interventions.
5. **Timing of fixation** in polytrauma is physiology-driven (DCO vs definitive).

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Open fracture** | Fracture with communication to the external environment through a wound. |
| **Compartment syndrome** | Raised pressure within a closed fascial compartment causing ischaemia; requires urgent fasciotomy. |
| **DCO** | Damage control orthopaedics: temporary stabilisation until physiological stabilisation allows definitive fixation. |
| **ORIF** | Open reduction and internal fixation. |
