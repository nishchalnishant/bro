# Chapter 41: The foot and ankle

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the basic anatomy and biomechanics of the foot and ankle
- Identify common foot and ankle problems across different age groups
- Learn the principles of conservative and surgical treatments
- Recognise the significance of progressive neurological diseases on foot health

---

## 1. Anatomy and Biomechanics
- **Bones:** 26 main bones (7 tarsal, 5 metatarsal, 14 phalanges) plus two sesamoids of the hallux.
- **Ankle Joint:** A "truncated section of a cone" (not a simple hinge). External rotation occurs with dorsiflexion; internal rotation with plantarflexion.
- **Stability:** Mortise congruence and ligaments (medial/deltoid, lateral, and inferior tibiofibular).
- **Subtalar Joint:** Responsible for inversion and eversion.
- **Transverse Arch Keystone:** The **second tarsometatarsal (TMT) joint**. Disruption (Lisfranc's injury) leads to flat foot.
- **Windlass Mechanism:** Plantar fascia tightening during toe-off accentuates the medial arch, creating a rigid lever arm for propulsion.
- **Gait Cycle:** Stance phase (60%) and Swing phase (40%). Stance includes heel strike, foot flat, and toe-off.

## 2. Forefoot Pathology

### 2.1 Hallux Valgus (Bunions)
- **Definition:** Lateral deviation of the big toe; often hereditary and accelerated by enclosed footwear.
- **Clinical Feature:** Bunion (bone + bursa) on the medial first metatarsal head. Often leads to second MTP joint overload (hammer toe).
- **Management:**
  - **Conservative:** Wide toe box, pressure relief.
  - **Surgical:** 
    - *Mild:* Distal osteotomy (e.g., Chevron).
    - *Moderate:* Shaft osteotomy (e.g., Scarf, Ludloff).
    - *Severe:* Basal osteotomy or Fusion (Lapidus procedure).
  - **Complications:** Recurrence, stiffness, transfer metatarsalgia (overload of 2nd MTP).

### 2.2 Hallux Rigidus
- **Definition:** Painful osteoarthritis of the first MTP joint with loss of dorsiflexion and dorsal osteophyte formation.
- **Management:**
  - **Conservative:** Stiff-soled or rocker-soled shoes.
  - **Surgical:** Cheilectomy (debridement/osteophyte excision), MTP fusion (gold standard for severe pain).

### 2.3 Lesser Toe Deformities
- **Hammer toe, Mallet toe, Claw toe:** Often secondary to hallux valgus or neurological disease.
- **Management:** Tenotomies (flexible) or bony procedures/fusions (fixed).

### 2.4 Morton’s Neuroma
- **Pathology:** Compression of the common digital nerve, most commonly between the 3rd and 4th metatarsal heads.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical (Mulder’s click), Ultrasound, or MRI.
- **Management:** Guided steroid injections (mainstay) or surgical excision (permanent numbness).

## 3. Midfoot and Hindfoot Pathology

### 3.1 Midfoot Arthritis
- **Causes:** Trauma (Lisfranc), Charcot, or obesity.
- **Management:** Footwear modification and orthotics; fusion (high-risk, moderate outcome).

### 3.2 Ankle Arthritis
- **Management:**
  - **Arthrodesis (Fusion):** Gold standard; often arthroscopic. Good function due to compensation by other joints.
  - **Total Ankle Replacement (TAR):** Preserves mobility; suitable for lower-demand patients. Higher revision rates compared to hip/knee replacements.

### 3.3 Acquired Flat Foot (PTTD)
- **Cause:** **Tibialis Posterior Tendon Dysfunction (PTTD)**.
- **Clinical Sign:** "Too many toes" sign and **inability to perform a single-leg heel raise**.
- **Management:** Medial displacement calcaneal osteotomy and tendon transfer (FDL or FHL).

---

## 4. Tendon Disorders

### 4.1 Achilles Tendon
- **Rupture:** Common in 40–50y age group ("weekend warriors").
- **Diagnosis:** **Simmonds test** (calf squeeze) must be recorded and documented.
- **Management:** Functional bracing (non-operative) is increasingly favoured; surgery reserved for high-demand athletes (risk of infection).

### 4.2 Achilles Tendinosis
- **Management:** Eccentric loading exercises, shockwave therapy. **Avoid steroid injections** (risk of rupture).

---

## 5. Neurological and Systemic Conditions

### 5.1 Pes Cavus (High Arch)
- **Rule:** Unilateral cavus requires scanning for spinal tumors/diastematomyelia.
- **Bilateral:** Often **Charcot-Marie-Tooth (CMT) disease**.
- **Management:** Tibialis posterior tendon transfer to the dorsum of the foot.

### 5.2 Diabetic Foot (High Yield)
- **Charcot Neuroarthropathy:** Acute red, hot, swollen foot. **Surgical Emergency.**
- **Management:** Immediate offloading in a total contact cast to prevent "rocker-bottom" deformity.
- **Ulceration:** Mandates referral to a multidisciplinary team (MDT) as per NICE guidelines.

---

## Summary Boxes

### Box 41.1: Clinical Flags
- **Unexplained hot, red, swollen foot in a diabetic:** Assume Charcot until proven otherwise.
- **Morning heel pain:** Plantar fasciitis (usually settles with stretching/time).
- **Inability to single-leg heel raise:** PTTD (Tibialis Posterior failure).

---

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **Lisfranc Injury:** Look for the "keystone" 2nd TMT joint alignment on X-ray.
2. **Morton’s Neuroma:** Presents with shooting pain/parasthesia into the toes.
3. **Simmonds Test:** Absence of plantarflexion on calf squeeze confirms Achilles rupture.
4. **Charcot Foot:** Can be mistaken for cellulitis; check for absence of fever and presence of neuropathy.
