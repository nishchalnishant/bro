# Chapter 11: Tissue and molecular diagnosis

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the value and limitations of tissue diagnosis.
- Understand approaches to tissue processing (histology, cytology, frozen section).
- Grasp the principles of microscopic diagnosis, distinguishing benign, malignant, and inflammatory conditions.
- Appreciate the critical importance of clinicopathological correlation.
- Understand the role and indications of special stains, immunohistochemistry, and evolving diagnostic molecular pathology.

---

## 1. Reasons for Assessment of Tissue

Tissue analysis has evolved far beyond simply confirming a diagnosis. It is deeply integrated into multidisciplinary clinical management.

**Core Purposes:**
- **Diagnosis:** Definitively establishing the nature of a lesion.
- **Classification:** Categorizing neoplasia (e.g., distinguishing adenocarcinoma from squamous cell carcinoma) and grading non-neoplastic disease.
- **Staging and Prognosis:** Determining the extent of spread (TNM staging) and assessing microscopic elements that predict outcome (e.g., vascular invasion).
- **Selection of Therapy:** Utilizing molecular and immunohistochemical markers to trigger specific targeted therapies (e.g., HER2, MMR).
- **Audit and Screening:** Confirming radiological findings and supporting national screening programs (e.g., Cervical HPV/Cytology, Bowel cancer screening).

## 2. Tissue Specimens

Pathological specimens broadly fall into three categories:

### 2.1 Histology
Solid tissue requiring preservation to maintain architecture.
- **Biopsies:** Small samples aiming for diagnosis (e.g., mucosal pinch, punch, needle core).
- **Excision Biopsy:** The entire lesion is removed for both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes.
- **Resections:** Large surgical specimens strictly aimed at treatment, staging, and comprehensive pathological mapping.
- *Fixation:* Standardly fixed immediately in **10% Formaldehyde (Formalin)**.

### 2.2 Cytology
Assessment of individually aspirated, exfoliated, or scraped cells.
- **Methods:** Cervical smears, washings/brushings, fluids (ascites, pleural), Fine-Needle Aspirations (FNA).
- **Advantages:** Fast, cheap, and often minimally invasive.
- **Disadvantages:** Loss of tissue architecture makes determination of 'invasion' difficult.

### 2.3 Fresh Tissue
Submitted *without* fixative. Used for:
- Intraoperative Rapid Frozen Section diagnosis.
- Tissue banking and specific research.
- Microbiological culture or precise genetic studies.

> [!WARNING] 
> **Risk Management:** Fresh (unfixed) tissue carries a significant risk of transmissible infection (e.g., HIV, Hepatitis B) and requires strict warning labels. Ensure all clinical data is provided to avoid severe diagnostic errors or sample mix-ups.

## 3. Specimen Processing

### Routine Histological Processing: Sequence of Events
1. **Macroscopic (Gross) Description:** Measuring, weighing, and describing the macroscopic features.
2. **Sampling:** Cutting standardized blocks of tissue ('cut up'). Inking surgical margins to allow microscopic margin assessment.
3. **Embedding:** placing samples into cassettes and embedding them in **Paraffin Wax**.
4. **Sectioning:** Microtomes slice the wax blocks into 5 $\mu$m sections, mounted on glass slides.
5. **Staining:** The default universal stain is **Haematoxylin & Eosin (H&E)**.
6. **Microscopic Examination & Reporting.**

### Intraoperative Frozen Section
- **Advantage:** Provides an extremely rapid diagnostic answer during surgery.
- **Disadvantages:** Inferior section quality, labor-intensive, increased infection risk, and requires freezing artifact interpretation.

## 4. Principles of Microscopic Diagnosis

### 4.1 Diagnosis of Malignancy
Categorization rests on the tumor's ability to behave aggressively. 

- **Absolute Proof of Malignancy:**
  - **Metastasis:** Tumor deposits in distant sites (e.g., liver, lymph nodes).
  - **Invasion:** Destruction of surrounding structural boundaries, including Vascular and Perineural invasion.
- **Architectural Abnormalities:** Loss of polarity, loss of normal glandular layout.
- **Cytological (Cellular) Abnormalities:**
  - Nuclear Pleomorphism (variation in size and shape) and Enlargement.
  - Hyperchromasia (dark staining due to excess DNA).
  - Prominent/multiple nucleoli and numerous, atypical mitotic figures.
  - Areas of tumor **Necrosis**.

### 4.2 Non-Neoplastic and Inflammatory Conditions
- **Acute Inflammation:** Characterized by large infiltrates of **Neutrophils** with multilobated nuclei, often alongside erosion or ulceration.
- **Chronic Inflammation:** Dominated by **Lymphocytes** and plasma cells.
- **Granulomas:** Aggregates of epithelioid histiocytes (macrophages). Characteristically seen in Tuberculosis (central caseating necrosis + Langhans giant cells), Sarcoidosis, and Crohn's disease.

### 4.3 General Pathological Terminology
- **Hyperplasia:** Increase in cell number.
- **Hypertrophy:** Increase in cell size.
- **Metaplasia:** Conversion of one mature cell type to another (e.g., Barrett’s Oesophagus).
- **Dysplasia (Intraepithelial Neoplasia):** Cytological features of malignancy *without* evidence of invasion.
- **Apoptosis:** Programmed cell death (cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation).

## 5. Further Work: Stains and Antibodies

If H&E is insufficient, pathologists utilize additional techniques.

### 5.1 Special Histochemical Stains
| Stain | Target Demonstrated | Clinical Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **PAS** | Glycogen, Fungi | Fungal infections |
| **Diastase-PAS** | Mucin | Adenocarcinoma differentiation |
| **Perls Prussian Blue** | Iron | Haemochromatosis |
| **Reticulin / van Gieson** | Fibrosis / Collagen | Cirrhosis staging |
| **Congo Red** | Amyloid | Amyloidosis |
| **Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN)** | Acid-fast bacilli | Mycobacterium tuberculosis |

### 5.2 Immunohistochemistry (IHC)
Utilizes tagged antibodies to target specific cellular antigens. It appears as a brown colored stain on the slide.

**Uses of IHC in Tumors:**
- **Cell Typing:** 
  - *Cytokeratin* $\rightarrow$ Carcinoma (Epithelial).
  - *CD45 / CD3 / CD20* $\rightarrow$ Lymphoma (Lymphoid).
  - *S100, MelanA, HMB45* $\rightarrow$ Melanoma.
  - *CD117, DOG-1* $\rightarrow$ GIST.
- **Site of Origin:** e.g., PSA (Prostate), TTF-1 (Lung/Thyroid).
- **Signaling & Prognosis:** 
  - **Ki67:** Proliferation index (especially crucial in neuroendocrine tumors).
  - **HER2 / ER:** Breast cancer therapeutic targeting.
  - **Mismatch Repair (MMR):** MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 screening in colorectal/endometrial cancers.

## 6. Diagnostic Molecular Pathology

Aimed at assessing tissue DNA and RNA to inform diagnosis, prognostication, and heavily influence "precision medicine".

### 6.1 Core Methods
- **In Situ Hybridization (ISH / FISH):** Tagged oligonucleotide probes visually binding to specific DNA/RNA sequences. E.g., assessing HER2 amplification or detecting EBV virus.
- **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR):** Rapidly multiplying tiny samples of DNA. Useful for identifying point mutations (e.g., BRAF).
- **Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS):** Massively parallel sequencing allowing simultaneous examination of millions of DNA fragments for hundreds of mutations, fusions, and copy-number changes via a single assay.

### 6.2 Key Genomic Targets in Surgical Oncology
- **Colorectal Carcinoma (CRC):**
  - *MMR status / Microsatellite Instability (MSI):* 15% of CRCs are MSI-High due to MMR deficiency (mostly sporadic MLH1 hypermethylation, rarely Lynch syndrome germline mutation). MSI-H predicts excellent response to Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICIs).
  - *KRAS / NRAS:* Mutations predict severe resistance to anti-EGFR therapies (Cetuximab).
  - *BRAF V600E:* Strongly predicts aggressive disease and poor prognosis.
- **Lung Carcinoma (NSCLC):** 
  - *EGFR mutations / ALK translocations* highly sensitive to specific Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors.
- **Malignant Melanoma:** BRAF mutations dictate use of kinase inhibitors directly (e.g., Vemurafenib).

### 6.3 Tumor Mutation Burden (TMB)
A biomarker measuring the total density of mutations in a tumor. High TMB (like MSI-H) often predicts a very strong response to Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor drugs (ICIs).

## 7. Digital Pathology 
The modern transition involves high-resolution scanning of histological glass slides straight into digital formats. 
- **Benefits:** Permits off-site and remote reporting, expert consultation via the cloud, and supports emerging Artificial Intelligence (AI) image analysis tools.

## 8. Autopsy
The principal goal is confirming the exact cause of death for legal (Coroner's autopsy - requires no consent) and clinical audit/educational reasons (Hospital autopsy - requires relative consent).
