# Chapter 19: Trauma in children

## Learning objectives

To be able to:
- Systematically assess an injured child
- Give examples of how mechanisms of, and responses to, injury differ from those in adults
- Distinguish the elements of the primary and secondary surveys
- Explain when and how to transfuse blood products in children
- Describe some common patterns of injury in children
- Request appropriate investigations for an injured child
- Outline the non-operative management of solid organ injuries
- Outline the principles of the trauma laparotomy and the clamshell thoracotomy
- Explain the principle of damage control surgery

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Trauma is the leading cause of death in children over 1 year old. Effective care depends on **structured assessment**, rapid recognition of time-critical threats, and early team-based management.

### Clinical significance
Children often maintain blood pressure until late (physiological compensation), so **hypotension is a late sign**. Early deterioration can be missed without systematic assessment.

---

## 2. Primary survey (cABCDE) and paediatric differences

### Framework
Use ATLS-style **cABCDE**:
- **c**: catastrophic haemorrhage control (direct pressure, tourniquet where appropriate)
- **A**: airway with C-spine protection
- **B**: breathing and oxygenation/ventilation
- **C**: circulation and haemorrhage control
- **D**: disability (neurology, glucose)
- **E**: exposure and environment (temperature control)

### Paediatric-specific considerations (high yield)
- **Airway**: avoid neck overextension in infants; use age-appropriate positioning.
- **Breathing**: hypoxia is a common cause of paediatric cardiac arrest; rapid correction can restore rhythm.
- **Circulation**: hypotension is late; assess perfusion, capillary refill, mental state, lactate, urine output.
- **Disability**: use age-modified GCS.
- **Environment**: hypothermia develops quickly and worsens coagulopathy; actively warm.

### Cardiac arrest differential in trauma (remember list)
Four Hs and four Ts:
- Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hypothermia/Hyperthermia, Hypo-/Hyperkalaemia
- Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombus

---

## 3. Secondary survey

### Key components
- Full head-to-toe examination after stabilisation
- History (AMPLE): Allergies, Meds, Past history, Last meal, Events
- Focused re-assessment: airway/breathing/circulation can deteriorate

### Patterns and safeguarding
- Consider non-accidental injury when history is inconsistent or injuries are suspicious for age/mechanism.

---

## 4. Transfusion and haemorrhage in children

### When to transfuse (practical)
Transfuse when there is:
- Ongoing haemorrhage with shock signs
- Failure to respond to initial fluid resuscitation
- Major trauma with anticipated blood loss

### How to transfuse (principles)
- Use weight-based dosing and balanced blood product resuscitation in major haemorrhage.
- Prevent the lethal triad: **hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy**.
- Early tranexamic acid may be considered per local paediatric trauma protocols.

---

## 5. Investigations and imaging

### Key concept
Imaging should be **question-driven** and balanced against radiation exposure.

Common approach:
- FAST/ultrasound for selected cases
- CT when it will change management (e.g. severe head injury, haemodynamic stability with suspected intra-abdominal injury)
- Plain films for chest/pelvis/limb injuries when indicated

---

## 6. Solid organ injury: non-operative management (NOM)

### Key concept
In haemodynamically stable children, many liver/spleen/kidney injuries can be managed non-operatively with:
- Observation and serial examinations
- Haemoglobin monitoring
- Activity restriction and follow-up

Escalate to intervention when:
- Haemodynamic instability
- Ongoing transfusion requirement
- Peritonitis or suspected hollow viscus injury

---

## 7. Trauma laparotomy and clamshell thoracotomy (principles)

### 7.1 Trauma laparotomy (high yield steps)
- Control haemorrhage (packing, vascular control)
- Control contamination (bowel injury control, suction, temporary closure)
- Prioritise rapid physiology restoration over definitive reconstruction in unstable patients

### 7.2 Clamshell thoracotomy (overview)
Rare and extreme rescue manoeuvre for catastrophic thoracic injury, providing rapid access to:
- Heart (tamponade relief)
- Great vessels / pulmonary hilum control

Performed only in appropriate settings with experienced teams.

---

## 8. Damage control surgery (DCS)

### Key concept
DCS is staged surgery for unstable patients:
- **Stage 1:** rapid haemorrhage control + contamination control
- **Stage 2:** ICU resuscitation (rewarm, correct coagulopathy/acidosis)
- **Stage 3:** definitive repair once physiology stabilised

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Children compensate well—until they don’t**: falling BP is late; treat shock earlier based on perfusion.
2. **Airway positioning differs by age**: avoid overextension in infants.
3. **Prevent hypothermia** aggressively during exposure and resuscitation.
4. **Stable solid organ injuries** often managed non-operatively with observation.
5. **Damage control** is physiology-first: stop bleeding/contamination, then ICU correction, then definitive repair.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Primary survey** | Structured rapid assessment/treatment of immediate life threats (cABCDE). |
| **Secondary survey** | Head-to-toe assessment and investigations after initial stabilisation. |
| **SCIWORA** | Spinal cord injury without radiological abnormality; neurological deficit despite normal X-ray/CT. |
| **Damage control surgery** | Staged operative strategy prioritising physiology stabilisation over definitive repair in unstable trauma. |
