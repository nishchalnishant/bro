# Chapter 28: Traumatic brain injury

## Learning objectives

To be familiar with:
- The physiology of cerebral blood flow and the pathophysiology of raised intracranial pressure
- The resuscitation, assessment, investigation and continuing care of head-injured patients
- The prevention and detection of secondary intracranial and systemic insults

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Traumatic brain injury (TBI) includes:
- **Primary injury**: damage at the moment of impact (not medically modifiable)
- **Secondary injury**: evolving injury over hours–days from hypoxia, hypotension, raised ICP, seizures, fever and metabolic derangement (modifiable)

### Clinical significance
TBI is a leading cause of death and disability. Improving outcomes depends on preventing secondary injury through meticulous physiology control.

---

## 2. Cerebral physiology essentials

### 2.1 Cerebral blood flow (CBF)
The brain depends on continuous perfusion for oxygen and glucose delivery.

### 2.2 Cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP)
\[
CPP = MAP - ICP
\]
Typical normal ICP is low, but rises in trauma can reduce CPP and cause ischaemia.

### 2.3 Autoregulation
Normal brains maintain relatively stable CBF across a range of MAP via vascular tone changes. Autoregulation can be impaired after TBI, so MAP and ICP must be actively managed to protect perfusion.

---

## 3. Raised intracranial pressure (ICP) and herniation

### 3.1 Monro–Kellie doctrine
The skull is a rigid box containing:
- Brain tissue
- Blood
- CSF

Increases in one component must be offset by decreases in others; once compensation is exhausted, ICP rises rapidly.

### 3.2 Herniation syndromes (high yield)
Uncontrolled ICP can cause brain shift/herniation, commonly:
- **Uncal herniation**: pupil changes, brainstem compression signs
- **Tonsillar herniation**: medullary compression → cardiorespiratory collapse

---

## 4. Initial assessment and resuscitation

### 4.1 ABC priorities
Prevent secondary injury by ensuring:
- Oxygenation and ventilation (avoid hypoxia and hypercapnia)
- Circulatory support (avoid hypotension)
- Temperature control (avoid fever)
- Glucose control (avoid severe hypo/hyperglycaemia)

### 4.2 Neurological assessment
- Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
- Pupils (size, reactivity, asymmetry)
- Focal deficits and seizures

### 4.3 Red flags for severe TBI
- GCS decline
- New pupillary abnormality
- Seizures
- Persistent hypotension/hypoxia

---

## 5. Investigation and monitoring

### Imaging
CT head is the primary acute imaging modality to identify:
- Extradural/subdural haematoma
- Contusions and diffuse swelling
- Midline shift and herniation risk

### ICP monitoring (selected patients)
Used in severe TBI to guide therapy and protect CPP (ICU context).

---

## 6. Preventing secondary insults (systemic + intracranial)

### 6.1 Systemic insults
Must avoid:
- Hypoxia
- Hypotension
- Anaemia (context dependent)
- Fever
- Seizures and hyperglycaemia extremes

### 6.2 Intracranial management principles
Interventions may include (specialist/ICU guided):
- Head-up positioning and neutral neck alignment (venous drainage)
- Sedation/analgesia to reduce metabolic demand
- Osmotherapy (hypertonic saline or mannitol where appropriate)
- Controlled ventilation in selected cases (avoid prolonged hypocapnia without indication)
- Surgical evacuation of mass lesions when indicated

---

## 7. Ongoing care and complications

### Common complications
- Cerebral oedema and rising ICP
- Seizures
- Ventilator-associated complications
- Neuroendocrine issues (rare but important)

### Rehabilitation
Early rehabilitation planning improves function and reduces long-term disability burden.

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Primary injury cannot be reversed**; outcomes depend on preventing secondary injury.
2. **Hypoxia and hypotension are the worst “second hits”**—treat aggressively.
3. **CPP depends on MAP and ICP**; protecting CPP is a central goal in severe TBI.
4. **Pupil changes are critical**: a new fixed dilated pupil suggests herniation until proven otherwise.
5. **CT is the key early investigation** to identify surgically treatable mass lesions.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **TBI** | Traumatic brain injury with primary and secondary injury components. |
| **ICP** | Intracranial pressure. |
| **CPP** | Cerebral perfusion pressure; \( CPP = MAP - ICP \). |
| **Autoregulation** | Brain’s ability to maintain CBF across a range of MAP by changing vascular tone. |
| **Herniation** | Brain displacement due to raised ICP/mass effect causing brainstem compression. |
