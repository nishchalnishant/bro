# Chapter 35: History taking and clinical examination in musculoskeletal disease

## Learning objectives

To understand how to:
- Take a comprehensive musculoskeletal history
- Perform a structured and systematic musculoskeletal examination
- Use and interpret special tests
- Use findings to understand the impact on a patient’s pain and function

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Musculoskeletal (MSK) diagnosis is built on a **structured history and examination**. A consistent system allows the clinician to identify:
- Common conditions (degeneration, soft tissue injury)
- Rare conditions (tumours, inflammatory disease)
- Urgent conditions (septic arthritis, cauda equina, neurovascular compromise)

---

## 2. MSK history: a reliable framework

### 2.1 Set-up (good clinical practice)
- Hand hygiene, introduction, confirm patient identity
- Explain process; obtain verbal consent
- Chaperone for intimate examinations when appropriate

### 2.2 Presenting complaint and patient goals
- Start open-ended (“Tell me about the problem.”)
- Clarify expectations and goals (return to work/sport, pain relief, function)

### 2.3 History of presenting complaint (“three Ws” style)
High-yield structure:
- When did it start? Sudden vs gradual
- What were they doing at onset? Mechanism/trigger
- What has happened since? Progression, variability

### 2.4 Associated symptoms (core)
- Pain (site, character, severity, radiation, mechanical vs inflammatory)
- Swelling
- Instability (“giving way”)
- Mechanical symptoms (locking, clicking)
- Weakness/power loss
- Sensory changes (numbness/tingling)

### 2.5 Functional impairment (ADLs)
Upper limb:
- Hygiene, dressing, feeding
Lower limb:
- Standing, walking distance, stairs, shoes/socks, sit-to-stand

### 2.6 Past history and risk factors
- PMH (diabetes, inflammatory disease, osteoporosis, vascular disease)
- Previous MSK problems and surgeries
- Medication (anticoagulants, steroids, immunosuppressants, OCP/HRT)
- Social history: occupation, sport, smoking, support at home

### 2.7 Red flags in MSK history
Must ask about:
- Fever, systemic illness (infection)
- Unexplained weight loss, night pain (malignancy)
- New neurological symptoms or bowel/bladder disturbance (spine emergency)
- Severe pain after trauma or anticoagulation (bleeding/compartment)

---

## 3. MSK examination: structured and reproducible

### 3.1 General principles
- Expose appropriately; compare both sides
- Follow “Look, Feel, Move” plus neurovascular examination
- Examine joints above and below (injury contexts)

### 3.2 Look
- Deformity, swelling, scars, erythema, bruising
- Muscle wasting, posture, gait (if relevant)

### 3.3 Feel
- Temperature (inflammation/infection)
- Tenderness localisation
- Effusion and fluctuance
- Bony landmarks and step deformities

### 3.4 Move
- Active then passive range
- Pain pattern and end-feel
- Assess functional movement (squat, single-leg stance) when safe

### 3.5 Neurovascular
- Pulses, capillary refill
- Sensation and motor testing
- Document deficits clearly (baseline and post-intervention)

### 3.6 Special tests
Use hypothesis-driven tests:
- Ligament tests for instability
- Impingement tests
- Nerve tension tests (e.g. straight leg raise in sciatica)

Interpretation:
- Sensitivity vs specificity varies; combine with history and imaging

---

## 4. Using findings to quantify impact

### Key concept
MSK disease is fundamentally about **pain and function**. Translate findings into:
- Activity limitation and participation restriction (work, sport, self-care)
- Baseline function and rehabilitation needs

Tools (conceptual):
- Patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs)
- Standardised functional tests (where used)

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Structure prevents misses**: use the same history/exam framework each time.
2. **Function matters**: document ADL impact and patient goals.
3. **Compare sides**: asymmetry is often the key clue.
4. **Neurovascular status is mandatory** in limb injury.
5. Always screen for **MSK red flags** (infection, malignancy, neuro compromise).

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Mechanical pain** | Pain worsened by use and relieved by rest; typical of degenerative disease. |
| **Inflammatory pain** | Pain with morning stiffness and improvement with movement; suggests inflammatory pathology. |
| **PROMs** | Patient-reported outcome measures quantifying pain/function. |
| **ADLs** | Activities of daily living (self-care and functional tasks). |
