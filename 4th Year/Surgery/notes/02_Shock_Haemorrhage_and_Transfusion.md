# Chapter 2: Shock, haemorrhage and transfusion

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the pathophysiology of shock
- Understand the different patterns of shock and the principles and priorities of resuscitation
- Understand appropriate monitoring and end points of resuscitation
- Recognise and manage bleeding
- Understand the use of blood and blood products, the benefits and risks of blood transfusion

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key Concept
**Shock** is a systemic state of low tissue perfusion that is inadequate for normal cellular respiration. With insufficient delivery of oxygen and glucose, cells switch from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism. If perfusion is not restored in a timely fashion, cell death ensues.

### Clinical Significance
- Most common cause of death of surgical patients.
- Death occurs either rapidly due to profound shock or later due to the consequences of organ ischaemia and reperfusion injury.

---

## 2. Pathophysiology of Shock

### 2.1 Cellular
- Cells deprived of oxygen switch to anaerobic metabolism.
- The product is **lactic acid**, leading to a systemic metabolic acidosis.
- Exhaustion of intracellular glucose → failure of sodium/potassium pumps → intracellular lysosomes release autodigestive enzymes → cell lysis.
- Potassium is released into the bloodstream (hyperkalaemia).

### 2.2 Microvascular
- Hypoxia and acidosis activate complement and prime leukocytes (oxygen free radicals and cytokine release).
- Capillary endothelial cells get damaged and become 'leaky', leading to tissue oedema, exacerbating hypoxia.
- Systemic immune and coagulation systems are continuously activated.

### 2.3 Systemic Effects

| System | Response/Mechanism |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Compensatory baroreceptor response → increased sympathetic activity → catecholamine release → tachycardia and systemic vasoconstriction. |
| **Respiratory** | Metabolic acidosis + sympathetic response → ↑ respiratory rate and minute ventilation → compensatory respiratory alkalosis. |
| **Renal** | ↓ Perfusion pressure → ↓ glomerular filtration rate → decreased urine output. Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone axis stimulated → vasoconstriction and sodium/water reabsorption. |
| **Endocrine** | Vasopressin (ADH) release → vasoconstriction and water resorption. Cortisol release → sodium/water resorption and sensitisation to catecholamines. |

## 3. Classification of Shock

### Summary Box 2.1: Classification of shock
- Haemorrhagic/hypovolaemic shock
- Cardiogenic shock
- Obstructive shock
- Distributive shock
- Endocrine shock

### 3.1 Hypovolaemic Shock
- Due to a reduced circulating volume.
- **Haemorrhagic:** bleeding.
- **Non-haemorrhagic:** dehydration, vomiting, diarrhoea, 'third-spacing' (pancreatitis, bowel obstruction).
- Most common form of shock; frequently coexists with other forms.

### 3.2 Cardiogenic Shock
- Primary failure of the heart to pump blood to the tissues.
- **Causes:** Myocardial infarction, cardiac dysrhythmias, valvular heart disease, blunt myocardial injury, cardiomyopathy.
- Evidence of venous hypertension with pulmonary or systemic oedema may coexist.

### 3.3 Obstructive Shock
- Reduction in preload due to mechanical obstruction of cardiac filling.
- **Causes:** Cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax, massive pulmonary embolus, air embolus.

### 3.4 Distributive Shock
- Inadequate organ perfusion accompanied by vascular dilatation with hypotension, low SVR, and an abnormally high cardiac output.
- **Causes:** Anaphylaxis (histamine release), high spinal cord injury (loss of sympathetic tone - neurogenic shock), sepsis.
- **Septic Shock:** Maldistribution of microvascular flow, arteriovenous shunting, cellular inability to utilise oxygen. (Later phases become hypovolaemic from fluid loss).

### 3.5 Endocrine Shock
- May present as hypovolaemic, cardiogenic, or distributive.
- **Causes:** Hypo/hyperthyroidism, adrenal insufficiency. (Addison's disease or relative insufficiency in sepsis).

### Table 2.1 Cardiovascular and metabolic characteristics of shock

| Type | Cardiac Output | SVR | Venous Pressure | Mixed Venous Saturation | Base Deficit |
|------|---------------|-----|-----------------|-------------------------|--------------|
| Hypovolaemic | Low | High | Low | Low | High |
| Cardiogenic | Low | High | High | Low | High |
| Obstructive | Low | High | High | Low | High |
| Distributive | High | Low | Low | High | High |

## 4. Clinical Consequences and Recognition

### 4.1 Consequences
- **Unresuscitatable shock:** Profound shock for prolonged periods leading to systemic ischaemic injury. Loss of response to fluids or vasopressors.
- **Reperfusion Injury & SIRS:** Restoring circulation flushes acid, potassium, and inflammatory mediators systematically → acute lung injury, ARDS, renal injury.
- **Multiple Organ Failure (MOF):** Two or more failed organ systems. 60% mortality. 

### 4.2 Recognition of Shock

| State | Clinical Features |
|-------|------------------|
| **Compensated** | Tachycardia, cool peripheries, normal BP, normal urine output. Major organs perfused at the expense of gut/skin (occult hypoperfusion). |
| **Mild** | Tachycardia, tachypnoea, ↓ pulse pressure, ↓ urine output, cool sweaty peripheries, mild anxiety. |
| **Moderate** | Urine output dips below 0.5 mL/kg/hr. BP starts to fall. Drowsiness/mild confusion. |
| **Severe** | Profound tachycardia & hypotension. Anuric. Unconscious with laboured respiration. |

**Important Pitfalls:**
- Blood pressure is one of the *last* signs to change (particularly in children/young adults).
- Capillary refill can be brisk in septic shock.
- Tachycardia may be absent if the patient is on β-blockers, has a pacemaker, or with some penetrating trauma (paradoxical bradycardia).

## 5. Haemorrhage and Damage Control Resuscitation

### 5.1 Definitions & Types
- **Revealed:** Obvious external bleeding (open wounds, haematemesis).
- **Concealed:** Contained within body cavities (thorax, abdomen, pelvis, retroperitoneum, limbs).
- **Primary:** Immediately resulting from injury/surgery.
- **Reactionary:** Delayed (<24 hrs) due to dislodgement of clot (resuscitation, vasodilatation).
- **Secondary:** Due to vessel sloughing (7-14 days), often from infection, pressure necrosis, or malignancy.
- **ATC (Acute Traumatic Coagulopathy):** Endogenous coagulopathy affecting up to 25% of trauma patients within minutes. Exacerbated by fluids (dilutional), hypothermia, and acidaemia (TIC vicious cycle).

### 5.2 Dynamic Assessment of Bleeding (Response to fluid)
- **Responder:** Sustained improvement in BP.
- **Transient responder:** Improvement not sustained (bleeding < fluids).
- **Non-responder:** No improvement. Identifies severe ongoing haemorrhage.

### 5.3 Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR)
*The paradigm prioritising haemorrhage control in actively bleeding patients.*

1. **Rapid haemorrhage control:** Direct pressure, tourniquets, operative/endovascular control. **Damage Control Surgery:** Arrest haemorrhage, control sepsis, protect from injury – *nothing else*.
2. **Permissive hypotension:** Let the patient set their BP, avoid "popping the clot" or inducing dilutional coagulopathy with excess crystalloids. Ensure palpable central pulse (MAP >50 mmHg).
3. **Avoid dilutional coagulopathy:** Minimise clear fluids. Administer whole blood equivalence (1:1 ratio of packed red cells to FFP).
4. **Treat existing coagulation deficits:** Tranexamic acid empirically. Give platelets and cryoprecipitate guided by ROTEM/TEG.

## 6. Shock Resuscitation & Fluid Therapy

*Once bleeding is controlled, resuscitation shifts to restoring end-organ perfusion.*

### 6.1 Principles
- Correct hypovolaemia and preload *first*.
- Never start vasopressors/inotropes on an empty heart (will deplete myocardial O2 reserves).
- Resuscitate via short, wide-bore catheters (not long CVP lines which have high resistance).

### 6.2 Fluids vs Pressors
- **Crystalloids** (Saline, Hartmann's) are standard. Colloids offer no outcome benefit and have worse side-effects.
- **Vasopressors** (Phenylephrine, Noradrenaline, Vasopressin): Indicated in distributive shock (low SVR/vasodilatation).
- **Inotropes** (Dobutamine): Indicated in cardiogenic shock or myocardial depression in septic shock.

### 6.3 Monitoring

| Tool | Normal/Target | Significance |
|------|--------------|--------------|
| **CVP (Central Venous Pressure)** | Dynamic response | A fluid challenge (250-500 mL) should rise CVP 2-5 cmH2O then settle. No rise = empty. Large sustained rise = overload/cardiac insufficiency. |
| **Base Deficit / Lactate** | < 2 mmol/L | Global indicator of hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism. |
| **Mixed Venous Saturation (SvO2)** | 50-70% | <50% = hypovolaemic/cardiogenic shock (low delivery). >70% in shocked patient = septic shock (shunting/inability to use O2). |
| **Urine Output** | > 0.5 mL/kg/hr | Reliable standard for renal perfusion. |

**Occult Hypoperfusion:** Normal HR, BP, and UO, but persistently high lactate/base deficit. Untreated occult hypoperfusion (>12 hrs) results in 2-3x higher mortality.

## 7. Blood Transfusion

### 7.1 Key Components
- **Packed Red Cells:** Haematocrit 50-70%. Stored in SAG-M (up to 5 weeks at 2-6°C).
- **FFP:** Rich in coagulation factors, shelf life up to 2 years frozen. First-line for coagulopathic haemorrhage.
- **Cryoprecipitate:** Supernatant of FFP, rich in Fibrinogen, Factor VIII, Factor XIII.
- **Platelets:** Shelf life of 5 days at 20-24°C.
- **Prothrombin Complex Concentrates (PCC):** Factors II, IX, X. Used for emergency reversal of warfarin.

### 7.2 Transfusion Triggers
- Transfuse to **Hb > 6 g/dL**. 
- Values > 8 g/dL offer no benefit in the absence of active bleeding, impending surgery, or profound symptoms.

### 7.3 Blood Groups and Cross-matching
- **ABO System:** O is universal donor. AB is universal recipient. Massive fatal haemolysis if incompatible.
- **Rhesus System:** Rh(D) is strongly antigenic (85% of population). Sensitisation can cause hydrops fetalis in subsequent pregnancies.
- Emergency: Give **O negative** (females) or **O positive** (males) if unmatched. Type-specific takes 10-15 mins. Full cross-match takes ~45 mins.

### 7.4 Complications of Massive Transfusion
- Coagulopathy (dilutional)
- Hypocalcaemia (from citrate toxicity)
- Hyperkalaemia (from lysed red cells in old units) or Hypokalaemia (alkalosis)
- Hypothermia

## Clinical Pearls for Exams
1. **Differentiating Septic Shock:** Distributive/Septic shock is unique with *warm* peripheries, *brisk* capillary refill, low SVR, and high mixed venous saturation (SvO2 >70%).
2. **Occult Hypoperfusion:** A patient with normal BP, HR, and Urine Output can still be in shock; rely on persistent lactate > 2 mmol/L.
3. **Vicious Cycle of Trauma-Induced Coagulopathy (TIC):** Coagulopathy is worsened by Acidaemia, Hypothermia, and Dilution from fluids.
4. **Resuscitation Priorities:** Never use vasopressors before adequately loading preload with IV fluids.
5. **Transfusion Trigger:** Restrictive transfusion criteria (Target Hb > 6 g/dL) actually improves morbidity/mortality compared to liberal strategies in non-bleeding patients.

## Important Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **CVP**| Central Venous Pressure; requires dynamic fluid challenge assessment to gauge preload accurately. |
| **SIRS** | Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome; inflammatory surge propagating microvascular injury. |
| **DCR** | Damage Control Resuscitation; focuses on haemostasis and matching coagulation function over restoring absolute BP parameters. |
| **Occult Hypoperfusion** | Persisting tissue hypoperfusion (raised lactate) despite supposedly 'normal' conventional vital signs. |
| **Transient Responder** | A patient whose blood pressure improves with a fluid bolus but the improvement drops quickly, indicating active bleeding exceeding fluid rate. |
