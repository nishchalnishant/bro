## 1. Introduction and definition

### Key concept
**Global health** concerns the health of populations in a global context. **Global surgery** is surgery grounded in public health: it aligns individual surgical care with population-level needs, aiming for **equitable, safe, timely, and affordable** surgical and anaesthesia care worldwide.

### Role of a global surgeon (conceptual)
Global surgeons are “population-aware” clinicians who:
- Understand the local **burden of surgical disease**
- Help design systems that deliver essential surgery reliably
- Improve access, quality, and outcomes across a community

---

## 2. Surgery as an essential and cost-effective intervention

### 2.1 Why surgery matters in public health
As communicable disease burdens fall, a large fraction of global disease is now due to **surgical conditions** (injury, cancers, congenital anomalies, obstetric emergencies).

### 2.2 Cost-effectiveness (high-yield framing)
Surgery can be highly cost-effective when:
- It addresses common, treatable conditions (e.g. appendicitis, hernia, fractures, obstructed labour)
- It is delivered as a **platform** (district hospital system) rather than isolated missions

Comparison point: many essential surgical interventions have cost-effectiveness comparable to widely adopted public-health measures.

---

## 3. Surgery and impoverishment (financial risk protection)

### Key concept
Even when surgery is clinically effective, costs can push households into poverty.

Important mechanisms:
- Direct costs (fees, medicines, diagnostics, transport)
- Indirect costs (lost wages, caregiver time)
- Catastrophic expenditure and long-term debt

Clinical systems implication:
- “Access” must include **financial affordability**, not just geographic availability.

---

## 4. Access to surgical care

### Components of access (practical)
- **Timeliness** (can the patient reach care quickly enough?)
- **Capacity** (workforce, theatre availability, supplies, blood, imaging)
- **Safety/quality** (trained staff, anaesthesia safety, infection control)
- **Affordability** (financial protection)

Common barriers:
- Distance and transport delays
- Lack of trained surgeons/anaesthetists/nurses
- Inadequate infrastructure (oxygen, sterility, blood bank)
- Weak referral pathways and data systems

---

## 5. Delivering essential surgery: platforms and systems

### 5.1 The “platform” idea
Essential procedures are best delivered via functional health-system platforms (often district hospitals) that can provide:
- Emergency and elective surgery
- Safe anaesthesia
- Basic imaging and laboratory services
- Post-op monitoring and infection prevention

### 5.2 Bellwether procedures (common global-surgery marker)
A facility’s ability to provide a small set of key operations is often used as a proxy for system capability, commonly including:
- Caesarean delivery
- Laparotomy
- Open fracture management

---

## 6. Workforce

### Key concept
Surgical care is team-based: surgeons, anaesthetists, obstetric providers, nurses, technicians and support services.

Core issues:
- Shortages and maldistribution (rural vs urban, public vs private)
- Training capacity and retention
- Task-sharing with appropriate governance

---

## 7. Metrics, research and improvement

### Why metrics matter
If you cannot measure access, volume, outcomes and financial risk, you cannot reliably improve them.

Common metric domains:
- Access within a defined time window
- Surgical volume per population
- Perioperative mortality and major morbidity
- Protection from catastrophic expenditure

Research priorities:
- Implementation science (what works in real systems?)
- Quality and safety improvement
- Costing and value

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Surgery is public health**: many high-burden conditions require operative care to prevent death and disability.
2. **Cost-effectiveness improves with systems**: platforms (district hospitals) outperform stand-alone missions for sustainable impact.
3. **Access is multi-dimensional**: distance alone is not access—financial protection and safety are crucial.
4. **Workforce is the bottleneck**: without anaesthesia and nursing capacity, surgical scaling fails.
5. **Metrics drive improvement**: measurement is a prerequisite for safe scale-up.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Global surgery** | Surgical care integrated with public health to improve equitable access and outcomes worldwide. |
| **Surgical platform** | A system (often district hospital-based) delivering a package of essential surgical services reliably. |
| **Catastrophic expenditure** | Healthcare costs that threaten a household’s financial stability and can cause impoverishment. |
| **Access to care** | Ability to receive timely, safe and affordable surgical and anaesthesia services. |
