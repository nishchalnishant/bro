# Chapter 22: Day case surgery

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- The key components of the day surgery pathway
- Which surgical procedures can be done as day surgery
- Patient selection and preparation for day surgery
- Basic principles of anaesthesia and surgery for day surgery
- How to achieve successful discharge after day surgery

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
**Day surgery** is planned surgery where the patient is admitted and discharged on the same day (distinct from “23-hour stay”). Success depends on a high-quality pathway that delivers:
- Appropriate case selection
- Standardised perioperative processes
- Effective analgesia/antiemesis
- Robust discharge criteria and safety-netting

### Benefits
- Patient preference (recover at home), reduced disruption
- Reduced hospital-acquired infection risk
- Increased inpatient bed availability and throughput

---

## 2. Day surgery pathway (end-to-end)

### Key components
- Pre-assessment clinic (medical/anaesthetic/surgical)
- Patient information and expectation setting
- Admission process and checklist
- Standardised anaesthesia + multimodal analgesia
- Early mobilisation, oral intake, nausea control
- Discharge criteria + written instructions + contact route

### Figure 22.1 (concept)
Referral/decision → pre-assessment → day-of-surgery admission → procedure + recovery → discharge + follow-up.

## 3. Selection criteria

### 3.1 Surgical criteria
Day-case suitability increases with minimally invasive techniques and low physiological disruption. Key principles:
- Low risk of immediate catastrophic complication (bleeding, airway compromise)
- Pain manageable with **oral analgesics** ± local infiltration/blocks
- Patient can **eat/drink** and mobilise safely post-op

### 3.2 Patient criteria (medical)
Consider:
- Stable comorbidities (cardiorespiratory, diabetes control)
- BMI/OSA considerations (anaesthetic planning)
- Anticoagulation plan (bleeding risk)
- Frailty and functional status

### 3.3 Social criteria
Essential elements:
- Responsible adult escort and supervision for first 24 hours (typical requirement)
- Safe home environment and transport
- Access to phone and clear instructions
- Ability to understand and follow post-op advice (interpreter support if needed)

---

## 4. Preoperative preparation

### Patient information (must cover)
- Fasting and medication instructions
- Expected pain/nausea and how they will be managed
- Wound care and activity restrictions
- Red flags (bleeding, fever, worsening pain, vomiting, urinary retention)
- Follow-up plan and contact route

### Optimisation priorities
- Smoking cessation advice, hydration, control of chronic disease
- Treat anaemia and malnutrition where relevant

---

## 5. Anaesthesia and surgical principles in day surgery

### 5.1 Anaesthetic goals
- Rapid recovery with minimal residual sedation
- Effective analgesia with minimal opioids where possible
- Prophylaxis for postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV)
- Early return to oral intake and mobilisation

### 5.2 Surgical technique goals
- Minimal tissue trauma and blood loss
- Meticulous haemostasis
- Local anaesthetic infiltration where helpful
- Avoid drains where safe (facilitates discharge)

---

## 6. Postoperative recovery and discharge

### 6.1 Discharge criteria (practical)
Patient should have:
- Stable observations and appropriate consciousness level
- Controlled pain with oral analgesia plan
- Minimal nausea/vomiting
- Able to mobilise safely (baseline appropriate)
- Able to tolerate oral fluids (and food if appropriate)
- Passed urine when relevant (urology/hernia/spinal anaesthesia contexts)
- Clear written instructions + supplies + follow-up plan

### 6.2 Unplanned admission (reasons)
Common causes:
- Pain or nausea uncontrolled
- Bleeding/haematoma
- Urinary retention
- Dizziness/sedation
- Unexpected surgical findings or complications

- Careful selection (surgical + medical + social)
- Standardised pathway and MDT coordination
- Multimodal analgesia and PONV prevention
- Objective discharge criteria + safety-netting

### Box 22.2: Day case “do not discharge” red flags
- Uncontrolled pain or vomiting
- Active bleeding or expanding swelling
- New neurological deficit
- Unable to mobilise safely
- No escort/safe home situation

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Selection is everything**: surgical, medical and social criteria all matter.
2. **PONV prevention** is a key determinant of successful discharge.
3. **Discharge criteria must be objective** and include clear safety-net instructions.
4. **Minimally invasive surgery** expands day-case eligibility.
5. **Unplanned admission** is often due to pain, nausea, bleeding or urinary retention—plan to prevent these.

---

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Day case surgery** | Planned admission and discharge on the same day for a surgical procedure. |
| **True day surgery** | Day-case surgery requiring theatre facilities and/or GA (excludes outpatient/minor endoscopy categories). |
| **Unplanned admission** | Need for inpatient stay after intended day-case procedure due to clinical or social reasons. |
