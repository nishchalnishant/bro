# Chapter 21: Preoperative care including the high-risk surgical patient

## Learning objectives

To understand preoperative preparation for surgery:
- Surgical, medical and anaesthetic aspects of assessment
- How to optimise patients and identify those at higher risk
- Importance of critical care in management
- Emergency cases

To be able to organise preoperative care and the operating list

---

## 1. Introduction

### Key concept
Major surgery triggers a predictable **stress response** (inflammation, endocrine changes, hypercoagulability, fluid shifts) that increases oxygen demand and can unmask limited physiological reserve. Preoperative care aims to:
- **Gather reliable baseline information**
- **Risk stratify** (identify the high-risk patient and high-risk operation)
- **Optimise modifiable factors** (medical, functional, nutritional, psychosocial)
- Choose the **least physiologically disruptive** effective procedure/anaesthetic plan
- Plan for adverse events and appropriate postoperative location (ward vs HDU/ICU)

### Summary box 21.1: Preoperative plan for best patient outcomes
- Gather and record all relevant information
- Optimise patient condition
- Choose surgery that offers minimal risk and maximum benefit
- Ensure informed consent (see Chapter 14)
- Anticipate and plan for adverse events
- Advise adequate hydration, nutrition and exercise (prehabilitation)

## 2. Patient assessment

### 2.1 History
Key domains:
- **Comorbidities**: cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, liver disease, diabetes, frailty, OSA
- **Previous surgery/anaesthesia**: difficult airway, malignant hyperthermia, suxamethonium apnoea, PONV
- **Medications**: anticoagulants/antiplatelets, steroids, insulin, antihypertensives, opioids
- **Allergies** (true allergy vs intolerance)
- **Functional capacity**: exercise tolerance, METs, stairs, ADLs
- **Social context**: support at home, communication barriers, safeguarding considerations
- **Substance use**: smoking, alcohol, recreational drugs (affects outcomes)

### 2.2 Examination
- Vitals, BMI, hydration, anaemia signs
- Cardiorespiratory exam; baseline oxygen saturation
- Airway assessment (anaesthetic planning)
- Frailty indicators (gait speed, grip strength, sarcopenia)

### 2.3 Baseline investigations (principle)
Investigations should be **risk-driven**, not routine. Common categories:
- Bloods (FBC, U&E, LFT, coagulation, glucose, group & screen)
- ECG ± echo (selected)
- CXR (selected)
- Crossmatch if major bleeding risk

## 3. Risk stratification (patient + procedure)

### 3.1 High-risk patient concept
Risk is a function of:
- **Physiological reserve** (frailty, cardiopulmonary fitness)
- **Comorbidity burden**
- **Urgency** (emergency surgery carries higher mortality)
- **Magnitude of surgical insult** (blood loss, fluid shifts, duration)

### 3.2 Tools (exam-oriented overview)
Examples of ways risk is estimated:
- ASA physical status
- Surgical risk calculators (procedure + patient variables)
- Cardiopulmonary exercise testing (CPET) to quantify functional reserve

### 3.3 Communicating risk
Risk discussions should be:
- Individualised and balanced (benefit vs burden)
- Anchored to patient goals (quality of life, independence)
- Documented clearly (supports valid consent)

## 4. Optimisation and prehabilitation

### 4.1 Optimise chronic disease
- **Cardiac**: control angina/heart failure, review stents/antiplatelets, periop beta-blocker strategy (avoid unsafe initiation)
- **Respiratory**: treat infection, optimise COPD/asthma, consider physiotherapy and inhaler technique
- **Diabetes**: aim for safe glucose control; plan periop insulin/agents
- **Renal**: avoid nephrotoxins, correct electrolytes, plan fluid strategy
- **Anaemia**: identify cause, treat iron deficiency, plan blood conservation

### 4.2 Modifiable lifestyle factors
- Smoking cessation (reduces pulmonary and wound complications)
- Alcohol reduction (reduces withdrawal/infection/bleeding risk)
- Nutritional optimisation (see Chapter 25)
- Exercise training and inspiratory muscle training in selected patients

### 4.3 Medication management (high yield examples)
- Anticoagulants/antiplatelets: balance thrombosis vs bleeding; bridging only when indicated
- Steroids: consider perioperative stress dosing when adrenal suppression risk
- ACEi/ARB: anaesthetic plan-dependent (hypotension risk)
- SGLT2 inhibitors: periop cessation to reduce ketoacidosis risk (local policy)

## 5. Fasting, thromboprophylaxis and infection prevention

### Fasting principles (ERAS-consistent)
- Avoid unnecessary prolonged fasting
- Clear fluids may be allowed up to a short interval pre-op in many protocols
- Consider carbohydrate drinks where appropriate (reduces insulin resistance)

### Thromboprophylaxis
Assess VTE risk vs bleeding risk and plan:
- Mechanical prophylaxis
- Pharmacological prophylaxis timing

### Antibiotic prophylaxis
Correct drug, dose, timing (often within 60 minutes of incision), and redose if prolonged surgery/major blood loss.

---

## 6. Planning postoperative destination (critical care)

### Key concept
High-risk patients often benefit from planned postoperative monitoring and organ support capability.

Indications to consider HDU/ICU:
- High-risk surgery (major vascular, major abdominal, complex emergency)
- Significant comorbidities or limited reserve (frailty, severe cardiopulmonary disease)
- Anticipated major bleeding, fluid shifts, or ventilatory support needs

---

## 7. Emergency surgery: special features

### Why emergency is different
- Minimal time for optimisation
- Higher prevalence of sepsis, hypovolaemia, electrolyte derangement
- Limited fasting and incomplete information

Priorities:
- Resuscitate and stabilise (sepsis bundles, haemorrhage control)
- Senior decision-making early (risk–benefit, goals of care)
- Antibiotics, VTE prophylaxis plan, temperature management
- Early critical care involvement when needed

---

## 8. Organising the operating list

### Key principles
- Ensure pre-op checks complete (identity, consent, site marking, investigations)
- List order considers:
  - Clinical urgency
  - Infection control (clean → contaminated)
  - Special equipment/teams availability
  - Patient factors (e.g. brittle diabetes, frailty)

### Team brief
A structured “team brief” at list start improves:
- Shared situational awareness
- Anticipation of complications
- Equipment readiness

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Risk is shared**: patient + operation + urgency determine outcome.
2. **Frailty outperforms age** as a predictor—assess function, not birthday.
3. **Consent must include perioperative risk** and alternatives, tailored to patient goals.
4. **Emergency surgery**: resuscitate first; senior review early; plan critical care.
5. **Optimisation windows** are valuable—treat anaemia, stop smoking, improve nutrition.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Prehabilitation** | Pre-op interventions to improve functional capacity and resilience before major surgery. |
| **ASA status** | Anaesthetic risk classification reflecting systemic disease burden. |
| **CPET** | Cardiopulmonary exercise testing to quantify functional reserve and perioperative risk. |
| **High-risk surgery** | Procedures with high physiological stress and/or significant complication/mortality risk. |
