# Chapter 39: The hip

## Learning objectives

To understand:
- The anatomy and biomechanics of the hip and their clinical implications
- The clinical presentation, aetiology and management of common hip pathologies
- The principles of joint replacement including important complications
- The advances in surgical practice in this field

---

## 1. Anatomy and biomechanics

### Key concept
The hip is a **ball-and-socket joint** formed by the femoral head and acetabulum. It has:
- High stability from bony architecture and labrum
- Static stabilisers: capsule and ligaments
- Dynamic stabilisers: muscles acting across the joint (gluteals, iliopsoas, short rotators)

### Summary box 39.1: Anatomy
- Hip joint is ball-and-socket with static and dynamic stabilisers
- Static stabilisers include capsule, ligaments and labrum
- Dynamic stabilisers are muscles acting across the joint
- Femoral head blood supply mainly from medial circumflex femoral artery

### Clinical implications (high yield)
- Labrum deepens the socket and acts as a fluid seal aiding lubrication.
- Femoral head vascular supply vulnerability underpins avascular necrosis risk in certain pathologies.

## 2. History and examination of hip pathology

### 2.1 History
Key points:
- Site of pain: groin (often intra-articular), lateral (trochanteric), buttock/back (spine/sacroiliac)
- Mechanical symptoms: catching, clicking (labral pathology)
- Stiffness and functional limits: shoes/socks, stairs, walking distance
- Risk factors: steroids, alcohol, trauma, inflammatory disease

### 2.2 Examination
- Observe gait (Trendelenburg suggests abductor weakness)
- ROM: internal rotation reduction is common early OA
- Provocation tests for impingement
- Neurovascular and lumbar spine screen (hip vs spine differentiation)

---

## 3. Common hip pathologies

### 3.1 Osteoarthritis (OA)
Presentation:
- Groin pain, stiffness, reduced ROM
- Functional decline

Management:
- Education, weight management, physiotherapy, analgesia
- Intra-articular injections (selected)
- Arthroplasty when symptoms and disability severe

### 3.2 Greater trochanteric pain syndrome
- Lateral hip pain, tenderness over greater trochanter
- Often abductor tendinopathy/bursitis spectrum

Management: physiotherapy focused on abductors, load modification, injections selected.

### 3.3 Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) and labral pathology
- Younger adults; groin pain, mechanical symptoms, reduced flexion/rotation
- Imaging and specialist assessment for arthroscopy in selected cases

### 3.4 Avascular necrosis (AVN)
- Risk factors: steroids, alcohol, trauma, haemoglobinopathies
- Early diagnosis important; MRI sensitive

---

## 4. Imaging

### Common modalities
- X-ray: joint space narrowing, osteophytes, alignment
- MRI: AVN, labrum/cartilage, early pathology
- CT: complex bony morphology (impingement planning)

---

## 5. Joint replacement (principles)

### 5.1 Indications
- Severe pain and functional limitation despite optimal conservative therapy
- Radiographic OA supports diagnosis but symptoms drive indication

### 5.2 Key complications (must know)
- Infection
- Dislocation
- VTE
- Periprosthetic fracture
- Leg length discrepancy
- Loosening and wear (long-term)

### 5.3 Advances (overview)
Areas of progress include:
- Implant materials and bearing surfaces
- Enhanced recovery pathways
- Navigation/robotic assistance in selected centres

## Clinical pearls for exams
1. **Groin pain is hip until proven otherwise**; buttock pain often suggests spine.
2. **Loss of internal rotation** is an early OA sign.
3. **Trendelenburg gait** indicates hip abductor weakness.
4. **AVN** needs early MRI; risk factors include steroids and alcohol.
5. Hip arthroplasty risks include **dislocation and infection**—know prevention and recognition.

## Important definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Labrum** | Fibrocartilaginous rim that deepens acetabulum and helps seal joint fluid. |
| **FAI** | Femoroacetabular impingement causing abnormal contact and labral/cartilage injury. |
| **AVN** | Avascular necrosis of femoral head from disrupted blood supply. |
| **THA** | Total hip arthroplasty. |
